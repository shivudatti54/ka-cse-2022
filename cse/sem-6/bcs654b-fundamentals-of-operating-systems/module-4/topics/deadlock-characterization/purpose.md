# Learning Objectives

After studying this topic, you should be able to:

1.  List and explain the four necessary conditions for a deadlock to occur.
2.  Construct and interpret a Resource-Allocation Graph (RAG) from a given scenario.
3.  Determine the presence of a deadlock by analyzing a Resource-Allocation Graph, distinguishing between cycles involving single-instance and multi-instance resources.
4.  Differentiate between the three primary strategies for handling deadlocks: prevention, avoidance, and detection/recovery.
5.  Identify examples of deadlocks in real-world or described computing scenarios.
6.  Apply the concept of deadlock characterization to explain why a particular system state is or is not deadlocked.
