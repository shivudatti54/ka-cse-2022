# Knowledge Representation Issues

## Overview

Knowledge Representation (KR) is fundamental to AI, providing formalisms for encoding world knowledge in forms that enable computational reasoning. Effective KR systems balance competing requirements including representational adequacy, inferential efficiency, and ease of knowledge acquisition.

## Key Points

- **Representational Adequacy**: Ability to capture all necessary domain knowledge including objects, relationships, and nuances
- **Inferential Adequacy**: Capability to manipulate knowledge to derive logically sound new conclusions
- **Inferential Efficiency**: Computational ease and speed of deriving new knowledge from existing facts
- **Acquisitional Efficiency**: Ease with which new knowledge can be added by domain experts, not just KR engineers
- **Declarative vs Procedural**: Declarative represents what is true (facts), procedural represents how to do things (algorithms)
- **Expressiveness vs Tractability**: Core trade-off where more expressive languages enable complex representations but harder reasoning

## Important Concepts

- Different KR formalisms suit different problems: logic for facts, rules for heuristics, frames for structured knowledge
- Semantic networks provide intuitive graphical representation but lack standardized formal semantics
- Production rules are highly modular but can lead to rule interaction conflicts
- Handling uncertainty requires extensions like probabilistic reasoning, fuzzy logic, or non-monotonic logic
