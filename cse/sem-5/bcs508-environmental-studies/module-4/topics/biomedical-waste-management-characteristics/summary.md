# Biomedical Waste Management: Characteristics - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Biomedical Waste:** Any waste generated during diagnosis, treatment, or immunisation of humans/animals, or in related research activities.
- **Biomedical Waste Management Rules, 2016:** Indian legislation governing handling, collection, storage, transportation, and disposal of biomedical waste.
- **Occupier:** Includes hospitals, nursing homes, clinics, and any establishment generating biomedical waste.

## Important Categories (9 Categories)

1. Human Anatomical Waste (tissues, organs, body parts)
2. Animal Waste (veterinary and research)
3. Microbiological Waste (cultures, vaccines, specimens)
4. Waste Sharps (needles, blades, broken glass)
5. Discarded Medicines and Cytotoxic Drugs
6. Solid Waste (soiled dressings, gloves, catheters)
7. Liquid Waste (blood, body fluids, chemicals)
8. Incineration Ash
9. Chemical Waste (disinfectants, solvents)

## Key Characteristics

- **Physical:** Mixed composition (plastics 40-50%, paper 20-30%), moisture content 30-80%, calorific value 2,000-4,500 kcal/kg
- **Chemical:** High organic content (40-60%), hazardous chemicals (formaldehyde, heavy metals), high chlorine content
- **Biological:** Contains pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi), variable pathogen load, biodegradable components

## Important Formulas

- Daily waste generation = Number of beds × waste per bed per day
- Infectious waste percentage = Total waste × infectious fraction

## Key Points

- Approximately 15% of healthcare waste is hazardous; rest is general waste
- Color coding: Yellow (anatomical/soiled), Red (microbiological/solid), Blue/White (sharps), Black (chemical)
- Treatment methods: Incineration (≥1200°C), Autoclaving, Microwave, Chemical disinfection
- Segregation at source reduces treatment costs by 70-80%
- Sharps require special handling to prevent needlestick injuries
- Pathological waste requires incineration or deep burial
- Proper documentation and annual returns are mandatory under the Rules

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing categories - all sharps go in Category 4, not mixed with solid waste
- Treating all waste as infectious - only ~25% requires special treatment
- Mixing chemical waste with general biomedical waste
- Improper storage leading to cross-contamination
- Non-compliance with color coding and labeling requirements
