# Solid Waste Management - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Solid Waste**: Any garbage, refuse, sludges, and other discarded materials from residential, commercial, and industrial sources.
- **Municipal Solid Waste (MSW)**: Waste from residential, commercial, and institutional sources that is collected and managed by municipal authorities.
- **Source Segregation**: Separation of different waste types at the point of generation to facilitate recycling and proper treatment.
- **Composting**: Biological decomposition of organic waste under controlled aerobic conditions to produce compost.
- **Vermicomposting**: Use of earthworms to convert organic waste into high-quality compost.
- **Incineration**: High-temperature oxidation of waste to reduce volume and generate energy.
- **Landfill**: Engineered disposal site where waste is placed and compacted with daily cover.
- **3R Principle**: Reduce, Reuse, Recycle - the hierarchy of sustainable waste management.
- **Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)**: Policy approach where producers take responsibility for end-of-life management of their products.
- **E-waste**: Discarded electrical and electronic equipment containing hazardous substances.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Organic waste quantity** = Total waste × Organic content percentage
- **Volume of waste** = Mass / Bulk density
- **Landfill area required** = (Total waste × Utilization factor) / (Compaction density × Depth)
- **Diversion rate** = (Waste diverted from landfill / Total waste generated) × 100
- **Composting area** = Organic waste (tonnes) × Area requirement per tonne

## Key Points

- Indian MSW typically contains 40-60% organic matter, 10-15% plastics, 10-15% paper, with high moisture content (40-50%).
- Collection accounts for 60-80% of total solid waste management costs, making efficiency crucial.
- Sanitary landfills require engineered components: liner systems, leachate collection, gas management, and daily cover.
- Composting achieves 40-50% volume reduction and produces valuable soil amendment.
- The 3R hierarchy prioritizes reduction over reuse over recycling for maximum environmental benefit.
- E-waste contains toxic substances like lead, mercury, and cadmium requiring specialized handling.
- The Municipal Solid Wastes Rules, 2000 and E-Waste Rules, 2022 govern waste management in India.
- Waste-to-energy through incineration can achieve up to 90% volume reduction.
- Source segregation is the most critical step for successful recycling and composting programs.
- Open dumping remains prevalent in India despite regulatory mandates for scientific disposal.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing between biodegradable and non-biodegradable wastes - remember both can be managed through different processes.
- Assuming all plastics are recyclable - only certain types (PET, HDPE) have established recycling markets.
- Overlooking hazardous waste segregation - mixing hazardous with municipal waste creates serious environmental and health risks.
- Underestimating the importance of public participation - source segregation fails without community cooperation.

## Revision Tips

- Focus on the regulatory framework (MSW Rules 2000, E-Waste Rules 2022) as these are frequently tested.
