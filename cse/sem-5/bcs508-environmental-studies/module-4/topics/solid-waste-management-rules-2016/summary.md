# Solid Waste Management Rules, 2016 - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Solid Waste**: Includes household waste, commercial waste, institutional waste, street sweepings, silt, construction/demolition waste, horticulture waste, and biomedical waste (excluding hazardous and e-waste)
- **Wet Waste**: Biodegradable waste including kitchen waste, vegetables, fruits
- **Dry Waste**: Recyclable waste including paper, plastics, metals, glass
- **Domestic Hazardous Waste**: Batteries, paints, chemicals, medical waste from households
- **Urban Local Body (ULB)**: Municipal corporation, municipality, or notified area committee responsible for urban governance

## Important Formulas and Guidelines

- Minimum landfill distance: 500 meters from residential areas
- Waste processing targets: 70% by 2018, 80% by 2020
- Bin colors: Green (wet), Blue (dry), Red (hazardous)
- Design capacity buffer: 1.25 times calculated capacity for operational flexibility
- Composting reduction: Approximately 40% volume reduction during composting

## Key Points

- SWM Rules, 2016 replaced Municipal Solid Wastes (Management and Handling) Rules, 2000
- Rules came into effect from April 8, 2016
- Applicable to urban local bodies, rural areas, SEZs, industrial estates
- Mandatory three-bin segregation for waste generators
- Open burning of waste is strictly prohibited
- ULBs must develop solid waste management plan within 2 years
- User fees can be collected from waste generators
- Village panchayats responsible for rural waste management
- New landfill sites require environmental impact assessment

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Confusing SWM Rules with Hazardous Waste Rules - they are separate regulations
2. Forgetting that rules cover both urban and rural areas
3. Not remembering the color coding for waste bins
4. Assuming biomedical waste is covered under SWM Rules (it's under BMW Rules, 2016)

## Revision Tips

1. Focus on key responsibilities: Generator segregation → ULB collection/processing → Scientific disposal
2. Remember the timeline: 2016 notification → 70% processing by 2018 → 80% by 2020
3. Practice with numerical problems on composting capacity and landfill sizing
4. Understand the comparison between 2000 and 2016 rules for exam advantages
5. Memorize minimum distances and buffer requirements for landfill sites
