# Biomedical Waste Management - Sources - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Biomedical Waste:** Any waste generated during healthcare activities (diagnosis, treatment, immunization) that may pose risks to human health and the environment.
- **Healthcare Waste:** Broader term including all waste from healthcare facilities, of which biomedical waste constitutes 10-25% (infectious portion).
- **Segregation:** The process of separating waste at the point of generation into color-coded containers based on category.
- **Sharps:** Waste items that can cause puncture injuries, including needles, blades, and broken glass.

## Important Categories (BMW Rules, 2016)

| Category   | Color  | Waste Type                                          |
| ---------- | ------ | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Category 1 | Yellow | Human anatomical, soiled waste, discarded medicines |
| Category 2 | Red    | Contaminated recyclable waste (plastic, glass)      |
| Category 3 | White  | Waste sharps (needles, syringes, blades)            |
| Category 4 | Blue   | Metallic implants, glassware                        |

## Key Points

- A 100-bed hospital generates approximately 100-150 kg of biomedical waste daily (1-2 kg per bed per day).
- Segregation at source can reduce waste management costs by 80-90%.
- The Biomedical Waste Management Rules, 2016 (amended 2018, 2019) govern waste handling in India.
- Primary sources include hospitals, diagnostic laboratories, blood banks, and research centers.
- Secondary sources include dental clinics, veterinary hospitals, and home healthcare.
- Treatment methods include incineration, autoclaving, microwaving, and chemical disinfection.
- Personal protective equipment (PPE) is mandatory for all personnel handling biomedical waste.
- Common treatment facilities include common biomedical waste treatment facilities (CBWTF) that serve multiple healthcare establishments.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing categories:** Do not mix sharps with general waste or infectious waste with general waste.
2. **Ignoring segregation:** Segregation is mandatory at the point of generation, not after collection.
3. **Incorrect color coding:** Remember: Yellow (anatomical/soiled), Red (recyclable), White (sharps), Blue (glass/metallic).
4. **Underestimating waste volume:** Many students underestimate the quantity of waste generated by healthcare facilities.

## Revision Tips

1. Practice drawing the waste categorization table until it becomes automatic.
2. Remember the waste generation rate formula: Total beds × 1-2 kg = daily waste generation.
3. Review the BMW Rules, 2016 provisions and color codes before the exam.
4. Solve numerical problems related to waste calculation to gain confidence.
5. Associate each category with real healthcare scenarios (e.g., OT waste → Yellow, Injection waste → White).
