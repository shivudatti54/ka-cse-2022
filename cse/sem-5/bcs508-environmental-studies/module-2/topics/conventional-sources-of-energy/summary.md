# Conventional Sources of Energy - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Conventional Energy Sources**: Non-renewable energy sources including coal, petroleum, natural gas, nuclear energy, and in some classifications, hydroelectric power.
- **Calorific Value**: The amount of heat energy produced by complete combustion of one kilogram of fuel, measured in MJ/kg.
- **Fossil Fuels**: Carbon-based energy sources formed from ancient organic matter over millions of years (coal, petroleum, natural gas).
- **Thermal Power Plant**: Facility that converts heat energy into electrical energy using steam turbines.
- **Nuclear Fission**: Process of splitting heavy atomic nuclei (like Uranium-235) to release energy.
- **Hydroelectric Power**: Electricity generated from the kinetic energy of flowing or falling water.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Hydroelectric Power**: P = ρ × g × Q × H × η
  - P = Power (W), ρ = Water density (1000 kg/m³), g = 9.81 m/s², Q = Flow rate (m³/s), H = Head (m), η = Overall efficiency

- **Efficiency Calculation**: η = (Output Energy / Input Energy) × 100%

- **Energy from Fuel**: E = m × CV
  - E = Energy (J), m = Mass of fuel (kg), CV = Calorific Value (J/kg)

- **Load Factor**: Load Factor = Average Load / Maximum Demand

## Key Points

- Coal dominates India's electricity generation, contributing over 60% of total power production.
- India has the fourth-largest coal reserves globally, with major coalfields in Jharkhand, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, and West Bengal.
- Petroleum is refined through fractional distillation, producing fuels like gasoline, diesel, kerosene, and bitumen.
- Natural gas is the cleanest fossil fuel with lowest CO₂ emissions per unit of energy.
- Natural gas is primarily methane (CH₄) with calorific value of 35-40 MJ/m³.
- Nuclear power in India uses Uranium-based fission with pressurized heavy water reactors (PHWR).
- Hydroelectric power has the highest efficiency among all power generation methods (85-95%).
- Combined Cycle Gas Turbine (CCGT) plants achieve 50-60% efficiency, higher than simple cycle plants.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing renewable and non-renewable classifications—coal, petroleum, and natural gas are non-renewable.
- Forgetting to multiply by efficiency in power calculations—this is a common error in numerical problems.
- Mixing up octane and cetane numbers—octane is for gasoline (resistance to knocking), cetane is for diesel (ignition quality).
- Overlooking that hydroelectric power, while renewable, is often included in conventional sources for exam purposes.
