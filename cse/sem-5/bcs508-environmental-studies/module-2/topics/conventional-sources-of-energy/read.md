# Conventional Sources of Energy

## Introduction

Energy is the cornerstone of modern civilization, driving industrial growth, transportation, and everyday life. Conventional sources of energy, also known as non-renewable or traditional energy sources, have been the backbone of global energy production for over a century. These include fossil fuels such as coal, petroleum, and natural gas, as well as nuclear energy and hydroelectric power in some classifications. Understanding conventional energy sources is crucial for engineering students as it provides the foundation for analyzing energy systems, understanding environmental impacts, and developing sustainable solutions.

The significance of conventional energy sources in India's energy mix cannot be overstated. According to the Ministry of Power, India, thermal power plants (primarily coal-based) account for over 60% of electricity generation in the country. 's curriculum includes this topic to ensure that future engineers comprehend the working principles, advantages, limitations, and environmental implications of these energy sources. This knowledge becomes particularly important as India transitions toward renewable energy while still relying heavily on conventional sources to meet its growing energy demands.

## Key Concepts

### 1. Coal

Coal is one of the most abundant conventional energy sources in India and globally. It is a fossil fuel formed from the remains of ancient plants that lived millions of years ago through a process called carbonization. Coal is primarily composed of carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and sulfur.

**Types of Coal:**

- **Peat**: The lowest grade with high moisture content and low carbon content (approximately 30% carbon)
- **Lignite**: Brown coal with 30-40% carbon content, used primarily in power generation
- **Bituminous**: The most commonly used coal with 40-80% carbon content, ideal for thermal power plants
- **Anthracite**: The highest grade with over 80% carbon content, used for domestic heating and industrial processes

**Coal Gasification**: This is the process of converting coal into synthetic gas (syngas) composed of hydrogen and carbon monoxide. The chemical reaction involves passing steam and oxygen through heated coal at high pressures. The resulting syngas can be used for power generation or as feedstock for producing chemicals and fertilizers.

**Thermal Power Plant Working**: In a coal-based thermal power plant, coal is crushed and fed into the boiler where it burns to produce heat. This heat converts water into steam at high pressure. The steam drives a turbine connected to a generator, producing electricity. The efficiency of such plants typically ranges from 30-40%, with modern supercritical and ultra-supercritical plants achieving efficiencies up to 45%.

### 2. Petroleum (Oil)

Petroleum is a liquid fossil fuel formed from the remains of marine organisms over millions of years. It is a complex mixture of hydrocarbons and serves as the primary source for transportation fuels, petrochemicals, and various industrial products.

**Refining Process**: Crude oil is processed in refineries through fractional distillation, where it is heated and separated into different components based on boiling points:

- **Petroleum ether**: Boiling range 30-90°C (solvent)
- **Gasoline/Petrol**: Boiling range 40-150°C (automotive fuel)
- **Kerosene**: Boiling range 150-250°C (domestic fuel)
- **Diesel**: Boiling range 250-400°C (diesel engine fuel)
- **Lubricating oil**: Boiling range above 400°C
- **Bitumen**: Residue (road construction)

**Octane Number**: This measures the resistance of gasoline to knocking in internal combustion engines. Higher octane numbers indicate better performance. Anti-knock agents like tetraethyl lead were historically used but have been phased out due to environmental concerns, replaced by compounds like MTBE (methyl tertiary-butyl ether).

**Cetane Number**: For diesel fuel, the cetane number measures ignition quality. Higher cetane numbers indicate easier ignition and smoother combustion. Typical diesel fuels have cetane numbers between 45-55.

### 3. Natural Gas

Natural gas is primarily methane (CH₄) with small amounts of other hydrocarbons. It is considered the cleanest fossil fuel due to its lower carbon emissions and absence of particulate matter.

**Composition and Properties**:

- Methane content: 70-90%
- Ethane, propane, butane: 5-15%
- Nitrogen, carbon dioxide: Trace amounts
- Calorific value: 35-40 MJ/m³

**Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG)**: Natural gas is liquefied by cooling it to -162°C at atmospheric pressure, reducing its volume by about 600 times for efficient transportation. LNG is regasified at receiving terminals before distribution.

**Compressed Natural Gas (CNG)**: Natural gas compressed to 200-250 bar for use as automotive fuel, offering lower emissions compared to gasoline and diesel.

### 4. Nuclear Energy

Nuclear energy is derived from nuclear fission reactions where heavy atoms like Uranium-235 split into lighter fragments, releasing massive amounts of energy according to Einstein's mass-energy equivalence (E=mc²).

**Nuclear Reactor Components**:

- **Nuclear Fuel**: Enriched uranium (3-5% U-235) or plutonium
- **Moderator**: Light water, heavy water, or graphite to slow down neutrons
- **Coolant**: Water, liquid sodium, or gas to remove heat
- **Control Rods**: Boron or cadmium to absorb excess neutrons and control reaction rate
- **Shielding**: Concrete and steel to contain radiation

**Power Generation**: The heat generated from fission reactions is used to produce steam, which drives turbines similar to thermal power plants. India's nuclear power program uses pressurized heavy water reactors (PHWR) and boiling water reactors (BWR).

### 5. Hydroelectric Power

Hydropower is often classified as a renewable energy source but is included in conventional sources in some contexts. It harnesses the kinetic energy of flowing water to generate electricity.

**Types of Hydroelectric Plants**:

- **Run-of-river**: Uses natural flow of rivers without storage
- **Reservoir-based**: Stores water in dams for consistent power generation
- **Pumped storage**: Acts as giant batteries, pumping water uphill during off-peak hours and generating power during peak demand

**Key Terms**:

- **Head**: Vertical distance between water level and turbine
- **Flow Rate**: Volume of water discharged per second
- **Power Equation**: P = ρ × g × Q × H × η
  Where: P = Power (W), ρ = Water density (1000 kg/m³), g = Gravitational acceleration (9.81 m/s²), Q = Flow rate (m³/s), H = Head (m), η = Efficiency

## Examples

### Example 1: Coal Consumption Calculation for Thermal Power Plant

**Problem**: A thermal power plant has an installed capacity of 500 MW and operates at a load factor of 70%. The calorific value of coal used is 25 MJ/kg, and the plant efficiency is 35%. Calculate the daily coal consumption.

**Solution**:

**Step 1**: Calculate average power generated
Average Power = Installed Capacity × Load Factor
Average Power = 500 MW × 0.70 = 350 MW = 350 × 10⁶ W

**Step 2**: Calculate energy generated per day
Energy = Power × Time
Energy = 350 × 10⁶ W × 24 × 3600 s
Energy = 30,240 × 10⁹ J = 30.24 × 10¹² J

**Step 3**: Calculate energy required from coal (considering efficiency)
Input Energy Required = Output Energy / Efficiency
Input Energy = 30.24 × 10¹² J / 0.35
Input Energy = 86.4 × 10¹² J

**Step 4**: Calculate coal consumption
Coal Consumption = Input Energy / Calorific Value
Coal Consumption = 86.4 × 10¹² J / (25 × 10⁶ J/kg)
Coal Consumption = 3,456,000 kg = 3,456 tonnes

**Answer**: The plant consumes approximately 3,456 tonnes of coal daily.

### Example 2: Natural Gas Turbine Efficiency

**Problem**: A combined cycle gas turbine (CCGT) plant generates 400 MW of electricity. The gas turbine produces 250 MW, and the steam turbine (using waste heat) produces 150 MW. If the natural gas input is 180 kg/s with a calorific value of 45 MJ/kg, calculate the overall efficiency.

**Solution**:

**Step 1**: Calculate total power output
Total Output = 250 MW + 150 MW = 400 MW = 400 × 10⁶ W

**Step 2**: Calculate energy input per second
Energy Input = Mass flow rate × Calorific Value
Energy Input = 180 kg/s × 45 × 10⁶ J/kg
Energy Input = 8.1 × 10⁹ J/s = 8.1 × 10⁹ W

**Step 3**: Calculate efficiency
Efficiency = (Output / Input) × 100
Efficiency = (400 × 10⁶ / 8.1 × 10⁹) × 100
Efficiency = 4.94%

Wait, let me recalculate:

Efficiency = (400 × 10⁶ W) / (8.1 × 10⁹ W) = 0.0494 = 4.94%

This seems low. Let me verify:
Actually, the efficiency calculation should consider the energy input rate:
Input Power = 180 × 45 = 8,100 MJ/s = 8,100 MW
Efficiency = 400/8100 × 100 = 49.4%

**Answer**: The overall efficiency of the CCGT plant is approximately 49.4%.

### Example 3: Hydroelectric Power Calculation

**Problem**: A hydroelectric power plant operates with a head of 100 meters and a flow rate of 50 m³/s. If the turbine efficiency is 90% and generator efficiency is 95%, calculate the power generated.

**Solution**:

**Step 1**: Use the power equation
P = ρ × g × Q × H × ηoverall

Where ηoverall = Turbine efficiency × Generator efficiency
ηoverall = 0.90 × 0.95 = 0.855

**Step 2**: Calculate power
P = 1000 kg/m³ × 9.81 m/s² × 50 m³/s × 100 m × 0.855
P = 1000 × 9.81 × 50 × 100 × 0.855
P = 419,347,500 W
P ≈ 419 MW

**Answer**: The hydroelectric power plant generates approximately 419 MW of power.

## Exam Tips

1. **Diagram Knowledge**: Be thorough with labeled diagrams of thermal power plants, nuclear reactors, and hydroelectric plants. frequently asks diagram-based questions.

2. **Formulas to Memorize**: Remember the hydroelectric power formula (P = ρghQη), efficiency calculations, and calorific value formulas. These are commonly asked in numerical problems.

3. **Classification Differences**: Clearly understand the difference between renewable and non-renewable energy sources. Hydroelectric power is often debated—know the perspective.

4. **Environmental Impacts**: Study the environmental concerns associated with each conventional source—air pollution from coal, oil spills, methane leakage from natural gas, and nuclear waste disposal.

5. **India-Specific Data**: Remember key statistics about India's energy sector—coal reserves, petroleum production, nuclear power plants in India, and major hydroelectric projects.

6. **Advantages and Limitations**: For each conventional source, list at least two advantages and two limitations. Comparative analysis questions are common.

7. **Recent Developments**: Stay updated on India's energy policies, coal gasification initiatives, and nuclear power agreements, as may include current affairs in exams.

8. **Numerical Practice**: Solve at least 5-10 numerical problems from each topic. Energy calculations, efficiency problems, and power generation problems are frequently asked.

9. **Terminology**: Know technical terms like calorific value, flue gas, boiler efficiency, reactor criticality, and load factor.

10. **Flow Diagrams**: Understand the step-by-step processes in coal handling, petroleum refining, and power generation cycles.
