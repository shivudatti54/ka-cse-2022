# Water-Borne Diseases and Water-Induced Diseases - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Water-Borne Diseases**: Illnesses caused by pathogenic microorganisms transmitted through contaminated water; examples include cholera, typhoid, dysentery, and hepatitis A

- **Water-Induced Diseases**: Diseases where water serves as a breeding ground for disease vectors (mainly mosquitoes); includes malaria, dengue fever, and filariasis

- **Fecal-Oral Route**: The transmission pathway where pathogens from infected feces enter water and are subsequently ingested

- **Water Quality Parameters**: pH (6.5-8.5), turbidity, coliform count (zero per 100ml for safe water), DO, and BOD

## Important Formulas and Concepts

- **Coliform Standard**: 0 coliforms per 100ml for drinking water
- **pH Range**: 6.5-8.5 for safe drinking water
- **Four Classifications**: Water-borne, water-washed, water-based, water-vector diseases

## Key Points

- Water-borne diseases spread through contaminated drinking water; water-induced diseases spread via insect vectors breeding in water

- Cholera is caused by Vibrio cholerae; typhoid by Salmonella typhi; dysentery by Shigella or Entamoeba

- Malaria is transmitted by Anopheles mosquitoes; dengue by Aedes mosquitoes

- Prevention of water-borne diseases requires water treatment, sanitation, and hygiene practices

- Prevention of water-induced diseases requires vector control—eliminating breeding sites, using insecticide-treated nets, and residual spraying

- Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) is critical for treating diarrheal diseases like cholera

- India runs the National Vector Borne Disease Control Programme (NVBDCP) for malaria and dengue control

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing water-borne with water-induced diseases—water-induced involves vectors, not ingestion

- Assuming all water-related diseases are water-borne; remember water-washed diseases occur due to insufficient water quantity

- Forgetting that malaria and dengue are classified as water-induced, not water-borne

- Overlooking the importance of vector control in preventing water-induced diseases
