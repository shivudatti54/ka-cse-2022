# Water-Borne Diseases and Water-Induced Diseases

## Introduction

Water is essential for life, but it can also be a carrier of numerous diseases that affect human health on a global scale. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), approximately 2 billion people lack access to safely managed drinking water, and 485,000 deaths annually are directly attributed to diarrheal diseases caused by unsafe water and inadequate sanitation. In India, water-related diseases remain a significant public health challenge, particularly in rural areas and urban slums where water quality monitoring and sanitation infrastructure are inadequate.

Water-borne diseases are caused by pathogenic microorganisms that are transmitted through contaminated water. These diseases primarily affect the gastrointestinal system and include illnesses such as cholera, typhoid, dysentery, and hepatitis A. On the other hand, water-induced diseases are those where water serves as a breeding ground for disease vectors, particularly mosquitoes. Diseases like malaria, dengue fever, and filariasis fall under this category. Understanding the distinction between these two categories is crucial for implementing effective prevention and control measures, which is why this topic holds significant importance in environmental science and public health studies for engineering students.

## Key Concepts

### Classification of Water-Related Diseases

Water-related diseases can be broadly classified into four categories:

**Water-Borne Diseases**: These are caused by ingesting water contaminated with pathogenic microorganisms. The contamination typically occurs when human or animal feces containing bacteria, viruses, or parasites enter water sources. Common water-borne diseases include:

- **Cholera**: Caused by Vibrio cholerae bacterium, characterized by severe watery diarrhea and dehydration
- **Typhoid Fever**: Caused by Salmonella typhi, spread through contaminated food and water
- **Dysentery**: Caused by Shigella (bacillary dysentery) or Entamoeba histolytica (amoebic dysentery)
- **Hepatitis A**: Viral infection affecting the liver, transmitted through fecal-oral route
- **Giardiasis**: Caused by Giardia lamblia parasite

**Water-Washed Diseases**: Also known as water-scarce diseases, these occur due to insufficient water quantity for proper hygiene rather than water contamination. Examples include trachoma, scabies, and typhus.

**Water-Based Diseases**: These diseases involve organisms that require water for part of their life cycle. Schistosomiasis (bilharzia) is a classic example, caused by parasitic flukes that breed in freshwater snails.

**Water-Vector Diseases**: These are water-induced diseases where insects that breed in water transmit the pathogens. Mosquitoes are the primary vectors, transmitting:

- **Malaria**: Caused by Plasmodium parasites (P. falciparum, P. vivax, P. malariae, P. ovale)
- **Dengue Fever**: Caused by dengue virus transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes
- **Filariasis**: Caused by Wuchereria bancrofti nematode worms

### Transmission Mechanisms

The transmission of water-borne diseases follows the fecal-oral route, also known as the enteric transmission pathway. This cycle involves:

1. **Contamination**: Human or animal feces containing pathogens enter water sources
2. **Consumption**: Humans consume contaminated water without proper treatment
3. **Infection**: Pathogens colonize the intestinal tract or other organs
4. **Excretion**: The infected person sheds pathogens in feces, continuing the cycle

For water-induced diseases, the transmission involves:

1. **Vector Breeding**: Standing water provides breeding sites for mosquitoes
2. **Vector Bite**: Infected mosquitoes bite humans to feed on blood
3. **Parasite/Vector Transmission**: Pathogens enter the human bloodstream
4. **Disease Development**: The pathogen multiplies and causes disease symptoms

### Water Quality Parameters

Understanding water quality is essential for disease prevention. Key parameters include:

- **pH**: Normal range 6.5-8.5; deviation indicates contamination
- **Turbidity**: Indicates suspended particles that may harbor pathogens
- **Coliform Count**: Indicates fecal contamination; zero coliforms per 100ml is safe
- **Dissolved Oxygen (DO)**: Low DO levels indicate organic pollution
- **Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD)**: Measures organic matter content

## Examples

### Example 1: Cholera Outbreak Analysis

A town reports 150 cases of cholera within a week. Investigation reveals the municipal water supply was contaminated due to a damaged pipe allowing sewage entry.

**Solution Steps**:

1. **Identify Source**: Damaged pipe near sewage line contaminated the water supply
2. **Pathogen**: Vibrio cholerae (comma-shaped bacterium)
3. **Symptoms**: Severe watery diarrhea, vomiting, rapid dehydration
4. **Immediate Actions**:

- Issue boil-water advisory
- Repair damaged pipeline
- Distribute oral rehydration salts (ORS)
- Hospitalize severe cases for IV fluid administration

5. **Prevention Measures**:

- Regular inspection of water infrastructure
- Chlorination of water supply
- Public awareness about safe water practices

### Example 2: Malaria Control in a Village

A village reports high malaria incidence. The area has numerous stagnant water bodies, including puddles, unused tires, and water storage containers.

**Solution Steps**:

1. **Identify Breeding Sites**: Standing water in puddles, tires, containers
2. **Vector**: Anopheles mosquitoes (malaria vector)
3. **Control Measures**:

- Drain stagnant water bodies
- Apply larvicides (like temephos) to remaining water bodies
- Distribute insecticide-treated mosquito nets (ITNs)
- Indoor residual spraying (IRS) with insecticides

4. **Community Participation**: Train villagers to eliminate breeding sites
5. **Monitoring**: Regular surveillance of mosquito population and malaria cases

### Example 3: Typhoid Fever Case Study

A 25-year-old student experiences continuous fever, headache, and abdominal pain. Blood tests confirm Salmonella typhi infection.

**Solution Steps**:

1. **Diagnosis**: Widal test positive for Salmonella typhi
2. **Transmission**: Likely from contaminated street food or untreated water
3. **Treatment**: Antibiotics (ciprofloxacin or azithromycin)
4. **Prevention**:

- Drink only boiled or filtered water
- Eat properly cooked food
- Vaccination for typhoid (Typhoid Vi polysaccharide vaccine)
- Proper sanitation and handwashing

## Exam Tips

1. **Remember the Four Types**: Water-borne, water-washed, water-based, and water-vector diseases are frequently asked in exams. Know examples of each category.

2. **Fecal-Oral Route**: Always explain transmission through the fecal-oral route for water-borne diseases—this is a common exam question.

3. **Distinguish Between Water-Borne and Water-Induced**: Water-borne requires ingestion of contaminated water; water-induced involves vector breeding in water.

4. **Know the Pathogens**: Memorize the causative organisms—Vibrio cholerae for cholera, Salmonella typhi for typhoid, Plasmodium for malaria.

5. **Prevention Methods**: Be prepared to list prevention and control measures—water treatment, sanitation, vector control, vaccination.

6. **Water Quality Standards**: Remember that zero coliforms per 100ml is the standard for safe drinking water.

7. **Indian Context**: Mention National Vector Borne Disease Control Programme (NVBDCP) and Swachh Bharat Mission when discussing India's approach to water-related diseases.

8. **Diagrams**: Practice drawing the transmission cycle diagrams for water-borne and water-induced diseases.
