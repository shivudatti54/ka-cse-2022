# Sustainability - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Sustainability**: Meeting present needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet theirs
- **Sustainable Development**: Development that balances economic growth, environmental protection, and social equity
- **Triple Bottom Line**: Framework evaluating performance across People, Planet, and Profit (3P framework)
- **Green Computing**: Environmentally responsible use of computing resources including energy efficiency and e-waste management
- **Carbon Footprint**: Total greenhouse gas emissions caused directly and indirectly by an individual, organization, or product
- **Circular Economy**: Economic system aimed at eliminating waste through continuous use, reuse, and recycling

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Energy Consumption Calculation**: Servers × Power (W) × Hours = Total Wh → Convert to MWh for large scale
- **Carbon Footprint Reduction**: Energy saved (kWh) × Emission factor (kg CO2/kWh) = CO2 reduction
- **Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)**: Include environmental costs alongside traditional capital and operational expenses for sustainability analysis

## Key Points

1. Sustainability operates on three pillars: Economic viability, Environmental protection, and Social responsibility
2. The Brundtland Commission (1987) established the modern definition of sustainable development
3. UN's 17 Sustainable Development Goals provide a global framework for sustainability efforts
4. Green computing specifically addresses IT sector's environmental impact through energy efficiency and e-waste management
5. Sustainable business practices often result in long-term cost savings despite higher initial investments
6. Corporate sustainability integrates ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) factors into business strategy
7. Software optimization can significantly reduce energy consumption and carbon emissions
8. E-waste management is crucial for CSE professionals due to rapid technology obsolescence

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing sustainability with environmentalism alone (social and economic aspects are equally important)
- Assuming sustainability is always costly—neglecting long-term financial benefits
- Overlooking the role of technology in both causing and solving sustainability challenges
- Ignoring e-waste and focusing only on energy consumption in computing

## Revision Tips

1. Memorize the three pillars (Economic, Environmental, Social) and recall examples for each
2. Remember the Brundtland definition word-for-word as it frequently appears in exams
3. Review case studies of tech companies implementing sustainability for practical understanding
4. Practice calculating energy consumption and carbon footprint for technology scenarios
5. Connect sustainability concepts to CSE applications like green software development and data center efficiency
