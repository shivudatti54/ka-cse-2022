# Structure of Ecosystem

## Introduction

Ecosystem is the fundamental unit of ecology, representing the interactive relationship between living organisms (biotic components) and their non-living environment (abiotic components). The term "ecosystem" was coined by Sir Arthur Tansley in 1935. Understanding the structure of ecosystem is crucial for engineering students as it provides insight into environmental sustainability, resource management, and ecological balance—topics increasingly important in modern technological development and industrial practices.

An ecosystem can range from a small pond to a large forest, or even the entire biosphere. The structure of ecosystem defines how energy flows, how nutrients cycle, and how different organisms interact within their environment. For students, comprehending ecosystem structure forms the foundation for understanding environmental impact assessments, sustainable development, and ecological conservation—essential knowledge for responsible engineering practices.

## Key Concepts

### 1. Components of Ecosystem

**Biotic Components (Living Elements):**
Biotic components are categorized into three main groups based on their nutritional relationships:

- **Producers (Autotrophs):** Organisms that can synthesize their own food through photosynthesis (plants, algae, cyanobacteria) or chemosynthesis (certain bacteria). They form the base of all food chains and are responsible for converting solar energy into chemical energy.

- **Consumers (Heterotrophs):** Organisms that depend on other organisms for food. They are classified as:
- Primary Consumers (Herbivores): Feed directly on producers
- Secondary Consumers (Carnivores): Feed on herbivores
- Tertiary Consumers: Feed on secondary consumers
- Omnivores: Feed on both plants and animals
- Decomposers (Saprotrophs): Break down dead organic matter (fungi, bacteria)

**Abiotic Components (Non-Living Elements):**

- **Physical factors:** Sunlight, temperature, water, humidity, wind
- **Chemical factors:** pH, soil composition, nutrients, dissolved oxygen
- **Edaphic factors:** Soil characteristics affecting organism distribution

### 2. Energy Flow in Ecosystem

Energy flow is unidirectional and non-cyclic. It enters the ecosystem as solar radiation and flows through different trophic levels. Key principles include:

- **10% Law of Energy Transfer:** Only about 10% of energy from one trophic level is transferred to the next level. The remaining 90% is used for metabolic processes, heat loss, and respiration.

- **Trophic Levels:** Each feeding level in a food chain represents a trophic level. Energy decreases as it moves up the trophic levels.

### 3. Food Chain and Food Web

**Food Chain:** A linear sequence showing how energy flows from producers to successive consumers.
Example: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Hawk

**Food Web:** An interconnected network of food chains showing multiple feeding relationships at each trophic level. Food webs demonstrate the complexity of ecological interactions and provide stability to ecosystems.

### 4. Ecological Pyramids

Ecological pyramids represent the structure of ecosystem graphically:

- **Pyramid of Numbers:** Shows the number of organisms at each trophic level. In a grassland, numbers decrease upward (more grass than grasshoppers). In a parasitic food chain, numbers may increase upward.

- **Pyramid of Biomass:** Represents the total dry weight or energy content at each trophic level. Typically pyramid-shaped, with maximum biomass at producer level.

- **Pyramid of Energy:** Always upright, showing energy transfer between trophic levels. Energy decreases at each successive level.

### 5. Biogeochemical Cycles

Nutrients circulate between biotic and abiotic components through:

- **Water Cycle (Hydrological Cycle):** Evaporation, precipitation, transpiration, runoff
- **Carbon Cycle:** Photosynthesis, respiration, decomposition, combustion
- **Nitrogen Cycle:** Nitrogen fixation, nitrification, denitrification, assimilation
- **Phosphorus Cycle:** Weathering, uptake, decomposition, sedimentation

### 6. Types of Ecosystems

- **Terrestrial Ecosystems:** Forest, grassland, desert, tundra
- **Aquatic Ecosystems:** Freshwater (lakes, rivers), Marine (oceans, coral reefs)
- **Artificial Ecosystems:** Human-created systems like crop fields, aquariums, cities

## Examples

### Example 1: Energy Calculation in Food Chain

Consider a food chain: Grass → Rabbit → Fox

Given: Grass produces 10,000 kcal of energy

**Solution:**

- Producer level (Grass): 10,000 kcal
- Primary consumer (Rabbit): 10% of 10,000 = 1,000 kcal
- Secondary consumer (Fox): 10% of 1,000 = 100 kcal

**Explanation:** This demonstrates the 10% law. Only 100 kcal reaches the top consumer, while 9,900 kcal is lost as heat and metabolic processes.

### Example 2: Identifying Trophic Levels

In a pond ecosystem:

- Phytoplankton (Producer)
- Zooplankton (Primary Consumer)
- Small Fish (Secondary Consumer)
- Large Fish (Tertiary Consumer)
- Decomposer Bacteria (Saprotroph)

**Task:** Classify each organism and explain their role.

**Solution:**

- Phytoplankton: Autotrophic producers, conduct photosynthesis
- Zooplankton: Herbivorous primary consumers
- Small Fish: Carnivorous secondary consumers
- Large Fish: Apex predator, tertiary consumer
- Decomposers: Break down dead matter, recycle nutrients

### Example 3: Analyzing Ecological Pyramid

A forest ecosystem has:

- 5,000 trees (Producers)
- 500 herbivorous insects (Primary consumers)
- 50 predatory birds (Secondary consumers)
- 5 hawks (Tertiary consumers)

**Analysis:**

- Pyramid of Numbers: Inverted initially (1 tree supports many insects)
- Pyramid of Energy: Always upright (less energy at higher levels)
- Biomass: Decreases upward in a typical forest

## Exam Tips

1. **Remember the 10% Law:** This is frequently asked in exams. Only 10% of energy transfers between trophic levels.

2. **Differentiate between food chain and food web:** Food chains are linear; food webs are interconnected and show multiple relationships.

3. **Know the three types of ecological pyramids:** Numbers, biomass, and energy. Remember that only the energy pyramid is always upright.

4. **Understand biogeochemical cycles:** Carbon, nitrogen, water, and phosphorus cycles are important for understanding ecosystem functioning.

5. **Distinguish biotic and abiotic components:** Biotic = living; Abiotic = non-living (temperature, light, soil, water).

6. **Remember key definitions:** Producer, consumer, decomposer, trophic level—these are often asked as short answer questions.

7. **Application-based questions:** Engineers may be asked about ecosystem structure in context of environmental impact or sustainable development.

8. **Energy flow direction:** Remember energy flows unidirectionally from producers to consumers, while nutrients cycle through biogeochemical processes.
