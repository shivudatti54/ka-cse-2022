# Wetlands - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Wetlands**: Transitional ecosystems between terrestrial and aquatic environments characterized by water-saturated soils and hydrophytic vegetation
- **Ramsar Convention**: International treaty signed in 1971 for wetland conservation; defines wetlands as areas with water static or flowing, fresh, brackish, or salt
- **Hydric soils**: Soils saturated with water during growing season, developing anaerobic conditions
- **Hydrophytic vegetation**: Plants adapted to grow in waterlogged conditions

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Pollutant Removal Efficiency**: (Initial Load - Final Load) / Initial Load × 100%
- **Carbon Sequestration**: 1 tonne of carbon = 3.67 tonnes of CO₂ equivalent
- **Peak Flow Attenuation**: Reduction in maximum flow rate when runoff is stored and released gradually

## Key Points

- Wetlands cover approximately 6% of Earth's land surface; India has about 4.1 million hectares
- India has 75 Ramsar sites covering approximately 1.33 million hectares
- Three main types: Inland (freshwater marshes, swamps, bogs), Coastal (mangroves, salt marshes), Human-made (reservoirs, ponds, rice fields)
- Key ecological functions: water filtration, flood control, groundwater recharge, carbon sequestration, habitat provision
- Wetlands (Conservation and Management) Rules, 2017 govern wetland conservation in India
- Major threats: habitat loss through drainage, pollution from industrial/agricultural sources, invasive species, climate change

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing marshes with swamps (marshes have herbaceous plants; swamps have woody vegetation)
- Forgetting that mangroves are coastal wetlands, not inland wetlands
- Overlooking human-made wetlands as legitimate wetland types
- Neglecting the economic values when focusing only on ecological importance
