# Water Pollution

## Introduction

Water pollution is one of the most critical environmental issues facing humanity in the 21st century. It refers to the contamination of water bodies such as rivers, lakes, oceans, groundwater, and wetlands, making them harmful to human health and aquatic life. Water is an essential resource for all living organisms, and its pollution has far-reaching consequences on ecosystems, agriculture, industry, and public health.

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), approximately 2 billion people globally use contaminated water sources, leading to millions of deaths annually from water-borne diseases. In India, rapid industrialization and urbanization have significantly exacerbated water pollution problems, with major rivers like the Ganga, Yamuna, and Godavari facing severe contamination threats. The prevention and control of water pollution is therefore not just an environmental necessity but a fundamental requirement for sustainable development and human survival.

For engineering students, understanding water pollution is crucial as they will be involved in designing industrial processes, infrastructure, and systems that can either contribute to or help mitigate water pollution. The curriculum emphasizes this topic to create awareness about environmental responsibilities and to equip future engineers with the knowledge needed to implement sustainable solutions.

## Key Concepts

### Sources of Water Pollution

Water pollution originates from two primary sources: point sources and non-point sources.

**Point Sources** are identifiable and localized pollution sources where contaminants enter water bodies at a specific point. These include:

- Industrial discharge outlets (factories, manufacturing plants)
- Sewage treatment plant outfalls
- Agricultural drainage pipes
- Mining operations
- Oil spills from specific locations

**Non-Point Sources** are diffuse pollution sources where contaminants enter water bodies over wide areas. These are harder to control and include:

- Agricultural runoff (fertilizers, pesticides, herbicides)
- Urban runoff (oil, heavy metals, debris)
- Atmospheric deposition (acid rain)
- Soil erosion carrying sediments
- Livestock waste runoff

### Types of Water Pollutants

Water pollutants can be classified into several categories based on their nature and origin:

**Physical Pollutants**: These include suspended solids, sediments, thermal waste (hot water from power plants), and radioactive materials. Suspended solids reduce light penetration in water bodies, affecting photosynthesis and aquatic plant life.

**Chemical Pollutants**: This category encompasses heavy metals (lead, mercury, cadmium, arsenic), organic compounds (pesticides, herbicides, industrial chemicals), nutrients (nitrogen and phosphorus), acids/alkalis, and salts. Chemical pollutants can be toxic, carcinogenic, or cause biochemical oxygen demand (BOD) increases.

**Biological Pollutants**: These include pathogenic microorganisms such as bacteria (E. coli, Salmonella), viruses, parasites, and fungi. They primarily originate from sewage and agricultural waste, causing water-borne diseases like cholera, typhoid, and dysentery.

**Nutrient Pollutants**: Excess nitrogen and phosphorus from fertilizers and sewage cause eutrophication - a process where nutrient enrichment leads to excessive algal growth, depleting oxygen and creating "dead zones."

### Water Quality Parameters

Several parameters are used to assess water quality:

1. **Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD)**: The amount of oxygen required by microorganisms to decompose organic matter. Higher BOD indicates greater organic pollution.

2. **Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD)**: Similar to BOD but measures oxygen required to chemically oxidize all organic matter, including those not biodegradable by microorganisms.

3. **pH Value**: Measures acidity or alkalinity of water. Most aquatic life thrives in pH range of 6.5-8.5.

4. **Dissolved Oxygen (DO)**: The amount of oxygen dissolved in water, essential for aquatic organisms. DO levels below 5 mg/L stress aquatic life.

5. **Total Dissolved Solids (TDS)**: Measures all inorganic salts and organic matter dissolved in water. High TDS indicates contamination.

6. **Turbidity**: Measures the clarity of water, indicating the amount of suspended particles.

### Effects of Water Pollution

**On Human Health**: Contaminated water causes water-borne diseases including cholera, diarrhea, dysentery, typhoid, hepatitis A, and parasitic infections. Heavy metal contamination can lead to neurological disorders, kidney damage, and cancer.

**On Aquatic Ecosystems**: Pollution disrupts aquatic food chains, causes fish kills, destroys coral reefs, and reduces biodiversity. Eutrophication creates hypoxic (low oxygen) conditions leading to massive fish deaths.

**On Agriculture**: Polluted water used for irrigation can contaminate soil, reduce crop yields, and introduce toxins into the food chain.

**On Economy**: Water pollution increases treatment costs, reduces tourism revenue, damages fisheries, and requires expensive remediation efforts.

### Water Pollution Control Measures

**Industrial Treatment**: Industries must implement wastewater treatment before discharge. Common methods include primary treatment (sedimentation), secondary treatment (biological processes), and tertiary treatment (chemical/physical processes like filtration, chlorination).

**Sewage Treatment**: Municipal sewage undergoes similar treatment stages. Primary treatment removes settleable solids, secondary treatment uses microorganisms to degrade organic matter, and tertiary treatment removes nutrients and pathogens.

**Agricultural Best Practices**: Using organic fertilizers, practicing crop rotation, creating buffer strips near water bodies, and implementing precision agriculture can reduce agricultural runoff.

**Legal Framework**: In India, the Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974 establishes legal standards for water quality and regulates industrial discharges. The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) and State Pollution Control Boards (SPCBs) monitor and enforce these regulations.

## Examples

### Example 1: Calculating BOD and Determining Pollution Level

**Problem**: A water sample has an initial dissolved oxygen (DO) concentration of 8 mg/L. After incubating for 5 days at 20°C, the DO concentration drops to 2 mg/L. Determine the BOD and classify the water quality.

**Solution**:

**Step 1**: Identify the formula
BOD = Initial DO - Final DO

**Step 2**: Substitute the values
BOD = 8 mg/L - 2 mg/L = 6 mg/L

**Step 3**: Interpretation

- BOD < 1 mg/L: Clean water
- BOD 1-2 mg/L: Light pollution
- BOD 2-8 mg/L: Moderate pollution
- BOD > 8 mg/L: Heavy pollution

**Result**: With BOD = 6 mg/L, the water is moderately polluted, likely receiving organic waste from domestic sources or light industrial discharge.

### Example 2: Determining Eutrophication Risk

**Problem**: A lake receives agricultural runoff containing 50 kg/day of phosphorus. If the lake volume is 10 million cubic meters and the critical phosphorus concentration for eutrophication is 0.01 mg/L, determine if the lake is at risk.

**Solution**:

**Step 1**: Convert lake volume to liters
Volume = 10 million m³ = 10 × 10⁶ m³ = 10 × 10⁹ liters

**Step 2**: Calculate phosphorus concentration from runoff
Concentration = Mass / Volume = 50,000,000 mg / 10 × 10⁹ L = 0.005 mg/L

**Step 3**: Compare with critical concentration
Phosphorus concentration (0.005 mg/L) < Critical concentration (0.01 mg/L)

**Result**: Currently, the lake is below the eutrophication threshold. However, with continuous accumulation and considering other sources, regular monitoring is essential.

### Example 3: Wastewater Treatment Efficiency

**Problem**: Industrial wastewater enters a treatment plant with BOD of 200 mg/L and leaves with BOD of 20 mg/L. Calculate the treatment efficiency percentage.

**Solution**:

**Step 1**: Use the efficiency formula
Efficiency (%) = [(Inlet BOD - Outlet BOD) / Inlet BOD] × 100

**Step 2**: Substitute values
Efficiency = [(200 - 20) / 200] × 100 = (180 / 200) × 100 = 90%

**Result**: The treatment plant achieves 90% BOD removal efficiency, which meets standard treatment requirements for industrial effluents before discharge into water bodies.

## Exam Tips

1. **Understand BOD vs COD**: Remember BOD measures biodegradable organic matter using microorganisms (takes 5 days), while COD measures all organic matter chemically (takes 2-3 hours). COD values are always higher than BOD for the same sample.

2. **Difference between Point and Non-Point Sources**: In exams, point sources are those with identifiable discharge points (pipes, outlets), while non-point sources are diffuse (agricultural runoff, rainfall runoff).

3. **Eutrophication Process**: Remember the sequence - excess nutrients → algal bloom → algae die → bacteria decompose → consume oxygen → aquatic life dies → dead zone created.

4. **Water Quality Standards**: For drinking water, pH should be 6.5-8.5, DO > 6 mg/L, BOD < 2 mg/L, and TDS < 500 mg/L. Know these standard values.

5. **Important Acts**: The Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act of 1974 is fundamental. Remember it established CPCB and SPCBs for pollution control.

6. **DO and Aquatic Life**: Less than 5 mg/L DO stresses aquatic organisms; less than 2 mg/L leads to fish death; anaerobic conditions (< 0.5 mg/L) cause anaerobic decomposition releasing methane and hydrogen sulfide.

7. **Heavy Metals**: Know common toxic heavy metals - Mercury (Minamata disease), Cadmium (Itai-itai disease), Lead (neurological damage), Arsenic (skin lesions, cancer).

8. **Treatment Methods**: For the exam, remember the three stages: Primary (physical removal of solids), Secondary (biological decomposition), and Tertiary (chemical/advanced treatment for nutrients and pathogens).
