# Air Pollution

## Introduction

Air pollution is one of the most critical environmental issues facing humanity in the 21st century. It refers to the introduction of harmful substances into the Earth's atmosphere, including gases, particulate matter, and biological molecules, that cause damage to living organisms and the environment. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), air pollution is responsible for approximately 7 million deaths annually worldwide, making it a leading environmental health risk.

In the context of engineering and technology, understanding air pollution is essential for CSE students because:

1. Industries and manufacturing processes are major contributors to air pollution
2. Software and IT companies consume significant energy, contributing to carbon footprints
3. Environmental regulations require engineers to design eco-friendly solutions
4. Emerging technologies like IoT and AI are being used for pollution monitoring and control

This module covers the types, sources, effects, and control measures of air pollution, which are essential for engineers to develop sustainable solutions and comply with environmental regulations.

## Key Concepts

### 1. Definition and Classification of Air Pollution

**Air Pollution** is defined as the presence of substances in the atmosphere that are harmful to humans, animals, plants, and property.

**Primary Pollutants**: These are substances directly emitted from sources such as vehicles, factories, and construction activities. Examples include:

- Carbon monoxide (CO)
- Nitrogen oxides (NOx)
- Sulfur dioxide (SO₂)
- Particulate matter (PM)
- Lead (Pb)

**Secondary Pollutants**: These are formed through chemical reactions between primary pollutants and atmospheric components. Examples include:

- Ground-level ozone (O₃)
- Smog
- Acid rain components

### 2. Major Air Pollutants and Their Sources

**Carbon Monoxide (CO)**: A colorless, odorless gas produced by incomplete combustion of fossil fuels. Primary sources include:

- Vehicle exhaust (especially in congested traffic)
- Industrial processes
- Residential heating systems
- Tobacco smoke

**Nitrogen Oxides (NOx)**: Produced at high temperatures during combustion. Sources include:

- Vehicle engines (major contributor)
- Power plants
- Industrial boilers
- Aircraft engines

**Sulfur Dioxide (SO₂)**: Released when sulfur-containing fuels are burned. Major sources:

- Coal-fired power plants
- Oil refineries
- Metal processing industries
- Ships using heavy fuel oil

**Particulate Matter (PM)**: Tiny particles suspended in the air, classified as:

- PM10: Particles with diameter ≤ 10 micrometers
- PM2.5: Fine particles with diameter ≤ 2.5 micrometers
  Sources include vehicle exhaust, construction activities, industrial processes, and natural sources like dust and wildfires.

**Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs)**: Emitted from paints, solvents, gasoline, and industrial processes. They contribute to ground-level ozone formation.

**Lead (Pb)**: Historically from leaded gasoline, now mainly from:

- Metal processing industries
- Battery manufacturing
- Some industrial processes

### 3. Effects of Air Pollution

**On Human Health**:

- Respiratory diseases (asthma, bronchitis, lung cancer)
- Cardiovascular problems
- Eye and skin irritation
- Neurological disorders (especially in children)
- Premature death

**On Environment**:

- Acid rain: SO₂ and NOx combine with water vapor to form sulfuric and nitric acids, damaging forests, aquatic life, and buildings
- Global warming: Greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O) trap heat in the atmosphere
- Ozone depletion: CFCs and other compounds damage the ozone layer
- Eutrophication: Excess nutrients from atmospheric deposition cause algal blooms in water bodies

**On Materials and Structures**:

- Corrosion of metals
- Weathering of stone and concrete
- Damage to paint and rubber
- Deterioration of textiles

### 4. Air Quality Standards and Monitoring

**Air Quality Index (AQI)**: A scale from 0-500 that indicates how clean or polluted the air is. Categories include:

- 0-50: Good (green)
- 51-100: Moderate (yellow)
- 101-150: Unhealthy for sensitive groups (orange)
- 151-200: Unhealthy (red)
- 201-300: Very unhealthy (purple)
- 301-500: Hazardous (maroon)

**National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS)** in India: The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has established standards for key pollutants including SO₂, NO₂, PM10, PM2.5, CO, O₃, Pb, and NH₃.

### 5. Control Measures for Air Pollution

**Source Control**:

- Using cleaner fuels (natural gas, biofuels, hydrogen)
- Implementing emission standards for vehicles and industries
- Installing pollution control equipment (scrubbers, electrostatic precipitators, catalytic converters)
- Process modification to reduce pollutant formation

**Technological Solutions**:

- **Electrostatic Precipitators**: Remove particulate matter from industrial emissions
- **Scrubbers**: Remove gases like SO₂ and HCl from exhaust streams
- **Catalytic Converters**: Convert harmful gases (CO, NOx, hydrocarbons) in vehicle exhaust to less harmful substances
- **Fabric Filters (Baghouses)**: Capture fine particles from industrial emissions
- **Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR)**: Reduces NOx emissions from power plants and diesel engines

**Urban Planning and Management**:

- Promoting public transportation
- Developing pedestrian-friendly cities
- Implementing congestion pricing
- Creating green belts and urban forests

**Legislative Measures**:

- The Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981 in India
- Environment Protection Act, 1986
- Bharat Stage VI emission standards (equivalent to Euro VI)

## Examples

### Example 1: Calculating Air Quality Index for PM2.5

**Problem**: If the 24-hour average concentration of PM2.5 is 55 μg/m³, determine the AQI category and health advisory.

**Solution**:

Step 1: Refer to the AQI breakpoints for PM2.5

- Good: 0-12 μg/m³
- Moderate: 12.1-35.4 μg/m³
- Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups: 35.5-55.4 μg/m³

Step 2: Compare the given concentration (55 μg/m³) with breakpoints

Step 3: Since 55 μg/m³ falls in the range 35.5-55.4 μg/m³, it falls in the "Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups" category.

**Answer**: The AQI is in the "Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups" category (orange). Sensitive groups (elderly, children, people with respiratory conditions) should limit prolonged outdoor exertion.

### Example 2: Emission Control in a Thermal Power Plant

**Problem**: A coal-fired power plant emits 500 tons/day of SO₂. Calculate the efficiency required for a flue gas desulfurization (FGD) system to meet the regulatory limit of 100 tons/day.

**Solution**:

Step 1: Determine the required reduction

- Current emission = 500 tons/day
- Allowed emission = 100 tons/day
- Required reduction = 500 - 100 = 400 tons/day

Step 2: Calculate efficiency

- Efficiency = (Required reduction / Current emission) × 100
- Efficiency = (400 / 500) × 100 = 80%

**Answer**: The FGD system must achieve at least 80% SO₂ removal efficiency to comply with regulations.

### Example 3: Catalytic Converter Efficiency

**Problem**: A vehicle's catalytic converter reduces CO emissions from 2.5% to 0.05% by volume in exhaust gases. Calculate the percentage reduction in CO emissions.

**Solution**:

Step 1: Identify initial and final concentrations

- Initial CO = 2.5%
- Final CO = 0.05%

Step 2: Calculate reduction

- Reduction = Initial - Final = 2.5 - 0.05 = 2.45%

Step 3: Calculate percentage reduction

- Percentage reduction = (2.45 / 2.5) × 100 = 98%

**Answer**: The catalytic converter achieves 98% reduction in CO emissions.

## Exam Tips

1. **Remember the difference between primary and secondary pollutants**: Primary pollutants are emitted directly, while secondary pollutants form from reactions in the atmosphere.

2. **Know the major sources of each pollutant**: Vehicles are the largest source of NOx and CO; power plants emit SO₂ and particulate matter.

3. **Understand the health effects**: Link specific pollutants to diseases (e.g., PM2.5 causes respiratory problems, CO reduces oxygen-carrying capacity of blood).

4. **Be familiar with control technologies**: Electrostatic precipitators for particulates, scrubbers for gases, catalytic converters for vehicle emissions.

5. **Know India's environmental regulations**: The Air Act of 1981, Environment Protection Act of 1986, and Bharat Stage emission standards.

6. **Understand the concept of AQI**: Remember the six categories and their health implications.

7. **Study the effects on environment**: Acid rain, global warming, ozone depletion, and eutrophication are important topics.

8. **Know the units of measurement**: Pollutants are measured in ppm (parts per million), ppb (parts per billion), or μg/m³.

9. **Be aware of indoor air pollution**: Include sources like cooking fuels, building materials, and biological pollutants.

10. **Understand greenhouse effect**: Know the major greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O) and their sources.
