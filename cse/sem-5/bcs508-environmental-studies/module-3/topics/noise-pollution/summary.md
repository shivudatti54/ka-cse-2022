# Noise Pollution - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Noise Pollution:** Unwanted or harmful sound that disrupts activities, causes annoyance, or damages health, typically exceeding 55 dB according to WHO guidelines.

- **Sound:** A mechanical vibration propagating through a medium as longitudinal waves, characterized by frequency (Hz), amplitude, wavelength, and speed (~343 m/s in air).

- **Decibel (dB):** A logarithmic unit measuring sound pressure level (SPL) using the formula: SPL = 20 log₁₀(P/P₀), where P₀ = 20 μPa.

- **Equivalent Sound Level (Leq):** Energy-average sound level over a specified period, accounting for varying noise exposures.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Inverse Square Law:** Doubling distance from a point source reduces SPL by 6 dB (intensity reduces by factor of 4).

- **OSHA Exchange Rate:** For every 5 dB increase in noise, permissible exposure time halves (90 dB = 8 hrs, 95 dB = 4 hrs, 100 dB = 2 hrs).

- **Time-Weighted Average:** Leq = 10 log₁₀[(1/T) Σ(10^(Li/10) × ti)]

## Key Points

1. Major noise sources include road traffic (85-90 dB), aircraft (120+ dB), industrial machinery (75-110 dB), and construction equipment (80-100 dB).

2. Hearing damage begins at prolonged exposure to 85 dB; 120 dB causes immediate pain and potential injury.

3. India Noise Rules 2000: Residential zones limited to 55 dB (day) and 45 dB (night); Silence zones to 50 dB (day) and 40 dB (night).

4. Control measures follow hierarchy: Source control → Path control (barriers, green belts) → Receptor control (ear protection, insulation).

5. Noise affects wildlife by disrupting communication, navigation, and breeding patterns.

6. Chronic exposure leads to cardiovascular diseases, sleep disorders, and psychological stress beyond hearing loss.

7. Green belts can reduce noise by 5-10 dB through absorption and scattering.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Confusing sound intensity with sound pressure—intensity follows inverse square law while pressure follows inverse distance law.

2. Assuming decibels add linearly—they add logarithmically; two identical sources increase by only 3 dB, not 6 dB.

3. Ignoring the 10 dB nighttime penalty in Ldn calculations for regulatory compliance.

4. Forgetting that lower frequencies propagate longer distances with less attenuation.

## Revision Tips

1. Memorize key decibel reference points: 0 (hearing threshold), 40 (library), 60 (conversation), 85 (danger), 120 (pain).

2. Practice numerical problems on distance-based attenuation and time-weighted averaging.

3. Remember the three control strategies with concrete examples: source (quiet machinery), path (noise barriers), receptor (earplugs).

4. Focus on Indian regulatory standards as exam questions frequently test specific zone-wise limits.
