# E-Waste and Hazardous Properties - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **E-Waste**: Discarded electrical or electronic devices including computers, mobile phones, televisions, and appliances that have reached end-of-life.
- **Hazardous Substances**: Toxic materials in electronics including lead, mercury, cadmium, hexavalent chromium, brominated flame retardants, and polyvinyl chloride.
- **Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)**: Regulatory principle holding manufacturers accountable for end-of-life management of their products.
- **Green Computing**: Sustainable approach to computing that minimizes environmental impact through efficient resource use and waste reduction.
- **Informal Recycling**: Unregulated e-waste processing activities, often involving primitive methods causing severe pollution.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **E-Waste Hierarchy**: Prevention > Reuse > Recycling > Energy Recovery > Safe Disposal
- **Collection Target Formula**: Producer must collect and channelize e-waste based on weight equal to preceding year's sales
- **Recycling Efficiency**: Percentage of materials recovered compared to total waste processed

## Key Points

1. E-waste is the fastest-growing waste stream globally, increasing at 3-4% annually.
2. A single computer contains hazardous materials including lead (2-4 kg), mercury, cadmium, and brominated flame retardants.
3. India generated approximately 2 million metric tons of e-waste in recent years, ranking among top five countries globally.
4. E-Waste Management Rules 2022 mandate Extended Producer Responsibility for electronics manufacturers.
5. Lead exposure causes neurological damage, especially in children; mercury affects brain development.
6. Improper e-waste disposal through landfilling and open burning releases toxic substances into environment.
7. Recycling one million mobile phones recovers 35 kg of gold, 350 kg of silver, and 15 kg of palladium.
8. The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) oversees e-waste management implementation in India.
9. Sustainable design and longer product lifespans are preferred strategies for e-waste minimization.
10. CSE professionals must integrate lifecycle thinking in designing and deploying technological solutions.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Confusing e-waste with general solid waste - e-waste requires specialized handling due to toxic content.
2. Overlooking the health impacts on workers in informal recycling sectors.
3. Assuming all e-waste recycling is safe - improper processes can cause worse pollution.
4. Ignoring the economic value of e-waste - precious metals recovery makes formal recycling viable.
5. Underestimating the role of consumers in proper e-waste disposal through authorized channels.
