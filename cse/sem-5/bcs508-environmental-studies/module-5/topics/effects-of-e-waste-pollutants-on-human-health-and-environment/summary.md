# Effects of E-Waste Pollutants on Human Health and Environment - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **E-Waste**: Discarded electrical or electronic devices at end-of-life including computers, mobile phones, televisions, and appliances
- **Heavy Metals**: Toxic metals like lead, mercury, cadmium, and chromium that persist in environment and bioaccumulate
- **Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs)**: Chemicals like dioxins, furans, and PCBs that resist degradation and accumulate in fatty tissues
- **Bioaccumulation**: Gradual buildup of toxins in organisms over time
- **Biagnification**: Increasing toxin concentration at higher trophic levels in food chain

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Lead Content**: CRT monitors contain 2-4 kg lead per unit
- **E-Waste Generation**: Global ~50 million tonnes/year, India ~2 million tonnes/year
- **Recycling Rate**: Only 17% formally recycled globally
- **Dioxin Toxicity**: Lethal at microgram per kilogram levels (parts per trillion concern)

## Key Points

1. E-waste contains over 1000 substances, many toxic: lead (CRTs, solder), mercury (displays), cadmium (batteries), chromium (casings)

2. Human health effects: neurological damage (lead, mercury), cancer (dioxins, chromium), reproductive disorders (PBDEs, phthalates), kidney damage (cadmium)

3. Environmental effects: soil contamination (permanent heavy metal accumulation), water pollution (leachate contamination), air pollution (dioxin release from burning)

4. Informal recycling in India involves open burning, acid leaching, and manual dismantling without protective equipment

5. Children and e-waste workers face highest exposure risk with severe health consequences

6. E-waste (Management) Rules, 2022 mandate Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) for manufacturers

7. Proper e-waste disposal requires authorized recyclers with environmental clearances

8. Sustainable solutions: 3R principle (Reduce, Reuse, Recycle), collection mechanisms, awareness generation

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing formal and informal recycling**: Informal recycling causes much higher pollution and health impacts due to primitive techniques

2. **Underestimating low-dose exposure risks**: POPs cause harm at parts per trillion concentrations due to bioaccumulation

3. **Ignoring secondary exposure**: People not directly handling e-waste still affected through food chain contamination

4. **Wrong disposal methods**: Throwing e-waste with municipal waste leads to landfill contamination; burning releases toxic fumes

## Revision Tips

1. Memorize the "big four" heavy metals (lead, mercury, cadmium, chromium) with their sources and health effects as this is most examined

2. Remember that children are most vulnerable to e-waste pollutants due to developing nervous systems and higher exposure per body weight

3. Practice drawing a simple diagram showing exposure pathways: e-waste → environment → food chain → human health

4. Know the difference between acute (high dose, immediate) and chronic (low dose, long-term) exposure effects

5. Review the E-Waste Rules 2022 provisions briefly - this adds relevance to Indian context questions
