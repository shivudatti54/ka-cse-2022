# Global Context of E-Waste - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **E-Waste**: Discarded electrical and electronic equipment that has reached its end-of-life stage, including computers, mobile phones, televisions, and appliances.
- **Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)**: Policy principle that makes producers responsible for end-of-life management of their products, including collection and recycling.
- **Transboundary Movement**: International shipment of e-waste, often from developed to developing countries, governed by the Basel Convention.
- **Informal Sector**: Unorganized e-waste collection and recycling operations using manual methods, prevalent in developing countries.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Collection Rate Formula**: (Total E-Waste Collected / Total E-Waste Generated) × 100
- **Projected Growth Rate**: Global e-waste expected to increase from 53.6 million tonnes (2019) to 74.7 million tonnes (2030), representing approximately 40% growth.
- **Global Collection Target**: Only 17.4% of global e-waste was formally collected and recycled as of 2019.

## Key Points

1. Global e-waste generation reached 53.6 million metric tonnes in 2019, with Asia accounting for 45% of total generation.

2. The Basel Convention (1989) is the primary international framework governing transboundary movement of hazardous wastes, requiring prior informed consent for exports.

3. The European Union's WEEE Directive is a landmark example of Extended Producer Responsibility, mandating producers finance e-waste collection and recycling.

4. India's E-Waste (Management) Rules 2016 (amended 2018, 2022) mandate producers meet specific collection and recycling targets through PROs.

5. Informal e-waste recycling in India employs approximately 100,000 workers, using primitive methods that cause severe environmental and health impacts.

6. E-waste contains hazardous substances (lead, mercury, cadmium) as well as valuable resources (gold, silver, copper, rare earth elements).

7. The Basel Convention amendments (2019) specifically addressed e-waste, requiring prior informed consent for certain e-waste shipments.

8. Only 17.4% of global e-waste is formally recycled, with the remainder landfilled, incinerated, or processed informally.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing the Basel Convention with other environmental agreements; it specifically addresses hazardous waste transboundary movements.
- Assuming all e-waste recycling in India is illegal; there is a growing formal sector operating under regulatory oversight.
- Overlooking the economic value in e-waste; it is both a liability and a resource opportunity.
- Ignoring the informal sector's role; solutions must consider the livelihoods of workers in this sector.

## Revision Tips

1. Focus on key statistics: memorize the global e-waste quantity, collection rate, and growth projections.

2. Understand the difference between formal and informal recycling sectors and their respective environmental impacts.

3. Review India's E-Waste Rules thoroughly, as they are directly relevant to the Indian context.

4. Practice explaining EPR in simple terms, as this is a frequently examined concept.

5. Connect the global context to local challenges by understanding how international frameworks apply to India.
