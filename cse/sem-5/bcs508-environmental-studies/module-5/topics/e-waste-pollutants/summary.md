# E-Waste and Pollutants - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **E-Waste**: Discarded electrical or electronic devices that have reached end-of-life, including computers, phones, printers, and peripheral devices.
- **Hazardous Substances**: Materials in e-waste that pose risks to human health and environment, including heavy metals, persistent organic pollutants, and toxic compounds.
- **EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility)**: Regulatory framework making manufacturers accountable for collecting and processing their products at end-of-life.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- Average computer composition: 30% metals, 40% plastics, 15% organic compounds, 15% trace elements
- CRT monitor lead content: 2-4 kg per unit
- Mercury in CFL: 3-5 mg per lamp
- Hazardous threshold: Lead >0.1%, Mercury >0.1%, Cadmium >0.01% by weight

## Key Points

- Four major heavy metals in e-waste: Lead (neurological damage), Mercury (brain damage), Cadmium (kidney/bone damage), Hexavalent Chromium (carcinogenic)
- Organic pollutants include PBDEs, PCBs, dioxins, and furans
- E-waste grows at 30% annually in India, one of the fastest waste streams
- E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 govern e-waste handling in India
- Proper recycling prevents lead leaching into groundwater
- Open burning of e-waste produces dioxins and furans
- Informal recycling exposes workers to toxic substances through direct handling
- Waste hierarchy: Prevention > Reuse > Recycling > Energy Recovery > Disposal

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing primary pollution (direct release) with secondary pollution (formed during processing)
- Assuming all e-waste recycling is safe - informal recycling is highly hazardous
- Overlooking the importance of EPR in regulatory frameworks
- Underestimating the toxicity of seemingly small amounts of mercury and cadmium

## Revision Tips

1. Create a table mapping each heavy metal to its sources, health effects, and common devices containing them.
2. Remember the key provisions of the 2022 E-waste Rules through the acronym PPE (Producer Responsibility, Collection targets, EPR).
3. Focus on the three main pollution pathways: air (burning), water (leaching), soil (direct contact).
4. Practice explaining why improper e-waste disposal is both an environmental and health issue.
