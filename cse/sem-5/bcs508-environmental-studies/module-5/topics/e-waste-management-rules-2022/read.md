# E-Waste Management Rules 2022

## Introduction

Electronic waste, commonly known as e-waste, refers to discarded electrical or electronic devices such as computers, mobile phones, televisions, refrigerators, and other consumer electronics. With the rapid advancement of technology and increasing consumption patterns, e-waste has become one of the fastest-growing waste streams globally. India generates approximately 1.7 million tonnes of e-waste annually, ranking among the top five e-waste generating countries in the world.

The E-Waste Management Rules, 2022 were notified by the Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change on 2nd November 2022, superseding the earlier E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2016. These updated rules aim to address the challenges posed by improper e-waste disposal, promote formal recycling, ensure environmentally sound management of electronic waste, and bring India in line with international best practices. The rules emphasize Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR), making producers accountable for collecting and recycling a specified percentage of their electronic products at the end of their useful life.

## Key Concepts

### Definition of E-Waste under the Rules

Under the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022, e-waste means "electrical and electronic equipment, including all components, consumables, parts and spares which become obsolete during the course of their use." This definition covers a wide range of products including IT and telecommunications equipment, consumer electricals and electronics, lamps, medical devices, monitoring and control instruments, and automatic dispensers.

### Scope and Applicability

The rules apply to every producer, manufacturer, refurbisher, dismantler, and recycler involved in the production, sale, transfer, or processing of electrical and electronic equipment. The rules also define the responsibilities of consumers and bulk consumers in ensuring proper e-waste management. The coverage includes 21 categories of electrical and electronic equipment, classified based on their end-of-life processing requirements.

### Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)

EPR is the cornerstone of the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022. Under EPR, producers are responsible for the end-of-life management of their products. Producers must register on the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) portal and set up collection mechanisms such as take-back systems, collection centers, or through authorized recyclers. The rules specify collection targets that increase progressively year by year, starting from 60% of the quantity of EEE placed in the market in the first year, reaching 70% in the second year, and 80% from the third year onwards.

### Producer Responsibility Plan and Targets

Every producer must prepare a Producer Responsibility Plan (PRP) outlining their e-waste collection and recycling strategy. The plan must include details of collection mechanisms, target setting, awareness generation activities, and the financial allocation for implementation. Collection targets are calculated based on the weight of electrical and electronic equipment placed in the market during the previous year, ensuring accountability and measurable outcomes.

### Role of Producers, Consumers, and Recyclers

**Producers** are responsible forEEE collection targets, creating awareness, establishing take-back systems, and ensuring that their products meet the mandatory Mark for E-waste collection. **Consumers** are mandated to dispose of e-waste only through authorized collection centers or recyclers and are prohibited from disposing of e-waste in municipal waste bins. **Refurbishers** must obtain authorization from the State Pollution Control Board and ensure that refurbished equipment meets environmental and safety standards. **Recyclers** must have EPR authorization and follow environmentally sound recycling practices as per guidelines.

### E-Waste Collection Mechanism

The rules mandate the establishment of a robust collection network. Producers must ensure that their collection centers are accessible to consumers and are prominently displayed. Collection can be done through: (i) Direct collection from consumers, (ii) Collection through authorized dealers, (iii) Collection through designated collection centers, (iv) Through producer-owned or operated return points, and (v) Through buy-back schemes. The rules also allow for channelization of e-waste through Municipal Solid Waste collection systems, though with proper segregation.

### Import Regulations

The E-Waste Management Rules, 2022 prohibit the import of electrical and electronic equipment into India for disposal. Import of e-waste for recycling, processing, or recovery is also restricted except for import of components or raw materials for legitimate recycling operations under the rules. This provision aims to prevent India from becoming a dumping ground for developed nations' e-waste.

### Role of Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) and State Pollution Control Boards (SPCBs)

CPCB is the nodal agency for implementing the rules at the national level. It maintains an online portal for registration, filing of annual returns, and monitoring compliance. SPCBs monitor implementation within their jurisdictions, grant authorizations to recyclers and refurbishers, and take action against violations. The rules establish a monitoring mechanism through quarterly and annual reporting requirements.

### Reduction of Hazardous Substances

The rules align with RoHS (Restriction of Hazardous Substances) provisions, restricting the use of lead, mercury, cadmium, hexavalent chromium, polybrominated biphenyls, and polybrominated diphenyl ethers in electrical and electronic equipment to protect human health and environment.

## Examples

### Example 1: Calculating Collection Target for a Producer

**Problem:** A mobile phone manufacturer placed 500 tonnes of mobile phones in the Indian market in Year 1. Calculate their collection target for Year 2.

**Solution:**

- According to E-Waste Management Rules 2022, the collection target for Year 2 is 70% of the quantity placed in the market in the previous year.
- Year 1 placed quantity: 500 tonnes
- Collection target for Year 2: 70% × 500 = 350 tonnes
- Therefore, the manufacturer must ensure collection and recycling of at least 350 tonnes of e-waste in Year 2.

### Example 2: Identifying Violations under the Rules

**Problem:** A consumer disposes of their old computer monitor in the municipal dustbin. Identify the violations and consequences.

**Solution:**

- **Violation by Consumer:** Under Rule 8, consumers are prohibited from disposing of e-waste in municipal waste bins or handing it over to unauthorized scrap dealers.
- **Consequence:** Consumers may face penalties as specified by the State Pollution Control Board.
- The consumer should have disposed of the monitor at an authorized collection center or through the producer's take-back scheme.
- Additionally, if the municipality improperly handles this e-waste, the municipal body may also be held responsible for violation of waste handling norms.

### Example 3: EPR Implementation by a Television Manufacturer

**Problem:** An LED TV manufacturer wants to comply with E-Waste Management Rules 2022. Outline the steps they must take.

**Solution:**

1. **Registration:** Register on the CPCB portal and obtain EPR registration.
2. **Producer Responsibility Plan:** Prepare and submit a PRP to CPCB within 90 days.
3. **Collection Targets:** Set up collection mechanisms to achieve 60% collection target in Year 1.
4. **Collection Centers:** Establish designated collection centers or partner with authorized recyclers.
5. **Awareness:** Conduct awareness programs about e-waste collection and recycling.
6. **Labeling:** Ensure all products bear the E-waste Mark indicating proper disposal instructions.
7. **Annual Returns:** File annual returns by April 30th each year.
8. **Recycler Partnership:** Partner with authorized recyclers to ensure environmentally sound processing.

## Exam Tips

1. **Remember the notification date:** The E-Waste Management Rules, 2022 were notified on 2nd November 2022 and came into force from 1st April 2023.

2. **Collection targets are crucial:** Memorize the progressive targets - 60% (Year 1), 70% (Year 2), and 80% (from Year 3 onwards).

3. **EPR is the key concept:** Understand Extended Producer Responsibility thoroughly as it's the backbone of these rules and frequently asked in exams.

4. **Know the 21 categories:** Be familiar with the categories of covered under the rules - IT & telecom, consumer electricals, medical devices, etc.

5. **Consumer responsibilities:** Remember that consumers cannot dispose of e-waste in municipal bins and must use authorized collection centers.

6. **Penalties and enforcement:** Understand that violations can lead to penalties under the Environmental Protection Act, 1986 and imprisonment in severe cases.

7. **Prohibited activities:** Note that import of e-waste for disposal is strictly prohibited under these rules.

8. **CPCB role:** The Central Pollution Control Board is the nodal agency for implementation and maintains the online portal.

9. **RoHS alignment:** The rules restrict hazardous substances like lead, mercury, cadmium, and hexavalent chromium.

10. **Difference from 2016 rules:** Be prepared to explain 2-3 key differences between the 2022 rules and the 2016 rules.
