# E-Waste Management - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **E-Waste**: Discarded electrical and electronic devices that have reached the end of their useful life, including computers, mobile phones, televisions, and other electronic equipment.

- **Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)**: A policy principle where manufacturers bear responsibility for collecting and recycling their products at end-of-life.

- **E-Waste Recycling**: The process of recovering valuable materials from electronic waste while safely disposing of hazardous substances.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Collection Rate** = (E-waste Collected / Total E-waste Generated) × 100

- **Material Recovery Rate** = (Material Recovered / Total Material in E-waste) × 100

- **E-Waste Management Hierarchy**: Source Reduction → Reuse → Recycling → Safe Disposal

## Key Points

1. E-waste is one of the fastest-growing waste streams globally, with India generating approximately 1.7 million tonnes annually.

2. E-waste contains both valuable materials (copper, gold, silver, aluminum) and hazardous substances (lead, mercury, cadmium, chromium).

3. Improper e-waste disposal leads to severe environmental impacts: soil contamination, water pollution, and air pollution.

4. The E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, govern e-waste management in India, mandating EPR for producers.

5. Formal recycling sectors use environmentally sound technologies, while informal sectors often employ harmful methods.

6. Source reduction, reuse, and recycling are preferred over landfilling or incineration.

7. Computer equipment, consumer electronics, and office equipment are major categories of e-waste.

8. Lead in cathode ray tubes, mercury in switches, and cadmium in batteries are primary health hazards.

9. Recycling 1 tonne of e-waste can recover up to 1.5 kg of gold and 130 kg of copper.

10. India prohibits e-waste import except for re-export purposes under the 2022 rules.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Confusing e-waste with general waste - e-waste requires specialized handling due to hazardous components.

2. Assuming all e-waste recycling is environmentally friendly - informal recycling often causes more harm than landfilling.

3. Ignoring the economic value of e-waste - proper recycling recovers valuable materials worth significant amounts.

4. Underestimating health impacts - even small quantities of hazardous substances can cause severe health problems.

5. Not understanding EPR - many students confuse producer responsibility with consumer responsibility.
