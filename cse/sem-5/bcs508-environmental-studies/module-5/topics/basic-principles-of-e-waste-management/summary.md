# E-Waste Management - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **E-Waste**: Electrical and electronic equipment that has reached the end of its useful life and is discarded by the original owner, including computers, mobile phones, televisions, refrigerators, and other electronic devices.

- **Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)**: A policy principle making producers financially and operationally responsible for collecting and recycling their products at the end of their life cycle.

- **Authorised Recycler**: A facility that holds valid approvals from State Pollution Control Boards to handle, process, and recycle e-waste in an environmentally sound manner.

- **Refurbishment**: The process of repairing and restoring used electronic equipment to working condition for resale or reuse.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Waste Hierarchy**: Prevention → Minimisation → Reuse → Recycling → Disposal (in order of preference)

- **EPR Target**: Under E-Waste Rules 2022, producers must ensure minimum 10% of their sales are recycled through authorised channels

- **Material Recovery Rate**: Percentage of valuable materials (metals, plastics) successfully recovered from e-waste during recycling processes

## Key Points

- E-waste contains toxic substances including lead, mercury, cadmium, and chromium that pose serious health and environmental risks.

- India generates approximately 3.2 million tonnes of e-waste annually, with only a fraction being formally recycled.

- The E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 govern e-waste handling in India, mandating producer responsibility and channelisation through authorised collection centres.

- Proper e-waste management follows a hierarchy: prevention is most preferred, disposal is least preferred.

- Informal e-waste recycling in India causes severe health issues including neurological damage, respiratory problems, and cancer among workers.

- Valuable materials like gold, silver, copper, and rare earth elements can be recovered through proper recycling, reducing the need for mining.

- Data security must be ensured before disposing of electronic equipment containing sensitive information.

- Consumer awareness and participation are critical for successful e-waste management programs.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Do not mix e-waste with regular municipal waste, as it contaminates landfills with hazardous substances.

- Avoid disposing of batteries and printed circuit boards in regular trash due to their toxic content.

- Never attempt informal recycling methods like open burning or acid leaching, which release toxic fumes and chemicals.

- Do not ignore data security concerns—always wipe or physically destroy storage media before disposal.

- Avoid dealing with unauthorized scrap dealers who do not follow environmental compliance procedures.
