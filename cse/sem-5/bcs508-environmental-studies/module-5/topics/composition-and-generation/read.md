# Composition and Generation of E-Waste

## Introduction

Electronic waste, commonly known as e-waste, encompasses discarded electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) that has reached the end of its useful life. The composition of e-waste is remarkably complex, containing over 1,000 different substances, many of which are hazardous to human health and the environment. Understanding the composition and generation patterns of e-waste is fundamental to developing effective management strategies and policies.

The global generation of e-waste has been increasing at an alarming rate, driven by rapid technological advancement, decreasing costs of electronic devices, and changing consumer behavior. According to the Global E-Waste Monitor, approximately 53.6 million metric tonnes of e-waste were generated worldwide in 2019, and this figure continues to grow by approximately 2-3 million tonnes annually. This exponential increase presents significant challenges for waste management systems, environmental protection, and public health.

In developing countries like India, the challenge is particularly acute due to the informal sector's dominant role in e-waste handling, inadequate infrastructure, and limited regulatory enforcement. The composition of e-waste varies significantly based on the category of electronic equipment, geographic region, and socio-economic factors, making comprehensive analysis essential for effective management planning.

## Key Concepts

### Composition of E-Waste

E-waste comprises a diverse mixture of materials that can be broadly categorized into two groups: hazardous components and recyclable components.

**Hazardous Materials:**
Heavy metals constitute a significant portion of hazardous e-waste components. Lead is found in cathode ray tubes (CRTs) and printed circuit boards (PCBs), typically comprising 1-3% of total e-waste weight. Mercury is present in switches, relays, and fluorescent lamps. Cadmium is used in batteries and semiconductor devices. Hexavalent chromium is used as a corrosion inhibitor in steel. These heavy metals can leach into soil and groundwater when e-waste is improperly disposed of, causing severe environmental contamination.

**Toxic Substances:**
Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) are released during incomplete combustion of plastics. Polybrominated diphenyl ethers (PBDEs) and polybrominated biphenyls (PBBs) are used as flame retardants in plastics and textiles. Polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) are found in capacitors and transformers. These substances are persistent organic pollutants (POPs) that bioaccumulate in the food chain and cause various health problems including cancer, endocrine disruption, and neurological damage.

**Precious and Rare Earth Metals:**
E-waste contains significant quantities of valuable metals including gold (approximately 0.1% in PCBs), silver, copper, palladium, and platinum. Rare earth elements such as neodymium, dysprosium, and praseodymium are present in magnets and electronic components. The concentration of these materials in e-waste is often higher than in natural ores, making e-waste a potential "urban mine" for resource recovery.

**Plastic Components:**
Plastics constitute approximately 20-30% of e-waste by weight. Common types include acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS), polycarbonate (PC), and polyvinyl chloride (PVC). While plastics are technically recyclable, the presence of flame retardants and other additives complicates the recycling process.

### Generation Sources and Patterns

**Major Categories of E-Waste Generating Equipment:**

Large household appliances (refrigerators, washing machines, air conditioners) account for approximately 40% of total e-waste generation by weight. Small household appliances (vacuum cleaners, microwaves, toasters) contribute around 12%. Information and communication technology (ICT) equipment including computers, laptops, mobile phones, and printers constitute approximately 15%. Consumer electronics such as televisions, radios, and audio equipment contribute roughly 10%. Lighting equipment, including fluorescent lamps and LEDs, accounts for about 8%. Electrical and electronic tools constitute approximately 5%, while medical devices, monitoring instruments, and automated dispensers make up the remaining 10%.

**Factors Influencing Generation Patterns:**

The obsolescence rate significantly impacts e-waste generation. Technological advancement has led to progressively shorter product lifecycles, with many electronic devices becoming obsolete within 2-5 years of purchase. Planned obsolescence, both psychological and technological, drives consumers to replace functional devices with newer models. Economic factors including decreasing costs of electronics and increasing disposable incomes have expanded consumer access to electronic devices. Additionally, changing lifestyle patterns have increased the number of electronic devices per household.

### Indian Context

In India, e-waste generation has been growing at a rate of approximately 30% annually. The country generates around 3.2 million tonnes of e-waste annually (as of 2021), with major contributing states including Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, and Gujarat. Delhi-NCR region alone generates significant quantities of e-waste, primarily from the IT and telecommunications sectors.

The informal sector handles approximately 95% of e-waste in India, with approximately 4.5 million workers engaged in collection, dismantling, and material recovery activities. This informal sector, while providing livelihoods, often employs primitive and environmentally unsound practices that pose significant health and environmental risks.

## Examples

### Example 1: Composition Analysis of a Personal Computer

Consider the composition breakdown of a typical personal computer weighing approximately 20 kg:

- Ferrous metals (steel casing, mounting brackets): 8 kg (40%)
- Non-ferrous metals (aluminum, copper): 3 kg (15%)
- Plastics (casing, connectors): 4 kg (20%)
- Printed circuit boards: 2 kg (10%)
- Glass (CRT or monitor): 1.5 kg (7.5%)
- Hazardous materials (batteries, capacitors): 0.5 kg (2.5%)
- Other materials (cables, rubber): 1 kg (5%)

From the PCB component (2 kg), the precious metal content includes approximately 2 grams of gold, 10 grams of silver, and 100 grams of copper. The hazardous content includes approximately 50 grams of lead, 5 grams of cadmium, and traces of mercury and hexavalent chromium.

### Example 2: Mobile Phone Waste Generation Calculation

A city with 10 million inhabitants, with an average of 2 mobile phones per person and an average replacement cycle of 2 years, generates:

Annual mobile phone waste = (Population × Phones per person) / Replacement cycle
= (10,000,000 × 2) / 2 = 10,000,000 phones per year

Assuming average phone weight of 150 grams:
Annual weight of mobile phone waste = 10,000,000 × 0.15 = 1,500,000 kg = 1,500 tonnes

Each phone contains approximately 0.03 grams of gold, 0.3 grams of silver, and 15 grams of copper. Thus, annually, this city generates approximately 300 kg of gold, 3,000 kg of silver, and 150,000 kg of copper from discarded mobile phones alone.

### Example 3: E-Waste Composition from Television Sets

A CRT television weighing 30 kg contains approximately:
- Lead: 1.5-2.5 kg (5-8%) mainly in glass and solder
- Barium: 0.5-1 kg (2-3%) in CRT glass
- Mercury: 0.005-0.02 kg (0.02-0.07%) in fluorescent coatings
- Cadmium: 0.01-0.05 kg (0.03-0.17%) in batteries and semiconductors
- Phosphors: 0.2-0.5 kg containing rare earth elements
- Plastics: 8-10 kg including PVC and flame-retarded ABS

In contrast, an LED television of similar size (25 kg) contains less hazardous materials but presents challenges for recycling due to the complex integration of components and use of specialized materials.

## Exam Tips

1. **Memorize key percentages**: The composition of e-waste by weight typically includes approximately 40% ferrous metals, 15% non-ferrous metals, 20-30% plastics, and 10% PCBs. Remember these standard proportions for exam questions.

2. **Identify hazardous components**: Be prepared to list at least 5 hazardous substances found in e-waste (lead, mercury, cadmium, hexavalent chromium, PBDEs, PCBs) and explain their environmental impacts.

3. **Understand generation trends**: The global e-waste generation rate of approximately 3-4% annual growth is important. India specifically generates around 3.2 million tonnes annually with 30% growth rate.

4. **Categorize e-waste sources**: Remember the major categories—large appliances (40%), small appliances (12%), ICT equipment (15%), consumer electronics (10%), and others.

5. **Differentiate between CRT and modern displays**: CRT devices contain significantly higher quantities of lead and barium compared to LED/LCD displays, which is a common exam comparison point.

6. **Concept of urban mining**: Understand that e-waste contains higher concentrations of precious metals than natural ores, making it a valuable secondary resource.

7. **Indian regulatory context**: Know that the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 have replaced the 2011 rules and that the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) oversees implementation.
