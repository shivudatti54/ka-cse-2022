# E-Waste Composition and Generation - Summary

## Key Definitions

- **E-Waste**: Discarded electrical or electronic equipment that has reached the end of its useful life and is no longer wanted by the owner.

- **Urban Mining**: The process of recovering valuable materials from waste streams, particularly from discarded electronic equipment containing precious and rare earth metals.

- **Obsolescence**: The state of becoming outdated or no longer usable, occurring in forms including functional, economic, psychological, and technological obsolescence.

- **Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)**: Policy approach where producers take responsibility for the end-of-life management of their products.

- **Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs)**: Chemical substances that persist in the environment, bioaccumulate, and pose risks to human health and ecosystems.

## Important Formulas

- **E-Waste Generation Rate**: Total weight = Number of devices × Average weight per device

- **Annual Generation Calculation**: Annual waste = (Population × Devices per capita) / Average lifespan

- **Material Composition Percentage**: Material percentage = (Weight of material / Total weight) × 100

## Key Points

1. E-waste contains over 1,000 different substances, with hazardous materials comprising approximately 2-5% by weight.

2. Heavy metals (lead, mercury, cadmium, chromium) are the primary hazardous components, leaching into soil and groundwater when improperly disposed.

3. Global e-waste generation reached approximately 53.6 million tonnes in 2019, growing at 2-3 million tonnes annually.

4. India generates around 3.2 million tonnes of e-waste annually, with a 30% growth rate—among the highest globally.

5. Large household appliances account for 40% of total e-waste by weight, making them the largest category.

6. E-waste contains higher concentrations of gold (0.1% in PCBs) compared to natural gold ores (typically 1-5 grams per tonne).

7. The informal sector handles approximately 95% of e-waste in India, presenting unique challenges for proper management.

8. CRT devices contain significantly higher lead (5-8%) compared to modern LED displays.

9. Plastic components constitute 20-30% of e-waste, with flame retardants complicating recycling processes.

10. Rare earth elements in e-waste are becoming increasingly valuable as primary sources become scarce.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing weight percentages**: Students often confuse the percentage of hazardous materials (small but significant) with recyclable materials (constituting majority of weight).

2. **Ignoring informal sector**: Many exam responses overlook the role of India's informal sector in e-waste handling, which processes approximately 95% of generated e-waste.

3. **Outdated rule references**: Some students cite the 2011 E-Waste Management Rules instead of the updated 2022 rules.

4. **Underestimating precious metal content**: Failing to recognize that e-waste is a richer source of gold than natural ores is a common oversight.

5. **Conflating global and Indian statistics**: India's e-waste growth rate (30%) is significantly higher than global rates (3-4%), and these should not be confused in answers.
