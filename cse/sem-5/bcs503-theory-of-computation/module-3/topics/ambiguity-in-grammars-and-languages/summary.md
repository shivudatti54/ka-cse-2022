# Ambiguity in Grammars and Languages - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Ambiguous Grammar:** A grammar G is ambiguous if there exists at least one string w ∈ L(G) that has two or more distinct parse trees or derivations.
- **Unambiguous Grammar:** Every string in the language has exactly one leftmost derivation and one parse tree.
- **Inherently Ambiguous Language:** A language that can only be generated by ambiguous grammars; no unambiguous grammar exists for it.
- **Parse Tree:** A tree representation of the derivational structure of a string according to the grammar rules.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- A CFG G = (V, Σ, P, S) is ambiguous if ∃w ∈ L(G) with multiple parse trees.
- A language L is inherently ambiguous if ∀G generating L, G is ambiguous.
- The language L = {a^n b^n c^m d^m} ∪ {a^n b^m c^m d^n} is a classic example of inherent ambiguity.

## Key Points

- Ambiguity is a property of the grammar, not the language (except for inherently ambiguous languages).
- The arithmetic expression grammar E → E + E | E \* E | (E) | id is ambiguous due to undefined precedence.
- The if-else ambiguity arises from the dangling else problem—else can bind to different if statements.
- Converting ambiguous to unambiguous grammars typically involves introducing new non-terminals for precedence levels.
- Ambiguous grammars cause problems for deterministic parsing in compilers.
- Left recursion does not necessarily imply ambiguity—it's a separate issue.
- Determining grammar ambiguity is theoretically undecidable, but practical heuristics exist.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing left recursion with ambiguity—they are distinct concepts; a grammar can be left-recursive and still unambiguous.
- Assuming inherent ambiguity when a grammar has ε-productions; check carefully for multiple derivation paths.
- Forgetting that ambiguity refers to multiple parse trees, not just multiple derivation sequences.

## Revision Tips

- Practice identifying ambiguous strings in common grammars (expressions, if-else, dangling else).
- Memorize the conversion technique for arithmetic expressions: introduce separate non-terminals for each precedence level.
- Review the classic examples until you can reproduce them from memory.
- Focus on understanding why certain grammars are ambiguous rather than just memorizing definitions.
