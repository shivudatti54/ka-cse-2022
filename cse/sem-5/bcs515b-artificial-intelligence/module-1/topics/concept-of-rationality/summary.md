# Concept of Rationality - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Rational Agent**: An agent that selects actions expected to maximize its performance measure, given available percept information and prior knowledge.
- **Perfect Rationality**: The ideal of making optimal decisions with unlimited computational resources.
- **Bounded Rationality**: Decision-making with limited cognitive resources, leading to "satisficing" rather than optimal solutions.
- **Computational Rationality**: Explicitly considering the cost of computation in decision-making, trading off decision quality against resource expenditure.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

The fundamental rationality principle states: For each possible percept sequence, a rational agent should select an action that is expected to maximize its performance measure, given the evidence provided by the percept sequence and whatever built-in knowledge the agent has.

## Key Points

- Rationality in AI is about making the best decisions given available information, not about achieving perfect outcomes.
- The performance measure defines success and guides all agent behavior—poorly designed measures lead to unintended consequences.
- PEAS framework (Performance, Environment, Actuators, Sensors) is essential for specifying agent requirements.
- Different environment types (observable, deterministic, static, discrete) require different agent architectures.
- A rational agent's behavior depends on its complete percept history, not just the current percept.
- Bounded rationality is more realistic than perfect rationality for practical AI systems.
- Rationality and intelligence are distinct concepts—an agent can be rational without being intelligent and vice versa.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing rationality with intelligence or consciousness
- Assuming rational agents need perfect information
- Designing performance measures without considering unintended consequences
- Ignoring computational limitations when designing agent strategies

## Revision Tips

1. Practice specifying PEAS for various agents (vacuum cleaner, taxi, chess player).
2. Memorize the formal definition of a rational agent and recall it during exams.
3. Understand why bounded rationality is more practical than perfect rationality.
4. Review how environment characteristics (observability, determinism) affect agent design.
