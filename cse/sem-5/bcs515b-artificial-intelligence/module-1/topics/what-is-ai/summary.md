# What is Artificial Intelligence (AI) - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Artificial Intelligence (AI):** The branch of computer science focused on building machines capable of performing tasks requiring human intelligence, including reasoning, learning, problem-solving, perception, and language understanding.

- **Machine Learning (ML):** A subset of AI that enables systems to automatically learn and improve from experience without being explicitly programmed, using statistical techniques to identify patterns in data.

- **Deep Learning:** A subset of machine learning that uses artificial neural networks with multiple layers (deep architectures) to learn hierarchical representations of data.

- **Turing Test:** A test proposed by Alan Turing (1950) where a machine passes if a human evaluator cannot distinguish its responses from a human's in text-based conversation.

- **Intelligent Agent:** A system that perceives its environment through sensors and acts upon it through actuators, forming the foundation for understanding AI system design.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

No specific formulas apply to this introductory topic, but understanding the conceptual framework is essential:

- AI ⊃ Machine Learning ⊃ Deep Learning (hierarchy)
- Intelligence = Ability to Learn + Ability to Reason + Ability to Perceive + Ability to Act

## Key Points

- AI is classified into three capability levels: Narrow AI (current, task-specific), General AI (theoretical, human-level), and Super AI (hypothetical, beyond human intelligence).

- The two major AI approaches are Symbolic AI (rule-based, explicit knowledge) and Statistical/Learning-based AI (data-driven, pattern recognition).

- The Turing Test measures conversational intelligence but has limitations—it focuses only on verbal behavior and can be passed through trickery rather than genuine understanding.

- Expert systems represent early symbolic AI applications encoding human expertise in specific domains.

- The current AI revolution is driven by increased computational power, big data availability, and algorithmic advances in deep learning.

- Narrow AI applications include image recognition, voice assistants, recommendation systems, and autonomous vehicles—all highly specialized for specific tasks.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning as equivalent terms—they have a hierarchical relationship.

- Assuming General AI or Super AI exists today—these remain theoretical goals, and all current AI is Narrow AI.

- Believing that passing the Turing Test proves a machine is truly "intelligent" or conscious.

- Overestimating current AI capabilities—machines lack common sense reasoning and general understanding that humans possess.
