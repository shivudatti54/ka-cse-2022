# Software Engineering Knowledge in Agile Context - Summary

## Key Definitions
- **Software Engineering Knowledge**: Collective expertise, skills, and understanding possessed by team members for software design, development, testing, and maintenance.
- **Technical Knowledge**: Programming skills, architecture expertise, testing methodologies, and tool proficiency.
- **Domain Knowledge**: Understanding of business domain, customer requirements, and problem space.
- **Process Knowledge**: Understanding of development methodologies, workflows, and project management procedures.
- **Explicit Knowledge**: Codified knowledge that can be documented and transmitted through written materials.
- **Tacit Knowledge**: Experiential understanding that resists easy articulation, transferred through direct interaction.
- **Bus Factor**: Minimum number of team members who must leave a project for it to fail due to knowledge loss.

## Important Formulas
- No specific formulas apply to this topic—knowledge management is qualitative rather than quantitative.

## Key Points
1. Software engineering knowledge comprises technical, domain, and process dimensions essential for successful software delivery.
2. Agile methodologies fundamentally depend on effective knowledge sharing within self-organizing teams.
3. Agile practices explicitly address tacit knowledge transfer through pair programming, collaborative sessions, and daily interactions.
4. The Agile Manifesto's preference for "individuals and interactions" prioritizes human knowledge transfer over static documentation.
5. Daily stand-ups serve as knowledge synchronization points where team members share current work understanding.
6. Sprint retrospectives enable continuous learning and process knowledge improvement.
7. Customer collaboration in agile builds shared domain understanding through iterative feedback.
8. Agile teams mitigate bus factor risks through role rotation, pair programming, and collective code ownership.
9. Traditional approaches treat knowledge as documentation; agile approaches treat knowledge as constructed through interaction.
10. Knowledge management effectiveness directly impacts a team's capacity to respond to changes and deliver value incrementally.

## Common Mistakes
1. Confusing explicit knowledge (documented) with tacit knowledge (experiential)—exam questions often test this distinction.
2. Assuming agile "devalues documentation" means ignoring knowledge transfer—it means prioritizing collaborative knowledge building.
3. Overlooking that agile practices like pair programming serve knowledge transfer purposes beyond code quality.
4. Failing to connect knowledge management to agility—always link concepts to how they enable adaptive response to change.
