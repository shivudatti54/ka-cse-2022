# Core Principles of Agile - Summary

## Key Definitions

- **Agile Manifesto**: A formal statement of values and principles for agile software development, created in 2001 by seventeen software practitioners at the Utah ski resort.

- **Agile Values**: The four fundamental preferences articulated in the manifesto that guide decision-making in agile projects: individuals and interactions, working software, customer collaboration, and responding to change.

- **Agile Principles**: The twelve statements providing specific guidance for implementing the four values in software engineering practice.

- **Self-Organizing Teams**: Teams that collectively own their work and determine how to accomplish goals without hierarchical direction, embodying the eleventh agile principle.

- **Working Software**: Functional code that provides value to users, recognized in the manifesto as the primary measure of progress.

## Important Formulas

This topic does not involve computational formulas. The principles are qualitative guidelines for software engineering practice rather than quantitative metrics.

## Key Points

1. The Agile Manifesto emerged in 2001 as a response to perceived limitations of traditional software development methodologies, particularly excessive documentation and rigid processes.

2. The four values represent preferences when trade-offs are necessary—not rejections of processes, documentation, contracts, or planning.

3. The first five principles focus on customer satisfaction and effective project dynamics through continuous delivery and collaboration.

4. Principles six through twelve address process efficiency, sustainability, and continuous improvement.

5. The principle of welcoming changing requirements views change as a competitive advantage rather than a problem to be minimized.

6. Face-to-face conversation is recognized as the most efficient method of information exchange within development teams.

7. Sustainable development pacing prevents burnout and enables long-term project success.

8. Technical excellence and good design enable easier adaptation to changing requirements.

9. Simplicity involves maximizing work not done, avoiding unnecessary complexity.

10. Self-organizing teams produce superior outcomes through distributed authority and collective responsibility.

11. Regular reflection and adjustment establish mechanisms for continuous process improvement.

12. Agile principles have been widely adopted across industries beyond software, though the core framework remains rooted in software engineering contexts.

## Common Mistakes

1. Confusing the four values with the twelve principles—the values provide philosophical direction while the principles offer specific actionable guidance.

2. Misinterpreting "working software over comprehensive documentation" as eliminating documentation entirely rather than subordinating it to delivering value.

3. Believing that agile principles eliminate all planning, when actually they treat plans as useful but flexible instruments.

4. Assuming agile principles can be selectively adopted without understanding their interconnected nature—all principles support the overall framework.
