The Cost of Change principle demonstrates that modifying software becomes progressively more expensive as projects advance through sequential phases. Under traditional Waterfall development, changes follow an exponential cost curve (C(p) = C₀ × e^(k×p)), with cost multipliers reaching 100-150× for modifications during maintenance. This occurs due to ripple effects, regression testing complexity, and coordination overhead. Agile methodologies flatten this curve by delivering working software in small iterations, enabling continuous feedback and regular course correction. Technical practices including Test-Driven Development, Continuous Integration, automated testing, and refactoring further reduce change costs by providing safety nets for modifications. Quantitative analysis reveals that agile approaches can reduce change costs by 10-50× compared to sequential models for change-intensive projects, providing the fundamental economic rationale for iterative development in modern software engineering.
