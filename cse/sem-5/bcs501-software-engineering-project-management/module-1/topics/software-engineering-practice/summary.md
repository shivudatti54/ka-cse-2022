Software engineering practice encompasses the systematic approaches, methods, and techniques used to develop, maintain, and deliver high-quality software. The software development life cycle (SDLC) provides the foundational framework comprising requirements analysis, design, implementation, testing, deployment, and maintenance phases. Process models including Waterfall, V-Model, Prototype, Spiral, Agile, and Unified Process offer different approaches to organizing these activities, each with distinct advantages and applicability contexts. Requirements engineering establishes the functional and non-functional specifications through structured elicitation, analysis, and validation. Design principles of modularity, abstraction, cohesion, and coupling determine software quality—high cohesion and low coupling are fundamental objectives. Testing strategies span unit, integration, system, and acceptance testing, with black-box and white-box techniques providing complementary coverage. Effort estimation models like COCOMO and Function Points enable quantitative project planning. Professional practice requires selecting and combining these elements appropriately based on project characteristics including requirement stability, team capability, regulatory constraints, and delivery expectations.
