1. Software Engineering applies engineering principles to systematic software development, emerging from the 1960s software crisis characterized by schedule/cost overruns and quality deficiencies.

2. Software possesses four essential properties per Brooks (1987): complexity (combinatorial growth with size), conformity (external constraints), changeability (ease of modification), and invisibility (abstract nature).

3. Software process models provide frameworks for organizing development activities: Waterfall (sequential, for stable requirements), Incremental (delivers in small versions), Spiral (risk-driven iterations), Prototype (rapid visualization for requirements), and Agile (flexible, collaborative, iterative delivery per the 2001 Manifesto).

4. Requirements Engineering involves elicitation (gathering stakeholder needs through interviews, workshops, prototyping), analysis (consistency and feasibility checking), specification (SRS documentation per IEEE 830), and validation (reviews, testing).

5. Software Design translates requirements into architecture, components, and interfaces using principles of modularity, abstraction, encapsulation, and established design patterns.

6. Testing spans unit, integration, system, and acceptance levels using black-box and white-box strategies to verify and validate software functionality.

7. Software maintenance (corrective, adaptive, perfective, preventive) constitutes 40-60% of lifecycle costs, with legacy systems presenting particular challenges.
