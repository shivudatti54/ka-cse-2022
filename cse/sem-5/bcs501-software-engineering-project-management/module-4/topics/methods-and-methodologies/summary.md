Software development methodologies are formalized frameworks that structure the software development lifecycle, evolving from sequential approaches like Waterfall to adaptive methodologies like Agile. The Waterfall model implements sequential phase progression with completion gates, suitable for stable-requirement projects but limited by late testing and inflexibility. The V-Model extends Waterfall by mapping development phases to corresponding testing activities, providing early verification-validation correspondence essential for safety-critical systems. The Spiral model integrates iterative development with systematic risk management through cyclical phases of planning, risk analysis, engineering, and evaluation, making it suitable for large, complex, high-risk projects. Agile methodologies, formalized through the 2001 Manifesto, emphasize adaptive planning, iterative development, customer collaboration, and responsiveness to change. Scrum implements fixed-duration sprints with defined roles (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Development Team) and ceremonies, while Kanban focuses on visual workflow management and WIP limits. DevOps bridges development-operations gaps through CI/CD, automation, and shared responsibility. Methodology selection depends on factors including requirement stability, team size, risk profile, regulatory constraints, and organizational culture, with hybrid approaches increasingly common in practice.
