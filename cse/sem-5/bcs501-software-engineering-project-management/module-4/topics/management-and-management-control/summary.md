# Management and Management Control - Summary

## Key Definitions

- **Management**: The systematic coordination of resources (human, financial, technological, temporal) to achieve predefined software project objectives through the application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques.

- **Management Control**: The subset of management activities that ensure project execution aligns with established plans and objectives through monitoring, measurement, variance identification, and corrective action implementation.

- **Baseline**: The approved version of a project plan (cost, schedule, and scope) used as a reference for measuring performance.

- **Earned Value Management (EVM)**: A quantitative methodology integrating scope, schedule, and cost measurements to assess project performance and predict outcomes.

- **Variance Analysis**: The process of comparing actual performance against established standards to identify deviations requiring management attention.

- **Control Chart**: A statistical tool displaying process performance over time with upper and lower control limits to distinguish common cause from special cause variation.

## Important Formulas

- **Schedule Variance (SV)** = Earned Value (EV) – Planned Value (PV)
- **Cost Variance (CV)** = Earned Value (EV) – Actual Cost (AC)
- **Schedule Performance Index (SPI)** = EV / PV
- **Cost Performance Index (CPI)** = EV / AC
- **Estimate at Completion (EAC)** = Budget at Completion (BAC) / CPI
- **Variance at Completion (VAC)** = BAC – EAC
- **Upper Control Limit (UCL)** = μ + 3σ
- **Lower Control Limit (LCL)** = μ – 3σ

## Key Points

1. Management in software projects extends general management principles by incorporating unique software development characteristics: evolving requirements, high complexity, and estimation uncertainty.

2. The five classical management functions—planning, organizing, staffing, directing, and controlling—provide the theoretical foundation for software project management.

3. The management control process comprises four stages: setting standards, measurement, comparison, and corrective action, forming a continuous feedback loop.

4. Feedforward control is proactive (risk assessment), concurrent control is real-time (sprint monitoring), and feedback control is reactive (post-project reviews).

5. Earned Value Management provides objective, quantitative indicators of project health through integrated cost-schedule-scope metrics.

6. SPI < 1 indicates schedule delay; CPI < 1 indicates cost overrun. Both conditions require corrective action.

7. Agile control mechanisms (burndown charts, velocity tracking) provide visual, real-time insights into project progress and predictability.

8. Statistical process control charts help identify special cause variation in quality metrics, enabling targeted quality improvement.

9. Anthony's framework distinguishes strategic planning, management control, and operational control as three hierarchical levels in organizational control systems.

10. Management Information Systems (MIS) integrate data from multiple sources to provide timely, accurate information for software project decision-making and control.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing management with control**: Management is the broader function; control is a subset focused on monitoring and correction.

2. **Incorrect EVM formula application**: Common errors include swapping EV and PV in variance calculations or misinterpreting negative variances as favorable.

3. **Misidentifying control types**: Students often confuse feedforward (preventive) with feedback (reactive) control mechanisms.

4. **Ignoring the software-specific context**: Applying general management examples without connecting to software engineering project scenarios.

5. **Misinterpreting control chart signals**: Treating special cause variation as normal process variation or vice versa leads to inappropriate management responses.
