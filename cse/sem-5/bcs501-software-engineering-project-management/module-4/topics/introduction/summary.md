Software Project Management applies project management principles to software development contexts, addressing unique challenges arising from software's intangible nature and complexity. A project is a temporary, unique endeavor creating novel deliverables, distinct from ongoing operational activities. The iron triangle (scope, time, cost) represents the fundamental constraint interdependence—changes to one vertex require adjustments to others. Software projects are characterized by intangibility, complexity, flexibility, novelty, and entropy. The project life cycle encompasses initiation, planning, execution, monitoring/controlling, and closure phases. Historical context reveals the "software crisis" of the 1960s prompted structured methodologies like Waterfall, though modern approaches increasingly adopt iterative and agile practices to address software's inherent changeability. Common failure factors include poor requirements management, unrealistic estimation, inadequate risk handling, stakeholder misalignment, and scope creep.
