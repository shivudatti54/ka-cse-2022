# Project Success and Failure - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Project Success**: Achieving project objectives within the triple constraint of scope, time, and cost, while also meeting stakeholder expectations and delivering business value.

- **Project Failure**: Inability to meet intended objectives, manifesting as schedule overruns, budget overruns, quality deficiencies, or complete project abandonment.

- **Critical Success Factors (CSFs)**: Essential conditions that must be satisfied for project success, including clear requirements, strong leadership, competent team, user involvement, and effective communication.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Cost Performance Index (CPI) = Earned Value (EV) / Actual Cost (AC)**
- **Schedule Performance Index (SPI) = Earned Value (EV) / Planned Value (PV)**
- **Estimate at Completion (EAC) = Budget at Completion (BAC) / CPI**
- **Variance at Completion (VAC) = BAC - EAC**
- **Iron Triangle**: Success = Meeting Scope + Time + Cost constraints

## Key Points

- Traditional success measurement focuses on the Iron Triangle; modern approaches include stakeholder satisfaction and business benefits.

- Poor requirements management is the single largest cause of software project failure.

- Projects can be partially successful or fail from one stakeholder's perspective while succeeding from another's.

- CPI < 1 indicates cost overrun; SPI < 1 indicates schedule delay.

- Scope creep without corresponding adjustments to time and budget leads to project failure.

- Regular monitoring and control through Earned Value Management helps detect problems early.

- User involvement throughout the project lifecycle significantly increases chances of success.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Assuming that on-time, on-budget delivery always means project success—product may still fail to meet user needs.

- Ignoring stakeholder management and focusing only on technical aspects.

- Treating all requirements as fixed—failure to implement change management leads to scope creep.

- Underestimating the importance of risk management and contingency planning.

- Confusing project success with product success—they are related but distinct concepts.

## Revision Tips

1. Memorize the Iron Triangle components and understand their interrelationships.

2. Practice numerical problems on CPI, SPI, EAC, and VAC calculations.

3. Prepare a list of 5-6 critical success factors and 5-6 failure causes with brief explanations.

4. Review at least one case study of both successful and failed projects for application-based questions.

5. Understand that different stakeholders (sponsor, user, team) may have different success criteria.
