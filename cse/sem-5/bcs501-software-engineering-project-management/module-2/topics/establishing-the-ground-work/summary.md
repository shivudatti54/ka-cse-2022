# Establishing The Ground Work - Summary

## Key Definitions

- **Requirements Engineering**: The systematic process of identifying, analyzing, specifying, validating, and managing requirements for software systems.

- **Stakeholder**: Any individual, group, or organization that may affect or be affected by the software system under development.

- **Stakeholder Analysis**: The process of assessing stakeholder characteristics including power, interest, influence, and importance to the project.

- **Project Vision Statement**: A concise articulation of the project's purpose, direction, and expected value delivery.

- **Scope Definition**: The process of establishing the boundaries distinguishing project deliverables from surrounding work activities.

- **Requirements Traceability**: The establishment and maintenance of links between requirements and other project artifacts.

- **Requirements Management Plan**: The document establishing policies, procedures, and organizational structures for requirements engineering activities.

- **Scope Creep**: The uncontrolled expansion of project boundaries beyond the originally defined scope.

## Important Formulas

- **Power/Interest Grid Classification**:
  - Manage Closely: High Power + High Interest
  - Keep Satisfied: High Power + Low Interest
  - Keep Informed: Low Power + High Interest
  - Monitor: Low Power + Low Interest

- **RACI Matrix Roles**:
  - **R**esponsible: Performs the work
  - **A**ccountable: Has ultimate decision authority
  - **C**onsulted: Provides input before decisions
  - **I**nformed: Notified of decisions

## Key Points

1. Establishing groundwork is the foundational phase of requirements engineering that determines project success likelihood.

2. Comprehensive stakeholder identification extends beyond obvious stakeholders to include all potentially affected parties.

3. The power/interest grid provides a systematic framework for stakeholder classification and engagement strategy determination.

4. Clear scope definition with explicit inclusions and exclusions prevents scope creep and manages stakeholder expectations.

5. Requirements traceability enables change impact analysis and ensures comprehensive requirements coverage.

6. Requirements management planning establishes governance structures that persist throughout the project lifecycle.

7. RACI matrices provide clear role definitions ensuring accountability and avoiding confusion in requirements activities.

8. Communication planning is stakeholder-specific, requiring different approaches for different stakeholder categories.

9. Groundwork activities create the structural framework within which all subsequent requirements processes occur.

10. Inadequate groundwork contributes to 40-60% of software project failures, highlighting its critical importance.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Incomplete Stakeholder Identification**: Failing to identify all stakeholders, particularly those with indirect influence, leads to missed requirements and stakeholder conflicts later in the project.

2. **Confusing Vision with Goals**: The vision statement articulates purpose and direction, not specific objectives or deliverables. Treating the vision as a detailed plan undermines its guiding function.

3. **Neglecting Out-of-Scope Definitions**: Focusing only on what the project includes without explicitly stating exclusions creates ambiguity that enables scope creep.

4. **Treating Traceability as Optional**: Without systematic traceability, change impact analysis becomes guesswork, leading to incomplete implementation of requirements changes.

5. **Static Stakeholder Analysis**: Stakeholder characteristics change throughout the project lifecycle. Failing to reassess stakeholder positions leads to outdated engagement strategies.

6. **Generic Communication Plans**: Applying uniform communication approaches to all stakeholders ignores their varying information needs, expertise levels, and decision-making authority.
