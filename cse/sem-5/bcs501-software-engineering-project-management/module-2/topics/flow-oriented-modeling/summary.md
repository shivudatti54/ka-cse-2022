Flow-oriented modeling employs Data Flow Diagrams (DFDs) to represent system behavior through data movement and transformation. DFDs use four fundamental symbols: External Entities (sources/destinations of data), Processes (data transformations), Data Stores (data repositories), and Data Flows (data movement paths). DFDs are constructed hierarchically beginning with Context Diagrams (Level 0) showing system-wide interactions, progressing through Level 1 decompositions into major subsystems, and continuing to Level 2+ detailed views. Balancing ensures consistency between decomposition levels by preserving input/output data flows. Logical DFDs capture essential system behavior independent of implementation, while physical DFDs represent specific technological realizations. Flow-oriented modeling complements object-oriented and structural approaches, excelling for data-intensive information systems. Proficiency in DFD construction, decomposition, and balancing equips systems analysts with essential requirements modeling capabilities.
