Requirements negotiation is the systematic process of reconciling competing stakeholder interests to produce acceptable software requirements specifications. The Fisher and Ury principled negotiation model provides the theoretical foundation, emphasizing separation of people from problems, focus on interests rather than positions, generation of mutually beneficial options, and use of objective decision criteria. BATNA analysis establishes reservation values guiding negotiation outcomes. Stakeholder conflicts are categorized as functional, quality attribute trade-offs, resource constraints, or organizational conflicts. Prioritization techniques including MoSCoW (Must/Should/Could/Won't), Kano model (Basic/Performance/Exciter needs), weighted scoring (criteria-weighted calculations), and Analytic Hierarchy Process (pairwise comparison with eigenvector computation) enable quantitative resource allocation. The five negotiation strategies—competing, collaborating, compromising, avoiding, and accommodating—offer flexible responses to stakeholder situations. Validation through requirements reviews, prototyping, and formal sign-off ensures negotiated agreements provide reliable development baselines. Empirical evidence indicates inadequate requirements negotiation contributes to approximately 40% of software project failures.
