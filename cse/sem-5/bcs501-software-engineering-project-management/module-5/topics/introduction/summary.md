# Introduction to Software Quality - Summary

## Key Definitions

- **Software Quality**: The degree to which a software product meets specified requirements and user expectations, encompassing both conformance to specifications and fitness for use
- **Quality Assurance (QA)**: Proactive, process-oriented activities that prevent defects by establishing standards, processes, and procedures
- **Quality Control (QC)**: Reactive, product-oriented activities that identify defects through testing and inspection
- **Internal Quality**: Quality attributes visible to developers, including code structure, maintainability, and readability
- **External Quality**: Quality attributes observable by end-users, including functionality, reliability, and performance

## Important Formulas

- **Cost of Quality**: Total = Prevention Costs + Appraisal Costs + Internal Failure Costs + External Failure Costs
- **Cost Ratio**: Defect prevention costs are typically 10-100 times lower than external failure costs

## Key Points

1. Software quality directly impacts product reliability, customer satisfaction, and maintenance costs

2. Quality management in software evolved from manufacturing industry pioneers (Shewhart, Deming, Juran)

3. ISO 9000 and Capability Maturity Model (CMM) established foundational quality frameworks for software

4. QA prevents problems through process management; QC detects problems through testing and inspection

5. The cost of quality includes prevention, appraisal, internal failure, and external failure components

6. Internal quality attributes serve developers while external quality attributes serve end-users

7. Proactive quality management is significantly more cost-effective than reactive defect detection

8. Quality planning must be integrated into project management from the earliest phases

## Common Mistakes

1. Confusing Quality Assurance with Quality Control - QA is preventive process work, QC is detective product work

2. Treating quality as an afterthought rather than integrating it throughout the project lifecycle

3. Focusing only on external quality (user-visible) while neglecting internal quality (developer-visible) attributes

4. Underestimating the cost differential between prevention and failure - many projects skimp on QA and pay heavily in maintenance
