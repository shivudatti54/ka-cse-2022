# Learning Objectives

After studying this topic, you should be able to:

1. **Define** the terms primitive and non-primitive data structures, and **list** examples of each.
2. **Explain** the characteristics of primitive data structures, including their predefined nature, simplicity, direct manipulation, and fixed size.
3. **Describe** the different types of primitive data structures, including integers, floats, characters, booleans, and pointers, and **identify** their typical sizes and variants.
4. **Classify** non-primitive data structures into linear and non-linear categories, and **explain** the characteristics of each, including their user-defined or library-defined nature, complexity, and variable size.
5. **Implement** common non-primitive data structures, such as arrays, linked lists, stacks, queues, trees, and graphs, and **analyze** their time and space complexity.
6. **Compare** and **contrast** primitive and non-primitive data structures, and **justify** the choice of data structure for a given problem based on factors such as type of operations, frequency of operations, memory constraints, access patterns, and time complexity requirements.
7. **Design** and **develop** algorithms using non-primitive data structures, and **assess** their efficiency and scalability.
8. **Critique** the use of primitive and non-primitive data structures in real-world applications, and **evaluate** their impact on software performance and maintainability.
