Performance measurement in computer architecture centers on minimizing execution time, the definitive metric for comparing processor implementations. The fundamental CPU performance equation ($T_{CPU} = IC \times CPI \times T_{clock}$) reveals that performance depends on three factors: instruction count (determined by algorithm and compiler), CPI (determined by microarchitecture), and clock frequency (determined by circuit technology). This interconnection means that higher clock rates do not guarantee superior performance—a processor with lower frequency but significantly better CPI may outperform one with higher frequency. Amdahl's Law establishes the theoretical limits of optimization, demonstrating that overall speedup is constrained by the unenhanced fraction of execution time. Benchmarking using standardized suites like SPEC enables objective comparison across implementations. Together, these concepts provide the analytical framework for systematic performance evaluation and optimization in computer systems.
