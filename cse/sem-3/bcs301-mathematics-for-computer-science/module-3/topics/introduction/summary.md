# Introduction to Statistical Inference - Summary

## Key Definitions

- **Statistical Inference**: The process of drawing conclusions about populations based on sample data
- **Population**: The complete set of all observations about which we wish to draw conclusions
- **Sample**: A subset of the population selected for study
- **Parameter**: A characteristic of a population (denoted by Greek letters: μ, σ, ρ)
- **Statistic**: A characteristic of a sample (denoted by Roman letters: x̄, s, p̂)
- **Simple Random Sampling**: A sampling technique where every population member has an equal chance of selection

## Important Formulas

- Sample Mean: x̄ = (Σxᵢ) / n
- Sample Proportion: p̂ = x / n (where x is number of successes, n is sample size)
- Population Mean: μ = (Σxᵢ) / N

## Key Points

1. Statistical inference enables generalizations from samples to populations
2. The two main types are Estimation (point and interval) and Hypothesis Testing
3. Valid inference requires random, representative sampling
4. Parameters are population characteristics; statistics are sample characteristics used to estimate parameters
5. Descriptive statistics summarizes data; inferential statistics makes predictions
6. Understanding data types (qualitative/quantitative, discrete/continuous) is essential for choosing appropriate methods
7. Statistical inference quantifies uncertainty in our conclusions through probability

## Common Mistakes

1. Confusing population with sample (e.g., treating sample statistics as population parameters)
2. Forgetting that statistical inference assumes random sampling; convenience samples may lead to biased conclusions
3. Misunderstanding that a sample statistic alone doesn't convey uncertainty—interval estimation or hypothesis testing is needed
4. Confusing descriptive statistics (summarizing what you have) with inferential statistics (making predictions about what you don't have)
