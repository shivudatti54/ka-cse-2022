# Purpose: Principles of Experimentation in Design

## Why This Topic Matters

This topic is a foundational component of **Module 5: Design of Experiments & ANOVA** in the BCS301 (Mathematics for Computer Science) syllabus. It establishes the core experimental design principles that underpin all subsequent topics in the module, including one-way and two-way ANOVA, hypothesis testing, and the F-test.

## Relevance to BCS301

- **Foundation for ANOVA**: Understanding replication, randomization, blinding, and control groups is essential before applying ANOVA techniques. Without a properly designed experiment, ANOVA results are meaningless.
- **Exam Importance**: frequently asks questions on defining and distinguishing the principles of experimental design. Students are expected to explain replication, randomization, blinding, and control groups with examples.
- **Practical Application**: The principles taught here are directly applicable to evaluating algorithms, testing software performance, and conducting computational experiments -- skills critical for computer science students.
- **Statistical Reasoning**: This topic bridges the gap between theoretical statistics and practical experimentation, teaching students how to minimize bias, control variability, and ensure the validity of experimental conclusions.

## Key Learning Outcomes

After studying this topic, students should students able to:

1. Define and explain the four core principles of experimentation: replication, randomization, blinding, and control groups.
2. Distinguish between single-blinding and double-blinding techniques.
3. Describe one-way and two-way factorial designs and when each is appropriate.
4. Explain how ANOVA connects to experimental design for comparing group means.
5. Apply these principles to design a simple experiment for algorithm performance comparison.

## Connection to Other Module 5 Topics

This topic serves as the entry point for the entire module. The principles learned here are prerequisites for understanding:

- The ANOVA technique and its basic principles
- One-way classification and analysis
- Two-way classification and interaction effects
- The Central Limit Theorem as applied to experimental data
- Confidence limits and hypothesis testing in experimental contexts
