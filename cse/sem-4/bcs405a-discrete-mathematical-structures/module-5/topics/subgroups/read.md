## Introduction

In the study of algebraic structures, groups form one of the most fundamental and versatile concepts in discrete mathematics. A **subgroup** is a subset of a group that retains the group structure under the same operation. Understanding subgroups is essential for comprehending the deeper properties of groups and their applications in cryptography, coding theory, and algebraic computations.

The concept of subgroups allows mathematicians and computer scientists to break down complex group structures into simpler, more manageable components. Just as a subset can inherit properties from its parent set, a subgroup inherits the binary operation and algebraic properties from its parent group. This hierarchical structure proves invaluable when analyzing symmetry, solving equations in groups, and understanding the internal architecture of algebraic systems.

In this module, we will explore the definition of subgroups, necessary and sufficient conditions for identifying subgroups, various types of subgroups, and important theorems that govern subgroup behavior. These concepts form the foundation for more advanced topics such as cyclic groups, cosets, and Lagrange's theorem, all of which are crucial for the university's discrete mathematics curriculum.

## Key Concepts

### Definition of a Subgroup

A subset H of a group G (with operation _) is called a **subgroup** of G if H itself forms a group under the same operation _ defined on G. In other words, H must satisfy four conditions:

1. **Non-empty**: H ≠ ∅ (H contains at least one element)
2. **Closure**: For all a, b ∈ H, a \* b ∈ H
3. **Identity**: The identity element e of G must be in H
4. **Inverses**: For every a ∈ H, the inverse element a⁻¹ ∈ H

We denote a subgroup H of G as H ≤ G. If H is a proper subgroup (meaning H ≠ G), we write H < G.

**Important Note**: Some textbooks include closure as part of the definition, while others prove closure as a consequence of the other conditions. The four-condition definition above is the most rigorous and commonly used approach in university examinations.

### One-Step Subgroup Test (Necessary and Sufficient Condition)

The **one-step subgroup test** provides a convenient method to verify whether a subset H of a group G is a subgroup. It states:

A non-empty subset H of a group G is a subgroup of G if and only if for all a, b ∈ H, a \* b⁻¹ ∈ H.

**Proof Outline**: The forward direction (⇒) is straightforward: if H is a subgroup, then for any a, b ∈ H, b⁻¹ ∈ H (by inverses), and a \* b⁻¹ ∈ H (by closure). For the reverse direction (⇐), we show:

- Taking a = e (identity), we get e \* b⁻¹ = b⁻¹ ∈ H, proving closure of inverses
- Taking b = e, we get a \* e⁻¹ = a ∈ H, proving closure
- Taking b = a, we get a \* a⁻¹ = e ∈ H, proving identity element exists
- Closure under the group operation follows from combining these results

This test is particularly useful in examination problems where you need to verify subgroup properties quickly.

### Two-Step Subgroup Test

An alternative approach is the **two-step subgroup test**:

A non-empty subset H of a group G is a subgroup if and only if:

1. For all a, b ∈ H, a \* b ∈ H (closure under multiplication)
2. For all a ∈ H, a⁻¹ ∈ H (closure under inverses)

This is essentially the definition with closure explicitly stated as one condition and inverses as another.

### Types of Subgroups

**1. Trivial Subgroup**: Every group G has at least two subgroups:

- The trivial subgroup {e} containing only the identity element
- The group G itself

These are called the **improper subgroups**, while any other subgroup is a **proper subgroup**.

**2. Cyclic Subgroups**: If G is a group and a ∈ G, the set ⟨a⟩ = {aⁿ : n ∈ ℤ} forms a cyclic subgroup of G, generated by the element a. This is one of the most important types of subgroups studied in group theory.

**3. Center of a Group**: The center Z(G) of a group G is defined as Z(G) = {a ∈ G : ag = ga for all g ∈ G}. The center is always a subgroup of G. This concept is particularly important in understanding the structure of non-abelian groups.

**4. Centralizer of an Element**: For a fixed element a ∈ G, the centralizer C(a) = {g ∈ G : ga = ag} is a subgroup of G. The centralizer helps analyze the conjugacy classes of a group.

**5. Generated Subgroups**: Given any subset S of a group G, the subgroup generated by S, denoted ⟨S⟩, is the smallest subgroup of G containing S. It consists of all finite products of elements from S and their inverses.

### Important Theorems

**Theorem 1 (Intersection of Subgroups)**: The intersection of any collection of subgroups of a group G is itself a subgroup of G.

**Proof**: Let {Hᵢ : i ∈ I} be a collection of subgroups of G, and let H = ∩ᵢ∈ᵢ Hᵢ. Since each Hᵢ contains the identity e, e ∈ H, so H is non-empty. For any a, b ∈ H, we have a, b ∈ Hᵢ for all i. Since each Hᵢ is a subgroup, a _ b⁻¹ ∈ Hᵢ for all i, hence a _ b⁻¹ ∈ H. By the one-step subgroup test, H is a subgroup of G.

**Theorem 2 (Subgroup Criteria for Finite Sets)**: If H is a non-empty finite subset of a group G, then H is a subgroup of G if and only if H is closed under the group operation.

**Proof**: If H is a subgroup, closure follows from the definition. Conversely, if H is finite and closed under multiplication, let a ∈ H. The set {a, a², a³, ...} must eventually repeat since H is finite, say aᵐ = aⁿ for some m > n ≥ 1. Multiplying by a⁻ⁿ (which exists in H since closure gives aⁿ ∈ H and we can use the one-step test), we get aᵐ⁻ⁿ = e ∈ H. Then a⁻¹ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ⁻¹ ∈ H by closure. Thus H is a subgroup.

This theorem is extremely useful for finite groups as it eliminates the need to check inverses explicitly.

## Examples

### Example 1: Subgroups of Integers under Addition

Consider the group (ℤ, +), the set of integers under addition.

**Claim**: For any n ∈ ℕ, nℤ = {nk : k ∈ ℤ} is a subgroup of (ℤ, +).

**Solution**: We verify using the one-step subgroup test:

- Non-empty: Since n·0 = 0 ∈ nℤ, the set is non-empty
- For any a = nk and b = nℓ in nℤ, we have a - b = nk - nℓ = n(k - ℓ) ∈ nℤ

Therefore, nℤ is a subgroup of ℤ. In particular:

- 2ℤ = {..., -4, -2, 0, 2, 4, ...} is a subgroup
- 3ℤ = {..., -6, -3, 0, 3, 6, ...} is a subgroup
- The trivial subgroup is 0ℤ = {0}

### Example 2: Subgroups of Modulo n Group

Consider the group (ℤ₆, +₆) = {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5} under addition modulo 6.

**Find all subgroups of ℤ₆**.

**Solution**: We need to find all subsets H ⊆ ℤ₆ that form groups under +₆.

- **Trivial subgroup**: {0} is a subgroup
- **Whole group**: ℤ₆ itself is a subgroup

Now check other subsets:

- **H₁ = {0, 3}**: Closed under +₆? 3 +₆ 3 = 6 mod 6 = 0 ∈ H₁. Yes! This is a subgroup of order 2.
- **H₂ = {0, 2, 4}**: Check closure: 2 +₆ 2 = 4 ∈ H₂, 2 +₆ 4 = 6 mod 6 = 0 ∈ H₂, 4 +₆ 4 = 8 mod 6 = 2 ∈ H₂. Also, inverses: 2⁻¹ = 4, 4⁻¹ = 2. This is a subgroup of order 3.

The subgroups of ℤ₆ are: {0}, {0, 3}, {0, 2, 4}, and ℤ₆.

**Key Observation**: The orders of subgroups (1, 2, 3, 6) divide the group order (6). This is a preview of Lagrange's theorem, covered in the next module.

### Example 3: Subgroups of Symmetric Group S₃

Consider S₃, the symmetric group on 3 elements, consisting of all 6 permutations: {e, (12), (13), (23), (123), (132)}.

**Find the subgroups of S₃**.

**Solution**:

- **Trivial**: {e}
- **Order 2 subgroups**: {e, (12)}, {e, (13)}, {e, (23)} - each is generated by a transposition
- **Order 3 subgroup**: {e, (123), (132)} - this is the alternating group A₃, generated by the 3-cycle
- **Order 6**: S₃ itself

So S₃ has exactly 4 proper subgroups and 1 trivial subgroup, totaling 5 subgroups.

## Exam Tips

1. **Always check non-emptiness first**: Many students lose marks by forgetting to verify that H is non-empty. The identity element's presence is often the quickest way to establish this.

2. **Know both subgroup tests**: The one-step test (a \* b⁻¹ ∈ H) is often faster, while the two-step test is more intuitive. Choose based on what's given in the problem.

3. **For finite groups, use closure only**: Remember Theorem 2 - if H is finite and non-empty with closure, it's automatically a subgroup. This saves checking inverses separately.

4. **Be careful with notation**: Distinguish between subgroup (≤) and proper subgroup (<). Also, ⟨a⟩ denotes the cyclic subgroup generated by a.

5. **Center and centralizer are always subgroups**: In any group G, Z(G) ≤ G and C(a) ≤ G for any a ∈ G. This is an exam-favorite fact.

6. **Intersection preserves subgroups**: If you need to find a subgroup, take the intersection of known subgroups. The result is guaranteed to be a subgroup.

7. **Union is NOT a subgroup**: Unless one contains the other, the union of two subgroups is never a subgroup. This is a common trick question.

8. **Order divides group order**: For finite groups, the order of any subgroup divides the order of the group (Lagrange's theorem, but you can use this to eliminate possibilities).
