# Multiplicative Group of Integers Modulo p and Permutation Groups - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Multiplicative Group Zp\*:** The set of nonzero integers modulo prime p that have multiplicative inverses, with order p-1.
- **Cyclic Group:** A group generated by a single element; Zp\* is always cyclic for prime p.
- **Primitive Root/Generator:** An element of order p-1 that generates all elements of Zp\* through its powers.
- **Order of Element:** Smallest positive integer k such that a^k ≡ 1 (mod p).
- **Permutation:** A bijective function from a set to itself; permutations form the symmetric group S_n.
- **Cycle Notation:** Representation of permutations as products of disjoint cycles.
- **Even/Odd Permutation:** Classification based on the number of transpositions needed.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Fermat's Little Theorem:** a^(p-1) ≡ 1 (mod p) for prime p and gcd(a,p) = 1
- **Euler's Theorem:** a^φ(n) ≡ 1 (mod n) for gcd(a,n) = 1
- **Order of S_n:** n!
- **Order of A_n:** n!/2
- **k-cycle decomposition:** A k-cycle can be written as (k-1) transpositions

## Key Points

- Zp\* is a cyclic group of order p-1 for every prime p
- Every element's order divides p-1 (Lagrange's Theorem)
- Primitive roots exist in Zp\* for all primes p
- S_n is non-abelian for n ≥ 3
- Disjoint cycles in permutations commute
- Every permutation has unique cycle decomposition (up to ordering)
- A_n is the subgroup of even permutations in S_n
- The sign of a permutation is +1 for even, -1 for odd

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing Zp (integers modulo p as additive group) with Zp\* (multiplicative group)
- Forgetting that Zp\* is only cyclic when p is prime
- Incorrectly counting transpositions when determining parity
- Not recognizing that disjoint cycles commute but overlapping ones don't
- Assuming all elements in Zp\* are generators (only some are primitive roots)

## Revision Tips

- Practice computing powers modulo small primes to understand the cyclic structure
- Write out cycle decompositions for various permutations to gain fluency
- Remember that testing if g is a generator requires checking g^k ≠ 1 for all k < p-1
- Focus on understanding why certain properties hold rather than memorizing
- Solve previous university exam questions on this topic to familiarise with question patterns
