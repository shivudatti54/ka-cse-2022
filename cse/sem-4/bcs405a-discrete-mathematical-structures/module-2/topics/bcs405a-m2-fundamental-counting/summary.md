# Fundamental Principles of Counting - Rules of Sum and Product - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Rule of Sum (Addition Rule):** If there are m ways to do task A and n ways to do task B, and these tasks cannot be done simultaneously, then there are m + n ways to choose one task.

- **Rule of Product (Multiplication Rule):** If there are m ways to do task A and n ways to do task B, then there are m × n ways to do both tasks in sequence.

- **Mutually Exclusive Events:** Events that cannot occur at the same time; required for applying the sum rule.

- **Independent Choices:** Choices where one selection does not affect another; required for applying the product rule.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Extended Rule of Sum:** If k tasks are mutually exclusive with m₁, m₂, ..., mₖ ways respectively, total ways = m₁ + m₂ + ... + mₖ

- **Extended Rule of Product:** If k tasks are sequential with m₁, m₂, ..., mₖ ways respectively, total ways = m₁ × m₂ × ... × mₖ

- **With Repetition Allowed:** For n choices at each of k positions: nᵏ possibilities

- **Without Repetition:** For n choices taken k at a time: n × (n-1) × (n-2) × ... × (n-k+1) = n!/(n-k)!

## Key Points

- The sum rule applies to "either...or" situations with mutually exclusive choices
- The product rule applies to "both...and" situations or sequential decisions
- Always check whether repetition is allowed before solving
- Complex problems often require combining both rules
- Complementary counting (total - invalid) simplifies many problems
- Zero choices in any sequential step makes the entire count zero

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Applying sum rule when choices are NOT mutually exclusive
2. Forgetting to reduce available options when repetition is not allowed
3. Confusing "or" with "and" in problem statements
4. Not accounting for restrictions (like leading zeros in numbers)
5. Overcounting due to order when order doesn't matter (or vice versa)

## Revision Tips

1. Practice identifying keywords: "either...or" → sum, "both...and" → product
2. Draw tree diagrams for complex sequential problems
3. Start with the most constrained positions when counting arrangements
4. Verify answers by considering extreme cases and boundary conditions
5. Solve at least 5-10 mixed problems before the exam to build intuition
