# Procurement in IT Capacity Planning - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Procurement**: The systematic process of acquiring IT resources including hardware, software, and services to meet organizational computing requirements
- **Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)**: Complete cost calculation including acquisition, operational, maintenance, support, upgrade, and downtime costs over the asset's lifecycle
- **Service Level Agreement (SLA)**: Formal contract defining expected service levels including availability, performance metrics, response times, and penalty clauses
- **Vendor Selection**: Process of evaluating and choosing suppliers based on criteria including technical capability, financial stability, and support infrastructure

## Important Formulas and Concepts

- **TCO = Acquisition Costs + Operational Costs + Maintenance Costs + Support Costs + Upgrade Costs - Benefits (e.g., downtime reduction savings)**
- **Procurement Lifecycle**: Requirements Identification → Vendor Selection → Contract Negotiation → Acquisition/Deployment → Asset Management
- **Licensing Models**: Per-user, per-core, perpetual, subscription-based

## Key Points

- Procurement in IT capacity planning extends beyond simple purchasing to encompass the entire lifecycle of IT resource acquisition
- Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) analysis is essential for making informed procurement decisions, not just initial purchase price
- The procurement lifecycle consists of five phases: requirements identification, vendor selection, contract negotiation, acquisition/deployment, and ongoing asset management
- Vendor selection should use weighted evaluation criteria considering technical capability, financial stability, support, and compliance
- Modern procurement includes cloud services offering scalability advantages but requiring understanding of usage-based pricing
- SLAs define measurable service expectations including availability (e.g., 99.9%), response times, and penalty provisions
- Different procurement models (CapEx vs OpEx) suit different organizational situations and budget structures

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Considering only upfront purchase costs instead of complete TCO
- Failing to clearly define requirements before initiating procurement process
- Not including SLA penalty clauses or unclear metrics in contracts
- Ignoring vendor financial stability and long-term viability
- Overlooking hidden costs such as training, integration, and ongoing support

## Revision Tips

1. Memorize the five phases of procurement lifecycle in order
2. Practice TCO calculations with sample scenarios
3. Remember key SLA components: availability, response time, penalties
4. Understand when to use CapEx (stable needs, long-term) vs OpEx (variable needs, flexibility)
5. Review vendor selection criteria and apply to example scenarios
