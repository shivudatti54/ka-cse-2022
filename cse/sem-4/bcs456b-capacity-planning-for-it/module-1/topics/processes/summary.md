# Processes in IT Capacity Planning - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Process**: A structured set of activities designed to accomplish a specific organizational objective, transforming inputs into measurable outputs.
- **Capacity Planning**: The systematic approach to forecast future IT resource requirements and ensure optimal resource utilization.
- **Process Components**: Inputs, Activities, Outputs, Roles & Responsibilities, and Interfaces.
- **Workload Characterization**: Analysis of workload types (batch, interactive, real-time) to determine appropriate resource allocation.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Little's Law**: L = λW (Average number in system = Arrival rate × Residence time)
- **Utilization Formula**: Utilization = (Actual usage / Maximum capacity) × 100%
- **Throughput**: Number of transactions or requests processed per unit time
- **Optimal Utilization Range**: 70-85% for most IT resources

## Key Points

1. Capacity planning operates at three levels: Business (strategic), Resource (tactical), and Service (operational).

2. The capacity planning process flow follows: Data Collection → Analysis → Forecasting → Gap Analysis → Solution Design → Implementation → Monitoring.

3. Process modeling techniques include flowcharts, queuing theory models, and simulation models.

4. Key metrics for capacity planning include throughput, utilization, response time, queue length, and service availability.

5. Process optimization techniques involve workload characterization, threshold management, right-sizing, and automation.

6. Under-provisioning causes service degradation; over-provisioning causes unnecessary costs.

7. Effective capacity planning requires alignment with business objectives and continuous monitoring.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Confusing process with procedure - a process is the "what," procedure is the "how."

2. Setting utilization targets at 100% - this leaves no buffer for bursts and leads to performance degradation.

3. Ignoring baseline data - without historical data, forecasts are inaccurate and unreliable.

4. Planning for average case only - capacity planning must account for peak loads and unusual demand patterns.

5. Treating capacity planning as a one-time activity - it requires continuous monitoring, review, and adjustment.

## Revision Tips

1. Memorize the five components of a process and be ready to identify them in any given scenario.

2. Practice Little's Law calculations - this formula appears frequently in university exams.

3. Remember the three levels of capacity planning and their strategic/tactical/operational classification.

4. Review the process flow steps and be able to sequence them correctly in exam answers.

5. Focus on understanding the "why" behind each step - examiners value explanations over memorized definitions.
