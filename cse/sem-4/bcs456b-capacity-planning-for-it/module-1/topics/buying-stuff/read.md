## Introduction

In the realm of IT Capacity Planning, the decision to "buy stuff" encompasses a critical set of strategic choices that organizations must make when acquiring technology resources, infrastructure, and services. This module introduces students to the fundamental concepts of IT procurement, vendor evaluation, and the make-versus-buy decision framework that forms the backbone of effective capacity planning.

The importance of proper procurement decisions in IT cannot be overstated. According to industry estimates, IT spending constitutes anywhere from 2% to 10% of organizational revenue, depending on the industry and company size. Poor procurement decisions can lead to significant financial losses, underutilized resources, scalability issues, and operational inefficiencies. Conversely, well-planned procurement aligned with business needs ensures that organizations have the right resources at the right time, at the right cost. This topic becomes particularly crucial in the context of cloud computing, where organizations can choose between traditional capital expenditure (CapEx) models and operational expenditure (OpEx) models, each with its own implications for capacity planning.

This topic also addresses the fundamental shift in IT procurement philosophy—from traditional on-premises infrastructure purchasing to flexible, scalable cloud-based solutions. Students will learn how to evaluate different procurement options, understand Total Cost of Ownership (TCO), and make data-driven decisions that align with organizational goals and budget constraints.

## Key Concepts

### 1. Make vs. Buy Decision

The make vs. buy decision is a fundamental analysis that organizations perform to determine whether to develop IT capabilities internally or purchase them from external vendors. This decision is critical in capacity planning as it directly impacts resource allocation, cost structure, and organizational flexibility.

**Factors favoring "Make" (In-house development):**

- Customization requirements that cannot be met by standard solutions
- Strategic importance of the capability (competitive advantage)
- Control over intellectual property and technology
- Long-term cost benefits for high-volume, stable requirements
- Security and compliance requirements demanding full control

**Factors favoring "Buy" (External procurement):**

- Lack of internal expertise or resources
- Need for faster deployment
- Uncertain or fluctuating demand requiring scalability
- Lower total cost of ownership for non-core functions
- Access to vendor expertise and continuous updates

### 2. Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)

TCO is a comprehensive financial estimate that helps organizations understand the full cost of acquiring, deploying, operating, and maintaining an IT solution over its entire lifecycle. TCO analysis is crucial for making informed procurement decisions.

**Components of TCO:**

- **Acquisition Costs:** Hardware, software licenses, implementation, training
- **Operational Costs:** Personnel, utilities, maintenance, support contracts
- **Infrastructure Costs:** Data center space, power, cooling, networking
- **Upgrade and Migration Costs:** Future expansions, data migration, system upgrades
- **Hidden Costs:** Downtime, productivity loss, security breaches, compliance penalties

### 3. Vendor Evaluation and Selection

The vendor selection process involves systematic evaluation of potential suppliers based on multiple criteria. A well-structured evaluation process ensures that the chosen vendor can meet organizational requirements both currently and in the future.

**Key Evaluation Criteria:**

- **Technical Capability:** Functionality, performance, scalability, integration capabilities
- **Financial Stability:** Vendor's financial health, market position, longevity
- **Cost Structure:** Pricing model, hidden costs, discount options
- **Support and Service:** Response time, SLA terms, expertise availability
- **Security and Compliance:** Certifications, security practices, compliance with regulations
- **References and Track Record:** Customer testimonials, case studies, industry reputation

### 4. Procurement Models

Different procurement models offer varying levels of commitment, flexibility, and cost structures:

**Traditional Purchasing (On-premise):**

- Capital expenditure model
- Full ownership and control
- High upfront investment
- Long-term commitment
- Responsibility for maintenance

**Cloud Computing Models:**

- **IaaS (Infrastructure as a Service):** Virtual machines, storage, networking
- **PaaS (Platform as a Service):** Development platforms, databases
- **SaaS (Software as a Service):** Complete software applications
- Pay-as-you-go pricing
- Operational expenditure model
- Scalability and elasticity

**Hybrid Solutions:**

- Combination of on-premise and cloud resources
- Optimizes for specific workload requirements
- Provides flexibility and control

### 5. Service Level Agreements (SLAs)

SLAs are formal contracts that define the expected level of service from a vendor. In capacity planning, SLA terms directly impact the organization's ability to meet its own service commitments to end users.

**Critical SLA Parameters:**

- Uptime guarantees (e.g., 99.9% = "three nines")
- Response and resolution times
- Service availability definitions
- Penalty and credit structures
- Escalation procedures
- Maintenance windows

### 6. Capacity Planning in Procurement Decisions

Procurement decisions must align with capacity planning requirements to ensure that acquired resources can meet current and projected demand.

**Capacity Planning Considerations:**

- Baseline capacity assessment
- Growth projections and forecasting
- Seasonal and peak demand handling
- Geographic distribution requirements
- Disaster recovery and business continuity needs

## Examples

### Example 1: Make vs. Buy Analysis for Email System

**Scenario:** A mid-sized company with 500 employees needs to decide between building a custom email system or purchasing a commercial solution.

**Analysis:**

| Factor              | Build (Make)  | Buy (Commercial) |
| ------------------- | ------------- | ---------------- |
| Initial Cost        | ₹50,00,000    | ₹15,00,000       |
| Annual Maintenance  | ₹8,00,000     | ₹5,00,000        |
| Implementation Time | 12 months     | 2 months         |
| Customization       | Full control  | Limited          |
| Support             | In-house team | Vendor provided  |

**TCO Calculation (5 years):**

- Make: ₹50,00,000 + (₹8,00,000 × 5) = ₹90,00,000
- Buy: ₹15,00,000 + (₹5,00,000 × 5) = ₹40,00,000

**Recommendation:** Buy the commercial solution, as TCO is significantly lower and implementation is faster. The company can focus internal resources on core business differentiating applications.

### Example 2: Cloud vs. On-Premise Server Procurement

**Scenario:** An organization needs server infrastructure to handle 1000 virtual machines. Compare cloud (AWS) vs. on-premise options over 3 years.

**On-Premise Costs:**

- Hardware: ₹30,00,000
- Data Center Setup: ₹10,00,000
- Annual Power & Cooling: ₹3,00,000 × 3 = ₹9,00,000
- Annual Maintenance: ₹4,00,000 × 3 = ₹12,00,000
- IT Staff: ₹6,00,000 × 3 = ₹18,00,000
- **Total TCO: ₹79,00,000**

**Cloud Costs (AWS Reserved Instances):**

- Monthly Cost: ₹1,50,000 × 36 = ₹54,00,000
- Data Transfer: ₹3,00,000 (estimated)
- **Total TCO: ₹57,00,000**

**Additional Considerations:**

- On-premise provides data sovereignty and potentially lower variable costs at scale
- Cloud provides elasticity and eliminates upfront capital requirement
- Cloud TCO savings: ₹22,00,000 (28% savings)

**Recommendation:** Cloud procurement offers better TCO and flexibility for this scenario.

### Example 3: Vendor Evaluation Matrix

**Scenario:** A company is selecting a CRM vendor. Three vendors (A, B, C) are being evaluated.

| Criteria      | Weight | Vendor A | Vendor B | Vendor C |
| ------------- | ------ | -------- | -------- | -------- |
| Functionality | 30%    | 8        | 7        | 9        |
| Cost          | 25%    | 6        | 8        | 5        |
| Support       | 20%    | 7        | 6        | 8        |
| Integration   | 15%    | 8        | 6        | 7        |
| Security      | 10%    | 9        | 8        | 8        |

**Weighted Score Calculation:**

- Vendor A: (8×0.30) + (6×0.25) + (7×0.20) + (8×0.15) + (9×0.10) = 2.4 + 1.5 + 1.4 + 1.2 + 0.9 = 7.4
- Vendor B: (7×0.30) + (8×0.25) + (6×0.20) + (6×0.15) + (8×0.10) = 2.1 + 2.0 + 1.2 + 0.9 + 0.8 = 7.0
- Vendor C: (9×0.30) + (5×0.25) + (8×0.20) + (7×0.15) + (8×0.10) = 2.7 + 1.25 + 1.6 + 1.05 + 0.8 = 7.4

**Result:** Vendor A and C are tied at 7.4. Further analysis of qualitative factors would be needed. Vendor A offers better overall balance while Vendor C excels in functionality but has higher cost concerns.

## Exam Tips

1. **Understand the Make vs. Buy framework thoroughly:** This is frequently tested in university exams. Be prepared to list at least 5 factors each for make and buy decisions.

2. **Remember TCO components:** Acquisition, operational, infrastructure, upgrade, and hidden costs are key components that should be mentioned in any TCO-related answer.

3. **Know the difference between CapEx and OpEx:** Capital expenditure involves large upfront investments and is suitable for stable, long-term needs. Operational expenditure offers flexibility and is suitable for fluctuating demand.

4. **Cloud service models (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS):** Be clear on what each model provides and when to use each. This is a common exam question.

5. **SLA importance:** Understand that SLAs define vendor accountability. Know what "three nines" (99.9%) means in terms of allowable downtime (approximately 8.76 hours per year).

6. **Vendor evaluation process:** Remember the key criteria—technical capability, financial stability, cost, support, security, and references. The weighted scoring model is a practical method to learn.

7. **Capacity planning alignment:** Always emphasize that procurement decisions must align with capacity requirements—neither under-procuring (causing shortages) nor over-procuring (causing waste).

8. **Current trends:** Be aware of hybrid cloud approaches and the trend toward OpEx models, as exam questions often include current industry contexts.
