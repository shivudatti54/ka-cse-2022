# Ecological Measures and Ethical Considerations in Green IT - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Ecological Measures**: Technical approaches and practices that minimize the environmental impact of IT operations, including energy efficiency, waste management, and sustainable software development.

- **Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE)**: A metric measuring data center energy efficiency, calculated as Total Facility Energy ÷ IT Equipment Energy. Lower PUE indicates better efficiency.

- **Greenwashing**: The practice of making misleading or false claims about environmental benefits of products or services.

- **Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)**: A policy approach making manufacturers accountable for the entire lifecycle of their products, including proper disposal and recycling.

- **Intergenerational Equity**: The ethical principle that current generations should not compromise the ability of future generations to meet their needs.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **PUE = Total Facility Energy / IT Equipment Energy**
- **Annual Energy = Power (kW) × Hours × Days**
- **Energy Savings = (Original Consumption - Optimized Consumption) × Time × Energy Cost**

## Key Points

- Data centers consume significant energy; hot aisle/cold aisle containment and efficient cooling reduce energy overhead.

- Virtualization improves server utilization from 15-25% to 60-80%, dramatically reducing energy consumption.

- Green software engineering focuses on optimized code, efficient algorithms, and minimal network traffic.

- Electronic waste contains toxic materials like lead and mercury, requiring proper recycling through certified e-waste handlers.

- Cloud computing offers ecological benefits through resource pooling and dynamic provisioning.

- The 3R principle (Reduce, Reuse, Recycle) forms the foundation of e-waste management.

- Ethical Green IT considers digital inclusion, ensuring sustainability initiatives don't worsen the digital divide.

- Organizations must avoid greenwashing and implement verified environmental reporting.

- Supply chain ethics extend to mineral sourcing, manufacturing processes, and labor conditions.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing PUE with its inverse (remember: higher PUE means less efficiency, not more).

- Assuming Green IT is only about energy efficiency - it also includes ethical and social considerations.

- Overlooking the environmental impact of data transmission and network infrastructure.

- Ignoring e-waste generated from personal devices and small office equipment.

- Focusing only on organizational benefits without considering broader societal and environmental impacts.

## Revision Tips

- Memorize the formula for PUE and practice calculating it with different values.

- Remember that ethical considerations are as important as technical ecological measures.

- Review real-world examples of greenwashing to understand what to avoid.

- Understand the connection between virtualization, cloud computing, and energy efficiency.

- Focus on the difference between reduce, reuse, and recycle in the context of e-waste management.
