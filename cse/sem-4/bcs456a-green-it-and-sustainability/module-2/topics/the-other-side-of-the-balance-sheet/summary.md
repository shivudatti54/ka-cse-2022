# The Other Side of the Balance Sheet - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Environmental Accounting**: Identification, measurement, and reporting of environmental costs in IT operations, including energy consumption, emissions, and waste disposal
- **Green IT TCO**: Expanded Total Cost of Ownership model incorporating acquisition, operational, environmental, end-of-life, and hidden costs
- **Carbon Footprint**: Total greenhouse gas emissions caused directly and indirectly, measured in kg CO2e (carbon dioxide equivalent)
- **Scope 1 Emissions**: Direct emissions from owned sources (fuel combustion, company vehicles)
- **Scope 2 Emissions**: Indirect emissions from purchased electricity and energy
- **Scope 3 Emissions**: All other indirect emissions in the value chain
- **ESG Reporting**: Environmental, Social, and Governance disclosure frameworks for sustainability performance
- **E-Waste Management**: Responsible handling of electronic waste through reduce, reuse, recycle principles
- **PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness)**: Ratio of total facility energy to IT equipment energy (ideal = 1.0)

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Annual Energy Cost**: kW × Hours × Rate (₹/kWh)
- **Carbon Emissions**: Energy Consumption (kWh) × Emission Factor (kg CO2e/kWh)
- **Simple Payback Period**: Initial Investment / Annual Savings
- **PUE**: Total Facility Energy / IT Equipment Energy

## Key Points

- Green IT accounting requires viewing investments from both financial and environmental perspectives
- Energy costs typically represent 30-50% of data center operational expenses over 5 years
- The three-scope carbon accounting framework (GHG Protocol) is the global standard
- Cloud computing often delivers lower TCO and carbon footprint through provider efficiency
- E-waste is one of the fastest-growing waste streams globally; responsible disposal is critical
- ESG reporting has evolved from voluntary to mandatory in many jurisdictions
- Green IT investments can deliver 1-3 year payback periods with long-term positive returns
- Indian grid emission factor is approximately 0.71 kg CO2e per kWh

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions—each has distinct measurement approaches
- Ignoring Scope 3 emissions, which often represent the largest portion of IT carbon footprint
- Calculating payback without considering all TCO components
- Overlooking end-of-life costs in hardware procurement decisions
- Focusing only on energy costs while ignoring water usage and e-waste impacts

## Revision Tips

1. Practice carbon footprint calculations with different emission factors and equipment configurations
2. Memorize the key ESG frameworks (GRI, CDP, TCFD, SASB) and their purposes
3. Understand the relationship between PUE, energy efficiency, and operational costs
4. Review case studies comparing on-premises vs. cloud computing from sustainability perspectives
5. Be familiar with India's E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 and relevant regulations
