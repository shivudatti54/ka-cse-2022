# Cloud Computing and Sustainability - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Green Cloud Computing**: Using cloud computing resources in an environmentally responsible manner to minimize energy consumption and carbon emissions throughout the IT lifecycle.

- **Virtualization**: Technology enabling multiple virtual machines to run on a single physical server, improving utilization rates from 15-30% to 50-80%.

- **Multi-Tenancy**: Architecture where multiple organizations share common computing infrastructure, achieving economies of scale.

- **PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness)**: Metric measuring data center energy efficiency; lower values (closer to 1.0) indicate better performance.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **PUE Formula**: PUE = Total Facility Energy / IT Equipment Energy
- **Energy Savings from Virtualization**: (1 - Utilization_new/Utilization_old) × 100%
- **Carbon Reduction Potential**: Cloud migration can achieve 30-60% reduction in carbon emissions vs. traditional data centers

## Key Points

1. Cloud computing reduces carbon footprint by 30-60% compared to on-premises data centers through improved utilization and efficient operations.

2. Server virtualization is the foundational technology enabling cloud sustainability benefits by maximizing hardware utilization.

3. Modern cloud data centers achieve PUE of 1.1-1.4, compared to 2.0-3.0 for legacy facilities.

4. Elasticity in cloud computing ensures resources match demand, avoiding waste from over-provisioned peak-capacity infrastructure.

5. Cloud providers increasingly commit to 100% renewable energy matching for their operations.

6. Free cooling, hot/cold aisle containment, and liquid cooling are key data center cooling efficiency techniques.

7. Multi-tenancy consolidates workloads that would otherwise require separate underutilized infrastructure.

8. Location selection for data centers considers climate, energy availability, and regulatory factors.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing cloud computing with sustainability: Not all cloud providers are equally sustainable; energy sources and data center efficiency vary significantly.

- Ignoring the energy consumption of data transmission: Network traffic between users and cloud services consumes energy and should be considered in overall assessments.

- Assuming all workloads benefit equally: Some applications may not be suitable for cloud deployment due to latency requirements or regulatory constraints.

- Overlooking embodied energy: Manufacturing servers and infrastructure has environmental costs that must be considered in overall sustainability analysis.

## Revision Tips

1. Focus on the relationship between virtualization and resource utilization rates as the primary sustainability mechanism.

2. Remember that PUE measures total facility energy divided by IT equipment energy—lower is better.

3. Review the three main sustainability benefits: reduced hardware requirements, improved energy efficiency, and consolidated resource utilization.

4. Practice explaining how elastic scaling prevents energy waste during low-demand periods.

5. Understand that cloud sustainability depends on provider commitments, particularly regarding renewable energy usage.
