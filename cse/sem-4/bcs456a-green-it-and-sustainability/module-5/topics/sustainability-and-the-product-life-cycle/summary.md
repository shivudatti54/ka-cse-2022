# Sustainability And The Product Life Cycle - Summary

## Key Definitions

- **Product Life Cycle (PLC)**: The complete sequence of phases through which a software product passes from initial conception through development, deployment, usage, maintenance, and eventual retirement.

- **Green IT**: The practice of employing computing resources in an environmentally responsible manner, encompassing energy efficiency, reduced environmental impact, and sustainable design principles.

- **Sustainable Software Design**: The discipline of creating software products that minimize environmental impact throughout their entire life cycle while meeting functional and quality requirements.

- **Carbon Footprint**: The total greenhouse gas emissions caused directly and indirectly by a software product, typically measured in equivalent carbon dioxide units (CO2e).

- **Circular Economy**: An economic model that emphasizes extending product lifespans, reducing waste, and maximizing resource utilization through reuse, recycling, and regenerative practices.

## Important Formulas

- **Total Energy Consumption = Σ(Energy at each life cycle phase)**

- **Carbon Footprint = Total Energy Consumption × Carbon Intensity Factor**

- **Energy Efficiency = Useful Output / Energy Input**

## Key Points

- The software product life cycle comprises six main phases: Conceptualization, Development, Distribution, Usage, Maintenance, and End-of-Life.

- Design decisions made during early phases determine approximately 80% of the environmental impact throughout the software's life cycle.

- The usage phase typically accounts for the highest direct energy consumption, but all phases contribute to the overall environmental footprint.

- Circular life cycle models promote sustainability through extended software lifespan, efficient resource utilization, and reduced electronic waste.

- Sustainable software design requires considering both direct energy consumption (running software) and indirect energy consumption (infrastructure, manufacturing, disposal).

- Programming language choice, algorithm efficiency, and architectural patterns significantly influence energy consumption across all operational phases.

- Backward compatibility and efficient update mechanisms help prevent premature hardware obsolescence, contributing to environmental sustainability.

- Cloud computing and virtualization can improve energy efficiency through resource sharing but require careful consideration of provider sustainability practices.

## Common Mistakes

- **Ignoring Non-Operational Phases**: Many students focus solely on the usage phase energy consumption, neglecting manufacturing, development, and disposal impacts that collectively represent significant environmental burden.

- **Confusing Software and Hardware Sustainability**: Software itself does not degrade physically, but the hardware infrastructure and the premature obsolescence caused by software requirements are important sustainability considerations.

- **Oversimplifying Energy Metrics**: Treating energy consumption as merely a function of runtime ignores the complex interactions between software design, hardware utilization, and infrastructure efficiency.

- **Neglecting Trade-offs**: Sustainable software design involves balancing environmental considerations with performance, cost, functionality, and user experience requirements. Failing to acknowledge these trade-offs represents an incomplete understanding of the topic.
