## Introduction

Green Information Technology (Green IT) refers to the practice of using computing resources in an environmentally responsible manner. It encompasses the design, manufacture, use, and disposal of computer hardware, software, and peripheral devices in ways that minimize their environmental impact. As organizations worldwide increasingly recognize their ecological footprint, Green IT has emerged as a critical discipline that bridges technology with environmental sustainability.

The significance of Green IT cannot be overstated in the current era of digital transformation. Data centers alone consume approximately 1-2% of global electricity, and this number continues to rise with the exponential growth of data and cloud computing. The Information and Communication Technology (ICT) sector is responsible for roughly 2-3% of global carbon emissions, comparable to the aviation industry. This alarming statistic has prompted governments, corporations, and educational institutions to prioritize sustainable computing practices. the university (university) has included Green IT and Sustainability in its curriculum to prepare future engineers with the awareness and skills needed to develop environmentally conscious technological solutions.

## Key Concepts

### Definition and Scope of Green IT

Green IT, also known as Green Computing, involves implementing policies, practices, and technologies that reduce the environmental impact of IT operations. The scope extends beyond mere energy efficiency to include waste reduction, sustainable sourcing, virtualisation, cloud computing, and lifecycle management of IT assets. The fundamental goal is to achieve business objectives while minimizing negative environmental effects and maximizing positive ecological outcomes.

### The Three Pillars of Green IT

**Environmental Sustainability**: This pillar focuses on reducing carbon emissions, minimizing electronic waste (e-waste), and conserving natural resources. It involves adopting renewable energy sources for data centers, implementing power management features on devices, and designing energy-efficient algorithms and software.

**Economic Viability**: Green IT must make economic sense for organizations to adopt it sustainably. This includes reducing energy costs, extending hardware lifespan, and creating new business opportunities through green products and services. Cost savings achieved through virtualisation and cloud computing often provide immediate return on investment.

**Social Responsibility**: Organizations have a responsibility to their stakeholders, including employees, customers, and communities. Green IT initiatives contribute to social responsibility by creating healthier work environments, reducing noise pollution, and demonstrating corporate citizenship.

### Dimensions of Green IT

**Green Data Centers**: Modern data centers consume massive amounts of electricity for computing and cooling. Green data centers address this through energy-efficient hardware, free cooling using outside air, hot/cold aisle containment, and renewable energy integration. Virtualisation technology allows multiple virtual machines to run on fewer physical servers, dramatically reducing energy consumption.

**Green Hardware**: This involves designing computers and peripherals with environmentally friendly materials, reducing toxic substances like lead and mercury, enabling easy disassembly for recycling, and using recycled or recyclable materials in manufacturing. The European RoHS (Restriction of Hazardous Substances) directive exemplifies regulatory efforts in this dimension.

**Green Software**: Energy-efficient software design includes writing optimized code that consumes less processing power, implementing power-saving features in applications, and designing systems that can scale down during low-activity periods. Software virtualisation and containerisation contribute significantly to resource optimization.

**Green IT Governance**: This refers to the policies, frameworks, and management practices that ensure IT operations align with environmental goals. It includes establishing green IT teams, setting sustainability targets, measuring carbon footprint, and integrating environmental criteria into IT procurement decisions.

### Sustainable IT Framework

The sustainable IT framework encompasses the entire lifecycle of technology:

1. **Design Phase**: Incorporating environmental considerations from the initial design stage
2. **Procurement Phase**: Selecting vendors and products based on environmental criteria
3. **Usage Phase**: Implementing practices that minimize energy consumption during operation
4. **Disposal Phase**: Proper e-waste management through recycling, refurbishing, or responsible disposal

### Green IT Strategies

**Virtualisation**: This technology allows multiple operating systems and applications to run on a single physical server, dramatically reducing hardware requirements and energy consumption. Studies show that virtualisation can reduce server energy consumption by 60-80%.

**Cloud Computing**: By moving applications and data to cloud infrastructure, organizations can benefit from the cloud provider's energy-efficient infrastructure and pay-as-you-go models that match resource usage to actual needs.

**Power Management**: Implementing aggressive power management policies, including sleep modes, processor throttling, and scheduled shutdowns, can reduce energy consumption significantly.

**Telecommuting and Video Conferencing**: Reducing business travel through remote work and virtual meetings decreases carbon emissions associated with transportation.

### Electronic Waste Management

E-waste is one of the fastest-growing waste streams globally. Green IT emphasizes responsible e-waste management through:

- **Recycling**: Proper processing of electronic components to recover valuable materials
- **Refurbishing**: Extending the life of functional equipment through upgrades and repairs
- **Donation**: Providing usable equipment to schools and charitable organizations
- **Responsible Disposal**: Using certified e-waste handlers who ensure compliance with environmental regulations

## Examples

### Example 1: Data Center Energy Optimization

A mid-sized company operates a data center with 50 physical servers, each consuming 500 watts of power continuously. The annual energy cost at ₹7 per kWh would be:

- Total power consumption: 50 servers × 500W = 25,000W = 25 kW
- Annual consumption: 25 kW × 24 hours × 365 days = 219,000 kWh
- Annual cost: 219,000 × ₹7 = ₹15,33,000

By implementing virtualisation, the company consolidates to 15 physical servers while maintaining the same computing capacity. The new annual consumption becomes:

- 15 servers × 500W = 7.5 kW
- Annual consumption: 7.5 × 24 × 365 = 65,700 kWh
- Annual cost: 65,700 × ₹7 = ₹4,59,900

**Savings**: ₹10,73,100 annually (70% reduction in energy costs)

### Example 2: Green IT Procurement Policy

An organization implements a green procurement policy requiring all new hardware purchases to meet the following criteria:

- Energy Star certification (minimum 80% efficiency)
- RoHS compliance (no hazardous substances)
- EPEAT rating (minimum Silver)
- Manufacturer provides end-of-life recycling program

When purchasing 100 desktop computers, the organization selects Energy Star 5.0 compliant systems that consume 35W during normal operation compared to 80W for non-compliant models. The annual energy savings per unit: (80-35)W × 8 hours/day × 260 working days = 93.6 kWh per year. For 100 units: 9,360 kWh annual savings, translating to approximately ₹65,520 in savings.

### Example 3: Implementing Power Management Software

A university computer lab with 50 computers implements power management policies:

- Sleep mode after 10 minutes of inactivity
- Hibernate after 30 minutes of inactivity
- Scheduled shutdown at 7 PM daily

Assuming each computer runs 8 hours daily and consumes 150W when active, the annual consumption without power management is:

- 50 computers × 150W × 8 hours × 260 days = 15,600 kWh

With power management, assuming 20% time in sleep (50W) and 30% time in hibernate (5W):

- Active: 50 × 150W × 4.16 hours × 260 = 8,124 kWh
- Sleep: 50 × 50W × 1.6 hours × 260 = 1,040 kWh
- Hibernate: 50 × 5W × 1.64 hours × 260 = 107 kWh
- Off (other hours): 50 × 5W × 0.6 hours × 260 = 39 kWh
- Total: 9,310 kWh

**Energy Saved**: 6,290 kWh (40% reduction)

## Exam Tips

1. **Green IT Definition**: Be prepared to define Green IT and explain its scope in 2-3 sentences for short answer questions.

2. **Three Pillars**: Remember the three pillars of Green IT - Environmental Sustainability, Economic Viability, and Social Responsibility.

3. **Virtualisation Benefits**: Virtualisation can reduce energy consumption by 60-80%, a frequently asked numerical problem in university exams.

4. **E-Waste Statistics**: Remember that e-waste is one of the fastest-growing waste streams globally; this is important for conceptual questions.

5. **Energy Star and EPEAT**: Know these certification standards as they are commonly asked in exam questions regarding green hardware.

6. **Green Data Center Techniques**: Hot/cold aisle containment, free cooling, and virtualisation are key techniques for green data centers.

7. **Green IT Strategies**: Be familiar with at least 4-5 strategies: virtualisation, cloud computing, power management, telecommuting, and green procurement.

8. **Lifecycle Approach**: Remember the four phases of sustainable IT - Design, Procurement, Usage, and Disposal.

9. **ICT Carbon Footprint**: The ICT sector contributes 2-3% of global carbon emissions, comparable to the aviation industry.

10. **Difference between Sleep and Hibernate**: Sleep mode retains data in RAM with minimal power, while Hibernate saves data to disk and completely powers down the system.
