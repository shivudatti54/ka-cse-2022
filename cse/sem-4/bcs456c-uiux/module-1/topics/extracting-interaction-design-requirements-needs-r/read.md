## Introduction

Interaction Design (IxD) is a critical discipline within User Experience (UX) that focuses on designing interactive products, systems, and services to facilitate meaningful user experiences. Before any design work can begin, it is essential to extract and understand the design requirements and user needs that will guide the entire development process. This process forms the foundation upon which successful interactive products are built.

In the context of the university's UI/UX curriculum, understanding how to extract interaction design requirements is fundamental to creating user-centered designs. The extraction process involves gathering information about user goals, tasks, contexts, and constraints through various research methods. This knowledge enables designers to make informed decisions about how users will interact with digital products, ensuring that the final solution meets real user needs rather than assumed requirements.

The importance of proper requirement extraction cannot be overstated in modern software development. Poorly understood requirements lead to products that fail to meet user expectations, resulting in wasted development resources and poor user satisfaction. By systematically extracting interaction design requirements, designers can create interactive systems that are intuitive, efficient, and enjoyable to use.

## Key Concepts

### 1. User Needs Analysis

User needs analysis is the systematic process of identifying what users require from an interactive system. It goes beyond simply asking users what they want and delves into understanding the underlying motivations, goals, and pain points. User needs are typically categorized into three levels:

- **Functional Needs**: What the product must do (e.g., allow users to send messages, process payments, create documents)
- **Emotional Needs**: How the product should make users feel (e.g., confident, comfortable, excited)
- **Social Needs**: How the product affects users' relationships and social interactions

### 2. Design Requirements

Design requirements translate user needs into specific, measurable criteria that the design must satisfy. These requirements are derived from user research and serve as a benchmark for evaluating design solutions. Design requirements should be:

- **Specific**: Clearly defined and unambiguous
- **Measurable**: Can be tested and verified
- **Achievable**: Technically and economically feasible
- **Relevant**: Directly linked to user needs
- **Time-bound**: Have clear deadlines or milestones

### 3. Stakeholder Analysis

Stakeholder analysis involves identifying all individuals or groups who have an interest in the interactive product. This includes:

- **Primary Stakeholders**: Direct users of the product
- **Secondary Stakeholders**: Those who support or maintain the product
- **Tertiary Stakeholders**: Those affected by the product's existence
- **Key Stakeholders**: Decision-makers and sponsors

Understanding stakeholder perspectives helps ensure that the design addresses diverse needs and gains necessary organizational support.

### 4. User Research Methods

Various methods are employed to extract user needs and design requirements:

**Interviews**: One-on-one conversations with users to understand their experiences, goals, and challenges. Interviews can be structured (fixed questions), semi-structured (flexible with follow-up questions), or unstructured (conversational).

**Surveys and Questionnaires**: Systematic data collection from large user samples. Effective for gathering quantitative data about user preferences, behaviors, and demographics.

**Observations**: Watching users perform tasks in their natural environment. This reveals actual behavior rather than reported behavior, which may differ significantly.

**Contextual Inquiry**: A combination of observation and interview conducted in the user's work context. The designer observes and asks questions while the user performs real tasks.

**Focus Groups**: Group discussions with 6-10 users to explore attitudes, ideas, and perceptions. Useful for generating ideas and understanding social dynamics.

### 5. Personas

Personas are fictional representations of target users created from research data. They encapsulate key user characteristics, goals, behaviors, and pain points. A well-crafted persona helps designers maintain user focus throughout the design process. Personas typically include:

- Demographic information (age, occupation, education)
- User goals and motivations
- Technical proficiency level
- Pain points with current solutions
- Preferred interaction patterns

### 6. User Scenarios

User scenarios are narrative descriptions of how users accomplish specific goals using the product. They describe the context, user actions, and system responses. Scenarios help designers visualize the user experience and identify necessary interactions. Effective scenarios include:

- The user's background and context
- The goal the user wants to achieve
- The steps taken to accomplish the goal
- Obstacles encountered and how they are overcome
- The final outcome

### 7. Task Analysis

Task analysis is the process of breaking down user tasks into their component steps to understand how users accomplish goals. It reveals the cognitive and physical activities involved in task completion. Task analysis methods include:

- **Hierarchical Task Analysis (HTA)**: Breaks tasks into sub-tasks arranged hierarchically
- **Cognitive Task Analysis (CTA)**: Focuses on mental processes, decision-making, and problem-solving
- **Entity-Relationship Modeling**: Represents relationships between objects and actions

### 8. User Stories and Use Cases

User stories are brief descriptions of user needs written from the user's perspective, typically following the format: "As a [type of user], I want [goal] so that [benefit]."

Use cases are more detailed descriptions that include actors, system responses, and alternative flows. Both techniques help communicate requirements clearly to development teams.

## Examples

### Example 1: E-Learning Platform Requirements Extraction

**Scenario**: A university wants to develop an e-learning platform for students students.

**Step 1: Stakeholder Identification**

- Primary: Students and faculty members
- Secondary: IT administrators, content creators
- Tertiary: Parents, accreditation bodies

**Step 2: User Research (Sample Interview Questions)**

1. What challenges do you face with current learning methods?
2. How do you prefer to access course materials?
3. What features would help you learn more effectively?
4. What frustrations have you experienced with existing platforms?

**Step 3: Persona Creation**

- "Academic Amit": A final-year students who is tech-savvy but struggles with time management. Prefers mobile access for studying during commutes. Needs offline reading capabilities.

**Step 4: Requirements Derivation**

- Functional: Access course materials offline, submit assignments, view grades, participate in discussions
- Non-functional: Mobile-responsive, fast loading, accessible design
- Emotional: Reduce anxiety about deadlines, increase confidence in understanding concepts

### Example 2: Healthcare Mobile App Requirements

**Scenario**: A hospital chain wants an appointment booking app.

**Research Findings from 50 patient surveys:**

- 70% prefer booking appointments from home
- 60% find phone calls stressful
- 80% want appointment reminders
- 45% need to manage family appointments

**Design Requirements:**

1. The app must allow users to book, reschedule, and cancel appointments
2. The app must send automated reminders 24 hours before appointments
3. The app must support managing appointments for up to 5 family members
4. The interface must be accessible to users aged 50+ (large text, simple navigation)
5. Booking confirmation must be provided within 3 seconds

### Example 3: Online Shopping Redesign

**Current Problem**: High cart abandonment rate (75%)

**User Research through Contextual Inquiry:**

- Users abandon carts when unexpected shipping costs appear late
- Users cannot easily find saved items
- Users want to compare prices before purchasing
- Users are concerned about return policies

**Extracted Requirements:**

1. Display all costs including shipping early in the checkout process
2. Create a persistent "wish list" feature
3. Add price comparison tool for similar products
4. Prominently display return policy on product pages
5. Implement one-click reorder for returning customers

## Exam Tips

1. **Understand the difference between user needs and design requirements**: User needs describe what users require, while design requirements are measurable criteria that must be satisfied.

2. **Remember the three levels of user needs**: Functional, emotional, and social needs are all important for comprehensive requirement extraction.

3. **Know when to use specific research methods**: Interviews for qualitative insights, surveys for quantitative data, observations for actual behavior, and focus groups for generating ideas.

4. **Personas vs. Scenarios**: Remember that personas represent WHO the user is, while scenarios represent WHAT the user does and HOW they accomplish goals.

5. **User story format**: The standard format "As a [user], I want [feature] so that [benefit]" is essential for writing user stories.

6. **Task analysis importance**: Understand that task analysis helps identify the steps users take, potential errors, and opportunities for design improvement.

7. **Requirement quality characteristics**: Remember the SMARTER framework - Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound, Extensible, and Reviewable.

8. **Stakeholder categorization**: Be able to identify and differentiate between primary, secondary, and tertiary stakeholders in exam scenarios.

9. **Difference between interviews and surveys**: Interviews provide depth and flexibility; surveys provide breadth and quantitative data.

10. **Non-functional requirements**: Don't forget non-functional requirements like performance, usability, reliability, and security alongside functional requirements.
