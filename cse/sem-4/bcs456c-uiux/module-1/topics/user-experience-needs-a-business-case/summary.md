# User Experience Needs a Business Case - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Business Case**: A strategic document that justifies investment in UX projects by demonstrating measurable financial returns and alignment with organizational goals.
- **UX ROI (Return on Investment)**: A metric calculated as ((Total Benefits - Total Costs) / Total Costs) × 100, measuring the financial return from UX investments.
- **Direct Benefits**: Tangible financial outcomes such as increased revenue, reduced costs, and improved conversion rates.
- **Indirect Benefits**: Intangible but valuable outcomes including brand perception, risk reduction, and employee productivity improvements.
- **Stakeholder Alignment**: The process of addressing different stakeholder priorities (executives, marketing, finance, development) in business case development.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Basic ROI Formula**: ROI = ((Net Benefit) / Cost of Investment) × 100
- **Net Benefit Calculation**: Net Benefit = Total Financial Benefits - Total Project Costs
- **Payback Period**: The time required to recoup the initial investment from project benefits
- **Cost of Poor UX**: Includes high abandonment rates, increased support costs, longer development cycles, and customer churn

## Key Points

- UX business cases bridge the gap between intangible design benefits and measurable business outcomes
- Five essential components: Executive Summary, Problem Statement, Solution, Benefits Quantification, and Resource Requirements
- ROI calculation requires connecting UX improvements to specific business metrics (conversion rates, retention, support costs)
- Direct benefits (revenue, cost savings) should be calculated separately from indirect benefits (brand value, risk reduction)
- Competitive analysis strengthens business cases by demonstrating market necessity
- Realistic timelines should show when investments will be recouped—the payback period
- Stakeholder alignment is critical—different groups prioritize different outcomes

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Overlooking Indirect Benefits**: Many students focus only on direct revenue and miss brand value, risk reduction, and productivity gains
2. **Unrealistic Projections**: Avoiding overly optimistic claims; use industry benchmarks and conservative estimates
3. **Ignoring Stakeholder Perspectives**: Failing to address different stakeholder concerns weakens the business case
4. **Missing Timeline Details**: Not including when benefits will be realized reduces credibility

## Revision Tips

1. Practice calculating ROI using the formula with different scenario numbers
2. Memorize the five key components of a business case
3. Prepare examples that demonstrate both direct and indirect benefits
4. Review common UX metrics (conversion rate, CSAT, NPS, task completion time) and their business implications
5. Understand how to align UX objectives with organizational goals for different stakeholder groups
