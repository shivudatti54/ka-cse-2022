# Measurement Instruments in UI/UX - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Measurement Instruments**: Tools used to collect data about user behavior, preferences, and performance in UX research
- **Quantitative Instruments**: Tools that produce numerical data (task completion rates, time measurements, rating scales)
- **Qualitative Instruments**: Tools that produce non-numerical data (interviews, think-aloud protocols, observations)
- **Task Completion Rate**: Percentage of users who successfully complete a given task
- **Error Rate**: Number of mistakes users make during task execution
- **Time on Task**: Duration required to complete a specific task

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **SUS Score**: Sum of odd-position item scores minus 5, then multiply by 2.5 (gives 0-100 scale)
- **NPS Calculation**: %Promoters (scores 9-10) - %Detractors (scores 0-6), range: -100 to +100
- **SEQ**: Post-task difficulty rating on 7-point scale (higher = easier)
- **Conversion Rate**: (Number of conversions / Total visitors) × 100%

## Key Points

1. Measurement instruments bridge subjective user experience and objective design decisions
2. SUS is the industry standard for perceived usability - scores above 68 are above average
3. Task-based metrics measure objective performance; questionnaires measure subjective perceptions
4. NPS measures loyalty through a single question about recommendation likelihood
5. Think-aloud protocols reveal user reasoning but are time-consuming to analyze
6. Behavioral analytics provide automatic, large-scale interaction data
7. Eye-tracking measures visual attention but requires expensive specialized equipment
8. A/B testing compares design versions using real user traffic with defined success metrics
9. Valid instruments measure what they claim; reliable instruments produce consistent results
10. Combining multiple instruments provides comprehensive understanding vs. single-method approaches

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing completion rate with conversion rate** - Completion rate measures task success; conversion rate measures business goal achievement
2. **Misremembering SUS scoring** - Many students forget to multiply by 2.5 or incorrectly handle reverse-worded questions
3. **Using qualitative data for statistical claims** - Interview findings cannot be statistically analyzed; they provide insights, not numbers
4. **Ignoring sample size** - Small samples (under 10) cannot reliably indicate real user behavior patterns
5. **Choosing instruments without considering research objectives** - Always match instrument to the question being asked

## Revision Tips

1. Practice calculating SUS scores from sample responses to reinforce the formula
2. Create a comparison table of all instruments covered, noting purpose, data type, and when to use
3. Memorize that SUS > 68 is above average - this is a frequently tested fact
4. Review the NPS classification: Promoters (9-10), Passives (7-8), Detractors (0-6)
5. Understand that measurement instruments are selected based on research objectives, not personal preference
