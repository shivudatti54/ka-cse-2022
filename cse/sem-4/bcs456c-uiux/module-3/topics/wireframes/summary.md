# Wireframes in UI/UX Design - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Wireframe:** A visual blueprint of a digital product showing layout structure, element placement, and functionality without visual styling.
- **Low-Fidelity (Lo-Fi) Wireframe:** Simple sketches using basic shapes, ideal for early brainstorming and quick iteration.
- **Mid-Fidelity Wireframe:** Detailed digital wireframes with accurate proportions and basic typography; most commonly used in practice.
- **High-Fidelity (Hi-Fi) Wireframe:** Detailed wireframes closely resembling the final product, used in later design stages.
- **Prototype:** An interactive simulation of user flows with clickable elements.
- **Mockup:** A static, high-fidelity visual design showing final aesthetics without interactivity.

## Important Formulas and Techniques

- **Wireframe Elements:** Navigation + Content Areas + Interactive Components + Layout Structure + Information Hierarchy
- **Design Process Flow:** Research → Sitemap → Wireframe → Prototype → Mockup → Final Product

## Key Points

- Wireframes focus on functionality and structure rather than visual aesthetics.
- They serve as communication tools between designers, developers, and stakeholders.
- Low-fidelity wireframes encourage creative exploration without design attachment.
- The wireframing process includes research, sketching, digital creation, and documentation.
- Annotation is essential for communicating functionality to developers.
- Wireframes help identify usability issues early, saving time and resources.
- Different tools (Balsamiq, Figma, Adobe XD) serve different wireframing needs.
- Mobile wireframing requires consideration of touch targets and screen constraints.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Adding too much detail too early—start with low-fidelity before progressing to high-fidelity.
2. Using actual images and full content instead of placeholders in early wireframes.
3. Skipping the research phase and jumping directly into wireframing.
4. Failing to annotate wireframes with functionality and interaction notes.
5. Creating wireframes without considering the target users and their goals.

## Revision Tips

1. Practice creating wireframes for common interface types (login, e-commerce, dashboard).
2. Review the differences between wireframe, prototype, and mockup until they are clear.
3. Familiarize yourself with at least one wireframing tool for hands-on practice.
4. Remember that wireframing is iterative—expect to create multiple versions.
5. Focus on understanding when to use each type of wireframe fidelity based on project stage.
