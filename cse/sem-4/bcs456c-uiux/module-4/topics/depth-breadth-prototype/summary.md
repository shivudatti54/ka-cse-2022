# Depth and Breadth of a Prototype - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Prototype Depth:** The level of detail, interactivity, and functional completeness within a single screen or feature of a prototype. Ranges from low-fidelity (basic sketches, wireframes) to high-fidelity (realistic interactions, animations, functional simulations).

- **Prototype Breadth:** The extent of coverage across the product's screens, features, or user journeys. A broad prototype covers many screens; a narrow prototype focuses on specific features.

- **Depth-Breadth Trade-off:** The inverse relationship where increased depth typically requires decreased breadth given fixed resources of time, budget, and effort.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

There are no specific formulas for this topic, but the core principle can be expressed as: **Design Effort = Depth × Breadth**. When effort is fixed, increasing one variable necessitates decreasing the other.

## Key Points

- Low-fidelity prototypes (paper, wireframes) enable rapid iteration and early-stage testing with minimal investment.

- High-fidelity prototypes provide realistic user experience but require significant time and skill to create.

- Early design phases benefit from broader prototyping to validate overall user flows and information architecture.

- Later design phases benefit from deeper prototyping to refine specific interactions and detailed behaviors.

- Paper prototypes remain valuable for collaborative design sessions and early stakeholder feedback.

- The choice between depth and breadth should align with the primary testing or communication objective.

- Iterative design often progresses from broad/shallow to narrow/deep as the design matures.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Over-investing in fidelity too early:** Creating high-fidelity prototypes before validating basic concepts wastes resources when fundamental changes are needed.

2. **Ignoring the depth-breadth trade-off:** Attempting to create comprehensive and detailed prototypes without adequate time leads to compromised results.

3. **Failing to match prototype to testing goals:** Using shallow prototypes to test complex interactions or deep prototypes for general flow validation provides poor insights.

4. **Neglecting stakeholder needs:** Creating technically excellent prototypes that don't address stakeholder communication requirements fails the project's broader objectives.

## Revision Tips

1. Remember that depth = detail/interaction within features, breadth = coverage across screens/features.

2. Associate low-fidelity with early phases and high-fidelity with later refinement stages.

3. Visualize the depth-breadth matrix: early testing (wide-shallow) → problem identification → detailed exploration (narrow-deep) → validated solution.

4. Practice identifying appropriate prototyping approaches for different scenario descriptions commonly tested in university exams.
