## Purpose
Multiplexing is a fundamental technique in computer networks that enables multiple signals to share a single transmission medium, thereby maximizing bandwidth utilization and reducing infrastructure costs. Understanding multiplexing is essential for designing efficient communication systems, from local area networks to wide‑area telecom backbones, and underpins many real‑world applications such as telephone networks, wireless communications, and internet data transport.

## Learning Objectives
- **Explain** the concept and need for multiplexing in network communications.  
- **Distinguish** between the main types of multiplexing: Frequency Division Multiplexing (FDM), Time Division Multiplexing (TDM), Code Division Multiplexing (CDM), and Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM).  
- **Describe** how each multiplexing technique allocates resources (frequency, time, code, or wavelength) to simultaneous transmissions.  
- **Analyze** the advantages and limitations of each multiplexing method in terms of bandwidth efficiency, latency, and implementation complexity.  
- **Compare** synchronous and asynchronous TDM, and discuss their suitability for different network scenarios.  
- **Design** a simple multiplexing/demultiplexing scheme for a given set of input signals and a specified medium.  
- **Evaluate** the impact of multiplexing on network performance metrics such as throughput, signal‑to‑noise ratio, and error rate.  
- **Illustrate** real‑world applications of multiplexing (e.g., DSL, cable TV, cellular radio, fiber‑optic WDM) and relate them to the theoretical concepts.
