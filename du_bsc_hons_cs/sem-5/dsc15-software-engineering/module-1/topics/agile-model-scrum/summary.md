# Agile Model and Scrum - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Agile Model**: An iterative approach to software development that emphasizes flexibility, collaboration, and customer satisfaction through incremental delivery
- **Agile Manifesto**: A declaration of four values and twelve principles for agile software development, created in 2001
- **Scrum**: A lightweight framework for developing, delivering, and sustaining complex products with an initial emphasis on software development
- **Empiricism**: The theory that knowledge comes from experience; Scrum's foundation based on observation and adaptation

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Scrum Pillars**: Transparency → Inspection → Adaptation
- **Scrum Values**: Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, Courage
- **Standard Sprint Duration**: 2-4 weeks (fixed throughout project)
- **Daily Scrum Duration**: 15 minutes (time-boxed)
- **Optimal Team Size**: 3-9 members (Development Team)
- **Velocity Calculation**: Average story points completed across previous sprints

## Key Points

1. Agile prioritizes individuals and interactions, working software, customer collaboration, and responding to change over rigid processes
2. Scrum is built on three pillars: transparency, inspection, and adaptation
3. Product Owner manages the Product Backlog and maximizes product value
4. Scrum Master serves as a facilitator, removing impediments (not a traditional manager)
5. Development Team is self-organizing, cross-functional, and collectively accountable
6. Sprint is a time-boxed iteration (2-4 weeks) producing a "Done" increment
7. Daily Scrum synchronizes the team in 15 minutes through three questions
8. Sprint Review demonstrates work to stakeholders; Sprint Retrospective improves team processes
9. Product Backlog is the single source of requirements, continuously refined
10. Definition of Done ensures shared understanding of completion criteria

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing Scrum Master with a project manager (it's a servant-leader role)
- Treating Agile as "no process" or "no documentation (documentation still exists but is purposeful)
- Changing sprint scope mid-sprint (scope changes happen in planning, not during sprint)
- Skipping or combining Scrum events (each has specific purpose)
- Assuming longer sprints are better (consistency matters more than duration)
