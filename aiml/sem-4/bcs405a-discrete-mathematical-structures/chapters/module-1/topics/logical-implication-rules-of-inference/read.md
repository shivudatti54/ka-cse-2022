# **Logical Implication – Rules of Inference**

## **Definition and Explanation**

Logical implication, also known as material implication, is a rule of inference in classical logic. It states that if the premise is true, then the conclusion must also be true. However, the converse and inverse of the implication may not necessarily be true.

Let's consider a simple example:

If it is raining, then the streets are wet.

In this case, "If P, then Q" (P → Q) is the logical implication. Here, P is "It is raining" and Q is "The streets are wet".

## **Rules of Inference**

There are two main rules of inference for logical implication:

### 1. **Modus Ponens**

Modus ponens is a rule of inference that states:

"If P, then Q" (P → Q)
P
∴ Q

This rule allows us to conclude Q from the premises P and P → Q.

### 2. **Modus Tollens**

Modus tollens is a rule of inference that states:

"If P, then Q" (P → Q)
∼Q
∴ ∼P

This rule allows us to conclude ∼P from the premises ∼Q and P → Q.

## **Examples**

### Example 1: Modus Ponens

Premise 1: If it is raining, then the streets are wet. (P → Q)
Premise 2: It is raining. (P)
∴ The streets are wet. (Q)

### Example 2: Modus Tollens

Premise 1: If it is raining, then the streets are wet. (P → Q)
Premise 2: The streets are not wet. (∼Q)
∴ It is not raining. (∼P)

## **Key Concepts**

- **Material implication**: a rule of inference that states if the premise is true, then the conclusion must also be true.
- **Modus ponens**: a rule of inference that allows us to conclude Q from the premises P and P → Q.
- **Modus tollens**: a rule of inference that allows us to conclude ∼P from the premises ∼Q and P → Q.
- **Converse and inverse**: the converse of P → Q is Q → P, while the inverse of P → Q is ∼P → ∼Q.
