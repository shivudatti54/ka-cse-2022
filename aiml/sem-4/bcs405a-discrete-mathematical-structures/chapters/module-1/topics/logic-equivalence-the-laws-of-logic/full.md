## **The Laws of Logic**

The laws of logic are a set of rules that govern the behavior of logical statements. They are used to derive new statements from existing ones and to show that certain statements are equivalent. The laws of logic are as follows:

### 1. The Law of Identity

- A statement is logically equivalent to itself.

Symbolically, this law can be written as:

A ≡ A

This law states that a statement is always true if it is identical to itself.

### 2. The Law of Non-Contradiction

- A statement cannot be both true and false at the same time.

Symbolically, this law can be written as:

¬A ≡ ¬¬A

This law states that a statement cannot be contradicted by itself.

### 3. The Law of Excluded Middle

- A statement is either true or false, with no middle ground.

Symbolically, this law can be written as:

A ∨ ¬A ≡ T

This law states that a statement is either true or false, with no possibility of being both.

### 4. The Law of Associativity

- The logical operators can be applied in any order.

Symbolically, this law can be written as:

(A ∨ B) ∨ C ≡ A ∨ (B ∨ C)

This law states that the logical operators can be applied in any order.

### 5. The Law of Commutativity

- The order of the statements does not change the outcome.

Symbolically, this law can be written as:

A ∨ B ≡ B ∨ A

This law states that the order of the statements does not change the outcome.

### 6. The Law of Absorption

- A statement can be absorbed by another statement.

Symbolically, this law can be written as:

A ∨ (A ∧ B) ≡ A

This law states that a statement can be absorbed by another statement.

### 7. The Law of Distributivity

- A statement can be distributed over another statement.

Symbolically, this law can be written as:

(A ∧ B) ∨ A ≡ (A ∨ A) ∧ B

This law states that a statement can be distributed over another statement.

### 8. The Law of De Morgan

- The negation of a statement is equivalent to the negation of its components.

Symbolically, this law can be written as:

¬(A ∧ B) ≡ ¬A ∨ ¬B

This law states that the negation of a statement is equivalent to the negation of its components.

### 9. The Law of Modus Ponens

- If a statement is true and its components are true, then the conclusion follows.

Symbolically, this law can be written as:

(A ∧ B) → C ≡ (A → C) ∧ (B → C)

This law states that if a statement is true and its components are true, then the conclusion follows.

### 10. The Law of Modus Tollens

- If a statement is false and its components are false, then the conclusion follows.

Symbolically, this law can be written as:

(A ∧ B) → C ≡ ¬(A → C) ∨ ¬B

This law states that if a statement is false and its components are false, then the conclusion follows.

**Example 1: Applying the Laws of Logic**

Suppose we want to show that the statement "A or B" is equivalent to the statement "B or A". We can use the Law of Commutativity:

A ∨ B ≡ B ∨ A

This shows that the two statements are equivalent.

**Example 2: Using the Law of Excluded Middle**

Suppose we want to show that the statement "A is true" is equivalent to the statement "A is false". We can use the Law of Excluded Middle:

¬A ≡ ¬¬A

This shows that the two statements are equivalent.
