# **Providing a Public Good**

## **Introduction**

In algorithmic game theory, a public good is a resource that is non-excludable and non-rivalrous, meaning that it cannot be excluded from those who do not pay for it and it can be consumed by multiple individuals at the same time. Providing a public good is a fundamental concept in economics and game theory, as it highlights the challenges of coordinating individual actions to achieve a common goal.

## **Key Definitions**

- **Non-excludable**: A good or service is non-excludable if it cannot be excluded from those who do not pay for it. In other words, it is impossible to deny someone access to the good or service if they are not willing to pay for it.
- **Non-rivalrous**: A good or service is non-rivalrous if its consumption does not reduce its availability for others. In other words, consuming the good or service does not decrease the amount available for others.
- **Public good**: A public good is a non-excludable and non-rivalrous good or service that provides benefits to all members of society.

## **Types of Public Goods**

There are two main types of public goods:

- **Excludable public goods**: These are public goods that can be excluded from those who do not pay for them. Examples include national defense and law enforcement.
- **Non-excludable public goods**: These are public goods that cannot be excluded from those who do not pay for them. Examples include public parks and public roads.

## **Examples of Public Goods**

- **National Defense**: The defense of a nation is a public good that is non-excludable and non-rivalrous. If one person pays for the defense, they can benefit from the security provided, and the defense is not reduced for others.
- **Public Parks**: Public parks are a type of public good that is non-excludable and non-rivalrous. If one person uses a public park, they can benefit from the amenities provided, and the park is not reduced for others.

## **Game Theory and Public Goods**

In game theory, providing a public good is a classic problem of the prisoner's dilemma. The dilemma arises because individual self-interest may lead people to free-ride on others, rather than contributing to the public good. This can lead to a suboptimal outcome for society as a whole.

**Example 1: Prisoner's Dilemma**

Suppose two prisoners, Alice and Bob, are accused of a crime and must decide whether to confess or remain silent. If both confess, they each receive a sentence of one year in prison. If one confesses and the other remains silent, the confessor receives a sentence of one year and the silent person receives a sentence of three years. If both remain silent, they each receive a sentence of one year.

|                    | Alice Confesses                             | Alice Remains Silent                        |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Bob Confesses      | Both receive 1 year                         | Bob receives 1 year, Alice receives 3 years |
| Bob Remains Silent | Alice receives 1 year, Bob receives 3 years | Both receive 1 year                         |

In this example, the dominant strategy is for both Alice and Bob to confess, even though this leads to a suboptimal outcome for society as a whole.

**Example 2: Public Good Tragedy**

Suppose two people, Sarah and John, own adjacent pieces of land and must decide whether to install a fence to prevent cattle from escaping. If both install fences, they each receive the benefit of preventing the cattle from escaping. However, if only one installs a fence, they each receive the benefit of preventing the cattle from escaping, but the other person's land is still accessible.

|                             | Sarah Installs Fence                  | Sarah Does Not Install Fence          |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| John Installs Fence         | Both receive benefit                  | John receives benefit, Sarah does not |
| John Does Not Install Fence | Sarah receives benefit, John does not | Neither receives benefit              |

In this example, the dominant strategy is for both Sarah and John to install fences, even though this leads to a suboptimal outcome for society as a whole.
