# Finitely and Infinitely Repeated Prisoner’s Dilemma

=============================================

## Introduction

---

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a fundamental concept in game theory that has been extensively studied in various fields, including economics, psychology, and computer science. In this study material, we will delve into the finitely and infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, exploring its key concepts, strategies, and implications.

## Definitions

---

### Prisoner's Dilemma

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a game where two prisoners, A and B, are arrested and interrogated separately by the police. Each prisoner has two options: to confess or remain silent. The payoffs for each option are as follows:

|                               | Prisoner A Confesses   | Prisoner A Remains Silent |
| ----------------------------- | ---------------------- | ------------------------- |
| **Prisoner B Confesses**      | A: 1 year, B: 1 year   | A: 10 years, B: 10 years  |
| **Prisoner B Remains Silent** | A: 10 years, B: 1 year | A: 1 year, B: 1 year      |

### Finitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

In a finitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, the game is played multiple times, with the prisoners making decisions based on the payoffs from previous periods.

### Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

In an infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, the game is played an infinite number of times, with the prisoners making decisions based on the payoffs from previous periods.

## Key Concepts

---

### Nash Equilibrium

A Nash Equilibrium is a state where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming the other player's strategy remains the same.

### Fermat’s Apparent Paradox

Fermat’s Apparent Paradox states that, in the finitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, both prisoners will confess, even though it is not the dominant strategy.

### Tit for Tat

Tit for Tat is a strategy where a player imitates the other player's previous move. This strategy is often used in finitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma.

### Prisoner’s Dilemma in Multiple Periods

In multiple periods, the Prisoner's Dilemma can be solved using backward induction. The prisoners can use this technique to determine the optimal strategy.

## Strategies

---

### Finitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

- **Cooperation**: Both prisoners cooperate and remain silent.
- **Defection**: Both prisoners defect and confess.
- **Mixed Strategies**: Each prisoner uses a mixed strategy, with a probability of cooperating and a probability of defecting.

### Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

- **Cooperative Equilibrium**: Both prisoners cooperate and remain silent.
- **Defector Equilibrium**: Both prisoners defect and confess.
- **Prisoner’s Dilemma with Reputation**: The prisoners take into account their past behavior and adjust their strategy accordingly.

## Examples

---

### Finitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

Suppose two prisoners, A and B, play the Prisoner's Dilemma five times. If both prisoners cooperate, they will receive 5 years each. If both prisoners defect, they will receive 10 years each. If one prisoner cooperates and the other defects, the cooperator will receive 10 years and the defector will receive 1 year.

| Period | Prisoner A | Prisoner B |
| ------ | ---------- | ---------- |
| 1      | C          | C          |
| 2      | C          | C          |
| 3      | C          | C          |
| 4      | C          | C          |
| 5      | C          | C          |

In this case, both prisoners will cooperate, as it is the dominant strategy.

### Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

Suppose two prisoners, A and B, play the Prisoner's Dilemma infinitely. If both prisoners cooperate, they will receive a constant payoff of 1 year each. If both prisoners defect, they will receive a constant payoff of 10 years each. If one prisoner cooperates and the other defects, the cooperator will receive 10 years and the defector will receive 1 year.

In this case, the prisoner with the higher payoff for defecting will defect, as it is the dominant strategy.
