## Mathematical Formulation

The Prisoner's Dilemma can be represented as a game with two players, A and B, who are arrested and interrogated separately. Each player has two options: cooperate (C) or defect (D). The payoffs for each player are as follows:

|                             | Player B Cooperates (C) | Player B Defects (D) |
| --------------------------- | ----------------------- | -------------------- |
| **Player A Cooperates (C)** | A and B both get 3      | A gets 0, B gets 5   |
| **Player A Defects (D)**    | A gets 5, B gets 0      | A gets 1, B gets 1   |

The game is repeated finitely or infinitely, and the players must make a sequence of decisions based on the outcomes of previous rounds.

## Finitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

In a finitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, the game is played a fixed number of times, say `n`. The players know that the game will be repeated `n` times. In this case, the optimal strategy for each player is to defect in the first round and cooperate in all subsequent rounds. This strategy is known as the "tit-for-tat" strategy.

The payoffs for the tit-for-tat strategy are as follows:

| ----- | ---------- | ---------- | ---------- | ---------- |
| 1     | C          | C          | 3          | 3          |
| 2     | D          | C          | 1          | 5          |
| 3     | D          | D          | 1          | 1          |
| ...   | ...        | ...        | ...        | ...        |
| n     | D          | D          | 1          | 1          |

In the first round, both players cooperate and get a payoff of 3. In the second round, player A defects and player B cooperates, resulting in payoffs of 1 and 5, respectively. In the third round, both players defect and get a payoff of 1.
