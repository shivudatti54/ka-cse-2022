# Strategies in an Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

## Overview

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a fundamental game theory concept. In the infinitely repeated version, players can make multiple moves, allowing for strategic analysis.

## Key Concepts

### Definitions

- **Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma (IRPD):** A repeated game where players make repeated decisions, with the goal of maximizing payoff in the long run.
- **Strategic Subgame Perfect Equilibrium (SSPE):** A Nash Equilibrium where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy.

### Formulas

- **Payoff Matrix:**
  - P(D, D) = (3, 3)
  - P(D, S) = (0, 5)
  - P(S, D) = (5, 0)
  - P(S, S) = (1, 1)
- **Nash Equilibrium:**
  - The equilibrium where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy.

### Strategies

- **Tit-for-Tat (TFT):** A strategy that starts with cooperation and mirrors the opponent's previous move.
- **Punishment (Pun):** A strategy that cooperates until the opponent defects, then punishes.
- **Griffin (G):** A strategy that cooperates on first moves, then switches to punishment.

### Theorems

- **Folk Theorem:** Any Nash Equilibrium can be sustained in an infinitely repeated game.
- **Evolutionary Stability (ES):** A strategy is ES if it is a Nash Equilibrium and can withstand small deviations.

### Important Results

- **Prisoners' Dilemma Implies No-Regret Equilibrium:** In an infinitely repeated game, players can achieve a Nash Equilibrium without knowing the opponent's strategy.
- **Tit-for-Tat is a Nash Equilibrium:** TFT is a stable strategy that can be sustained in an infinitely repeated game.
