# **Strategies in an Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma**

## **Introduction**

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a fundamental game theory paradox that has been extensively studied in the field of game theory. In this study material, we will focus on the strategies employed by players in an infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma. The infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma is a model used to study the consistency of the Nash Bargaining Solution (NBS) and the Evolutionary Stable Strategy (ESS).

## **Definitions**

- **Prisoner's Dilemma**: A game theory paradox in which two players (Prisoner 1 and Prisoner 2) are given the opportunity to confess or remain silent regarding their involvement in a crime.
- **Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma**: A repeated game where the same game is played an infinite number of times, allowing players to adjust their strategies based on past outcomes.
- **Nash Bargaining Solution (NBS)**: A solution concept that seeks to find a fair agreement between two players in a repeated game.
- **Evolutionary Stable Strategy (ESS)**: A strategy that is stable over time, meaning that it is more likely to be adopted by other players in the population.

## **Key Concepts**

- **Cooperation**: Both prisoners remain silent, resulting in a better outcome for both.
- **Defection**: Both prisoners confess, resulting in a worse outcome for both.
- **Punishment**: One prisoner confesses, while the other remains silent, resulting in a better outcome for the silent prisoner.
- **Rewards**: The prisoners receive rewards for cooperating or punishing the other prisoner.

## **Strategies in an Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma**

### 1. **Tit for Tat**

- **Definition**: A strategy where a player imitates the opponent's previous action, starting with a random action.
- **Example**: Player 1 starts with a random action (cooperate or defect), and then imitates Player 2's previous action.
- **Advantages**: Tit for Tat is a cooperative strategy that can lead to long-term cooperation.
- **Disadvantages**: If the opponent defects first, Tit for Tat will eventually defect as well.

### 2. **Prisoners' Dilemma (PD) Strategy**

- **Definition**: A strategy where a player cooperates if the opponent cooperates and defects if the opponent defects.
- **Example**: Player 1 cooperates if Player 2 cooperates, and defects if Player 2 defects.
- **Advantages**: PD is a cooperative strategy that can lead to long-term cooperation.
- **Disadvantages**: If the opponent defects first, PD will eventually defect as well.

### 3. **Communication**

- **Definition**: Players communicate with each other before making their decisions.
- **Example**: Players can use a pre-agreed signal (e.g. "I will cooperate") to communicate their intentions.
- **Advantages**: Communication can lead to more cooperation and better outcomes.
- **Disadvantages**: Communication can be time-consuming and may not always be effective.

### 4. **Bayesian Nash Equilibrium (BNE)**

- **Definition**: A solution concept that finds the best response to the expected actions of the opponent.
- **Example**: Players update their beliefs about their opponent's actions based on past outcomes and make their decisions accordingly.
- **Advantages**: BNE can lead to more cooperation and better outcomes.
- **Disadvantages**: BNE may not always be stable over time.

## **Conclusion**

In an infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, players can employ various strategies to achieve better outcomes. Tit for Tat and PD strategies are cooperative strategies that can lead to long-term cooperation, while communication and BNE can also lead to more cooperation and better outcomes. However, these strategies may not always be stable over time, and players must be aware of their opponent's actions and adapt their strategies accordingly.
