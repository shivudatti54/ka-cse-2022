# Nash Equilibrium of an Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

## **Introduction**

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a classic game theory paradox that has been widely studied in the fields of economics, politics, and social sciences. In this topic, we will delve into the concept of Nash equilibrium in the context of an infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma.

## **What is the Prisoner's Dilemma?**

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a game theory paradox that consists of two players, often referred to as prisoners, who are given the opportunity to cooperate or defect against each other. The payoffs for each player are as follows:

|                           | Prisoner A Defects | Prisoner A Cooperates |
| ------------------------- | ------------------ | --------------------- |
| **Prisoner B Defects**    | A: 3, B: 3         | A: 0, B: 5            |
| **Prisoner B Cooperates** | A: 5, B: 0         | A: 1, B: 1            |

In this game, the dominant strategy for each prisoner is to defect, regardless of what the other prisoner chooses. However, if both prisoners cooperate, they will both receive a higher payoff than if they both defect.

## **What is a Nash Equilibrium?**

A Nash Equilibrium is a state in a game where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged. In other words, a Nash Equilibrium is a stable state where no player has an incentive to deviate from their chosen strategy.

## **Nash Equilibrium in the Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma**

In the context of an infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, we can think of the game as being played multiple times. The key idea is that the prisoners have the opportunity to improve their payoffs by deviating from their initial strategy.

Here's how the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma works:

1. The prisoners play the game multiple times, with the payoffs from each game being used to update their strategies.
2. Each time the prisoners play the game, they can improve their payoffs by deviating from their current strategy.
3. The prisoners continue to play the game indefinitely, with the goal of maximizing their payoffs.

## **Key Concepts:**

- **Retaliation**: The prisoner's threat to punish the other prisoner if they defect.
- **Trust**: The prisoner's willingness to trust the other prisoner to cooperate.
- **Punishment**: The punishment imposed on the other prisoner if they defect.
- **Cooperation**: The prisoner's choice to cooperate with the other prisoner.
- **Defection**: The prisoner's choice to defect against the other prisoner.

## **Examples:**

Suppose we have two prisoners, Alice and Bob. They play the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma multiple times, with the payoffs from each game being updated to reflect their strategies.

- Initially, both Alice and Bob choose to cooperate. They receive a payoff of 5, and they update their strategies to reflect this.
- However, Alice becomes frustrated with Bob's lack of cooperation and decides to defect. Bob receives a payoff of 0, and he updates his strategy to reflect this.
- Alice realizes that Bob is no longer trustworthy, and she decides to defect as well. Bob receives a payoff of 5, and he updates his strategy to reflect this.
- This cycle of cooperation and defection continues indefinitely, with neither Alice nor Bob able to achieve a long-term Nash Equilibrium.

## **Conclusion**

In conclusion, the Nash Equilibrium in the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma is a state where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged. The key to achieving a Nash Equilibrium is to find a balance between cooperation and defection, taking into account the retaliation and punishment imposed by the other player.
