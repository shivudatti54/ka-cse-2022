### Introduction

Repeated games are a fundamental concept in game theory, which studies the interaction between rational decision-makers. The main idea of repeated games is to analyze the strategic interactions between two or more players in a sequential setting, where the players make repeated decisions over time. This concept has far-reaching implications in various fields, including economics, politics, and biology.

### Historical Context

The concept of repeated games was first introduced by John Nash in the 1950s, as a refinement of his earlier work on non-cooperative games. Nash showed that repeated games can lead to stable and desirable outcomes, even when players have conflicting interests. The study of repeated games gained momentum in the 1980s, with the work of David Kreps and Robert Wilson, who developed the concept of "repeated games" as a tool for analyzing strategic interactions.

### Definition and Background

A repeated game is a game that is played multiple times, with each player making a sequence of decisions. The game can be played in any number of stages, and each stage can be considered a separate game. The key feature of repeated games is that the players have an opportunity to observe and respond to the actions of other players over time.

Let's consider a simple example to illustrate this concept:

Suppose two players, Alice and Bob, play a game of chess. Each game is played in two stages: the first stage is the opening, where Alice and Bob make their first moves, and the second stage is the endgame, where Alice and Bob make their final moves. The outcome of the game is determined by the combination of the opening and endgame moves.

### Key Concepts

Before diving into the main idea of repeated games, it's essential to understand some key concepts:

- **Stochastic games**: These are games where the outcome is uncertain, and the players must make decisions based on probabilities.
- **Deterministic games**: These are games where the outcome is certain, and the players can make decisions based on fixed rules.
- **Strategic complementarity**: This refers to the phenomenon where the actions of two players are complementary, meaning that one player's action increases the value of the other player's action.
- **Strategic substitution**: This refers to the phenomenon where the actions of two players are substitutable, meaning that one player's action can be replaced by the other player's action.

### Types of Repeated Games

There are several types of repeated games, including:

- **Zero-sum games**: These are games where one player's gain is equal to the other player's loss.
- **Non-zero-sum games**: These are games where the gain of one player is not necessarily equal to the loss of the other player.
- **Sequential games**: These are games where the players make decisions in a sequential manner.
- **Simultaneous games**: These are games where the players make decisions simultaneously.

### Strategies and Equilibrium

In repeated games, players must develop strategies to make decisions that will lead to a stable outcome. One common approach is to use equilibrium analysis, which involves finding the set of strategies that no player can improve upon by unilaterally changing their behavior.
