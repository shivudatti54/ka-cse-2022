# Repeated Games: The Main Idea

### Introduction

Repeated games are a fundamental concept in algorithmic game theory, which studies the strategic interactions between rational players. In this section, we will explore the main idea of repeated games and their significance in understanding strategic decision-making.

### Definition

A repeated game is a sequence of staged games played between the same set of players over a finite or infinite number of periods. Each stage game is a finite game with a finite number of players, and the players' strategies are chosen for each stage game, taking into account the strategies of the other players.

### Key Characteristics

- **Finite or infinite number of periods**: Repeated games can be played for a fixed number of periods or indefinitely.
- **Stage games**: Each period consists of a stage game, which is a finite game with a finite number of players.
- **Strategic interactions**: Players choose their strategies for each stage game, considering the strategies of other players.

### Types of Repeated Games

- **Finite horizon**: Repeated games are played for a fixed number of periods.
- **Infinite horizon**: Repeated games are played indefinitely, with no termination date.
- **Zero-sum games**: The sum of the payoffs to all players is zero, e.g., one player's gain is equal to another player's loss.

### Equilibrium Concepts

- **Nash Equilibrium**: A concept in game theory that describes a situation in which no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.
- **Pareto Optimum**: A concept that describes a situation in which no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off.

### Strategies for Repeated Games

- **Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium**: A concept that describes a situation in which players make strategic decisions based on their beliefs about the future actions of other players.
- **Percivalian Equilibrium**: A concept that describes a situation in which players make strategic decisions based on their expectations about the future actions of other players.

### Examples

- **Prisoner's Dilemma**: A classic game theory example of a repeated game, where two prisoners choose between cooperating or betraying each other.
- **Tragedy of the Commons**: A classic example of a repeated game, where multiple players choose between consuming a shared resource and conserving it for future generations.

### Conclusion

Repeated games are a fundamental concept in algorithmic game theory, which studies the strategic interactions between rational players. Understanding repeated games is crucial for developing strategies for competition in various fields, including economics, politics, and social sciences.
