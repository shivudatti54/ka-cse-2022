# Nash Equilibrium

### Definition

- A Nash equilibrium is a situation in a game where no player can improve their payoff (or outcome) by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.
- Introduced by John Nash in 1950.

### Key Points

- A Nash equilibrium is a stable state where no player has an incentive to deviate from their strategy.
- It is a concept in game theory that helps analyze strategic decision-making in situations where multiple players have conflicting interests.
- The Nash equilibrium is a solution concept that is used to study competitive and cooperative games.

### Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Nash Equilibrium Equation**: There is no specific formula, but it involves the concept of payoff matrices and iterative improvement.
- **Iterated Dominance Theorem**: A useful theorem that helps establish the existence of a Nash equilibrium.
- **Berge Minimax Theorem**: A theorem that provides a condition for the existence of a Nash equilibrium.

### Key Theorems

- **Nash's Equilibrium Theorem**: States that a Nash equilibrium exists in a game with a finite number of players and pure strategy games.
- **Pareto Optimality**: A concept that relates to Nash equilibrium, where no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off.

### Important Concepts

- **Strategic Form**: A representation of a game that is used to analyze Nash equilibria.
- **Mixed Strategies**: A randomization technique used to analyze games with multiple pure strategies.
- **Zero-Sum Games**: Games where one player's gain is equal to another player's loss.

### Example

- A classic example is the Prisoner's Dilemma, where two prisoners have to decide whether to cooperate or defect. The Nash equilibrium is when both prisoners defect, resulting in a worse outcome for both.
