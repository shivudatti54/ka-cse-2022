# Examples: The Prisoner's Dilemma

=====================================================

### Introduction

- The Prisoner's Dilemma is a fundamental concept in Game Theory, illustrating the conflict between individual and group rationality.
- It was first introduced by Merrill Flood and Melvin Dresher in 1950, and later popularized by Albert Tucker.

### Key Points

- **Game Theory Definition:** A game is a situation where the outcome depends on the actions of multiple individuals or parties.
- **Prisoner's Dilemma Game:**
  - Two prisoners, A and B, are arrested and interrogated separately.
  - Each prisoner can either confess (C) or remain silent (S).
  - The payoffs for each outcome are as follows:
    - Both confess: -2
    - Both silent: 0
    - One confesses, one remains silent: -1
- **Nash Equilibrium:** A concept in game theory that describes a stable state where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.
- **Prisoner's Dilemma Theorem:** A theorem stating that in a situation where individual rationality leads to a suboptimal outcome, the group's overall outcome will be worse than if each individual had cooperated.

### Important Formulas and Definitions

- **Nash Equilibrium:** a stable state where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.
- **Pareto Optimality:** a state where no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off.

### Key Theorems

- **Prisoner's Dilemma Theorem:** states that in a situation where individual rationality leads to a suboptimal outcome, the group's overall outcome will be worse than if each individual had cooperated.
- **Gambler's Ruin Theorem:** a theorem that describes the probability of a gambler ultimately losing or winning a game, given a certain initial fortune and a set of rules for the game.

### Revision Notes

- The Prisoner's Dilemma highlights the conflict between individual and group rationality.
- It demonstrates that individual rationality can lead to a suboptimal outcome for the group.
- The Nash Equilibrium and Pareto Optimality concepts are essential in understanding the Prisoner's Dilemma.
