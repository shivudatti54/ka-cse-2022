# **Examples of Nash Equilibrium**

## **Introduction**

In game theory, a Nash equilibrium is a state in a game where no player can improve their payoff (or outcome) by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged. In this section, we will explore some classic examples of Nash equilibrium to illustrate its concept and significance.

## **Definition of Nash Equilibrium**

A Nash equilibrium is a state where no player has an incentive to deviate from their strategy, given the strategies of the other players. In other words, it is a stable state where no player can benefit by changing their strategy.

## **Examples of Nash Equilibrium**

### 1. Prisoner's Dilemma

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a classic example of a game that demonstrates a Nash equilibrium. In this game, two prisoners are given the option to confess or remain silent.

- **Payoff Matrix:**
  | | Prisoner A Confesses | Prisoner A Remains Silent |
  | --- | --- | --- |
  | **Prisoner B Confesses** | 2 years in prison, 2 years in prison | 1 year in prison, 3 years in prison |
  | **Prisoner B Remains Silent** | 3 years in prison, 1 year in prison | 1 year in prison, 1 year in prison |

**Key Points:**

- Both prisoners have an incentive to confess, regardless of the other prisoner's action.
- The Nash equilibrium occurs when both prisoners confess, resulting in 2 years in prison for each.

### 2. Battle of the Sexes

The Battle of the Sexes is a game where two players choose between two different activities: going to the beach or attending a music festival.

- **Payoff Matrix:**
  | | Player 1 Goes to Beach | Player 1 Goes to Music Festival |
  | --- | --- | --- |
  | **Player 2 Goes to Beach** | 5 points, 0 points | 0 points, 5 points |
  | **Player 2 Goes to Music Festival** | 2 points, 3 points | 3 points, 2 points |

**Key Points:**

- Each player prefers going to the beach, but going to the beach is less popular than going to the music festival.
- The Nash equilibrium occurs when both players go to the music festival, resulting in 2 points for each.

### 3. Hawk-Dove Game

The Hawk-Dove game is a simple game where two players can choose between being a hawk (aggressive) or a dove (non-aggressive).

- **Payoff Matrix:**
  | | Hawk | Dove |
  | --- | --- | --- |
  | **Hawk** | 3 points, 0 points | 0 points, 5 points |
  | **Dove** | 5 points, 0 points | 2 points, 2 points |

**Key Points:**

- The hawk prefers the high payoff, while the dove prefers the low payoff.
- The Nash equilibrium occurs when both players are hawks, resulting in 3 points for each.

## **Conclusion**

Nash equilibrium is a fundamental concept in game theory that helps us understand how players make strategic decisions in games. The examples of Prisoner's Dilemma, Battle of the Sexes, and Hawk-Dove game demonstrate how Nash equilibrium can provide insights into human behavior and decision-making. By analyzing these examples, we can gain a deeper understanding of the concept of Nash equilibrium and its significance in game theory.
