# **Nash Equilibrium Examples**

## **Introduction**

A Nash equilibrium is a concept in game theory that describes a stable state in a game where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.

## **Key Points**

- **Definition:** A Nash equilibrium is a state where no player can improve their payoff by changing their strategy, given the strategies of all other players.
- **Formula:** Not applicable, but can be represented as:
  - (S, P(S)) where S is the set of strategies and P(S) is the probability distribution of each strategy.
- **Theorem:** Nash's Equilibrium Theorem (1950) states that a Nash equilibrium exists if a game is finite and the players are rational.

## **Examples**

- **Prisoner's Dilemma:**
  - Two prisoners can choose to confess or remain silent.
  - The Nash equilibrium is when both prisoners confess, as this maximizes the individual's expected payoff.
- **Battle of the Sexes:**
  - Two people can choose to go to the beach or the movies.
  - The Nash equilibrium is when both people go to the beach, as this maximizes the individual's expected payoff.
- **Oligopoly Game:**
  - Two firms can choose to produce more or less of a product.
  - The Nash equilibrium is when both firms produce a certain quantity, as this maximizes their individual profits.

## **Important Definitions**

- **Rational choice:** A decision that maximizes the individual's expected payoff, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.
- **Strategy:** A set of actions that a player can take in a game.
- **Payoff:** The outcome or reward that a player receives as a result of their actions.

## **Key Formulas**

- **Expected utility:** E(U) = ∑p_i\*U_i, where p_i is the probability of each outcome and U_i is the utility of each outcome.
- **Maximum expected utility:** The highest expected utility that a player can achieve by changing their strategy.
