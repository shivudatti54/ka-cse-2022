# Examples of Nash Equilibrium

### Introduction

The Nash equilibrium is a fundamental concept in game theory, named after John Nash, who introduced it in 1950. It is a state in a game where no player can improve their payoff (or outcome) by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged. In this section, we will explore various examples of Nash equilibria, including classical and modern cases.

### Historical Context

John Nash's work on game theory revolutionized the field of economics and mathematics. His paper, "Non-Cooperative Games," published in 1950, introduced the concept of the Nash equilibrium. Nash's work was built upon earlier ideas by other mathematicians and economists, such as John von Neumann and Oskar Morgenstern. Their work laid the foundation for modern game theory.

### Classic Examples of Nash Equilibrium

#### 1. Prisoner's Dilemma

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a classic example of a Nash equilibrium. Two prisoners, A and B, are arrested and interrogated separately. Each prisoner has two options: confess or remain silent. The payoffs for each option are as follows:

|                               | Prisoner B remains silent              | Prisoner B confesses                   |
| ----------------------------- | -------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| **Prisoner A remains silent** | A: 3 years in jail, B: 3 years in jail | A: 10 years in jail, B: 1 year in jail |
| **Prisoner A confesses**      | A: 1 year in jail, B: 10 years in jail | A: 5 years in jail, B: 5 years in jail |

In this game, the Nash equilibrium occurs when both prisoners confess. This is because, assuming the other prisoner remains silent, confessing yields a higher payoff for each prisoner. If both prisoners remain silent, the payoff is lower than if both confess.

#### 2. Ultimatum Game

The Ultimatum Game is another classic example of a Nash equilibrium. Two players, A and B, are given a sum of money, and player A proposes a division of the money. Player B can either accept the proposal or reject it. The payoffs for each option are as follows:

| Proposal | A: receives $x, B: receives $y |
| -------- | ------------------------------ |
| $x, $y   | A: $x, B: $y                   |
| x', y'   | A: $x', B: $y'                 |

If player B rejects the proposal, both players receive nothing. In this game, the Nash equilibrium occurs when player A proposes a division of the money that player B finds unacceptable. This is because, assuming player B accepts the proposal, player A will not improve their payoff by changing their strategy.

#### 3. Hawk-Dove Game

The Hawk-Dove Game is a game between two players, Hawk and Dove. The game is played in rounds, and each player can choose to be Hawk or Dove. The payoffs for each option are as follows:

|          | Hawk             | Dove             |
| -------- | ---------------- | ---------------- |
| **Hawk** | Hawk: 5, Dove: 0 | Hawk: 3, Dove: 5 |
| **Dove** | Hawk: 0, Dove: 5 | Hawk: 5, Dove: 3 |

In this game, the Nash equilibrium occurs when both players are Hawks. This is because, assuming the other player is a Dove, being a Hawk yields a higher payoff. If both players are Doves, the payoff is lower than if both are Hawks.

### Modern Examples of Nash Equilibrium

#### 1. Bertrand Competition

Bertrand Competition is a game between two firms, A and B, competing in a market. Each firm can choose to set a price. The payoffs for each option are as follows:

|                          | Firm A sets price p              | Firm A sets price p'             |
| ------------------------ | -------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| **Firm B sets price p**  | Firm A: revenue, Firm B: revenue | Firm A: revenue, Firm B: revenue |
| **Firm B sets price p'** | Firm A: revenue, Firm B: revenue | Firm A: revenue, Firm B: revenue |

In this game, the Nash equilibrium occurs when both firms set the same price. This is because, assuming the other firm sets a different price, setting the same price yields a higher revenue for each firm.

#### 2. Auctions

Auctions are a type of game where players bid on a good or service. The payoffs for each bid are as follows:

|                       | Bidder 1 bids $x, Bidder 2 bids $y   | Bidder 1 bids $x, Bidder 2 bids $y'  |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------ |
| **Bidder 1 bids $y**  | Bidder 1: revenue, Bidder 2: revenue | Bidder 1: revenue, Bidder 2: revenue |
| **Bidder 2 bids $y'** | Bidder 1: revenue, Bidder 2: revenue | Bidder 1: revenue, Bidder 2: revenue |

In this game, the Nash equilibrium occurs when both bidders bid the same amount. This is because, assuming the other bidder bids a different amount, bidding the same amount yields a higher revenue.

### Applications of Nash Equilibrium

Nash equilibrium has numerous applications in economics, politics, and computer science. Some examples include:

1. **Industrial organization**: Nash equilibrium is used to analyze the behavior of firms in competitive markets.
2. **Game theory**: Nash equilibrium is used to study the behavior of players in games, including strategic and non-cooperative games.
3. **Economics**: Nash equilibrium is used to analyze the behavior of firms and individuals in various economic scenarios, such as auctions and negotiations.
4. **Computer science**: Nash equilibrium is used in artificial intelligence and machine learning to analyze the behavior of agents in complex systems.

### Case Studies

1. **Oligopoly**: An oligopoly is a market with a small number of firms competing with each other. Nash equilibrium is used to analyze the behavior of firms in oligopolistic markets.
2. **Auctions**: Auctions are a type of game where players bid on a good or service. Nash equilibrium is used to analyze the behavior of bidders in auctions.
3. **Negotiations**: Negotiations are a type of game where players try to reach an agreement. Nash equilibrium is used to analyze the behavior of players in negotiations.
