# Equilibrium in a Single Population

## Introduction

In the realm of algorithmic game theory, understanding equilibrium concepts is crucial for analyzing and optimizing decision-making processes in strategic interactions. This topic delves into the concept of equilibrium in a single population, where a single player or population makes decisions without considering the actions of other players.

## What is Equilibrium?

Equilibrium refers to a state where no player can improve their payoff (outcome) by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged. In other words, equilibrium is a stable state where no player has an incentive to deviate from their chosen action.

## Types of Equilibrium

There are several types of equilibrium, including:

- **Pareto Optimality**: a state where no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off.
- **Nash Equilibrium**: a state where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.
- **Mixed Strategy Equilibrium**: a state where players use random strategies to achieve equilibrium.

## Game Theory and Equilibrium

Game theory is the study of strategic decision-making in situations where the outcome depends on the actions of multiple individuals or parties. Equilibrium is a fundamental concept in game theory, as it helps identify the optimal strategy for a player in a given situation.

## The Minimax Theorem

The minimax theorem states that in a zero-sum game, where one player's gain is equal to another player's loss, the Nash equilibrium is the optimal strategy for the player with the highest payoff potential. The minimax theorem is a key concept in game theory and is used to determine the optimal strategy for a player in a given situation.

## Pareto Optimality and the Nash Equilibrium

Pareto optimality and the Nash equilibrium are two related concepts in game theory. While Pareto optimality refers to a state where no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off, the Nash equilibrium refers to a state where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.

## Mixed Strategy Equilibrium

Mixed strategy equilibrium is a type of equilibrium in which players use random strategies to achieve equilibrium. This concept is particularly useful in situations where players have multiple strategies available, and it allows for the analysis of risk aversion and risk tolerance.

## Example: The Prisoner's Dilemma

The prisoner's dilemma is a classic game theory example that illustrates the concept of equilibrium. Two prisoners, A and B, are arrested and interrogated separately by the police. Each prisoner has two options: to confess or remain silent. The payoffs for each prisoner depend on the actions of both prisoners:

|                             | Prisoner B Remains Silent | Prisoner B Confesses   |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------- | ---------------------- |
| **Prisoner A Stays Silent** | A: 3 years, B: 3 years    | A: 10 years, B: 1 year |
| **Prisoner A Confesses**    | A: 1 year, B: 10 years    | A: 5 years, B: 5 years |

In this example, the Nash equilibrium is (Confess, Confess), as each prisoner can improve their payoff by confessing, assuming the other prisoner remains silent. However, the mixed strategy equilibrium is (Stay Silent, 50% Chance of Confessing), as each prisoner can improve their payoff by staying silent with a 50% chance of confessing.

## Case Study: The Ultimatum Game

The ultimatum game is a classic experiment in game theory that illustrates the concept of equilibrium. Players are presented with a sum of money and must divide it between themselves. One player proposes a split, and the other player can either accept or reject the proposal. If the proposal is accepted, the players receive their allocated amounts. If the proposal is rejected, neither player receives anything.

The ultimatum game has been used to study fairness and cooperation in various contexts, including:

- **Economics**: the ultimatum game has been used to study the behavior of firms and individuals in markets.
- **Psychology**: the ultimatum game has been used to study fairness and cooperation in human behavior.
