# Equilibrium

### Introduction

In Algoritmic Game Theory, equilibrium is a concept that helps us understand the behavior of players in strategic games. It's a state where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.

### Definition

Equilibrium is a concept that helps us analyze the behavior of players in strategic games. It's a state where no player can improve their payoff by changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.

### Types of Equilibrium

There are several types of equilibrium:

- **Nash Equilibrium**: A Nash equilibrium is a concept in game theory that describes a situation in which no player can improve their payoff (or outcome) by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.
- **Pareto Equilibrium**: A Pareto equilibrium is a situation in which no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off.
- **Mixed Strategy Equilibrium**: A mixed strategy equilibrium is a situation in which players use random strategies to make their actions unpredictable.

### Nash Equilibrium

A Nash equilibrium is a concept in game theory that describes a situation in which no player can improve their payoff (or outcome) by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.

**Key Characteristics of Nash Equilibrium**

- No player can improve their payoff by changing their strategy.
- All players are making rational decisions based on their strategy.
- The equilibrium is stable, meaning that if all players keep their strategies unchanged, the equilibrium will persist.

**Example**

Suppose two players, Alice and Bob, are playing a game where they can either cooperate or defect. The payoffs for each player are as follows:

|                | Alice Cooperates | Alice Defects |
| -------------- | ---------------- | ------------- |
| Bob Cooperates | 3, 3             | 5, 0          |
| Bob Defects    | 0, 5             | 0, 0          |

The Nash equilibrium for this game is (Alice Cooperates, Bob Cooperates), because no player can improve their payoff by changing their strategy.

### Mixed Strategy Equilibrium

A mixed strategy equilibrium is a situation in which players use random strategies to make their actions unpredictable.

**Key Characteristics of Mixed Strategy Equilibrium**

- Players use random strategies to make their actions unpredictable.
- The equilibrium is unstable, meaning that if one player changes their strategy, the equilibrium may not persist.
- The mixed strategy equilibrium is often used in games where players have multiple strategies to choose from.

**Example**

Suppose two players, Alice and Bob, are playing a game where they can either cooperate or defect. The payoffs for each player are as follows:

|                | Alice Cooperates | Alice Defects |
| -------------- | ---------------- | ------------- |
| Bob Cooperates | 3, 3             | 5, 0          |
| Bob Defects    | 0, 5             | 0, 0          |

The mixed strategy equilibrium for this game is Alice's random strategy (0.5 cooperate, 0.5 defect) and Bob's random strategy (0.5 cooperate, 0.5 defect).

Equilibrium is a concept that helps us understand the behavior of players in strategic games. It's a state where no player can improve their payoff by changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged. There are several types of equilibrium, including Nash equilibrium, Pareto equilibrium, and mixed strategy equilibrium.
