# Stackelberg's Model of Duopoly

## **Introduction**

Stackelberg's model of duopoly is a fundamental concept in game theory and industrial organization, introduced by Johanakis Stackelberg in 1934. This model describes a situation where two firms compete with each other, with one firm (the "leader") setting its price or quantity first, and the other firm (the "follower") responding to the leader's move. The model is widely used to analyze duopoly situations in various industries, including oligopolistic markets, where a small number of firms dominate the market.

## **Historical Context**

Stackelberg's model was first proposed in the context of the German steel industry in the 1930s. Stackelberg, a German economist, was working at the Reichsbank in Berlin when he developed the model to explain the behavior of steel producers during World War II. The model was initially met with skepticism by some economists, but it gained popularity in the 1950s and 1960s, particularly in the field of industrial organization.

## **Key Assumptions**

Stackelberg's model assumes the following:

1.  **Two firms**: There are two firms, one leader and one follower.
2.  **Sequential decision-making**: The leader makes its decision first, and the follower responds to the leader's move.
3.  **Perfect information**: Both firms have perfect information about the market, including the demand function, production costs, and the other firm's characteristics.
4.  **Non-negative profits**: Both firms aim to maximize their profits.
5.  **Zero production costs**: Production costs are zero, meaning that firms do not incur fixed costs.

## **Game Theory Framework**

Stackelberg's model can be represented using game theory, specifically the Nash equilibrium concept. The model can be solved using backward induction, starting with the follower's decision and working backward to the leader's decision.

## **Leader's Decision**

The leader's goal is to maximize its profits by setting its price or quantity. Since the follower responds to the leader's move, the leader must consider the follower's potential response. The leader can choose to set its price or quantity, and the follower will adjust its price or quantity accordingly.

## **Follower's Decision**

The follower's goal is to maximize its profits by responding to the leader's move. The follower must consider the leader's price or quantity and adjust its own price or quantity accordingly.

## **Equilibrium**

The equilibrium is reached when both firms cannot improve their profits by unilaterally changing their strategies. In Stackelberg's model, the equilibrium is characterized by a Nash equilibrium, where each firm chooses a strategy that maximizes its profits, given the other firm's strategy.

## **Types of Equilibria**

Stackelberg's model can lead to different types of equilibria, including:

- **Cournot equilibrium**: Both firms produce a non-zero quantity, and the leader's price is set at the intersection of the two firms' supply curves.
- **Bertrand equilibrium**: Both firms set their prices at the same level, and the leader's quantity is set at the intersection of the two firms' demand curves.
- **Mixed equilibrium**: Both firms produce a non-zero quantity, and the leader's price is set at a level that does not coincide with the follower's supply curve.
