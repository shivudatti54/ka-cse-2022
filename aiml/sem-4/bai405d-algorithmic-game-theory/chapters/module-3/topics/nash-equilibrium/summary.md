# Nash Equilibrium Summary

### Definition

- A concept in game theory, introduced by John Nash in 1950
- A state in a game where no player can improve their payoff (outcome) by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged

### Key Points

- **Nash Equilibrium Theorem (1950)**:
  - A game is in Nash equilibrium if no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy
  - The equilibrium is stable, meaning that if one player changes their strategy, the other players will not change their strategies, maintaining the equilibrium
- **Optimal Strategy**: A strategy that yields the best possible outcome for a player, given the opposing player's strategy
- **Mixed Strategy**: A strategy that involves randomizing one's actions, making it difficult for the opponent to predict the best response

### Important Formulas and Definitions

- **Payoff Matrix**: A table that represents the payoff for each player for each possible combination of strategies
- **Strategy Space**: The set of all possible strategies for each player
- **Game Theory**: The study of how people make decisions when the outcome depends on the actions of multiple individuals

### Key Theorems

- **Nash Equilibrium Theorem**: A game is in Nash equilibrium if and only if no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy
- **Pareto Optimality**: A state where no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off

### Important Concepts

- **Dominant Strategy**: A strategy that is the best choice for a player regardless of the opposing player's strategy
- **Subgame Perfect Equilibrium**: A strategy that is the best response to the current state of the game, given the players' past actions
