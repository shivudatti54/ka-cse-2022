# Strategies and Outcomes in Algorithmic Game Theory

=====================================================

## Introduction

---

Algorithmic game theory is a field of study that deals with the analysis of strategic decision-making in situations where the outcome depends on the actions of multiple parties. In this topic, we will explore the concepts of strategies and outcomes in algorithmic game theory.

## Definitions

---

### Strategy

A strategy is a plan or set of actions that a player can take in a game. It is a sequence of moves that a player can make to achieve a desired outcome.

### Outcome

An outcome is the result or consequence of a game. It is the payoff or reward that a player receives for their actions.

## Types of Strategies

---

### Pure Strategy

A pure strategy is a strategy that specifies a single action for every possible situation. For example, in a game of rock-paper-scissors, a pure strategy might be to always choose rock.

### Mixed Strategy

A mixed strategy is a strategy that specifies a probability distribution over a set of possible actions. For example, in the same game of rock-paper-scissors, a mixed strategy might be to choose rock with a 50% probability and paper with a 50% probability.

### Nash Equilibrium

A Nash equilibrium is a concept in game theory that describes a situation where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.

## Types of Outcomes

---

### Pareto Optimal Outcome

A Pareto optimal outcome is an outcome that cannot be improved upon by any one player without making another player worse off.

### Pareto Inefficient Outcome

A Pareto inefficient outcome is an outcome that can be improved upon by some player without making any other player worse off.

## Examples

---

### The Prisoner's Dilemma

The prisoner's dilemma is a classic game theory thought experiment in which two prisoners have the option to confess or remain silent. The outcome depends on the actions of both prisoners.

|                               | Prisoner A Confesses   | Prisoner A Remains Silent |
| ----------------------------- | ---------------------- | ------------------------- |
| **Prisoner B Confesses**      | A: 2 years, B: 2 years | A: 1 year, B: 3 years     |
| **Prisoner B Remains Silent** | A: 3 years, B: 1 year  | A: 1 year, B: 1 year      |

In this example, the Nash equilibrium is for both prisoners to confess, as this outcome is better for each individual than remaining silent.

### The ultimatum game

The ultimatum game is a game in which one player proposes a division of a sum of money, and the other player can either accept or reject the proposal. The outcome depends on the actions of both players.

|                     | Proposal | Accept | Reject |
| ------------------- | -------- | ------ | ------ |
| **Proposal Amount** | 30       | 0      | 0      |
| **Accept**          | 20       | 20     | 0      |
| **Reject**          | 0        | 0      | 30     |

In this example, the Nash equilibrium is for the proposer to propose a small amount, as this is more likely to lead to acceptance.

## Key Concepts

---

- **Payoff matrix**: A table that summarizes the possible outcomes for each player
- **Game tree**: A diagram that represents the possible moves and outcomes in a game
- **Strategic complementarity**: A situation where the best action for one player depends on the action of another player
- **Strategic substitutability**: A situation where the best action for one player is independent of the action of another player
