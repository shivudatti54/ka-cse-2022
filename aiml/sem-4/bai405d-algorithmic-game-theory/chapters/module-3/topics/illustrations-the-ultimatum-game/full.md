## Introduction

The Ultimatum Game is a classic game in the field of game theory, introduced by Werner Güth, Rüdiger Schmittberger, and Bernd Schwarze in 1982. It is a simple yet powerful tool for understanding human behavior in decision-making situations. The game has been widely used in experimental economics and has been the subject of extensive research and analysis.

## Historical Context

The Ultimatum Game was first proposed by Güth, Schmittberger, and Schwarze as a way to study fairness and cooperation in economic decision-making. The game was inspired by the concept of "ultimatum" in economics, which refers to a situation where one party has the power to make a proposal to another party, and that proposal can be either accepted or rejected.

The game was initially designed to study the behavior of individuals in a division of labor setting, where two people are given a set of goods to divide between them. The proposal is made by one person to the other, who has the option to either accept or reject the proposal. If the proposal is accepted, the goods are divided according to the proposal; if it is rejected, neither person receives anything.

## Game Theoretic Foundations

The Ultimatum Game mechanism involves the following steps:

1. **Proposal**: The proposer makes a proposal to the responder, specifying a percentage of the total sum of money to be received.
2. **Acceptance or Rejection**: The responder either accepts or rejects the proposal.
3. **Outcome**: If the proposal is accepted, the goods are divided according to the proposal. If the proposal is rejected, neither person receives anything.

The Ultimatum Game has several limitations and criticisms:

- **Simplistic Model**: The game assumes a simple model of human behavior, which may not accurately reflect real-world decision-making situations.
- **Limited Context**: The game is typically played in a laboratory setting, which may not accurately reflect real-world contexts.
- **Cultural Bias**: The game may be culturally biased, as the proposer's and responder's preferences and valuations may vary across cultures.
