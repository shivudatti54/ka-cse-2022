# Extensive Games with Perfect Information

=====================================

## Definitions and Notations

---

- **Extensive game**: A game where players make decisions at different stages, with the outcome of each stage depending on the previous stages.
- **Perfect information**: Players have complete knowledge of the game's state at each stage.
- **Game tree**: A tree-like representation of the game's stages and possible moves.

## Key Concepts

---

- **Nash Equilibrium**: A stable state where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.
- **Strategic form**: A representation of the game as a set of strategies and payoffs, without considering the game's structure.
- **Sequential rationality**: Players make decisions based on their best response to the current state of the game.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

---

- **Nash Equilibrium (NE)**:
  \[ \text{NE} = \left\{ x \in S^_ : \text{for all } y \in S^_, \sum*{i \in N} u_i(x_i, y) \leq \sum*{i \in N} u_i(x_i, x) \right\} \]
- **Ponzi's Theorem**:
  \[ \text{If } \Gamma \text{ is a game with perfect information and finite perfect information games, then the best response correspondence of } \Gamma \text{ is a correspondence of Nash equilibria.} \]

## Important Results

---

- **Minimax theorem**: The minimax value of a game is the minimum expected payoff of the best response strategy.
- **Game tree decomposition**: Any game tree can be decomposed into a sequence of games with perfect information.

## Conclusion

---

Extensive games with perfect information are essential in game theory, as they provide a framework for analyzing strategic interactions between rational players. Understanding the concepts, formulas, and theorems outlined above is crucial for mastering the subject.
