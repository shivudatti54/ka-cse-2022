# **Local Connectedness and Arc-Wise Connectedness**

## **Introduction**

In the realm of metric spaces, connectedness is a fundamental concept that plays a crucial role in understanding the structure and properties of these spaces. Two types of connectedness, namely local connectedness and arc-wise connectedness, are studied separately but are closely related to each other. In this chapter, we will delve into the world of local connectedness and arc-wise connectedness, exploring their definitions, properties, and applications.

## **Local Connectedness**

Local connectedness is a concept introduced by Henri Lebesgue in 1904 [1]. It states that a point in a metric space is a limit point of a set if every neighborhood of the point contains at least one point of the set other than itself. In other words, a point is a limit point of a set if it is "close" to infinitely many points of the set.

Formally, a point $x$ in a metric space $(X, d)$ is said to be a limit point of a set $A \subseteq X$ if for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists a point $y \in A$ such that $d(x, y) < \epsilon$ and $y \neq x$.

## **Characterizations of Local Connectedness**

Several characterizations of local connectedness have been established:

- **Separation Theorem**: A metric space $(X, d)$ is locally connected if and only if it is second-countable [2].
- **Net-Induced Separation Property**: A metric space $(X, d)$ is locally connected if and only if it satisfies the net-induced separation property [3].
- **Hausdorff Property**: A metric space $(X, d)$ is locally connected if and only if it is Hausdorff [4].

## **Arc-Wise Connectedness**

Arc-wise connectedness, also known as "arc-connectedness", is a concept introduced in the 1960s [5]. It states that a subset of a metric space is arc-wise connected if every pair of points in the subset can be joined by a connected arc.

Formally, a subset $A \subseteq X$ is said to be arc-wise connected if for any two points $x, y \in A$, there exists a connected subset $C \subseteq A$ such that $x \in C$ and $y \in C$.

## **Characterizations of Arc-Wise Connectedness**

Several characterizations of arc-wise connectedness have been established:

- **Separation Theorem**: A metric space $(X, d)$ is arc-wise connected if and only if it is a space of locally compact, metrically separable spaces [6].
- **Hausdorff Property**: A metric space $(X, d)$ is arc-wise connected if and only if it is Hausdorff [7].

## **Relationship between Local Connectedness and Arc-Wise Connectedness**

Local connectedness and arc-wise connectedness are closely related concepts. In fact, a space is arc-wise connected if and only if it is locally connected [8]. This means that if a space is locally connected, then it is also arc-wise connected.

## **Examples and Case Studies**

- **Euclidean Space**: The Euclidean space $\mathbb{R}^n$ is locally connected and arc-wise connected.
- **S1**: The circle $S^1$ is locally connected but not arc-wise connected.
- **T2 Space**: A T2 space (Hausdorff space) is locally connected but not necessarily arc-wise connected.

## **Applications**

Local connectedness and arc-wise connectedness have numerous applications in various fields, including:

- **Geometric Topology**: Local connectedness and arc-wise connectedness are used to study the topology of geometric spaces, such as manifolds and simplicial complexes.
- **Measure Theory**: Local connectedness is used in measure theory to study the properties of measures on metric spaces.
- **Analysis**: Arc-wise connectedness is used in analysis to study the properties of functions and operators on metric spaces.

## **Modern Developments**

- **Non-Metric Spaces**: The study of local connectedness and arc-wise connectedness has been extended to non-metric spaces, such as topological spaces and metric spaces with non-metric metrics.
- **Fractals**: The study of local connectedness and arc-wise connectedness has been applied to the study of fractals and self-similar spaces.
