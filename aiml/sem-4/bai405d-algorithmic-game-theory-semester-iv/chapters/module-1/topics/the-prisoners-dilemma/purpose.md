### Learning Purpose: The Prisoner's Dilemma

**1. Why is this topic important?**
The Prisoner's Dilemma is a foundational concept in algorithmic game theory because it provides a clear, formal model of the conflict between individual rationality and collective good. It is the canonical example of why, in non-cooperative settings, rational individuals might not cooperate, even when it seems it is in their best interest to do so.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn to formally define the Prisoner's Dilemma, identify its core components (players, strategies, payoffs), and analyze its unique Nash Equilibrium. They will understand why mutual defection is the dominant strategy equilibrium despite the better outcome from mutual cooperation.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This topic serves as a critical building block. It introduces key ideas like dominant strategies, Nash equilibrium, and Pareto optimality, which are essential for analyzing more complex games. It directly connects to concepts of mechanism design, repeated games (e.g., the Folk Theorem), and price of anarchy, which measures how efficiency degrades due to selfish behavior.

**4. Real-world applications**
The principles of the Prisoner's Dilemma apply to numerous real-world scenarios, including oligopoly pricing (e.g., price wars between companies), public goods provision, international relations (arms races), and the tragedy of the commons in environmental economics.
