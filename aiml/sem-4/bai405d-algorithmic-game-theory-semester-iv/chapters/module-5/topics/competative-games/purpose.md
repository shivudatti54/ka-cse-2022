### Learning Purpose: Competitive Games

**1. Why is this topic important?**
Competitive games model strategic interactions where participants' goals are in direct conflict. Understanding these models is crucial because they form the foundational bedrock of auction theory, market design, and oligopoly competition, providing a formal framework to analyze how rational, self-interested agents behave in adversarial scenarios.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn to formally define and analyze zero-sum games using fundamental solution concepts like the Minimax Theorem and Nash Equilibrium. They will develop the skills to compute optimal strategies, identify pure and mixed strategy equilibria, and understand the value of a competitive game.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This topic directly builds upon the core concepts of strategic-form games and Nash Equilibrium introduced earlier. It provides a critical bridge to more complex cooperative and non-zero-sum games, while also relying on optimization techniques and probability theory to solve for mixed strategies.

**4. Real-world applications**
The principles of competitive game theory are applied in numerous real-world contexts, including designing parlor games (poker, chess), developing robust security protocols (e.g., randomized patrols), formulating military strategies, and creating algorithms for competitive environments like trading markets.
