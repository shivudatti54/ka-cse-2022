The purpose of this module is to introduce students to the algorithmic principles behind game theory and demonstrate their direct application in real-world scenarios.

**Why is this topic important?**
Game theory provides a formal framework for analyzing strategic interactions between rational decision-makers. Its principles are foundational to economics, political science, computer science (especially AI and multi-agent systems), and even biology. Understanding these algorithmic foundations is crucial for designing systems where multiple autonomous agents must interact, cooperate, or compete.

**What will students learn?**
Students will learn to model real-world strategic situations as formal games (e.g., Normal Form, Extensive Form). They will study key solution concepts like the Nash Equilibrium and learn algorithms for finding these equilibria in various game types. This includes understanding the complexity of such computations (e.g., the PPAD-completeness of finding a Nash Equilibrium). They will also explore specific game classes like zero-sum games and their connection to linear programming.

**How does it connect to other concepts?**
This topic is a direct application of algorithms and complexity theory studied earlier. It connects optimization techniques (like Linear Programming) to strategic problems. It also links probabilistic reasoning (for mixed strategies) and provides a crucial foundation for multi-agent artificial intelligence.

**Real-world applications**
Applications are vast: designing auction mechanisms (e.g., for online advertising markets), formulating strategies in markets and politics, developing AI for poker or other strategic games, and modeling evolutionary competition in biology.
