# Module 5: Semester-End Examination Guide - Ethics and Public Policy for AI

## Introduction
This module, often the final one in the syllabus, is dedicated to synthesizing and evaluating the knowledge you have gained throughout the course. The semester-end examination is not merely a test of memory but an assessment of your ability to critically analyze the complex interplay between artificial intelligence, ethical principles, and public policy. Success hinges on your capacity to articulate well-reasoned arguments, apply theoretical frameworks to real-world scenarios, and demonstrate a nuanced understanding of the challenges in governing AI.

## Core Concepts for Examination Preparation
To excel, you must be proficient in the core themes covered in previous modules. The exam will likely test your understanding through essay-type questions, case studies, and short notes. Focus on mastering these interconnected areas:

### 1. Foundational Ethical Frameworks
You must be able to recall, explain, and, most importantly, **apply** major ethical theories to AI dilemmas.
*   **Consequentialism/Utilitarianism:** Judging an AI system's action based on its outcomes. The goal is to maximize overall good (e.g., utility, happiness, welfare).
    *   *Example:* An autonomous vehicle swerving to hit one person to save five. The ethical justification is utilitarian.
*   **Deontology:** Emphasizes duties, rules, and intentions. Certain actions are inherently right or wrong, regardless of their consequences.
    *   *Example:* Argues that an AI should never violate a fundamental right like privacy, even if doing so could lead to a greater good (e.g., mass surveillance for security).
*   **Virtue Ethics:** Focuses on the character and intentions of the moral agent (e.g., the AI developer or company). Asks, "What would a virtuous person do?"
    *   *Example:* Promoting transparency and honesty in AI design not just because it's a rule, but because it is the right character trait for a responsible engineer.

### 2. Key Policy and Governance Instruments
Understand the mechanisms through which ethics is operationalized into enforceable rules.
*   **Hard Law vs. Soft Law:** Distinguish between binding regulations (e.g., the EU AI Act) and non-binding guidelines (e.g., OECD AI Principles, company ethics charters).
*   **The EU AI Act:** A pivotal example of comprehensive legislation. Be prepared to explain its risk-based categorization (Unacceptable, High, Limited, Minimal Risk) and its implications for AI developers.
*   **Algorithmic Accountability & Auditing:** The concept of mandating assessments of AI systems for fairness, bias, and transparency *before* and *after* deployment. Understand what an audit entails.

### 3. Critical Application: Bias and Fairness
This is a quintessential topic for exam questions. Move beyond simple definitions.
*   **Technical vs. Societal Bias:** Explain how biased training data (technical) reflects and amplifies historical and social prejudices (societal).
*   **Bias Mitigation Strategies:** Be able to discuss methods at various stages: pre-processing (fixing the data), in-processing (changing the algorithm), and post-processing (adjusting the outputs).
*   **Case Study Ready:** Have concrete examples ready, such as bias in facial recognition (e.g., higher error rates for darker-skinned individuals), hiring algorithms, or predictive policing tools.

### 4. Future-Oriented and Global Challenges
Demonstrate an awareness of the broader, evolving landscape.
*   **The Alignment Problem:** The long-term challenge of ensuring that highly advanced AI systems (AGI/ASI) have goals that are aligned with human values and interests.
*   **Global Governance Divide:** Contrast the EU's rights-based regulatory approach with the US's more sectoral, innovation-focused approach and China's state-driven model. Discuss the challenges of creating global standards.

## Examination Strategy
1.  **Structure Your Answers:** Use a clear format: Introduction, Body (with logical paragraphs), and Conclusion. For essays, start by defining key terms.
2.  **Use Examples:** Ground your theoretical arguments in specific, contemporary examples (e.g., ChatGPT, Midjourney, Clearview AI, COMPAS). This shows applied knowledge.
3.  **Present Balanced Arguments:** For contentious issues (e.g., "Should there be a ban on facial recognition?"), present both sides before concluding with a reasoned personal stance.
4.  **Link Concepts:** The highest marks are awarded for answers that show how different modules connect. For instance, link a case of algorithmic bias (Module 3) to the specific policy measures proposed in the EU AI Act (Module 4).

## Key Points & Summary
*   **The exam tests synthesis:** Your ability to weave together ethical theory, technical understanding, and policy knowledge is crucial.
*   **Master the frameworks:** Be fluent in utilitarianism, deontology, and virtue ethics to apply them to any scenario.
*   **Know the key legislation:** The EU AI Act is a central piece of legislation; understand its risk-based approach inside and out.
*   **Bias and Fairness are key themes:** Be prepared to discuss causes, consequences, and mitigation strategies in depth with strong examples.
*   **Practice application:** Don't just memorize definitions. Practice writing answers that use theories to analyze real-world AI ethics cases. Think like an engineer who is also an ethical and policy-aware professional.

Approach the exam as an opportunity to demonstrate your readiness to contribute responsibly to the field of AI engineering. Good luck!
