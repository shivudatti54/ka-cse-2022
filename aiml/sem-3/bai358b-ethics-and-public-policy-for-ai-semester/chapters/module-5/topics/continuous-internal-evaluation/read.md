# Module 5: Continuous Internal Evaluation (CIE) in Ethics and AI

## Introduction

For engineering students at , the Continuous Internal Evaluation (CIE) system is a familiar framework designed to assess learning progressively throughout the semester, moving beyond the limitations of a single final examination. When applied to a subject as critical and nuanced as **Ethics and Public Policy for AI**, the CIE becomes more than just an assessment tool; it is a crucial mechanism for fostering deep, reflective, and practical understanding. This module explains how CIE is structured and why it is particularly suited for this subject.

## Core Concepts of CIE in an Ethics-Driven Course

The CIE system typically consists of a blend of assessments that collectively evaluate a student's grasp of the subject. For a technical-ethical subject like this, the breakdown is designed to test not just memory, but comprehension, application, and critical thinking.

### 1. Components of CIE

CIE marks are usually divided into two primary components:

*   **Theory-Based Assessments (e.g., Mid-Semester Exams):** These are written tests that cover fundamental concepts from the first half of the semester. Questions are designed to move beyond rote learning.
    *   **Example:** Instead of "Define algorithmic bias," a question might be: "A healthcare AI is trained on data from urban, private hospitals. Analyze three potential ethical risks and forms of bias if this AI is deployed in rural public health centers. Suggest a policy guideline to mitigate one of these risks." This tests understanding, application, and synthesis.

*   **Skill-Based / Continuous Assessments:** This is where the interdisciplinary nature of AI ethics truly shines. It often includes:
    *   **Case Study Analysis:** Students are presented with real-world scenarios (e.g., a fatal autonomous vehicle accident, controversy over generative AI creating art, or racial bias in predictive policing software). They must deconstruct the case, identify the ethical dilemmas (e.g., accountability, transparency, justice), and critique the existing or propose new public policies.
    *   **Seminar Presentations / Technical Talks:** Students research a specific topic (e.g., "The EU AI Act and its impact on Indian AI startups" or "Ethical frameworks for Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems") and present their findings. This hones research, communication, and public speaking skills—essential for any engineer who must explain their work's societal impact.
    *   **Quizzes & Assignments:** Short, focused assessments to ensure continuous engagement with the reading material and core concepts like utilitarianism vs. deontology in AI, the right to explanation, and privacy-preserving techniques.

### 2. The Philosophy Behind CIE for This Subject

Ethics is not a set of rules to be memorized; it is a mode of thinking to be cultivated. The CIE system is perfectly aligned with this goal because:

*   **Promotes Continuous Engagement:** AI ethics is a rapidly evolving field. Regular assessments encourage students to stay updated with current events, new regulations, and emerging debates, fostering a habit of lifelong learning.
*   **Develops Critical Thinking:** By repeatedly applying ethical frameworks (e.g., IEEE Ethically Aligned Design, EU AI Act guidelines) to different problems, students move from *knowing* the theory to *using* it critically.
*   **Reduces Exam Pressure:** The high-stakes, comprehensive nature of a single final exam can be ill-suited for a subject requiring nuanced argumentation. CIE distributes this pressure and allows for more thoughtful, developed responses over time.
*   **Provides Timely Feedback:** Low scores on an early quiz on core concepts (e.g., confusing fairness metrics) give students and instructors a chance to address misunderstandings long before the end-semester examination.

## Example: A CIE Assignment on Algorithmic Fairness

**Task:** "You are a data scientist on a team developing an AI-powered resume screening tool for a large IT company. The initial model, trained on historical hiring data, shows a significant bias against female applicants for technical roles."

**CIE Activities:**
1.  **Quiz (Conceptual):** Define and differentiate between *group fairness* and *individual fairness*.
2.  **Case Study (Application):** Identify the source of the bias (e.g., biased historical data). Propose two technical mitigation strategies (e.g., pre-processing the data, using a fairness-aware algorithm) and one policy-based strategy (e.g., mandatory transparency reports).
3.  **Seminar (Policy Analysis):** Research and present on how India's upcoming Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023, might influence the data collection and auditing processes for such an AI system.

This integrated approach ensures the student understands the theory, can apply it technically, and can contextualize it within the broader policy landscape.

## Key Points & Summary

*   **Purpose:** CIE is a continuous assessment model designed to evaluate a student's understanding progressively throughout the semester, not just in a single final test.
*   **Structure:** It combines theory-based exams (e.g., mid-terms) with skill-based assessments like case studies, seminars, and quizzes.
*   **Relevance for AI Ethics:** This structure is ideal for a subject that requires critical thinking, real-world application, and constant engagement with a fast-changing field.
*   **Benefits:** CIE reduces end-semester pressure, provides timely feedback, and fosters deeper learning by encouraging students to apply ethical frameworks to contemporary AI challenges.
*   **Goal for the Engineer:** The ultimate aim is to produce engineers who are not only technically proficient but also ethically conscious and capable of contributing to responsible AI development and informed public policy.
