# Learning Purpose: Risks in Ethics and Public Policy for AI

**1. Why is this topic important?**
Understanding the risks associated with AI is critical because these systems are increasingly integrated into high-stakes domains like healthcare, criminal justice, and finance. Unmitigated risks can lead to tangible harm, including the amplification of societal biases, breaches of privacy, erosion of accountability, and even physical safety threats. Proactively identifying and analyzing these risks is the essential first step toward developing ethical guidelines and effective public policies that ensure AI is developed and deployed responsibly for the benefit of society.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn to systematically categorize and evaluate a spectrum of AI risks. This includes technical risks (e.g., model brittleness, security vulnerabilities), ethical risks (e.g., algorithmic bias, discrimination, lack of transparency), and societal risks (e.g., labor market disruption, misinformation, and threats to democratic processes). They will also be introduced to foundational risk assessment and mitigation frameworks.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This topic provides the crucial "why" behind many other concepts in the module. It directly sets the stage for subsequent learning about **ethical principles** (like fairness and accountability, which are responses to identified risks) and **policy mechanisms** (such as regulation and oversight, which are tools for risk mitigation). You cannot effectively govern what you do not first understand to be dangerous.

**4. Real-world applications**
This knowledge is applied in roles across the AI ecosystem: **policymakers** drafting regulations for high-risk AI systems; **corporate auditors** conducting impact assessments; **developers** implementing safety-by-design principles; and **journalists** investigating and reporting on AI failures. Analyzing real-world cases of AI harm will ground the theoretical concepts in practical consequences.
