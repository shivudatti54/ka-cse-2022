# **Probability Distributions: Review of Basic Probability Theory**

## **Introduction**

Probability theory is the foundation of probability distributions. In this section, we will review the basic concepts of probability theory and its application in probability distributions.

## **Definition of Probability**

Probability is a measure of the likelihood of an event occurring. It is a number between 0 and 1, where:

- 0 represents impossible events
- 1 represents certain events
- A value between 0 and 1 represents the probability of an event occurring

## **Basic Concepts**

### 1. Experimental Probability

Experimental probability is the probability of an event occurring based on repeated trials of an experiment. It is calculated as the ratio of the number of successful outcomes to the total number of possible outcomes.

- Formula: P(event) = (Number of successful outcomes) / (Total number of possible outcomes)
- Example: A coin is flipped 10 times. If heads appears 5 times, the experimental probability of getting heads is 5/10 = 0.5.

### 2. Theoretical Probability

Theoretical probability is the probability of an event occurring based on the number of favorable outcomes divided by the total number of possible outcomes.

- Formula: P(event) = (Number of favorable outcomes) / (Total number of possible outcomes)
- Example: A fair six-sided die has 6 possible outcomes (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6). The theoretical probability of rolling a 6 is 1/6 = 0.1667.

### 3. Joint Probability

Joint probability is the probability of two or more events occurring together. It is calculated as the product of the individual probabilities of each event.

- Formula: P(A and B) = P(A) \* P(B)
- Example: Two coins are flipped. The probability of both coins landing heads is P(Heads) \* P(Heads) = 0.5 \* 0.5 = 0.25.

### 4. Conditional Probability

Conditional probability is the probability of an event occurring given that another event has occurred. It is calculated as the ratio of the probability of the event occurring and the probability of the given event.

- Formula: P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B)
- Example: A coin is flipped and lands heads. What is the probability that it was heads before the flip? P(Heads|Heads) = P(Heads and Heads) / P(Heads) = 1/1 = 1.

## **Important Theorems**

### 1. The Addition Rule

The addition rule states that the probability of two or more events occurring is the sum of the individual probabilities.

- Formula: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B)
- Example: Two coins are flipped. The probability of either coin landing heads is P(Heads) + P(Heads) - P(Heads and Heads) = 0.5 + 0.5 - 0.25 = 0.75.

### 2. The Multiplication Rule

The multiplication rule states that the probability of an event occurring and another event occurring is the product of the individual probabilities.

- Formula: P(A and B) = P(A) \* P(B)
- Example: Two coins are flipped. The probability of both coins landing heads is P(Heads) \* P(Heads) = 0.5 \* 0.5 = 0.25.

## **Key Concepts**

- Experimental probability
- Theoretical probability
- Joint probability
- Conditional probability
- Addition rule
- Multiplication rule
