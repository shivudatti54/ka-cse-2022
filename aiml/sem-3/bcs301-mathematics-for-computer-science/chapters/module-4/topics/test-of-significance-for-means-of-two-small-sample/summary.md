# Test of Significance for means of two small samples

**Objectives**

- To understand the concept of hypothesis testing for comparing means of two small samples
- To learn the key formulas, definitions, and theorems involved in the test

**Key Points**

- **Null Hypothesis (H0)**: μ1 = μ2 (The means of the two populations are equal)
- **Alternative Hypothesis (H1)**: μ1 ≠ μ2 (The means of the two populations are not equal)
- **Test Statistic**: z = (x̄1 - x̄2) / √(s1^2/n1 + s2^2/n2)
- **Critical Region**: region where the test statistic falls, leading to the rejection of H0
- **p-value**: probability of obtaining a test statistic as extreme or more extreme than the one observed, assuming H0 is true
- **Significance Level (α)**: probability of rejecting H0 when H0 is true

**Formulas**

- z-score: z = (x̄1 - x̄2) / √(s1^2/n1 + s2^2/n2)
- Standard Error (SE): SE = √(s1^2/n1 + s2^2/n2)
- p-value: p = 2(1 - Φ(z))

**Theorems**

- Central Limit Theorem (CLT): sample means are approximately normally distributed
- z-score distribution: z follows a standard normal distribution

**Assumptions**

- Normality: samples are normally distributed or approximately normally distributed
- Independence: observations are independent
- Equal Variances: variances of the two populations are equal

**Common Tests**

- Two-Sample T-Test: used when variances are assumed equal
- Two-Sample Z-Test: used when variances are assumed equal or unknown

**Important Notes**

- Small sample sizes require more conservative test methods
- Use of non-parametric tests is often recommended for small samples
- Consider using confidence intervals as an alternative to hypothesis testing
