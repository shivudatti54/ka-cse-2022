### Learning Purpose: Linear Nonstationary Models

**1. Why is this topic important?**
Most real-world time series data (e.g., stock prices, economic indicators) are nonstationary, meaning their statistical properties change over time. Analyzing them with standard methods for stationary data leads to invalid results and spurious relationships. Understanding nonstationary models is therefore fundamental to accurate forecasting and inference.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn to identify nonstationary behavior and master techniques to transform such series into stationary ones. This includes the core concepts of **differencing** and **unit root tests** (e.g., the Dickey-Fuller test). They will then learn to model the transformed data using tools like Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) models.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This topic builds directly on the foundation of **stationary ARMA models** from Module 1. It is a critical prerequisite for more advanced concepts like **cointegration** (Module 3), which deals with nonstationary series that share a long-run equilibrium relationship. It also provides the essential groundwork for building predictive models.

**4. Real-world applications**
These models are indispensable in fields like finance for modeling stock returns, in economics for forecasting GDP and inflation, and in industry for predicting demand, sales, and energy consumption. They transform unpredictable, trending data into a form that can be reliably analyzed and forecast.
