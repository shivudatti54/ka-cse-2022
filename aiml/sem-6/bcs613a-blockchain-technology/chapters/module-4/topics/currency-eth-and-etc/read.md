# Module 4: Blockchain Currency - Ethereum (ETH) & Ethereum Classic (ETC)

## 1. Introduction: Beyond Simple Currency

While Bitcoin pioneered the concept of decentralized digital money, its scripting language is limited. **Ethereum**, proposed in 2013 by Vitalik Buterin, was designed as a next-generation blockchain. It isn't just a cryptocurrency; it's a **decentralized global computer**. Its native currency, **Ether (ETH)**, is the fuel that powers this computer, used to pay for computation and transaction fees. **Ethereum Classic (ETC)** is a direct result of a fundamental philosophical split in the Ethereum community, representing the original, unaltered blockchain.

## 2. Core Concepts

### Ethereum (ETH): The Programmable Blockchain

Ethereum's core innovation is the **Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM)**. Think of the EVM as a global, decentralized computer where thousands of nodes all run the same calculations to ensure validity. Instead of just processing financial transactions, the EVM executes code of arbitrary algorithmic complexity—these are called **smart contracts**.

*   **Smart Contracts:** These are self-executing contracts with the terms of the agreement directly written into code. They run exactly as programmed without any possibility of downtime, censorship, fraud, or third-party interference. For example, a smart contract can automatically release payment to a freelancer once a project is verified as complete.
*   **Gas and Ether (ETH):** Every operation on the Ethereum network (a computation, a transaction, data storage) requires computational resources. **Gas** is the unit that measures the computational effort required to execute operations. **Ether (ETH)** is the cryptocurrency used to pay for this Gas. This mechanism prevents network spam and allocates resources efficiently.
*   **Decentralized Applications (dApps):** By combining multiple smart contracts and a front-end user interface, developers can build full-stack applications that run on the blockchain. These are called dApps. Use cases range from decentralized finance (DeFi) and NFTs to gaming and supply chain management.

### The DAO Hack and The Birth of Ethereum Classic (ETC)

To understand ETC, we must understand **The DAO**. In 2016, a highly ambitious smart contract project called "The DAO" (Decentralized Autonomous Organization) was launched as a form of investor-directed venture fund. It raised over $150 million in Ether.

*   **The Exploit:** A vulnerability in The DAO's smart contract code was exploited, allowing an attacker to drain approximately $50 million worth of Ether. However, due to rules coded into The DAO, the stolen funds were locked in a child contract for 28 days before they could be moved.
*   **The Fork:** The Ethereum community faced a dilemma. The two options were:
    1.  **Do nothing:** Uphold the core blockchain principle of "code is law" — the transactions, even malicious ones, were valid according to the rules of the protocol.
    2.  **Intervene:** Execute a **hard fork** to rewind the blockchain to a state before the hack and return the stolen funds, violating immutability but protecting users.

The community overwhelmingly voted for the fork. The new chain, where the hack was "reversed," continued as **Ethereum (ETH)**. The original chain, where the stolen transactions remained valid, was continued by a smaller faction of the community and renamed **Ethereum Classic (ETC)**.

### Ethereum Classic (ETC): The Philosophy of Immutability

Ethereum Classic exists to preserve the original, unaltered Ethereum history. Its proponents believe firmly in the principle that **"code is law"** — that a blockchain's immutability is sacred and should never be violated, even to correct a theft. The network continues to operate with the same core technology but has diverged in its development path and security protocols.

## 3. Key Differences Summary: ETH vs. ETC

| Feature | **Ethereum (ETH)** | **Ethereum Classic (ETC)** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Origin** | Result of a hard fork to reverse The DAO hack. | The original, pre-fork Ethereum blockchain. |
| **Core Philosophy** | Pragmatism; willing to intervene for the ecosystem's health. | **"Code is Law"**; absolute immutability is paramount. |
| **Development** | Large, active ecosystem with rapid innovation (e.g., moving to Proof-of-Stake). | Smaller development community, more conservative upgrades. |
| **Market Position** | The second-largest cryptocurrency by market cap, the primary platform for dApps and DeFi. | A much smaller cryptocurrency with a niche community. |
| **Consensus Mechanism** | **Proof-of-Stake (PoS)** post-Merge (Sept 2022). | Remains on **Proof-of-Work (PoW)**, similar to Bitcoin. |

## 4. Key Takeaways

*   Ethereum introduced **smart contracts** and the **EVM**, transforming blockchains from mere ledgers into programmable platforms.
*   **Ether (ETH)** is the fuel (or "gas") used to power transactions and computations on the Ethereum network.
*   **Ethereum Classic (ETC)** was created due to a **philosophical split** over the DAO hack, embodying the principle of unbreakable immutability.
*   The split represents a critical debate in blockchain: the tension between **absolute adherence to protocol rules** and **pragmatic intervention** to protect users.
