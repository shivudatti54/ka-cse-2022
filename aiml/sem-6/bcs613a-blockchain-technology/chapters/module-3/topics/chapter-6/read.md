# **Chapter 6: Smart Contracts**

## **6.1 Introduction**

Smart contracts are self-executing contracts with the terms of the agreement written directly into lines of code. They are a fundamental component of blockchain technology, enabling the creation of decentralized, autonomous, and trustless applications.

## **6.2 Definition and Characteristics**

- **Self-executing**: Smart contracts automatically execute the terms of the agreement when certain conditions are met.
- **Decentralized**: Smart contracts are stored and replicated on a blockchain network, ensuring their integrity and transparency.
- **Autonomous**: Smart contracts operate without the need for intermediaries, such as lawyers, judges, or banks.

## **6.3 How Smart Contracts Work**

Here's a step-by-step explanation of how smart contracts work:

1.  **Creation**: A smart contract is created by writing code that defines the terms of the agreement. This code is deployed on a blockchain network.
2.  **Deployment**: The smart contract is deployed on the blockchain network, where it is stored and replicated.
3.  **Trigger**: A trigger event occurs, such as the expiration of a deadline or the occurrence of a specific condition.
4.  **Execution**: The smart contract executes the terms of the agreement, automating the necessary actions.

## **6.4 Types of Smart Contracts**

There are two main types of smart contracts:

- **Reputation-based smart contracts**: These contracts reward users for contributing value to the network.
- **Commodity-based smart contracts**: These contracts use commodities, such as energy or bandwidth, as the underlying asset.

## **6.5 Benefits of Smart Contracts**

- **Increased Efficiency**: Smart contracts automate the execution of agreements, reducing the need for intermediaries and increasing efficiency.
- **Improved Security**: Smart contracts are stored on a blockchain, ensuring their integrity and security.
- **Increased Transparency**: Smart contracts provide a transparent and tamper-proof record of all transactions.

## **6.6 Use Cases for Smart Contracts**

- **Supply Chain Management**: Smart contracts can automate the tracking and verification of goods in a supply chain.
- **Voting Systems**: Smart contracts can ensure the integrity and transparency of voting systems.
- **Digital Identity Verification**: Smart contracts can verify the identity of individuals and organizations.

## **6.7 Limitations of Smart Contracts**

- **Scalability**: Smart contracts can be slow to execute due to the complexity of the code and the need for network consensus.
- **Regulatory Uncertainty**: Smart contracts are subject to regulatory uncertainty, which can make it difficult to implement them in certain industries.
- **Security Risks**: Smart contracts are vulnerable to security risks, such as 51% attacks and smart contract attacks.

**Key Concepts:**

- **Self-executing contracts with the terms of the agreement written directly into lines of code**
- **Decentralized, autonomous, and trustless applications**
- **Reputation-based smart contracts**
- **Commodity-based smart contracts**
- **Increased efficiency, improved security, and increased transparency**
