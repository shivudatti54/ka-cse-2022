# Learning Purpose: Continuous Internal Evaluation

### 1. Why is this topic important?
Continuous Internal Evaluation (CIE) shifts the focus from high-stakes, end-of-term exams to a more consistent and holistic assessment of a student's understanding. It is crucial because operating systems are complex, foundational systems; CIE ensures comprehension is built progressively through regular feedback, reducing last-minute cramming and promoting deeper, long-term knowledge retention of intricate concepts like process scheduling and memory management.

### 2. What will students learn?
Students will learn to articulate core OS concepts through various CIE methods. This includes demonstrating practical skills via hands-on lab assignments (e.g., shell scripting, process simulation), analyzing and solving problems in scheduled quizzes, and effectively communicating system functionalities through presentations or technical reports. The goal is to prove competency incrementally across theoretical knowledge and applied skills.

### 3. How does it connect to other concepts?
CIE activities are designed to integrate and reinforce knowledge from all modules. For instance, a lab assignment on thread creation (Module 3: Process Management) may rely on understanding system calls (Module 2: OS Structures). A case study on disk scheduling algorithms (Module 4: I/O Systems) tests the ability to apply theoretical learning from Module 1 (Introduction) to a practical scenario, creating a cohesive learning journey.

### 4. Real-world applications
This process mirrors the agile development and continuous integration/continuous deployment (CI/CD) practices used in modern software engineering. Regularly demonstrating and getting feedback on small units of work is exactly how complex systems, including operating systems themselves, are built, tested, and refined in the industry, preparing students for professional workflows.
