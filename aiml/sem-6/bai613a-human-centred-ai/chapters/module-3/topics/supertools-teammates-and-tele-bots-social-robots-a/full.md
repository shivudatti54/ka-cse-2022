## **Chapter 11: Supertools**

### Introduction

In the realm of Human-Centred AI, supertools refer to advanced technologies that significantly augment human capabilities, often blurring the lines between humans and machines. These tools are designed to make our lives easier, more efficient, and enjoyable. Supertools can be thought of as the "superpowers" that AI and automation can grant us.

### History of Supertools

The concept of supertools dates back to the 1960s, with the development of the first personal computers. However, it wasn't until the 21st century that supertools began to emerge as a distinct category of AI applications.

- **Example 1:** Google's self-driving cars, which use AI to navigate roads and avoid obstacles, can be considered a supertools.
- **Example 2:** Amazon's Echo, a smart speaker that can perform various tasks, such as playing music and setting reminders, is another example of a supertools.

### Characteristics of Supertools

Supertools typically share certain characteristics, including:

- **Augmenting human capabilities**: Supertools aim to enhance human abilities, making tasks easier and more efficient.
- **Autonomy**: Supertools often operate independently, with minimal human intervention.
- **Learning and adaptation**: Supertools can learn from data and adapt to new situations.

### Applications of Supertools

Supertools have numerous applications across various industries, including:

- **Healthcare**: AI-powered diagnostic tools, such as IBM's Watson, can help doctors diagnose diseases more accurately.
- **Education**: AI-powered tutoring systems, such as Knewton, can provide personalized learning experiences.

### Case Study 1: NASA's Supertools

NASA's supertools are designed to assist astronauts in their missions. For example, the agency's Robonaut 2, a humanoid robot, can perform tasks such as maintenance and repairs.

### Case Study 2: Self-Driving Cars

Companies like Waymo and Tesla are developing self-driving cars that use AI to navigate roads and avoid obstacles. These supertools have the potential to revolutionize transportation and reduce accidents.

## **Chapter 12: Teammates**

### Introduction

In the context of Human-Centred AI, teammates refer to AI systems that collaborate with humans to achieve common goals. These AI systems are designed to work alongside humans, rather than replacing them.

### History of Teammates

The concept of teammates emerged in the 1980s, with the development of the first collaborative robots. However, it wasn't until the 21st century that teammates began to emerge as a distinct category of AI applications.

- **Example 1:** Amazon's virtual customer service agents, which use AI to answer customer queries, can be considered teammates.
- **Example 2:** NASA's Curiosity rover, which uses AI to analyze data and make decisions, is another example of a teammate.

### Characteristics of Teammates

Teammates typically share certain characteristics, including:

- **Collaboration**: Teammates work alongside humans to achieve common goals.
- **Mutual adaptation**: Teammates adapt to changing situations and learn from humans.
- **Human-AI symbiosis**: Teammates rely on human input to make decisions.

### Applications of Teammates

Teammates have numerous applications across various industries, including:

- **Customer Service**: AI-powered customer service agents can provide 24/7 support to customers.
- **Healthcare**: AI-powered medical diagnosis tools can assist doctors in diagnosing diseases.

### Case Study 1: NASA's Teaming Robots

NASA's teaming robots are designed to work alongside humans in space exploration. For example, the agency's Canadarm2 robot can be controlled by a human operator, but it also has autonomy to perform tasks independently.

### Case Study 2: Amazon's Virtual Customer Service Agents

Amazon's virtual customer service agents use AI to answer customer queries. These agents can learn from customer feedback and adapt to changing situations.

## **Chapter 13: Tele-bots**

### Introduction

In the context of Human-Centred AI, tele-bots refer to AI systems that can be controlled remotely using devices such as smartphones or tablets. Tele-bots are designed to perform tasks that require human intervention, but can be controlled from a distance.

### History of Tele-bots

The concept of tele-bots emerged in the 1980s, with the development of the first remote-controlled robots. However, it wasn't until the 21st century that tele-bots began to emerge as a distinct category of AI applications.

- **Example 1:** Amazon's Echo, which can be controlled using a smartphone app, can be considered a tele-bot.
- **Example 2:** NASA's Mars Exploration Rovers, which can be controlled remotely, are another example of tele-bots.

### Characteristics of Tele-bots

Tele-bots typically share certain characteristics, including:

- **Remote control**: Tele-bots can be controlled from a distance using devices such as smartphones or tablets.
- **Autonomy**: Tele-bots often have autonomy to perform tasks independently.
- **Human-AI interaction**: Tele-bots rely on human input to make decisions.

### Applications of Tele-bots

Tele-bots have numerous applications across various industries, including:

- **Healthcare**: Tele-bots can be used to monitor patients remotely and provide care.
- **Logistics**: Tele-bots can be used to track packages and manage inventory.

### Case Study 1: Amazon's Echo

Amazon's Echo is a tele-bot that can be controlled using a smartphone app. The Echo can perform tasks such as playing music and setting reminders.

### Case Study 2: NASA's Mars Exploration Rovers

## **Chapter 16: Active Appliances**

### Introduction

In the context of Human-Centred AI, active appliances refer to AI-powered devices that can perform tasks autonomously without human intervention. These devices are designed to make our lives easier and more efficient.

### History of Active Appliances

The concept of active appliances emerged in the 1980s, with the development of the first smart home devices. However, it wasn't until the 21st century that active appliances began to emerge as a distinct category of AI applications.

- **Example 1:** Nest's learning thermostat, which can adjust the temperature automatically, can be considered an active appliance.
- **Example 2:** Samsung's smart fridge, which can automatically order groceries and keep track of expiration dates, is another example of an active appliance.

### Characteristics of Active Appliances

Active appliances typically share certain characteristics, including:

- **Autonomy**: Active appliances can perform tasks autonomously without human intervention.
- **Learning and adaptation**: Active appliances can learn from data and adapt to changing situations.
- **Human-AI interaction**: Active appliances rely on human input to make decisions.

### Applications of Active Appliances

Active appliances have numerous applications across various industries, including:

- **Home Automation**: Active appliances can be used to automate home tasks, such as lighting and temperature control.
- **Healthcare**: Active appliances can be used to monitor health metrics and provide personalized recommendations.

### Case Study 1: Nest's Learning Thermostat

Nest's learning thermostat is an active appliance that can adjust the temperature automatically. The thermostat can learn from user behavior and adapt to changing temperatures.

### Case Study 2: Samsung's Smart Fridge

Samsung's smart fridge is an active appliance that can automatically order groceries and keep track of expiration dates. The fridge can also provide personalized recommendations for meals and ingredients.
