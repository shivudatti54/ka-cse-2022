# E-Waste Summary

## Definition

- E-waste: Waste generated by the use, reuse, and disposal of electronic devices.
- Electronic devices include computers, smartphones, televisions, and other electronic equipment.

## Components of E-Waste

- Hazardous materials:
  - Heavy metals (e.g. lead, mercury, cadmium)
  - Batteries (e.g. lead-acid, nickel-cadmium)
  - Plastics and other polymers
  - Flame retardants and other chemicals
- Non-hazardous materials:
  - Metals (e.g. copper, aluminum)
  - Plastics and other polymers
  - Glass and ceramics

## Environmental Impacts of E-Waste

- Air pollution: Release of toxic gases and particulate matter
- Water pollution: Release of heavy metals and other chemicals into waterways
- Soil pollution: Release of heavy metals and other chemicals into soil
- Loss of natural resources: Extraction and processing of raw materials for e-waste

## E-Waste Management Theories

- Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR): Manufacturers take responsibility for the waste generated by their products.
- closed-loop production: Manufacturers design products for recycling and reuse.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- Half-life: The time it takes for the concentration of a radioactive substance to decrease by half.
- Waste hierarchy:
  1.  Reduce: Minimize waste generation
  2.  Reuse: Reuse products and materials
  3.  Recycle: Recover valuable materials from waste
  4.  Dispose: Manage waste as a last resort

## Statistics

- Electronic waste generated globally: 50 million metric tons (2018)
- Number of e-waste generation per capita: 2.5 kg (2018)

## Important Terms

- e-scrap: The collection and processing of electronic waste.
- Digital divide: The gap between individuals or communities with access to technology and those without.
- Responsible e-waste management: The management of e-waste in a way that minimizes harm to the environment and human health.
