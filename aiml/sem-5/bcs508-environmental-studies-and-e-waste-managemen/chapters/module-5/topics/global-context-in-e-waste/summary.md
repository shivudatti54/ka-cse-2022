# **Global Context in E-Waste**

### Definitions and Concepts

- **E-Waste**: Electronic waste generated from the disposal of electrical and electronic equipment.
- **Global E-Waste Management Framework**: A structured approach to manage e-waste, considering economic, environmental, and social aspects.
- **Leakage**: The release of hazardous substances into the environment, often occurring during e-waste disposal.

### Environmental and Health Impacts

- **Toxic substances**: Heavy metals (e.g., lead, mercury), halogens (e.g., cadmium, chromium), and PCBs in e-waste.
- **Pollution hotspots**: Areas with high concentrations of e-waste, such as landfills, informal dumpsites, and waterways.
- **Health risks**: Exposure to toxic substances, cancer, neurological damage, and reproductive issues.

### Global E-Waste Generation and Management

- **Global e-waste generation**: Approximately 50 million metric tons in 2018, projected to triple by 2030.
- **Recycling rates**: 17% in 2018, with significant variations between regions.
- **Challenges in e-waste management**: Lack of infrastructure, inadequate regulations, and lack of awareness.

### Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Leachate toxicity**: The concentration of hazardous substances in leachate (e.g., BOD5, COD5).
- **E-Waste Management Index**: A framework to assess e-waste management practices in countries (e.g., Basel Convention, e-Waste Management Index).

### Theories and Models

- **Circular economy**: A systems approach to managing resources, aiming to minimize waste and pollution.
- **Life cycle assessment**: A method to evaluate the environmental impacts of e-waste throughout its entire life cycle.

### Important Statistics

- **Global e-waste generation**: 50 million metric tons in 2018, projected to 150 million metric tons by 2030.
- **Recycling rates**: 17% in 2018, with significant variations between regions.
- **Top e-waste generating countries**: China, United States, Japan, Germany, and India.
