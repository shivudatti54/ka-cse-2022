### Historical Context

The term "e-waste" was first coined in the late 1990s to describe the growing problem of electronic waste generated by the rapid obsolescence of electronic devices. In the 1970s and 1980s, electronic waste was primarily generated by the disposal of large appliances such as televisions and refrigerators. However, with the advent of personal computers, mobile phones, and other electronic devices, the generation of e-waste accelerated.

In the 1990s, the European Union (EU) began to take notice of the growing problem and established the WEEE (Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment) directive in 2003. The directive aimed to reduce the environmental impacts of e-waste by setting standards for the design, disposal, and recycling of electronic devices.

### Modern Developments

Today, e-waste is a global problem, with an estimated 50 million metric tons of e-waste generated annually. The World Economic Forum estimates that the global e-waste management market will reach $64.8 billion by 2025.

In response to the growing problem, governments, industries, and civil society organizations are working together to develop more sustainable e-waste management practices. Some of the key developments include:

- **Design for recyclability**: Many companies are designing their products with recyclability in mind, using materials that can be easily recycled and reused.
- **E-waste recycling technologies**: New technologies are being developed to recycle e-waste, including plasma arc gasification, hydrometallurgy, and mechanical separation.
- **Circular economy approaches**: Companies are adopting circular economy approaches, where products are designed to be reused, recycled, or biodegradable.

### E-Resource: Definition and Characteristics

An e-resource is a digital or electronic resource that is used to support learning, teaching, and research. E-resources can take many forms, including:

- **E-books and articles**: Digital versions of books and articles that can be accessed online.
- **Online courses and tutorials**: Interactive learning resources that provide step-by-step instructions and guidance.
- **Databases and repositories**: Collections of digital resources that are organized and curated for easy access.
- **Digital images and videos**: Online resources that provide visual support for learning and teaching.

### E-Resource Management: Best Practices

Effective e-resource management is crucial for ensuring that digital resources are accessible, usable, and sustainable. Some best practices for e-resource management include:

- **Quality assurance**: Ensuring that e-resources meet quality standards for content, design, and accessibility.
- **Digital preservation**: Preserving e-resources for future generations by archiving and migrating them to new formats.
- **Accessibility**: Ensuring that e-resources are accessible to all users, regardless of their abilities or disabilities.
- **Usage monitoring**: Monitoring usage patterns to identify areas for improvement and optimize resource allocation.

### Case Studies

#### Case Study 1: The University of California, Berkeley

The University of California, Berkeley has implemented a comprehensive e-resource management system that includes:

- **Digital repository**: A centralized repository that stores and provides access to e-resources.
- **Discovery layer**: A search engine that indexes e-resources and provides discovery features.
- **Metadata management**: A system for managing and maintaining metadata for e-resources.

#### Case Study 2: The British Library

The British Library has developed a digital preservation strategy that includes:

- **Digital preservation policy**: A policy that outlines the library's approach to digital preservation.
- **Digital curation**: A process for selecting, preserving, and providing access to digital resources.
- **Digital forensics**: A process for analyzing and preserving digital artifacts for research and educational purposes.

### Applications

E-resources have many applications in various fields, including:

- **Education**: E-resources can support learning and teaching in a variety of subjects, including science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM).
- **Research**: E-resources can support research in various fields, including social sciences, humanities, and natural sciences.
- **Business**: E-resources can support business operations, including marketing, finance, and human resources.
- **Government**: E-resources can support government operations, including policy-making, public administration, and citizen engagement.
