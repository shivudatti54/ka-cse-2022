# Water Borne Diseases & Water Induced Diseases

## Introduction

Water is essential for human survival, and access to clean water is a fundamental human right. However, water can also be a source of disease, particularly when it is contaminated with pollutants, pathogens, and other harmful substances. Water borne diseases and water induced diseases are a significant public health concern, causing millions of illnesses and deaths worldwide every year. In this chapter, we will delve into the causes, effects, and solutions of these diseases, as well as discuss historical context and modern developments.

## Causes of Water Borne Diseases

Water borne diseases are caused by the presence of pathogens, such as bacteria, viruses, and parasites, in contaminated water. These pathogens can come from a variety of sources, including:

- **Sewage and wastewater**: When sewage and wastewater are not properly treated and disposed of, they can contaminate water sources, such as rivers, lakes, and groundwater.
- **Agricultural runoff**: Fertilizers, pesticides, and manure from agricultural activities can enter waterways, causing eutrophication and contamination.
- **Industrial effluent**: Industrial processes can release pollutants into waterways, including chemicals, heavy metals, and other hazardous substances.
- **Human waste**: Human waste, including feces and urine, can contaminate water sources through poor sanitation and hygiene practices.

## Effects of Water Borne Diseases

Water borne diseases can have severe consequences for human health, including:

- **Acute diarrhea**: Water borne pathogens can cause acute diarrhea, which can lead to dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, and even death.
- **Cholera**: Cholera is a water borne disease caused by Vibrio cholerae, which can cause severe diarrhea and dehydration.
- **Typhoid fever**: Typhoid fever is a water borne disease caused by Salmonella Typhi, which can cause fever, headache, and abdominal pain.
- **Schistosomiasis**: Schistosomiasis is a water borne disease caused by Schistosoma parasites, which can cause liver and intestinal damage.

## Case Study: Cholera Outbreak in Haiti

In 2010, a cholera outbreak occurred in Haiti, which resulted in over 700,000 cases and 10,000 deaths. The outbreak was caused by contaminated water and poor sanitation, and it highlighted the need for improved water and sanitation infrastructure in developing countries.

## Historical Context

Water borne diseases have been a public health concern for centuries. The ancient Greeks and Romans recognized the importance of clean water for health, and they developed sophisticated systems for treating and distributing water. However, with the rise of urbanization and industrialization, water treatment and sanitation infrastructure were often neglected, leading to outbreaks of water borne diseases.

## Modern Developments

In recent years, there have been significant advances in water treatment and sanitation technology, including:

- **Advanced water treatment technologies**: New technologies, such as membrane bioreactors and advanced oxidation processes, have been developed to remove contaminants and pathogens from water.
- **Water reuse**: Water reuse, including wastewater reclamation and recycling, has become a growing practice, particularly in water-scarce regions.
- **Sanitation infrastructure**: Improved sanitation infrastructure, including septic systems and sewerage networks, has been developed to reduce the risk of water borne diseases.

## Examples of Water Induced Diseases

Water induced diseases are caused by the physical and chemical properties of water, rather than by the presence of pathogens. Examples include:

- **Frostbite**: Prolonged exposure to cold water can cause frostbite, which can lead to permanent damage to the skin and underlying tissues.
- **Hyponatremia**: Drinking large amounts of water can cause hyponatremia, which can lead to seizures, coma, and even death.
- **Cold shock response**: Sudden exposure to cold water can cause a cold shock response, which can lead to cardiac arrest and other serious complications.

## Prevention and Control

Preventing and controlling water borne diseases requires a multi-faceted approach, including:

- **Proper water treatment**: Using advanced water treatment technologies and disinfection methods to remove contaminants and pathogens.
- **Improved sanitation infrastructure**: Building and maintaining effective sanitation infrastructure, including septic systems and sewerage networks.
- **Public education and awareness**: Educating the public about the risks of water borne diseases and the importance of proper hygiene and sanitation practices.
- **Monitoring and surveillance**: Monitoring and surveillance of water quality and disease outbreaks to identify trends and patterns.

## Applications

Water borne diseases and water induced diseases have significant implications for public health, economy, and environment. Applications of prevention and control measures include:

- **Water treatment and reuse**: Developing and implementing advanced water treatment technologies and water reuse systems to provide clean drinking water and reduce the risk of water borne diseases.
- **Sanitation infrastructure**: Building and maintaining effective sanitation infrastructure, including septic systems and sewerage networks, to reduce the risk of water borne diseases.
- **Public health programs**: Implementing public health programs to educate the public about the risks of water borne diseases and promote proper hygiene and sanitation practices.
