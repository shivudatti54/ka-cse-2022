## \*\*1.1 Image Formation: Photometric Image

### 1.1.1 Definition of Photometric Image

A photometric image is a numerical representation of the intensity of light that falls on a 2D surface, such as a camera sensor. It is a measure of the amount of light that is reflected or absorbed by an object.

### 1.1.2 Formation of Photometric Image

The formation of a photometric image occurs through a process called radiometry. Radiometry involves measuring the intensity of light emitted or reflected by an object.

- **Incident Light**: Light that falls on an object from the scene.
- **Reflected Light**: Light that is reflected by an object and reaches the camera sensor.
- **Photometric Image**: The numerical representation of the intensity of reflected light.

### 1.1.3 Components of a Photometric Image

A photometric image is composed of the following components:

- **Intensity**: A measure of the amount of light that falls on a given pixel.
- **Color**: A measure of the wavelength of light that is reflected by an object.
- **Radiance**: A measure of the amount of light that is emitted or reflected per unit solid angle.

### 1.1.4 Image Formation Process

The image formation process involves the following steps:

1.  **Object Illumination**: The object is illuminated by a light source.
2.  **Radiation**: The light source emits radiation that illuminates the object.
3.  **Reflection**: The object reflects the radiation and sends it back to the light source.
4.  **Transmission**: The reflected radiation is transmitted through the scene.
5.  **Receive**: The transmitted radiation is received by the camera sensor.
6.  **Photometric Image Formation**: The received radiation is converted into a photometric image.

### 1.1.5 Key Concepts

- **Radiometry**: The measurement of the intensity of light.
- **Photometric Image**: A numerical representation of the intensity of reflected light.
- **Intensity**: A measure of the amount of light that falls on a given pixel.
- **Color**: A measure of the wavelength of light that is reflected by an object.
- **Radiance**: A measure of the amount of light that is emitted or reflected per unit solid angle.

### 1.1.6 Example

Suppose we have a camera that captures a photometric image of a scene. The camera sensor receives radiation that is reflected by objects in the scene. The intensity of the radiation that falls on each pixel of the camera sensor determines the value of that pixel in the photometric image.

For example, if the camera sensor receives 100 units of radiation on a given pixel, the value of that pixel in the photometric image would be 100.

### 1.1.7 Applications

Photometric images have a wide range of applications in computer vision, including:

- **Image Enhancement**: Photometric images can be enhanced to improve the quality of the image.
- **Object Recognition**: Photometric images can be used to recognize objects in the scene.
- **Image Segmentation**: Photometric images can be used to segment the image into different regions.

By understanding the formation of photometric images, we can better appreciate the importance of radiometry in computer vision.
