## 2.2.1 Introduction

In computer vision, image formation is the process of capturing and representing visual information from the physical world into a digital format. This process involves the conversion of light into electrical signals, which are then processed and stored as digital data. In this chapter, we will explore the history of image formation, the principles behind it, and the various techniques used to capture and represent visual information.

## 2.2.2 Historical Context

The study of image formation dates back to the early 20th century, when the first cameras were invented. The first camera, the Kodak No. 1, was invented by George Eastman in 1888. This camera used a roll of film to capture images, which were then developed into physical photographs.

In the mid-20th century, the development of electronic imaging technologies led to the creation of the first digital cameras. The first digital camera, the Dycam Model 1, was invented in 1975. This camera used a charge-coupled device (CCD) to capture images, which were then stored on a cassette tape.

## 2.2.3 Principles of Image Formation

Image formation involves the conversion of light into electrical signals, which are then processed and stored as digital data. The process of image formation can be broken down into several stages:

1. **Light Gathering**: Light from the physical world enters the camera and is focused onto a light-sensitive surface, such as a CCD or complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) sensor.
2. **Excitation**: The light-sensitive surface is excited by the incoming light, causing a change in the electrical properties of the material.
3. **Signal Generation**: The change in electrical properties generates an electrical signal, which is proportional to the intensity of the incoming light.
4. **Signal Processing**: The electrical signal is processed and amplified to increase its strength and quality.
5. **Storage**: The processed signal is stored as digital data, which can be used to reconstruct the original image.

## 2.2.4 Image Formation Techniques

There are several techniques used to capture and represent visual information, including:

1. **Charge-Coupled Device (CCD)**: A CCD is a light-sensitive surface that converts light into electrical signals. It is commonly used in digital cameras and other imaging devices.
2. **Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor (CMOS)**: A CMOS sensor is a type of light-sensitive surface that converts light into electrical signals. It is commonly used in digital cameras and other imaging devices.
3. **Complementary Photodiodes (CPDs)**: A CPD is a type of light-sensitive surface that converts light into electrical signals. It is commonly used in digital cameras and other imaging devices.
4. **CCD-CMOS Hybrid**: A CCD-CMOS hybrid is a type of light-sensitive surface that combines the benefits of both CCDs and CMOS sensors. It is commonly used in digital cameras and other imaging devices.

## 2.2.6 Applications of Image Formation

Image formation has numerous applications in various fields, including:

1. **Digital Photography**: Image formation is used in digital cameras to capture and store images.
2. **Medical Imaging**: Image formation is used in medical imaging devices, such as X-ray machines and MRI machines, to capture and store medical images.
3. **Surveillance**: Image formation is used in surveillance systems to capture and store video footage.
4. **Autonomous Vehicles**: Image formation is used in autonomous vehicles to capture and process visual data from cameras and other sensors.
