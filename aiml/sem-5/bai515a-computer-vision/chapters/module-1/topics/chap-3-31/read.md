## Image Formation

---

Image formation is the process by which an image is created in our brain from the light that enters our eye. The image formation process involves several steps:

### 1. Light Entrance

Light from an object enters our eye through the cornea, the transparent outer layer of the eye.

### 2. Refraction

The light is then refracted, or bent, as it passes through the cornea.

### 3. Focus

The light is focused onto the retina, which is the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye.

### 4. Signal Transmission

The retina converts the light into electrical signals, which are transmitted to the brain.

### 5. Image Formation

The brain interprets the electrical signals as an image.

## Characteristics of a Photometric Image

---

A photometric image is a mathematical representation of the light intensity of an object. It is a 2D representation of the light intensity distribution of an object.

### Characteristics:

- **Intensity**: The amount of light emitted or reflected by an object.
- **Radiance**: The amount of light emitted or reflected per unit solid angle.
- **Illuminance**: The amount of light incident on a surface per unit area.
- **Exposure**: The amount of time that light is incident on an object.

## Types of Photometric Images

---

There are several types of photometric images, including:

### 1. Grayscale Image

A grayscale image is a photometric image that represents the light intensity of an object using different shades of gray.

### 2. Color Image

A color image is a photometric image that represents the light intensity of an object using different colors.

### 3. Binary Image

A binary image is a photometric image that represents the light intensity of an object using two different intensities, typically black and white.

## Notations

---

The following notations are used to describe photometric images:

- **I(x, y)**: The light intensity at point (x, y) on an object.
- **L(x, y)**: The light intensity at point (x, y) on a surface.
- **I(x)**: The light intensity at point x on an object.

## Mathematical Representation

---

A photometric image can be mathematically represented using the following equations:

- **I(x, y) = f(x, y) \* g(x, y)**: The light intensity at point (x, y) is the product of the reflectance of the surface and the incident light.
- **L(x, y) = I(x, y) \* cos(θ)**: The light intensity at point (x, y) is the product of the light intensity and the cosine of the angle between the normal to the surface and the direction of the light.

## Applications

---

Photometric images have many applications in computer vision, including:

- **Image Processing**: Photometric images are used in image processing techniques such as filtering, thresholding, and segmentation.
- **Object Recognition**: Photometric images are used in object recognition techniques such as feature extraction and comparison.
- **3D Reconstruction**: Photometric images are used in 3D reconstruction techniques such as stereo vision and structure from motion.
