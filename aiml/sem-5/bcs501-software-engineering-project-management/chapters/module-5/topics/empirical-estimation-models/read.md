# **Empirical Estimation Models**

## **Introduction**

Empirical estimation models are a class of estimation techniques used in software engineering and project management. These models are based on empirical evidence and statistical analysis to predict the complexity and effort required to complete a software project. In this study material, we will explore the different types of empirical estimation models, their characteristics, and examples.

## **Types of Empirical Estimation Models**

### 1. **Planning Poker**

- **Definition:** A team-based estimation technique where team members make verbal estimates of the effort required to complete a task.
- **Characteristics:**
  - Team-based
  - Verbal estimates
  - No specific formula or scale used
  - Can be affected by team dynamics and communication

Example:

- A team member estimates the effort required to fix a bug as "3" (e.g., 1-5 scale).
- Another team member estimates the same effort as "4".
- The average of the two estimates is used as the final estimate.

### 2. **Story Points**

- **Definition:** A relative estimation technique where estimators assign a relative value to a user story or task based on its complexity.
- **Characteristics:**
  - Relative estimation
  - Uses a specific scale (e.g., 1-13)
  - Can be used for both tasks and user stories
  - Can be affected by the estimator's experience and bias

Example:

- A user story has three acceptance criteria.
- The estimator assigns a story point value of 5 (e.g., 1-13 scale).
- Another user story with two acceptance criteria is assigned a story point value of 3.

### 3. **Six Thinking Hats**

- **Definition:** A mental model that encourages team members to consider different perspectives when estimating effort.
- **Characteristics:**
  - Encourages team discussion and collaboration
  - Uses a specific framework (e.g., white hat, red hat, etc.)
  - Can be used for both tasks and user stories
  - Can be affected by team dynamics and communication

Example:

- A team member estimates the effort required to fix a bug as "low" (white hat).
- Another team member estimates the same effort as "medium" (red hat).
- The team discusses and reconciles the estimates to arrive at a final estimate.

### 4. **Epic Estimation**

- **Definition:** A technique used to estimate the effort required to complete a large-scale software project.
- **Characteristics:**
  - Used for large-scale projects
  - Involves estimating the effort required to complete a high-level user story or feature
  - Can be affected by the project's complexity and scope

Example:

- A team estimates the effort required to complete a new feature as 100 story points.
- The team breaks down the feature into smaller tasks and estimates the effort required for each task.
- The total effort required for the feature is calculated as the sum of the individual task estimates.

## **Conclusion**

Empirical estimation models are a useful tool for software engineers and project managers to estimate the complexity and effort required to complete a software project. By understanding the different types of empirical estimation models, their characteristics, and examples, you can choose the best model for your project's specific needs.

**Key Takeaways**

- Empirical estimation models are based on empirical evidence and statistical analysis.
- There are four main types of empirical estimation models: Planning Poker, Story Points, Six Thinking Hats, and Epic Estimation.
- Each model has its strengths and weaknesses, and the choice of model depends on the project's specific needs.
- Empirical estimation models can be used for both small-scale and large-scale projects.
- Understanding the characteristics and examples of each model can help you make informed decisions about which model to use for your project.
