# Deriving Functional and NonFunctional Requirements

### Introduction

In software engineering, requirements gathering is a critical phase of the software development life cycle. Requirements are the foundation of a software system, and ensuring that they are accurately identified and documented is essential for the success of the project. There are two types of requirements: functional requirements and nonfunctional requirements. Functional requirements describe what the software should do, while nonfunctional requirements describe how the software should behave, perform, and interact with its environment.

In this chapter, we will delve into the world of requirements gathering, focusing on functional and nonfunctional requirements. We will explore the historical context, modern developments, and provide examples and case studies to illustrate the concepts.

### Historical Context

The concept of requirements gathering has been around since the early days of software development. In the 1960s, the US Department of Defense developed the first software development methodologies, which emphasized the importance of requirements gathering. The 1970s saw the introduction of the first software requirements specification (SRS) documents, which provided a standard format for documenting functional and nonfunctional requirements.

In the 1980s, the concept of requirements engineering emerged, recognizing the need for a more structured approach to requirements gathering. The 1990s saw the introduction of the Unified Modeling Language (UML), which provided a graphical representation of software systems and their requirements.

### Modern Developments

Today, requirements gathering is a critical component of agile software development methodologies. Agile emphasizes the importance of continuous requirements gathering and iteration, recognizing that software systems are complex and dynamic.

The introduction of cloud computing, artificial intelligence, and the Internet of Things (IoT) has further emphasized the need for nonfunctional requirements. Nonfunctional requirements now include considerations such as security, scalability, and interoperability.

### Functional Requirements

Functional requirements describe what the software should do. They are typically expressed in natural language and include descriptions of the software's features, functions, and behavior. There are three types of functional requirements:

- **Functional requirements**: Describe the software's features and functions.
- **Interface requirements**: Describe the interactions between the software and its users, other software systems, and hardware devices.
- **Performance requirements**: Describe the software's performance characteristics, such as response time and throughput.

## Example of Functional Requirements

- The software should be able to process transactions up to 10,000 per second.
- The software should be able to handle a minimum of 100 concurrent users.
- The software should provide a user-friendly interface with easy-to-use menus and buttons.

### Nonfunctional Requirements

Nonfunctional requirements describe how the software should behave, perform, and interact with its environment. There are several types of nonfunctional requirements:

- **Performance requirements**: Describe the software's performance characteristics, such as response time and throughput.
- **Security requirements**: Describe the software's security features, such as encryption and access control.
- **Usability requirements**: Describe the software's user interface and user experience.
- **Availability requirements**: Describe the software's availability and reliability.

## Example of Nonfunctional Requirements

- The software should be able to process transactions within 2 seconds.
- The software should have end-to-end encryption to protect user data.
- The software should be accessible from anywhere in the world.
- The software should be available 99.9% of the time.

### Case Study

The following is a case study that illustrates the derivation of functional and nonfunctional requirements:

**Case Study: Online Shopping Platform**

A company wants to develop an online shopping platform that allows users to browse and purchase products from the comfort of their own homes. The platform should be able to handle a large volume of transactions, provide a secure and user-friendly interface, and be accessible from anywhere in the world.

Functional Requirements:

- The platform should be able to display a list of available products.
- The platform should allow users to add products to their shopping cart.
- The platform should allow users to checkout and complete their purchase.
- The platform should be able to handle a minimum of 10,000 concurrent users.

Nonfunctional Requirements:

- The platform should be able to process transactions within 2 seconds.
- The platform should have end-to-end encryption to protect user data.
- The platform should be accessible from anywhere in the world.
- The platform should be available 99.9% of the time.

### Deriving Functional and Nonfunctional Requirements

Deriving functional and nonfunctional requirements involves a systematic approach to identifying the software system's requirements. The following are the steps involved in deriving functional and nonfunctional requirements:

1.  **Stakeholder analysis**: Identify the stakeholders who will be affected by the software system.
2.  **Use case analysis**: Identify the use cases that represent the software system's functional requirements.
3.  **Requirements gathering**: Gather information from stakeholders and use cases to derive functional requirements.
4.  **Requirements analysis**: Analyze the functional requirements to ensure that they are complete and accurate.
5.  **Nonfunctional requirements analysis**: Analyze the nonfunctional requirements to ensure that they are complete and accurate.
