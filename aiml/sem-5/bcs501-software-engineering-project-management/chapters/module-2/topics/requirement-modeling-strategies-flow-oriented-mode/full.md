# **Requirement Modeling Strategies : Flow Oriented Modeling**

## **Introduction**

Requirement modeling is a crucial stage in the software development process where the requirements of a system are defined and documented. It involves identifying, analyzing, and documenting the functional and non-functional requirements of a system. Requirement modeling strategies are essential to ensure that the system requirements are complete, accurate, and unambiguous. In this module, we will delve into the topic of flow-oriented modeling, a requirement modeling strategy that focuses on capturing the flow of processes and interactions between components.

## **Historical Context**

The concept of requirement modeling dates back to the 1960s, when the first structured analysis techniques were developed. These techniques emphasized the importance of identifying and documenting the requirements of a system. Over the years, various requirement modeling strategies have emerged, including:

1. **Data Flow Diagrams (DFDs)**: Introduced in the 1970s, DFDs represent the flow of data between components and processes.
2. **Entity-Relationship Diagrams (ERDs)**: Developed in the 1980s, ERDs represent the relationships between entities and attributes.
3. **Use Cases**: Introduced in the 1990s, use cases represent the interactions between a system and its users.

## **Flow Oriented Modeling**

Flow oriented modeling is a requirement modeling strategy that focuses on capturing the flow of processes and interactions between components. This approach emphasizes the importance of understanding the sequence of events and the interactions between components in a system.

## **Key Principles**

1. **Focus on Processes**: Flow oriented modeling focuses on capturing the processes and interactions between components, rather than individual components.
2. **Sequence of Events**: The approach emphasizes the sequence of events and the interactions between components.
3. **High-Level Abstraction**: Flow oriented modeling uses high-level abstractions to represent the flow of processes and interactions.

## **Types of Flow Oriented Models**

1. **Data Flow Diagrams (DFDs)**: DFDs represent the flow of data between components and processes.
2. **Process Flow Diagrams (PFDs)**: PFDs represent the flow of processes and interactions between components.
3. **Sequence Diagrams**: Sequence diagrams represent the sequence of events and interactions between components.

## **Creating a Flow Oriented Model**

1. **Identify the Processes**: Identify the processes and interactions between components.
2. **Model the Flow**: Model the flow of processes and interactions between components.
3. **Add Control Flows**: Add control flows to represent the sequence of events.
4. **Use High-Level Abstractions**: Use high-level abstractions to represent the flow of processes and interactions.

## **Example**

Suppose we are developing a simple banking system that allows users to deposit and withdraw money. A flow oriented model for this system might include:

1. **Process**: Deposit money
2. **Process**: Withdraw money
3. **Flow**: User initiates deposit or withdrawal process
4. **Control Flow**: System checks user's account balance
5. **Flow**: System updates account balance
