# Module 2: Water Resources – Availability & Quality Aspects

## Introduction

Water is the most crucial natural resource for all life and a fundamental requirement for industrial and agricultural development. For engineers, understanding water's availability, distribution, and quality is paramount, as our work directly impacts and is impacted by this vital resource. This module explores the global and national scenario of water availability, the mounting pressures on it, and the critical parameters that define its quality for various uses.

## Core Concepts

### 1. Availability of Water Resources

Despite Earth being the "blue planet," only a tiny fraction of its water is readily available for human use.

*   **Global Distribution:**
    *   **97.5%** is saline water in oceans and seas.
    *   **2.5%** is freshwater.
    *   Of this freshwater, about **70%** is locked in glaciers and ice caps.
    *   **30%** is groundwater (often deep and inaccessible).
    *   **<1%** is surface water (rivers, lakes, reservoirs) and atmospheric water, which is the primary source for most human requirements.

*   **Indian Scenario:** India has about 4% of the world's freshwater resources to support nearly 18% of the global population. The availability is highly uneven in both time (concentrated in the monsoon season) and space (with flood-prone areas like Assam and water-scarce regions like Rajasthan). The per capita water availability is declining rapidly due to population growth, placing us under **"water stress."**

*   **Sources of Water:**
    *   **Surface Water:** Rivers, lakes, ponds, and reservoirs. The health of a river basin is a key indicator of regional water sustainability.
    *   **Groundwater:** Water stored in underground aquifers. It is a critical source for irrigation and drinking in India, but its uncontrolled extraction through tube wells has led to severe depletion and falling water tables in many states.

### 2. Quality Aspects of Water

Water quality refers to the physical, chemical, and biological characteristics of water, which determine its suitability for a specific purpose (e.g., drinking, irrigation, industrial cooling).

The quality of both surface and groundwater is deteriorating due to pollution. The primary sources are:

*   **Point Sources:** Discharge from a single, identifiable source (e.g., industrial effluent outlet, sewage treatment plant outfall).
*   **Non-Point Sources (Diffuse Pollution):** Runoff from agricultural fields (carrying fertilizers, pesticides), urban areas (carrying oil, grease, trash), and construction sites (carrying sediments).

#### Key Water Quality Parameters

Engineers use these parameters to assess and monitor water quality:

1.  **Physical Parameters:**
    *   **Turbidity:** Measures the cloudiness caused by suspended solids. High turbidity blocks sunlight for aquatic plants and can carry pathogens.
    *   **Temperature:** Affects dissolved oxygen levels (cold water holds more oxygen) and the rate of chemical/biological reactions.
    *   **Color and Odor:** Indicate the presence of organic matter, chemicals, or biological decay.

2.  **Chemical Parameters:**
    *   **pH:** Measures the acidity or alkalinity. Most natural waters have a pH between 6.5 and 8.5. Extreme pH can be harmful to aquatic life and corrode pipes.
    *   **Dissolved Oxygen (DO):** The amount of oxygen dissolved in water. It is essential for aquatic life. Low DO (< 5 mg/L) indicates pollution, often from organic waste that decomposes and consumes oxygen.
    *   **Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD):** The amount of oxygen required by microorganisms to decompose organic matter in a water sample. **High BOD is a direct indicator of organic pollution** (e.g., from sewage or food processing waste).
    *   **Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD):** Measures the oxygen equivalent of the organic matter that can be chemically oxidized. It's a faster but less specific test than BOD.
    *   **Nutrients (Nitrates & Phosphates):** Essential for plant growth, but excess amounts from fertilizers and detergents cause **eutrophication** (algal blooms that deplete oxygen).
    *   **Toxic Substances:** Heavy metals (Lead, Mercury, Arsenic), pesticides, and industrial chemicals that are harmful even in low concentrations.

3.  **Biological Parameters:**
    *   **Pathogens:** Disease-causing microorganisms like bacteria (e.g., *E. coli*), viruses, and protozoa. Their presence indicates fecal contamination and a high risk of waterborne diseases (cholera, typhoid).
    *   The presence of **coliform bacteria** is a common test for fecal contamination.

**Example:** A river downstream of a city might show **low DO, high BOD/COD, and high nitrate levels** due to the discharge of untreated sewage and agricultural runoff, leading to fish kills and unfit water for drinking without extensive treatment.

## Key Points / Summary

*   **Scarcity:** Freshwater is a limited and unevenly distributed resource. India faces significant water stress.
*   **Sources:** We rely on surface water (rivers, lakes) and groundwater, both of which are under severe strain from overuse.
*   **Pollution:** Water quality is degraded by point source (industrial, sewage) and non-point source (agricultural, urban runoff) pollution.
*   **Key Parameters:** Quality is assessed through:
    *   **Physical:** Turbidity, Temperature.
    *   **Chemical:** pH, DO, BOD, COD, Nutrients, Toxins.
    *   **Biological:** Presence of pathogens/coliforms.
*   **Engineering Responsibility:** As future engineers, whether in civil, chemical, or environmental fields, you will be involved in designing systems to protect water resources, treat wastewater, and ensure sustainable water management. Understanding these fundamentals is the first step.
