# Learning Objectives

After studying this topic, you should be able to:

1. Explain the evolution and characteristics of major disk interface standards including ATA, SATA, SCSI, and NVMe, and identify scenarios where each is most appropriate.

2. Describe the architecture of disk controllers and their role in abstracting physical disk characteristics from the operating system.

3. Compare programmed I/O, DMA, and interrupt-driven I/O mechanisms in terms of CPU overhead, implementation complexity, and suitable applications.

4. Analyze different disk scheduling algorithms (FCFS, SSTF, SCAN, C-SCAN, LOOK) and evaluate their performance characteristics under various workload patterns.

5. Calculate average disk access time by integrating seek time, rotational delay, and transfer time components.

6. Design appropriate buffering and caching strategies for specific application requirements, understanding the trade-offs between performance and data durability.

7. Apply disk scheduling algorithms to determine head movement and compare efficiency across different request sequences.
