# Learning Objectives

After studying this topic, you should be able to:

1. Explain the critical section problem and its three fundamental requirements: mutual exclusion, progress, and bounded waiting.

2. Analyze hardware-based synchronization mechanisms including Test-and-Set and Swap instructions, evaluating their advantages and limitations.

3. Implement process synchronization using semaphores, distinguishing between counting semaphores and binary semaphores and applying them appropriately to different scenarios.

4. Design solutions for classical synchronization problems including the producer-consumer, readers-writers, and dining philosophers problems.

5. Compare and contrast different synchronization approaches including hardware primitives, semaphores, and monitors in terms of ease of use, deadlock prevention, and efficiency.

6. Evaluate the correctness of synchronization solutions by verifying whether they satisfy the critical section requirements and do not introduce race conditions or deadlock.

7. Identify potential synchronization issues such as deadlock, starvation, and priority inversion in concurrent programs and propose appropriate solutions.

8. Apply theoretical concepts to practical operating system scenarios, understanding how real-world systems implement process synchronization mechanisms.
