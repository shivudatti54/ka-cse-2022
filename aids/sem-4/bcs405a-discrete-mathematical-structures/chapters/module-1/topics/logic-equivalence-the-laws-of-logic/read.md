# **Logic Equivalence – The Laws of Logic**

## **Introduction**

Logic Equivalence is a fundamental concept in discrete mathematical structures, which deals with the relationship between logical statements. In this topic, we will explore the laws of logic, including the rules and principles that govern logical equivalence.

## **Definition of Logical Equivalence**

Logical equivalence is a relationship between two or more logical statements that have the same truth value, regardless of the truth values of the individual statements. In other words, two statements are logically equivalent if they have the same truth value under all possible interpretations.

## **Laws of Logic**

### 1. The Law of Identity

- **Statement**: A statement is logically equivalent to itself.
- **Example**: p ≡ p
- **Explanation**: This law states that a statement is always true if it is identical to itself.

### 2. The Law of Commutativity

- **Statement**: The order of two statements does not affect their logical equivalence.
- **Example**: p ≡ q
- **Explanation**: This law states that the truth value of a statement remains the same regardless of the order in which the statements are presented.

### 3. The Law of Associativity

- **Statement**: The order in which three statements are combined does not affect their logical equivalence.
- **Example**: (p ∧ q) ≡ (q ∧ p)
- **Explanation**: This law states that the truth value of a statement remains the same regardless of the order in which the statements are combined.

### 4. The Law of Absorption

- **Statement**: If two statements are logically equivalent, then the truth value of one statement is determined by the truth value of the other statement.
- **Example**: p ∨ (p ∧ q) ≡ p
- **Explanation**: This law states that the truth value of a statement is determined by the truth value of one of its components.

### 5. The Law of Excluded Middle

- **Statement**: A statement is either true or false, with no middle ground.
- **Example**: p ∨ ¬p ≡ T
- **Explanation**: This law states that a statement is either always true or always false, with no middle ground.

### 6. The Law of Double Negation

- **Statement**: A statement is logically equivalent to its negation.
- **Example**: ¬(¬p) ≡ p
- **Explanation**: This law states that a statement is logically equivalent to its negation, which means that it is always true if its negation is true.

### 7. The Law of Distributivity

- **Statement**: The distribution of a statement over another statement does not affect their logical equivalence.
- **Example**: (p ∧ (q ∨ r)) ≡ ((p ∧ q) ∨ (p ∧ r))
- **Explanation**: This law states that the truth value of a statement remains the same regardless of the order in which the statements are combined.

## **Key Concepts**

- Logical equivalence: A relationship between two or more logical statements that have the same truth value.
- Laws of logic: Rules and principles that govern logical equivalence.
- Identity: A statement is always true if it is identical to itself.
- Commutativity: The order of two statements does not affect their logical equivalence.
- Associativity: The order in which three statements are combined does not affect their logical equivalence.
- Absorption: If two statements are logically equivalent, then the truth value of one statement is determined by the truth value of the other statement.
- Excluded middle: A statement is either true or false, with no middle ground.
- Double negation: A statement is logically equivalent to its negation.
- Distributivity: The distribution of a statement over another statement does not affect their logical equivalence.

Logic Equivalence is a fundamental concept in discrete mathematical structures, which deals with the relationship between logical statements.
