# **Providing a Public Good**

## **Key Points**

- A public good is a non-excludable and non-rivalrous good that provides benefits to all members of a group.
- Examples: national defense, public health, social welfare
- Game-theoretic models:
  - **Prisoner's Dilemma**: Three prisoners, each with two actions (confess or stay silent), with payoffs that depend on the actions of all prisoners.
  - **Tragedy of the Commons**: A group of individuals sharing a common resource, with incentives to overuse the resource, leading to its depletion.
- **Public Goods Theory**:
  - **Free Rider Problem**: Individuals may free ride on others' contributions, leading to underprovision of the public good.
  - **Exclusion Problem**: Mechanisms are needed to exclude free riders and ensure that everyone contributes.
- **Mechanism Design**:
  - **Pay-As-You-Go**: Contributions are paid for each use, with revenue generated from users.
  - **Subscription-Based**: A flat fee is charged for access to the public good.
- **Theorems and Formulas**:
  - **Public Goods Theorem**: A public good is provided if and only if the social surplus is greater than the costs of provision.
  - **Free Rider Theorem**: A public good is underprovided if free riders outnumber those willing to pay for it.

## **Important Concepts**

- **Non-excludability**: It is impossible to prevent someone from using the public good without also preventing others from using it.
- **Non-rivalry**: The use of the public good by one person does not reduce its availability to others.
- **Social surplus**: The total benefit received by all members of the group from the public good.

## **Revision Tips**

- Focus on the key differences between public goods and private goods.
- Understand the free rider problem and the exclusion problem.
- Review mechanism design concepts, including pay-as-you-go and subscription-based models.
- Practice applying the public goods theorem and free rider theorem.
