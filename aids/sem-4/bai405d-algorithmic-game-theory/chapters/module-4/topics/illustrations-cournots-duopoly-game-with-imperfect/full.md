## Introduction

---

Cournot's duopoly game is a classic model in game theory that demonstrates the complex behavior of two firms competing in a market with imperfect information. In this illustration, we will delve into the details of Cournot's duopoly game, exploring its historical context, key concepts, and applications.

## Historical Context

---

Cournot's duopoly game was first introduced by French mathematician Augustin Cournot in his 1838 book "Researches in the Mathematical Analysis of the Theory of Wealth". Cournot's work was influenced by the economic theories of Jean-Baptiste Say and David Ricardo, who discussed the concept of monopoly power and its impact on market prices.

Cournot's game was initially intended to model the competition between two firms in a market with imperfect information. The game was later refined by other economists, including Bertrand and Edgeworth, who introduced additional concepts and assumptions.

## Key Concepts

---

Cournot's duopoly game is played by two firms, each with a production technology that produces a homogeneous good. The firms have imperfect information about their competitors' production levels, which means they cannot observe their competitors' actions directly.

The game is characterized by the following key concepts:

- **Production Technology**: Each firm has a production technology that produces a homogeneous good. The production technology is a function that maps the firm's input (e.g., labor, capital) to the firm's output.
- **Competition**: The two firms compete to produce the same good in the market.
- **Imperfect Information**: The firms have imperfect information about their competitors' production levels, which means they cannot observe their competitors' actions directly.
- **Strategic Interdependence**: The firms' actions are interdependent, meaning that the firm's strategy depends on the other firm's strategy.

## The Game

---

The Cournot game is played as follows:

1.  **Stage 1**: Each firm chooses its production level, denoted as x1 and x2.
2.  **Stage 2**: The firms observe each other's production levels and update their beliefs about the market demand.
3.  **Stage 3**: The firms choose their pricing strategy, denoted as p1 and p2.
4.  **Stage 4**: The firms collect revenue from the sale of their output and the firms that sell the most output at the highest price receive the most revenue.

The game can be represented by the following payoffs:

|                | Firm 1     | Firm 2     |
| -------------- | ---------- | ---------- |
| **Firm 1**: x1 | p1(x1, x2) | -          |
| **Firm 2**: -  | -          | p2(x1, x2) |

The payoffs are determined by the production technology and the market demand. The market demand is a function of the total output produced by the two firms.

## Equilibrium

---

The Cournot game has a unique equilibrium, which is the Nash equilibrium. The Nash equilibrium is a pair of strategies that no firm can improve upon by unilaterally changing its strategy.

The Nash equilibrium of the Cournot game can be found by solving the following equations:

- **Firm 1's Inverse Demand Function**: p1(x1, x2) = a - bx1 - cx2
- **Firm 2's Inverse Demand Function**: p2(x1, x2) = a - bx1 - cx2
- **Firm 1's Profit Maximization**: max\_{x1} p1(x1, x2) - cx1
- **Firm 2's Profit Maximization**: max\_{x2} p2(x1, x2) - cx2

The Nash equilibrium is characterized by the following conditions:

- **Firm 1's Strategy**: x1 = x2
- **Firm 2's Strategy**: x2 = x1

The Nash equilibrium of the Cournot game is a symmetric equilibrium, where both firms produce the same amount of output.

## Applications

---

The Cournot game has numerous applications in economics, including:

- **Monopoly Power**: The Cournot game demonstrates the power of monopoly in a market with imperfect information.
- **Industry Competition**: The Cournot game can be used to model the competition between firms in different industries.
