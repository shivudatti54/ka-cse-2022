# **Illustration: Expert Diagnosis**

## **Introduction**

Expert diagnosis is a crucial aspect of decision-making in various fields, including medicine, engineering, and economics. In this context, we will explore the concept of expert diagnosis in the context of algorithmic game theory, specifically focusing on mixed strategy Nash equilibrium.

## **Definition of Expert Diagnosis**

Expert diagnosis refers to the process of identifying the best course of action or solution to a complex problem, often relying on the expertise and knowledge of a specialist. In game theory, expert diagnosis can be viewed as a game between a decision-maker (the player) and an opponent (the game).

## **Key Concepts**

- **Mixed Strategy Nash Equilibrium (MSNE):** A state where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming the other player's strategy remains unchanged. In the context of expert diagnosis, MSNE represents a situation where the decision-maker has adopted a mixed strategy, incorporating both rational and intuitive elements.
- **Nash Equilibrium:** A concept in game theory that describes a stable state where no player can improve their outcome by changing their strategy, assuming all other players' strategies remain unchanged.
- **Game Theory:** The study of strategic decision-making in situations where the outcome depends on the actions of multiple individuals or parties.

## **Expert Diagnosis in Algorithmic Game Theory**

In algorithmic game theory, expert diagnosis is often represented as a game between a decision-maker and an opponent. The decision-maker's goal is to identify the best course of action, while the opponent's goal is to mislead or confuse the decision-maker.

- **Types of Expert Diagnosis Games:**
  - **Symmetric Games:** Both players have the same information and goals.
  - **Asymmetric Games:** The players have different information and goals.
  - **Discrete Games:** The game is divided into discrete stages.
  - **Continuous Games:** The game is modeled as a continuous-time process.

## **Example: Medical Diagnosis**

Consider a medical diagnosis scenario where a doctor needs to identify the underlying cause of a patient's symptoms. The doctor's goal is to accurately diagnose the patient, while the patient's goal is to mislead or confuse the doctor.

- **Decision-Maker's Strategy:** The doctor's strategy can be modeled as a mixed strategy, incorporating both rational and intuitive elements.
- **Opponent's Strategy:** The patient's strategy may involve providing misleading or incomplete information to confuse the doctor.
