## Introduction

Game theory is a branch of mathematics that studies strategic decision making. In the context of game theory, a game is a situation where multiple individuals or parties make decisions that affect the outcomes of the game. The study of games has numerous applications in economics, politics, biology, and many other fields.

One of the fundamental concepts in game theory is the concept of equilibrium. An equilibrium is a state where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged. In the context of finite horizon games, finding the sub-game perfect equilibrium is crucial to determine the optimal strategy for each player.

## Historical Context

The concept of backward induction was first introduced by John von Neumann and Oskar Morgenstern in their 1944 book "Theory of Games and Economic Behavior". They introduced the concept of backward induction as a method to find the equilibrium of a game. However, it was John Nash who popularized the concept of backward induction in his 1950 paper "Dynamically Determined Strategic Form Games".

## Basic Concepts

Before diving into the topic of backward induction, let's define some basic concepts:

- **Game**: A situation where multiple individuals or parties make decisions that affect the outcomes of the game.
- **Strategy**: A set of actions taken by a player to achieve their goals.
- **Payoff**: The outcome or reward received by a player as a result of their strategy.
- **Equilibrium**: A state where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.
- **Finite horizon game**: A game with a finite number of stages or periods.

## Sub-Game Perfect Equilibrium

A sub-game perfect equilibrium is an equilibrium that holds for each sub-game or stage of the game, assuming rational behavior from all players. In other words, a sub-game perfect equilibrium is a state where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy within a specific sub-game.

## Backward Induction

Backward induction is a method to find the sub-game perfect equilibrium of a finite horizon game. The idea is to work backward from the last stage of the game, assuming that the outcome of the last stage is known.

Here's a step-by-step explanation of the backward induction process:

1. **Work backward**: Start from the last stage of the game and work backward to the first stage.
2. **Assume rational behavior**: Assume that all players are rational and make decisions based on their own self-interest.
3. **Determine the optimal strategy**: Determine the optimal strategy for the current stage, assuming the optimal strategies for all previous stages.
4. **Repeat steps 2-3**: Repeat the process for each stage, working backward from the last stage.

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a classic game theory example. Two prisoners, A and B, are arrested and interrogated separately by the police. The police offer each prisoner a deal: if one prisoner confesses and the other remains silent, the confessor will be released and the silent prisoner will receive a harsh sentence. If both prisoners confess, they will receive a moderate sentence. If both prisoners remain silent, they will each receive a light sentence.

The game can be represented as follows:

|                               | Prisoner B Remains Silent | Prisoner B Confesses |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------- | -------------------- |
| **Prisoner A Remains Silent** | (2, 2)                    | (0, 3)               |
| **Prisoner A Confesses**      | (3, 0)                    | (1, 1)               |

Using backward induction, we can determine the optimal strategy for each prisoner.
