### Introduction

The Ultimatum Game is a classic game in game theory, first introduced by Werner Güth, Rolf Schmittberger, and Bernd Schwarze in 1982. The game is a simple, yet powerful tool for understanding decision-making under uncertainty and the role of fairness in human behavior. In this section, we will delve into the world of the Ultimatum Game, exploring its definition, gameplay, payoffs, strategies, applications, and modern developments.

### Definition and Historical Context

The Ultimatum Game is a two-player game where one player, the proposer, is given a sum of money and must split it between themselves and the other player, the responder. The proposer presents a pair of numbers, x and y, where x is the amount the proposer will receive and y is the amount the responder will receive. The responder then has two options: either accept the split or reject it. If the responder accepts, the game ends, and both players receive their assigned amounts. If the responder rejects, the game ends, and neither player receives anything.

The Ultimatum Game was first proposed as a way to study fairness and cooperation in human behavior. The game's designer, Werner Güth, was interested in understanding how people would behave when faced with a situation where they had to make a decision that affected another person. The game was played with small groups of people in various settings, including laboratories, classrooms, and communities.

### Payoffs and Strategies

The payoffs in the Ultimatum Game are based on the amounts received by the proposer and responder. The proposer's payoff is x, and the responder's payoff is y. The goal is to maximize the total payoff.
