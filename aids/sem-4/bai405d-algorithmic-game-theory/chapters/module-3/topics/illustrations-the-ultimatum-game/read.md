# **Illustrations: The Ultimatum Game**

## **Introduction**

The Ultimatum Game is a classic game in the field of game theory and behavioral economics. It is a simple, yet powerful tool for understanding human decision-making and bargaining behavior. In this study material, we will delve into the world of the Ultimatum Game, exploring its concept, design, and implications.

## **What is the Ultimatum Game?**

The Ultimatum Game is a game where two players, typically referred to as the proposer and the responder, are presented with a situation where they must divide a sum of money. The proposer is given a amount of money and must split it with the responder, who has the option to accept or reject the split. If the responder accepts, the game ends, and both players receive their respective shares. If the responder rejects, the game ends, and neither player receives anything.

## **Design of the Ultimatum Game**

The game is typically designed as follows:

- The proposer is given a amount of money, say $100.
- The responder is given the remaining amount of money, say $0.
- The proposer proposes a split between the two amounts of money.
- The responder can accept or reject the split.
- If the responder accepts, the game ends, and both players receive their respective shares.
- If the responder rejects, the game ends, and neither player receives anything.

## **Key Concepts**

- **Proposer**: The player who is given the amount of money to propose a split.
- **Responder**: The player who is given the remaining amount of money and can accept or reject the proposed split.
- **Split**: The division of the total amount of money between the two players.
- **Acceptance**: The responder's decision to accept the proposed split.
- **Rejection**: The responder's decision to reject the proposed split.

## **Key Results and Insights**

- **Satisficing**: Research has shown that responders tend to accept splits that are close to 50% of the total amount of money, rather than trying to negotiate a more favorable split.
- **Minimum Acceptable Split**: Responders have been found to have a minimum acceptable split, which is the minimum amount they are willing to accept.
- **Maximum Acceptable Split**: Responders have also been found to have a maximum acceptable split, which is the maximum amount they are willing to accept.

## **Example**

Suppose we have a pair of players, Alice and Bob. Alice is given $100, and Bob is given $0. Alice proposes a split of $90 for herself and $10 for Bob. Bob has the option to accept or reject the split. If Bob accepts, both Alice and Bob receive their respective shares. If Bob rejects, neither Alice nor Bob receives anything.

| Alice's Share | Bob's Share |
| ------------- | ----------- |
| $90           | $10         |
| $50           | $50         |
| $0            | $100        |

In this example, Bob's minimum acceptable split is $20, and his maximum acceptable split is $50. If Alice proposes a split of $20, Bob is likely to accept, as it is close to 50% of the total amount of money. However, if Alice proposes a split of $50, Bob is likely to reject, as it is significantly higher than his maximum acceptable split.

## **Conclusion**

The Ultimatum Game is a powerful tool for understanding human decision-making and bargaining behavior. By exploring the key concepts, results, and insights of the game, we can gain a deeper understanding of how people make decisions in situations where they must divide resources. The game has numerous applications in fields such as economics, psychology, and business, and continues to be an important area of research and study.
