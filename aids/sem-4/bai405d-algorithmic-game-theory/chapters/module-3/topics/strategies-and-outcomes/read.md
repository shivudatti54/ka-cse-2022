# Strategies and Outcomes

=====================================

## Introduction

---

In Algorithmic Game Theory, strategies and outcomes are fundamental concepts that enable us to analyze and understand the behavior of self-interested players in competitive situations. In this study material, we will explore the definitions, types, and consequences of strategies and outcomes in game theory.

## Definitions

---

### Strategy

A strategy is a plan of action that an individual player can take in a game. It is a set of rules that defines how a player will make decisions at each stage of the game.

- Example: A chess player's strategy might involve controlling the center of the board, developing their pieces quickly, and attacking their opponent's king.
- Definition: A strategy is a function that maps each stage of the game to a set of actions.

### Outcome

An outcome is the result of a game, which can be a combination of the actions taken by all players.

- Example: In a game of poker, the outcome might be the winner of the pot, which is determined by the player who makes the best five-card hand.
- Definition: An outcome is a set of values associated with each possible combination of actions taken by all players.

## Types of Strategies

---

There are several types of strategies that players can employ in a game:

### 1. Pure Strategies

A pure strategy is a strategy that specifies a single action for each stage of the game.

- Example: A player might choose to play a specific card in a game of poker, without considering the actions of other players.
- Definition: A pure strategy is a function that maps each stage of the game to a single action.

### 2. Mixed Strategies

A mixed strategy is a strategy that specifies a probability distribution over a set of actions.

- Example: A player might randomly choose between two cards in a game of poker, with a 50% chance of playing the first card and a 50% chance of playing the second card.
- Definition: A mixed strategy is a function that maps each stage of the game to a probability distribution over a set of actions.

### 3. Nash Equilibrium

A Nash equilibrium is a pair of strategies, one for each player, such that no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy.

- Example: In a game of chicken, where two players simultaneously choose to swerve or not swerve, the Nash equilibrium is when both players choose to swerve.
- Definition: A Nash equilibrium is a pair of strategies, one for each player, such that no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy.

## Consequences of Strategies and Outcomes

---

The strategies and outcomes of players in a game can have several consequences:

### 1. Payoff Matrix

A payoff matrix is a table that summarizes the payoffs associated with each possible combination of actions taken by all players.

- Example: In a game of poker, the payoff matrix might show the payoffs associated with each possible combination of cards played by the two players.
- Definition: A payoff matrix is a table that summarizes the payoffs associated with each possible combination of actions taken by all players.

### 2. Pareto Optimality

A Pareto optimal outcome is an outcome that cannot be improved by any player without making another player worse off.

- Example: In a game of resource allocation, where two players must allocate a limited resource between two goods, a Pareto optimal outcome might be when both players receive the goods they value most highly.
- Definition: A Pareto optimal outcome is an outcome that cannot be improved by any player without making another player worse off.

### 3. Nash Impossibility Theorem

The Nash impossibility theorem states that, in a game where there are multiple players and a finite number of actions, there may not always be a Nash equilibrium.

- Example: In a game of prisoner's dilemma, where two players must choose to cooperate or defect, there may not always be a Nash equilibrium, because the optimal action for one player may depend on the action taken by the other player.
- Definition: The Nash impossibility theorem states that, in a game where there are multiple players and a finite number of actions, there may not always be a Nash equilibrium.

## Key Concepts

---

- **Payoff matrix**: a table that summarizes the payoffs associated with each possible combination of actions taken by all players.
- **Nash equilibrium**: a pair of strategies, one for each player, such that no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy.
- **Pareto optimality**: an outcome that cannot be improved by any player without making another player worse off.
- **Mixed strategies**: a strategy that specifies a probability distribution over a set of actions.
- **Pure strategies**: a strategy that specifies a single action for each stage of the game.

## Conclusion

---

In conclusion, strategies and outcomes are fundamental concepts in algorithmic game theory that enable us to analyze and understand the behavior of self-interested players in competitive situations. Understanding the different types of strategies, including pure and mixed strategies, and the consequences of strategies and outcomes, such as payoff matrices and Pareto optimality, is essential for analyzing and solving games.
