# Stackelberg’s Model of Duopoly

## Introduction

Stackelberg's model of duopoly is a fundamental concept in industrial organization theory, explaining how two firms compete with each other in a market. The model was first introduced by Hans Jacob Stackelberg in 1934 and has since become a cornerstone of game theory and economics. In this article, we will delve into the world of Stackelberg's duopoly model, exploring its historical context, theoretical framework, and real-world applications.

## Historical Context

Hans Jacob Stackelberg was a German economist who first proposed the duopoly model in his 1934 paper "The Theory of Market Structure." Stackelberg's work built upon the foundations of Alfred Marshall's neoclassical economics and the concept of oligopoly. He introduced the Stackelberg model as a way to explain how firms in a duopoly situation make decisions about prices and output.

## Theoretical Framework

The Stackelberg model consists of two firms, Firm A and Firm B, that compete with each other in a market. The model assumes that each firm has a complete information about the market and the other firm's strategies. The model can be divided into two stages:

### Stage 1: Firm A's Decision-Making Process

Firm A makes a decision about its price and output before Firm B makes its decision. Firm A's objective is to maximize its profit, given the market demand and the other firm's strategy.

### Stage 2: Firm B's Decision-Making Process

Firm B makes a decision about its price and output after observing Firm A's decision. Firm B's objective is also to maximize its profit, given the market demand and the other firm's strategy.

Mathematically, the Stackelberg model can be represented as follows:

Let P be the price, x be the output, and λ be the profit of each firm.

Firm A's profit function is given by:

λA = Px - C(x)

where C(x) is the cost function of Firm A.

Firm B's profit function is given by:

λB = P(1-x) - C(1-x)

where C(1-x) is the cost function of Firm B.

The Stackelberg model assumes that Firm A makes its decision first and then Firm B makes its decision. Firm A's decision is denoted by xA and Firm B's decision is denoted by xB.

The model can be solved using the following equations:

1. Firm A's profit-maximizing condition:

∂λA/∂xA = 0

2. Firm B's profit-maximizing condition:

∂λB/∂xB = 0

Solving these equations simultaneously yields the optimal price and output for each firm.

## Optimal Price and Output

The optimal price and output for each firm can be calculated using the following formulas:

P = (C'(x)) / (1 - x)

x = (C'(x)) / (C'(x) + λA - λB)

where C'(x) is the marginal cost function.

## Example

Suppose we have two firms, Firm A and Firm B, competing in a market. The market demand is given by the inverse demand function:

P = 100 - x

The cost functions for each firm are:

C(x) = 2x^2 + 3x + 10

Firm A's profit function is given by:

λA = Px - C(x)

Firm B's profit function is given by:

λB = P(1-x) - C(1-x)

Using the Stackelberg model, we can calculate the optimal price and output for each firm.

## Real-World Applications

The Stackelberg model has numerous real-world applications, including:

1. **Price Wars**: The model can be used to analyze price wars between firms, where both firms try to undercut each other's prices.
2. **Output Decisions**: The model can be used to analyze output decisions, where firms try to maximize their profits by adjusting their output levels.
3. **Market Structure**: The model can be used to analyze the structure of markets, where firms compete with each other to maximize their profits.
