# Stackelberg's Model of Duopoly

## **Introduction**

Stackelberg's model of duopoly is a game theory model that describes the behavior of two firms competing in a market. It was first introduced by Hans von Stackelberg in 1934. The model is widely used to study the strategic interaction between two firms in oligopolistic markets.

## **Definitions**

- **Oligopoly**: A market structure characterized by the presence of a few firms, each with significant market share.
- **Duopoly**: A specific type of oligopoly where there are only two firms competing in the market.
- **Stackelberg leader**: A firm that makes the first move in the market, setting the price and production levels for the other firm.
- **Stackelberg follower**: A firm that responds to the leader's actions, setting its own price and production levels.

## **The Model**

### Assumptions

- The market is perfect, with perfect information and no externalities.
- The firms have perfect price and production flexibility.
- The firms have similar costs and production technologies.

### Steps in the Model

1.  **Leader's Stage**: The Stackelberg leader sets the price and production levels for the market, taking into account the follower's reaction.
2.  **Follower's Stage**: The Stackelberg follower sets its own price and production levels in response to the leader's actions.

### Equilibrium

The equilibrium is reached when the leader and follower set their price and production levels such that the quantity sold by each firm is maximized.

## **Key Concepts**

- **Price leadership**: The leader sets the market price, and the follower sets its price based on the leader's price.
- **Price competition**: The follower sets its price based on the leader's price and the follower's own costs and market share.
- **Non-cooperative game**: The firms make independent decisions, with no coordination or cooperation.

## **Example**

Suppose we have two firms, Firm A and Firm B, competing in a market for a product. Firm A is the Stackelberg leader, and Firm B is the follower.

### Step 1: Leader's Stage

Firm A sets the market price at $10, taking into account Firm B's potential reaction.

- Firm A's profit function: P = 100Q - 2Q^2
- Firm B's reaction: Firm B sets its price at $9.99, based on Firm A's price.

### Step 2: Follower's Stage

Firm B sets its production level at 20 units, based on Firm A's price and its own costs.

- Firm B's profit function: P = 9.99Q - 3Q^2

### Equilibrium

The equilibrium is reached when Firm A sets its price at $10.01 and Firm B sets its production level at 20 units.

## **Advantages and Disadvantages**

Advantages:

- **Simplifies the analysis**: The Stackelberg model simplifies the analysis of oligopolistic competition by reducing the number of variables.
- **Provides insights**: The model provides insights into the strategic interaction between firms.

Disadvantages:

- **Assumes perfect information**: The model assumes perfect information, which may not be the case in real-world markets.
- **Ignores externalities**: The model ignores externalities, such as advertising or research and development costs.

## **Conclusion**

Stackelberg's model of duopoly provides a framework for analyzing the strategic interaction between two firms in an oligopolistic market. The model has been widely used to study the behavior of firms in various industries. However, it has its limitations, such as assuming perfect information and ignoring externalities.
