# Nash Equilibrium

### Definition

- A concept in game theory that describes a stable state where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.
- Named after John Nash, who introduced the concept in 1950.

### Key Points

- **Payoff Matrix**: A table that represents the potential payoffs for each player in a game, where rows represent player 1's strategies and columns represent player 2's strategies.
- **Strategy-Value Pair**: A pair of strategies, one for each player, that forms a Nash equilibrium.
- **Nash Equilibrium Condition**: A strategy-value pair is a Nash equilibrium if no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.

### Important Formulas and Definitions

- **Nash Equilibrium Formula**: There is no single formula for finding a Nash equilibrium. Instead, it is often found through iterative processes such as:
  - Backward induction: Starting from the final stage of the game and working backwards to find the optimal strategy.
  - Iterated elimination of dominated strategies: Eliminating strategies that are dominated by other strategies, until only one strategy remains.
- **Mixed Strategy**: A strategy that involves choosing a random action with a probability distribution over the possible actions.
- **Pure Strategy**: A strategy that involves choosing a single action.

### Important Theorems

- **Nash's Theorem**: A theorem that states that a Nash equilibrium is a stable state in a game, where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy.
- **Pareto Optimality**: A concept that states that a Nash equilibrium is optimal if no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off.
