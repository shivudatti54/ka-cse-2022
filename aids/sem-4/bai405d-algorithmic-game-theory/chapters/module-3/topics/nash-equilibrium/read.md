# Nash Equilibrium

=====================

## Introduction

---

The Nash equilibrium is a fundamental concept in game theory, named after John Nash, who introduced it in the 1950s. It provides a mathematical framework for analyzing strategic decision-making in situations where multiple individuals or entities interact with each other. In this study material, we will delve into the definition, explanation, and examples of the Nash equilibrium.

## Definition

---

The Nash equilibrium is a state in a game where no player can improve their payoff (or outcome) by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.

Mathematically, a Nash equilibrium can be represented as follows:

Let N be the set of players in the game.
Let S be the set of possible strategies for each player.
Let u_i(s) be the payoff function for player i, where s is the strategy chosen by player i.

A Nash equilibrium is a tuple (s^_, u^_) where:

- s^_ is a strategy vector, where each element s^_\_i is a strategy chosen by player i.
- u^_ is a payoff vector, where each element u^_\_i is the payoff for player i when the strategy vector is s^\*.

The payoff function u_i(s) assigns a numerical value to each strategy s for player i.

## Examples

---

### Example 1: Prisoner's Dilemma

Suppose two prisoners, A and B, are arrested and interrogated separately. The payoff matrix for this game is as follows:

|             | A Stay | A Leave |
| ----------- | ------ | ------- |
| **B Stay**  | 3, 3   | 0, 0    |
| **B Leave** | 0, 0   | 1, 1    |

In this game, the Nash equilibrium occurs when both prisoners choose to leave. If either prisoner stays, the other prisoner will leave, resulting in a lower payoff.

### Example 2: Chess

In the game of chess, a Nash equilibrium can occur when a player chooses a defensive strategy, forcing their opponent to choose an offensive strategy, which, in turn, leads to a stalemate.

## Key Concepts

---

- **Strategic Interdependence**: The payoff of one player depends on the strategies chosen by all other players.
- **Rational Choice**: Players make decisions based on their own self-interest.
- **Non-Cooperative Game**: Players do not coordinate their actions; each player makes their own decisions.
- **Mixed Strategy**: A strategy that involves randomizing over possible actions.

## Implications

---

The Nash equilibrium has far-reaching implications in various fields, including:

- **Economics**: Nash equilibrium is used to analyze the behavior of firms and individuals in markets.
- **Politics**: Nash equilibrium is used to model the behavior of nations and international organizations.
- **Biology**: Nash equilibrium is used to study the evolution of strategies in biological systems.

## Conclusion

---

The Nash equilibrium is a fundamental concept in game theory that helps us understand how individuals and entities make strategic decisions in situations where multiple parties interact. By analyzing the Nash equilibrium, we can gain insights into the behavior of players in various fields and make more informed decisions.
