# Sub-Game Perfect Equilibrium

### Definitions

- **Sub-game perfect equilibrium**: A concept in game theory that generalizes Nash equilibrium to situations where the game is divided into sub-games.
- **Sub-game**: A subset of the original game, where the payoffs for the players depend on the outcome of the sub-game.

### Key Concepts

- **Nash Equilibrium**: A stable state where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.
- **Sub-game perfect equilibrium (SGPE)**: An extension of Nash equilibrium to situations with sub-games, where the players' strategies are optimal given the outcome of the sub-game.

### Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Nash Equilibrium Theorem**: If every player's best response to the other players' strategies is their own strategy, then the resulting payoff is the Nash equilibrium.
- **Sub-game perfect equilibrium theorem**: If every sub-game has a Nash equilibrium, then the entire game has a sub-game perfect equilibrium.

### Key Points

- **Constructibility**: SGPE can be constructed by finding a Nash equilibrium in each sub-game and then using backward induction to construct the overall equilibrium.
- **Computational complexity**: Finding a SGPE can be computationally expensive, especially for large games with many sub-games.
- **Applications**: SGPE has applications in fields such as economics, politics, and biology, where strategic decision-making is crucial.

### Important Formulas

- **Payoff matrix**: A table that shows the payoffs for each player in each possible outcome of the game.
- **Best response function**: A function that shows the optimal strategy for a player given the strategies of the other players.

### Important Theorems

- **Minimax theorem**: A theorem that states that the minimum payoff of the maximizing player is equal to the maximum payoff of the minimizing player.
- **Prisoner's dilemma**: A classic game that illustrates the concept of SGPE and the trade-offs between cooperation and competition.
