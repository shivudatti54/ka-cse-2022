# Examples: The Prisoner's Dilemma

=====================================

## Introduction

---

The prisoner's dilemma is a fundamental concept in game theory, which studies the strategic interactions between rational decision-makers. This problem was first introduced by Merrill Flood and Melvin Dresher in 1950 and later popularized by Albert Tucker. The dilemma is a simple yet powerful example of how individual rationality can lead to suboptimal outcomes in a situation where cooperation is desirable.

## Historical Context

---

In 1950, Merrill Flood and Melvin Dresher, two engineers at Random Laboratories, developed a game theory model to test the concept of game theory. They created a scenario where two prisoners were arrested and interrogated separately by the police. The prisoners had two options: to confess or remain silent. The payoffs for each option were as follows:

|                               | Prisoner A Confesses   | Prisoner A Remains Silent |
| ----------------------------- | ---------------------- | ------------------------- |
| **Prisoner B Confesses**      | A: 2 years, B: 2 years | A: 10 years, B: 10 years  |
| **Prisoner B Remains Silent** | A: 10 years, B: 1 year | A: 1 year, B: 1 year      |

The game was played many times, and the results showed that, despite both prisoners having an incentive to cooperate (i.e., remain silent), the dominant strategy was to confess. This led to the conclusion that, in the absence of external enforcement, cooperation could not be sustained.

## Analysis

---

The prisoner's dilemma is a classic example of a zero-sum game, where one prisoner's gain is equal to the other prisoner's loss. In this case, if both prisoners confess, they both receive a sentence of 2 years. However, if one prisoner confesses and the other remains silent, the confessor receives a sentence of 10 years, while the silent prisoner receives a sentence of 1 year.

The dominant strategy for both prisoners is to confess, regardless of what the other prisoner chooses. This is because confessing guarantees a sentence of 2 years, which is better than the sentence of 10 years that will be received if one remains silent.

## Equilibrium

---

The equilibrium of the prisoner's dilemma is a state where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged. In this case, the equilibrium is (confess, confess).

## Cooperative Game Theory

---

In cooperative game theory, the prisoner's dilemma is often used to study the concept of cooperation. In a cooperative game, players can form coalitions to achieve a common goal. In the prisoner's dilemma, a coalition of two prisoners can form an agreement to remain silent, which would result in both prisoners receiving a sentence of 1 year.

However, if one prisoner defects from the coalition and confesses, the other prisoner is left with a sentence of 10 years. This makes it difficult for the prisoners to maintain an agreement, as the dominant strategy is still to confess.

## Pareto Optimality

---

The prisoner's dilemma is also a classic example of a Pareto optimality problem. Pareto optimality is a concept in game theory that refers to a situation where no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off.

In the prisoner's dilemma, the optimal outcome is where both prisoners receive a sentence of 2 years. However, there is no way for both prisoners to achieve this outcome, as one prisoner's gain (receiving a sentence of 2 years) is exactly equal to the other prisoner's loss (receiving a sentence of 10 years).
