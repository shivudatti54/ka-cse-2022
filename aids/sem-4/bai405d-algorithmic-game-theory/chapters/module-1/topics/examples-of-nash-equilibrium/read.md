# Examples of Nash Equilibrium

=====================================

## Introduction

---

In game theory, a Nash equilibrium is a state in a game where no player can improve their payoff (or outcome) by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged. This concept was introduced by John Nash in the 1950s and has since become a fundamental concept in game theory.

## Definition of Nash Equilibrium

---

A Nash equilibrium is a pair of strategies, one for each player, such that:

- No player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.
- All players are making the best decision they can given the strategies of the other players.

## Examples of Nash Equilibrium

---

### Example 1: The Prisoner's Dilemma

In the Prisoner's Dilemma, two prisoners are arrested and interrogated separately. Each prisoner has two options: to confess or to remain silent. The payoffs for each possible combination of actions are:

|                               | Prisoner A Confesses | Prisoner A Remains Silent |
| ----------------------------- | -------------------- | ------------------------- |
| **Prisoner B Confesses**      | A: 10, B: 10         | A: 0, B: 10               |
| **Prisoner B Remains Silent** | A: 10, B: 0          | A: 0, B: 0                |

The Nash equilibrium in this game is for both prisoners to confess. If one prisoner confesses and the other remains silent, the silent prisoner can improve their payoff by confessing, and the confessing prisoner can also improve their payoff by remaining silent. Therefore, both prisoners confess, resulting in a suboptimal outcome for both.

### Example 2: The Battle of the Sexes

In the Battle of the Sexes, two people have to choose between attending a baseball game or a music concert. The payoffs for each possible combination of actions are:

|                               | Person 1 Attends Baseball | Person 1 Attends Concert | Person 2 Attends Baseball | Person 2 Attends Concert |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------- | ------------------------ | ------------------------- | ------------------------ |
| **Person 2 Attends Baseball** | A: 10, B: 10              | A: 5, B: 5               | A: 5, B: 5                | A: 5, B: 5               |
| **Person 2 Attends Concert**  | A: 5, B: 5                | A: 0, B: 0               | A: 0, B: 0                | A: 0, B: 0               |

The Nash equilibrium in this game is for both people to attend the concert. If one person attends the baseball game and the other attends the concert, the person attending the concert can improve their payoff by attending the baseball game, and the person attending the baseball game can also improve their payoff by attending the concert. Therefore, both people attend the concert, resulting in a suboptimal outcome for both.

### Example 3: Oligopoly

Consider an oligopoly with two firms, X and Y. The payoffs for each possible combination of prices are:

|                          | Firm X Sets Price $x | Firm X Sets Price $y | Firm Y Sets Price $x | Firm Y Sets Price $y |
| ------------------------ | -------------------- | -------------------- | -------------------- | -------------------- |
| **Firm Y Sets Price $x** | X: 1000, Y: 800      | X: 1200, Y: 600      | X: 900, Y: 700       | X: 600, Y: 1000      |
| **Firm Y Sets Price $y** | X: 900, Y: 700       | X: 600, Y: 1000      | X: 1200, Y: 600      | X: 1000, Y: 800      |

The Nash equilibrium in this game is for both firms to set price $x. If one firm sets price $y, the other firm can improve their payoff by setting the opposite price. Therefore, both firms set price $x, resulting in a competitive outcome.

## Key Concepts

---

- **Nash equilibrium**: a state in a game where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy.
- **Best response**: the best decision a player can make given the strategies of the other players.
- **Mixed strategies**: a strategy that involves randomizing over multiple actions.
- **Pareto optimality**: a state in a game where no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off.

## Conclusion

---

Nash equilibrium is a fundamental concept in game theory that helps us understand how players make decisions in strategic situations. By analyzing examples of Nash equilibrium, we can gain insights into how players can improve their payoffs and make better decisions in games with multiple players.
