# Nash Equilibrium

### Definition

- A Nash equilibrium is a state in a strategic game where no player can improve their payoff (or outcome) by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.
- Introduced by John Nash in 1950.

### Key Formulas and Theorems

- **Nash Equilibrium Condition**: For a game with N players, a Nash equilibrium is a tuple (s1, s2, ..., sN) where every player i satisfies:
  - u(i, s1, s2, ..., sN) ≥ u(i, si', s2, ..., sN) for all si' in Si
  - u(j, s1, s2, ..., sj, ..., sN) ≥ u(j, s1, s2, ..., sj', ..., sN) for all sj' in Sj
- **Pareto Optimality**: A Nash equilibrium is Pareto optimal if there is no other strategy profile that improves the payoff of at least one player without decreasing the payoff of at least one other player.

### Key Examples

- **Prisoner's Dilemma**: Two prisoners have two options: confess or stay silent. The Nash equilibrium is for both prisoners to confess, as this maximizes the payoff for each individual.
- **Chicken Game**: Two drivers approach an intersection and must decide to swerve or stay straight. The Nash equilibrium is for both drivers to swerve, as this reduces the probability of a collision.
- **Oligopoly Game**: A firm in an oligopolistic market must decide to produce more or less. The Nash equilibrium is for the firm to produce at a level that maximizes its profit, assuming the other firms produce at the same level.
- **Blind Men and the Elephant**: A group of blind men are asked to describe an elephant by touching different parts of its body. Each man reaches a different conclusion, but a Nash equilibrium is reached when each man concludes that their portion of the elephant is the whole elephant.

### Important Concepts

- **Strategic Subgame**: A subgame is a smaller game that is part of the larger game.
- **Sequential Rationality**: A player makes a rational decision based on the payoffs of the game at the current stage.
- **Perfect Information**: All players have complete knowledge of the game and its payoffs.
