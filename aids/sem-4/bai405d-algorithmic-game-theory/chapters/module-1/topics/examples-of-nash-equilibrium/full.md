# Examples of Nash Equilibrium

=====================================

## Introduction

---

A Nash equilibrium is a fundamental concept in game theory, introduced by John Nash in 1950. It describes a situation where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged. In this section, we will explore various examples of Nash equilibrium, its applications, and historical context.

## The Prisoner's Dilemma

---

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a classic example of a Nash equilibrium. Two prisoners, A and B, are arrested and interrogated separately by the police. Each prisoner has two options: cooperate (C) and betray (B). The payoff matrix for this game is as follows:

|                                 | Prisoner B stays silent (C)            | Prisoner B betrays (B)                 |
| ------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| **Prisoner A stays silent (C)** | A: 3 years in jail, B: 3 years in jail | A: 1 year in jail, B: 10 years in jail |
| **Prisoner A betrays (B)**      | A: 10 years in jail, B: 1 year in jail | A: 5 years in jail, B: 5 years in jail |

In this game, there are two Nash equilibria:

1.  **Prisoner A stays silent (C) and Prisoner B betrays (B)**: Both prisoners choose the option that maximizes their payoff, given the other prisoner's strategy. Since Prisoner B is betraying and Prisoner A is staying silent, both prisoners get a higher payoff than if they both stayed silent.
2.  **Prisoner A betrays (B) and Prisoner B stays silent (C)**: Both prisoners choose the option that maximizes their payoff, given the other prisoner's strategy. Since Prisoner B is staying silent and Prisoner A is betraying, both prisoners get a higher payoff than if they both stayed silent.

## The Ultimatum Game

---

The Ultimatum Game is another example of a Nash equilibrium. Two players, X and Y, are offered a sum of money, say 100. They are asked to divide the money between them in a way that they like. X makes the first offer, and Y accepts or rejects it. The payoff matrix for this game is as follows:

|                 | Y accepts the offer | Y rejects the offer |
| --------------- | ------------------- | ------------------- |
| **X offers 50** | X: 50, Y: 50        | X: 0, Y: 100        |
| **X offers 20** | X: 20, Y: 80        | X: 0, Y: 0          |

In this game, there are two Nash equilibria:

1.  **X offers 40 and Y accepts**: Both players choose the option that maximizes their payoff, given the other player's strategy. Since X offers 40 and Y accepts, both players get a higher payoff than if they both stayed silent.
2.  **X offers 20 and Y rejects**: Both players choose the option that maximizes their payoff, given the other player's strategy. Since X offers 20 and Y rejects, both players get a higher payoff than if they both stayed silent.

## The Tragedy of the Commons

---

The Tragedy of the Commons is a classic example of a Nash equilibrium in the context of resource management. A group of farmers are allowed to graze their cattle on a common pasture. Each farmer has two options: graze (G) and not graze (N). The payoff matrix for this game is as follows:

|                                  | Farmer N grazes (G)                      | Farmer N does not graze (N)              |
| -------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| **All farmers graze (G)**        | All farmers: 10 cattle, pasture degraded | All farmers: 40 cattle, pasture degraded |
| **All farmers do not graze (N)** | All farmers: 0 cattle, pasture intact    | All farmers: 10 cattle, pasture intact   |

In this game, there is one Nash equilibrium:

1.  **All farmers graze (G)**: All farmers choose the option that maximizes their payoff, given the other farmers' strategies. Since all farmers choose to graze, they each get more cattle, but the pasture is degraded.

## The Hot Potato Game

---

The Hot Potato Game is a game that illustrates the concept of Nash equilibrium in a more subtle way. Three players, A, B, and C, are standing in a circle, holding hands. One player is randomly chosen to be "it." The player who is "it" must pass a ball to one of the other two players, who then become "it." The payoff matrix for this game is as follows:

|                          | Player A passes to B                  | Player A passes to C                  |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| **Player B passes to A** | Player A: 1, Player B: 2, Player C: 0 | Player A: 1, Player B: 0, Player C: 2 |
| **Player B passes to C** | Player A: 2, Player B: 1, Player C: 0 | Player A: 2, Player B: 0, Player C: 1 |
| **Player C passes to A** | Player A: 0, Player B: 2, Player C: 1 | Player A: 1, Player B: 2, Player C: 0 |
| **Player C passes to B** | Player A: 1, Player B: 0, Player C: 2 | Player A: 0, Player B: 1, Player C: 2 |
| **Player C passes to C** | Player A: 2, Player B: 0, Player C: 1 | Player A: 1, Player B: 2, Player C: 0 |

In this game, there are several Nash equilibria, but one of them is:

1.  **Player A passes to B and Player B passes to A**: Both players choose the option that maximizes their payoff, given the other players' strategies. Since Player A passes to B and Player B passes to A, both players get a higher payoff than if they both stayed silent.

## Historical Context

---

The concept of Nash equilibrium was first introduced by John Nash in 1950, in his PhD thesis at Princeton University. Nash's work built upon the earlier research of John von Neumann, who had developed the concept of a "mixed strategy" in the context of game theory. Nash's equilibrium was a major breakthrough in the field of game theory, as it provided a framework for analyzing strategic interactions between multiple players.

## Modern Developments

---

Since the introduction of Nash equilibrium, many researchers have developed new techniques and tools for analyzing strategic interactions. Some of the key developments include:

- **Pareto Optimality**: A concept that describes a situation in which no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off.
- **Sequential Games**: A type of game in which players make decisions one after the other, rather than simultaneously.
- **Evolutionary Game Theory**: A approach that studies how strategies evolve over time in response to changing environmental conditions.

## Applications

---

Nash equilibrium has many applications in a wide range of fields, including:

- **Economics**: Nash equilibrium is used to study the behavior of firms and consumers in oligopolistic markets.
- **Biology**: Nash equilibrium is used to study the evolution of strategies in animal populations.
- **Computer Science**: Nash equilibrium is used to study the behavior of agents in multi-agent systems.
