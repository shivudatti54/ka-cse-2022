# Repeated Games

### Introduction

Repeated games are a fundamental concept in algorithmic game theory, which studies strategic decision-making in competitive environments. In a repeated game, the same set of players and game situation repeats over time, allowing them to learn and adapt to each other's strategies.

### Definition

A repeated game is a sequence of finite games, where each game is played between the same set of players, and the outcome of each game depends on the strategies chosen by the players. The key characteristic of a repeated game is that the game situation repeats over time, allowing players to learn and adjust their strategies.

### Types of Repeated Games

There are several types of repeated games, including:

- **Static repeated games**: In these games, the payoffs are time-invariant, and the game situation remains the same over time.
- **Dynamic repeated games**: In these games, the payoffs are time-varying, and the game situation changes over time.
- **Perfect information games**: In these games, all players have complete information about the game situation and each other's strategies.
- **Imperfect information games**: In these games, players have limited information about the game situation and each other's strategies.

### Equilibrium Concepts

In repeated games, several equilibrium concepts are used to study the behavior of players. These include:

- **Nash Equilibrium**: A Nash equilibrium is a stable state in which no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy.
- **Pareto Optimality**: A Pareto optimal outcome is one in which no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off.
- **Evolutionary stable strategy (ESS)**: An ESS is a strategy that is stable over time, meaning that it will be chosen by players even if they have no prior knowledge of the game.

### Strategies in Repeated Games

Players in repeated games use various strategies to achieve their goals. These include:

- **Pure Strategies**: Pure strategies are actions that a player chooses to take in a given game.
- **Mixed Strategies**: Mixed strategies are random actions that a player chooses to take in a given game.
- **Learning Strategies**: Learning strategies involve players updating their strategies based on their observations of other players' behavior.
- **Adaptive Strategies**: Adaptive strategies involve players adjusting their strategies based on their observations of other players' behavior.

### Examples of Repeated Games

Several examples illustrate the concept of repeated games, including:

- **Prisoner's Dilemma**: The prisoner's dilemma is a classic example of a repeated game, where two prisoners must decide whether to cooperate or defect.
- **Repeated Auctions**: Repeated auctions are a common example of repeated games, where bidders must decide whether to bid in a given auction or wait for a better opportunity.
- **Communication Games**: Communication games are a type of repeated game where players must decide whether to communicate their strategies to each other.

### Learning and Adaptation

Players in repeated games can learn and adapt to each other's strategies over time. This can occur through:

- **Self-Selection**: Players may select their strategies based on their observations of other players' behavior.
- **Learning-by-Doing**: Players may learn their strategies through experience and trial-and-error.
- **Communication**: Players may communicate their strategies to each other to improve their payoffs.

### Conclusion

Repeated games are a fundamental concept in algorithmic game theory, which studies strategic decision-making in competitive environments. Understanding the different types of repeated games, equilibrium concepts, and strategies used by players is essential for analyzing and predicting their behavior in competitive situations.
