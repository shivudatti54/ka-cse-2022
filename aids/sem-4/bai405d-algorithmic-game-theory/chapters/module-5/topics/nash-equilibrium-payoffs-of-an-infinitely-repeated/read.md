# Nash Equilibrium Payoffs of an Infinitely Repeated Prisoner’s Dilemma

### Introduction

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a classic game theory paradox that illustrates the conflict between individual and group rationality. In this study material, we will explore the concept of Nash equilibrium payoffs in the context of an infinitely repeated Prisoner’s dilemma.

### What is the Prisoner's Dilemma?

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a game played between two individuals, often referred to as prisoners, who are given the opportunity to confess or remain silent. The payoffs for each possible combination of actions are as follows:

|                               | Prisoner A confesses    | Prisoner A remains silent |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------- | ------------------------- |
| **Prisoner B confesses**      | A: 3 years, B: 3 years  | A: 10 years, B: 10 years  |
| **Prisoner B remains silent** | A: 10 years, B: 3 years | A: 1 year, B: 1 year      |

### What is Nash Equilibrium?

Nash equilibrium is a concept in game theory that describes a situation where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged. In the context of the Prisoner's Dilemma, the Nash equilibrium is the combination of actions that yields the highest payoff for each player.

### Infinitely Repeated Prisoner’s Dilemma

The infinitely repeated Prisoner’s dilemma is a variation of the original game, where the same game is played repeatedly over an infinite number of periods. This allows players to consider long-term consequences and potentially cooperate with each other to achieve better outcomes.

### Nash Equilibrium Payoffs in the Infinitely Repeated Prisoner’s Dilemma

In the infinitely repeated Prisoner’s dilemma, the Nash equilibrium payoffs are determined by the following game tree:

|                           | Prisoner A defects       | Prisoner A cooperates    |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------ | ------------------------ |
| **Prisoner B defects**    | A: Payoff 1, B: Payoff 1 | A: Payoff 2, B: Payoff 2 |
| **Prisoner B cooperates** | A: Payoff 1, B: Payoff 2 | A: Payoff 2, B: Payoff 2 |

The Nash equilibrium payoffs in this game tree are as follows:

- Prisoner A's Nash equilibrium payoff: (1, 2)
- Prisoner B's Nash equilibrium payoff: (2, 1)

### Key Concepts

- **Stationary Nash Equilibrium**: The combination of actions that yields the highest payoff for each player, assuming no changes in the game or the other player's actions.
- **Time Consistent Strategy**: A strategy that is consistent over time, taking into account the long-term consequences of the game.
- **Reputation**: The expectation of future payoffs based on past behavior.
- **Trust**: The assumption that the other player will cooperate in the future.

### Examples

- **Prisoner A Cooperates, Prisoner B Cooperates**: Both prisoners receive a payoff of 2, indicating a mutually beneficial outcome.
- **Prisoner A Defects, Prisoner B Cooperates**: Prisoner A receives a payoff of 1 and Prisoner B receives a payoff of 2, indicating an asymmetric outcome.
- **Prisoner A Defects, Prisoner B Defects**: Both prisoners receive a payoff of 1, indicating a non-cooperative outcome.

### Conclusion

The Nash equilibrium payoffs in the infinitely repeated Prisoner’s dilemma provide insights into the strategic interactions between individuals. By considering the long-term consequences of the game, players can make informed decisions about their actions, potentially leading to mutually beneficial outcomes. However, the paradoxical nature of the Prisoner’s dilemma also highlights the challenges of cooperation and trust in game theory.
