# Strategies in an Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

### Key Definitions

- **Prisoner's Dilemma**: A game theory scenario where two prisoners must decide whether to cooperate or defect, leading to a suboptimal outcome for both.
- **Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma**: A variant of the Prisoner's Dilemma where the game is repeated infinitely.

### Key Concepts

- **Nash Equilibrium**: A stable state where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming the other player's strategy remains the same.
- **Pareto Optimality**: A state where no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off.
- **Finitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma**: A variant of the Prisoner's Dilemma where the game is repeated a finite number of times.

### Strategies in the Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

- **Tit-for-Tat**: A strategy where a player imitates the opponent's previous action and then switches to a random action.
- **Grudges**: A strategy where a player always defects if the opponent defecting in the previous period.
- **Random Cooperation**: A strategy where a player cooperates with probability 1/2, regardless of the opponent's previous action.

### Formulas and Theorems

- **Nash Equilibrium Theorem**: A stable state where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming the other player's strategy remains the same.
- **Pareto Optimality Theorem**: A state where no player can improve their payoff without making another player worse off.

### Important Results

- **Folk Theorem**: Any strategy profile that is a Nash equilibrium can be sustained in the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma.
- **Nash-Bayes Theorem**: The Nash equilibrium is given by the weighted average of the player's payoffs for all possible strategies.

### Important Research Papers

- **Rapoport and Chammans (1959)**: The original paper introducing the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma.
- **Fudenberg and Tirole (1991)**: A comprehensive analysis of the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, including the Folk Theorem and Nash-Bayes Theorem.
