# Strategies in an Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

## Introduction

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a fundamental game theory concept that has been extensively studied in the fields of economics, politics, and sociology. In its classic form, the dilemma is played between two individuals, often referred to as prisoners, who are presented with a choice: to cooperate or defect. In this paper, we will delve into the strategies employed in an infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, a variant of the game where the same scenario is repeated indefinitely.

## History and Background

The Prisoner's Dilemma was first introduced by Merrill Flood and Melvin Dresher in 1950, as a thought experiment to illustrate the tension between individual and group rationality. The classic version of the game is as follows:

- Two prisoners, A and B, are arrested and interrogated separately by the police.
- Each prisoner has two options: to confess (D) or remain silent (C).
- If both prisoners confess, they each receive a moderate sentence (2 years).
- If one prisoner confesses and the other remains silent, the confessor receives a light sentence (1 year), while the silent prisoner receives a harsh sentence (3 years).
- If both prisoners remain silent, they each receive a light sentence (1 year).

The game is generally analyzed using a payoff matrix, which shows the possible outcomes and the payoffs for each prisoner.

## Infinitely Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

In the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, the game is played repeatedly, with the same scenario and payoffs. This raises the question: what strategy should an individual employ in this repeated game?

## **Pareto-Nash Equilibrium**

In a finite Prisoner's Dilemma, the Nash Equilibrium (NE) is the dominant strategy for both prisoners, where both prisoners defect, resulting in a suboptimal outcome for both. However, in the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, the NE is not necessarily the optimal strategy.

The Pareto-Nash Equilibrium (PNE) is a concept that extends the NE to repeated games. In the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, the PNE is a pair of strategies, (C, C) or (D, D), where both prisoners cooperate or both prisoners defect.

## **Evolutionary Stable Strategy (ESS)**

In a repeated game, the ESS is a strategy that is not immediately dominated by any other strategy, and it will persist in the population over time. In the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, the ESS is the strategy (C, C), where both prisoners cooperate.

## **Evolutionary Game Theory (EGT)**

EGT is a framework for analyzing repeated games, developed by Robert Axelrod in the 1980s. EGT models the evolution of strategies over time, using concepts such as fitness, mutation, and selection.

In the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, EGT predicts that the strategy (C, C) will be the dominant strategy over time, as it is the ESS.

## **Tit-for-Tat (TFT) Strategy**

The Tit-for-Tat (TFT) strategy is a simple, non-cooperative strategy that was first introduced by Robert Axelrod in his book "The Evolution of Cooperation". In the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, TFT works as follows:

- Initially, TFT cooperates (C) on the first move.
- On subsequent moves, TFT imitates the opponent's previous move.
- If the opponent has previously cooperated, TFT cooperates.
- If the opponent has previously defected, TFT defects.

The TFT strategy has been shown to be a stable strategy in the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, as it is not immediately dominated by any other strategy.

## **Other Strategies**

Other strategies that have been proposed for the infinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma include:
