# Repeated Games: The Main Idea

### Introduction

Repeated games are a fundamental concept in game theory and economics, which studies strategic decision making in situations where the outcome depends on the actions of multiple individuals or parties. In this topic, we will delve into the main idea of repeated games, exploring their historical context, key concepts, and applications.

### Historical Context

The concept of repeated games dates back to the 18th century, when economists such as Daniel Bernoulli and Adam Smith studied the behavior of individuals in repeated interactions. However, it wasn't until the 1950s and 1960s that the modern theory of repeated games emerged, thanks to the work of mathematicians and economists such as John Nash, Reinhard Selten, and Robert Aumann.

### Key Concepts

Repeated games can be broadly classified into two categories:

1. **Finite Repeated Games**: In these games, the number of periods is fixed, and the payoffs are determined by a fixed mechanism.
2. **Infinite Repeated Games**: In these games, the number of periods is infinite, and the payoffs are not fixed.

Infinite repeated games are further divided into two subcategories:

1. **Perfectly Repeated Games**: In these games, the players have complete knowledge of the game's rules and payoffs.
2. **Imperfectly Repeated Games**: In these games, the players have incomplete knowledge of the game's rules and payoffs.

### Main Idea: Nash Equilibrium

The main idea of repeated games is to find a stable equilibrium, known as the Nash Equilibrium, where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep their strategies unchanged.

To illustrate this concept, let's consider the following example:

**Example 1: Prisoner's Dilemma**

Two prisoners, A and B, are arrested and interrogated separately. Each prisoner has two options: confess or remain silent. The payoffs for each prisoner are as follows:

|                               | Prisoner A Confesses   | Prisoner A Remains Silent |
| ----------------------------- | ---------------------- | ------------------------- |
| **Prisoner B Confesses**      | A: 2 years, B: 3 years | A: 1 year, B: 1 year      |
| **Prisoner B Remains Silent** | A: 1 year, B: 2 years  | A: 0 years, B: 0 years    |

The Nash Equilibrium in this game is (Confess, Confess), where both prisoners confess, resulting in a suboptimal outcome for both.

### Repeated Games with Multiple Periods

In repeated games with multiple periods, players can choose from a set of strategies, each with its own payoff profile. The goal is to find a strategy that maximizes the payoff over an infinite number of periods.

**Example 2: Cooling Lamp Problem**

A manufacturer produces a light bulb that can be either "good" or "bad". The probability of a good bulb is 0.9, and the probability of a bad bulb is 0.1. The manufacturer offers a discount to the buyer if they buy a good bulb, but the buyer can only purchase one bulb per period.

The payoffs for each period are as follows:

|                  | Good Bulb   | Bad Bulb    |
| ---------------- | ----------- | ----------- |
| \*\*Buyer 1 Good | 1 + (0.9)^t | 1 + (0.1)^t |
| \*\*Buyer 1 Bad  | 1 + (0.9)^t | 1 + (0.1)^t |
| \*\*Buyer 2 Good | 1 + (0.9)^t | 1 + (0.1)^t |
| \*\*Buyer 2 Bad  | 1 + (0.9)^t | 1 + (0.1)^t |

where t is the number of periods.

The Nash Equilibrium in this game is to buy a good bulb every period, as it maximizes the payoff over an infinite number of periods.

Repeated games have numerous applications in various fields, including:
