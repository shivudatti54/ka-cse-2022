### Learning Purpose: The Ultimatum Game

**1. Why is this topic important?**
The ultimatum game is a foundational experiment in behavioral economics and game theory. It is crucial because it starkly demonstrates a major limitation of classic game theory—the assumption of perfectly rational actors—and bridges the gap to more realistic models of human behavior that incorporate fairness, reciprocity, and emotion.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn to analyze a simple sequential bargaining game using backward induction and predict the subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. More importantly, they will study the vast discrepancy between this theoretical prediction and empirical results from human subjects, introducing them to concepts of social preferences and bounded rationality.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This topic connects directly to backward induction and subgame perfection from sequential games. It serves as a critical link to other behavioral game theory models (like the trust game or dictator game) and challenges the purely rational-agent models used in earlier modules, setting the stage for the study of mechanism design and auctions.

**4. Real-world applications**
The principles of the ultimatum game apply to real-world negotiations, corporate fair division problems, political bargaining, and the design of economic mechanisms and platforms where perceived fairness is critical for participant engagement and system stability.
