# Module 5: Continuous Internal Evaluation (CIE)

## Introduction

For  engineering students, the assessment of a subject is not solely based on the final semester examination. A significant portion of the final grade is allocated to Continuous Internal Evaluation (CIE), a process designed to assess a student's performance and understanding **continuously** throughout the semester. In the context of a challenging subject like Algorithmic Game Theory, CIE serves as a crucial tool to ensure students keep pace with complex theoretical concepts and their practical applications, rather than resorting to last-minute cramming.

## Core Concepts of CIE

CIE is a multifaceted assessment system. Its primary objectives are to:
*   **Encourage consistent study habits.**
*   **Provide regular feedback** to students on their understanding.
*   **Reduce the burden and risk** associated with a single final exam.
*   **Assess a broader range of skills** like application, analysis, and problem-solving, which are vital in Algorithmic Game Theory.

The CIE component for a subject is typically worth 50 marks out of the total 100. These 50 marks are divided into two main parts:

### 1. Internal Assessment Tests (IATs)

*   **What it is:** These are formal written examinations conducted by the department during the semester, similar to mini-semester exams but covering a portion of the syllabus.
*   **Structure:** Usually, **two** Internal Assessment Tests are conducted. For a 4-credit subject like Algorithmic Game Theory, each test is often conducted for **50 marks**.
*   **Calculation:** The better score of the two tests is chosen. This score is then scaled down to contribute **20 marks** out of the total 50 CIE marks.
    *   *Example: If a student scores 45/50 in IAT1 and 38/50 in IAT2, the score of 45 is chosen. This is then scaled as (45/50)*20 = 18 marks out of 20.*

### 2. Other Evaluation Components (20 Marks)

The remaining **20 marks** of the CIE are earned through various activities designed to evaluate different skills. The distribution is typically as follows:

*   **Assignments (5 Marks):** Students are given problems or case studies (e.g., "Model the Netflix recommendation system as a game and find its Nash Equilibrium"). This evaluates their ability to apply theoretical concepts to new problems.
*   **Seminars / Presentations (5 Marks):** Students research a advanced or applied topic from Algorithmic Game Theory (e.g., "Mechanism Design in Online Ad Auctions" or "The Price of Anarchy in Traffic Routing") and present it to the class. This assesses research, comprehension, and communication skills.
*   **Quizzes / Case Studies (5 Marks):** Short, surprise quizzes test immediate recall and understanding of recent topics. Case studies involve analyzing real-world scenarios through the lens of game theory.
*   **Laboratory / Practicals (5 Marks):** For a subject like Algorithmic Game Theory, this could involve implementing algorithms to find Nash Equilibria in simple games, simulating auctions, or calculating the Price of Anarchy for a given game matrix. This tests practical coding and simulation skills.

## Why CIE is Critical for Algorithmic Game Theory

Algorithmic Game Theory is built on layered concepts. You cannot understand "Mechanism Design" (Module 4) without a firm grasp of "Nash Equilibrium" (Module 2). CIE acts as a checkpoint:
1.  An IAT after Module 3 ensures you understand the fundamentals of games and equilibria.
2.  A seminar on "Combinatorial Auctions" forces you to delve deeper into a specific application, solidifying your knowledge.
3.  A lab session where you code the Lemke-Howson algorithm makes the abstract concept of finding a Nash equilibrium tangible.

Failure to perform in CIE indicates a misunderstanding of foundational blocks, allowing for course correction long before the final exam.

## Key Points & Summary

*   **Purpose:** CIE is a continuous assessment method to evaluate a student's performance throughout the semester, promoting consistent learning.
*   **Total Weightage:** It constitutes **50 marks** out of the total 100 for a subject.
*   **Components:**
    *   **Internal Assessment Tests (IATs):** 20 marks (Based on the better of two test scores).
    *   **Other Components:** 20 marks (From Assignments, Seminars, Quizzes, and Labs).
*   **Minimum Requirement:**  mandates a minimum of **40% (i.e., 20/50)** in the CIE component to be eligible to appear for the semester end examination.
*   **Benefit for Complex Subjects:** For Algorithmic Game Theory, CIE is invaluable as it breaks down the learning process, provides regular feedback, and assesses both theoretical and practical understanding incrementally.

**Remember:** Your consistent effort in all CIE activities is the most effective strategy to master Algorithmic Game Theory and secure a strong grade.
