### **Learning Purpose: Competitive Games**

**1. Why is this topic important?**
Competitive games model strategic interactions between self-interested agents with conflicting goals. This topic is crucial as it provides the foundational framework for analyzing a vast range of real-world scenarios, from corporate rivalry and auctions to network routing and political campaigns. Understanding these models is key to designing systems and mechanisms that perform well even when participants act selfishly.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn to formally define and analyze zero-sum games using fundamental solution concepts like the Minimax Theorem and Nash Equilibrium. They will develop the ability to compute optimal strategies in these strictly competitive environments and understand the concept of a game’s "value." This includes solving small games analytically and appreciating the computational aspects of finding equilibria in larger ones.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This module builds directly on the core concepts of Nash Equilibrium introduced earlier. It acts as a critical subset and contrast to cooperative and general-sum games, highlighting how outcomes change when cooperation is impossible. It also connects to algorithmic mechanism design, as zero-sum games often represent worst-case scenarios for stability and performance.

**4. Real-world applications**
Applications are widespread in security (e.g., designing patrol schedules), sports (strategic play-calling), financial trading (hedging strategies), and politics (campaign resource allocation). Any domain characterized by direct competition between two parties with perfectly opposing interests can be modeled and better understood through the lens of competitive game theory.
