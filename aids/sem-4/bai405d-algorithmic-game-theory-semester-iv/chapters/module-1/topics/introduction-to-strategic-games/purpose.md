# Learning Purpose: Introduction to Strategic Games

### 1. Importance
This topic is the foundational pillar of algorithmic game theory. It provides the essential language and models (like the normal form) to analyze situations where multiple self-interested agents (players) interact. Understanding these strategic foundations is crucial for modeling and predicting outcomes in complex, interconnected systems, from economics to computer science.

### 2. Learning Outcomes
Students will learn to formally define a strategic game, identify players, strategies, and payoffs. They will master the concept of a **Nash Equilibrium**—a state where no player can benefit by unilaterally changing their strategy—and learn how to compute it in simple games. This equips them to analyze basic strategic interactions mathematically.

### 3. Connection to Other Concepts
This module directly connects to subsequent topics, such as the computational complexity of finding equilibria (Module 2) and extensive-form games (which add sequential moves). It provides the strategic groundwork for analyzing more complex auction mechanisms, network routing games, and other algorithmic settings studied later in the course.

### 4. Real-World Applications
The principles of strategic games are applied ubiquitously: modeling competition between firms (**oligopolies**), designing **auctions** (like those used by Google and the FCC for spectrum rights), analyzing **voting systems**, and even in **networking** (e.g., routing traffic) and **cybersecurity** (e.g., attacker-defender models).
