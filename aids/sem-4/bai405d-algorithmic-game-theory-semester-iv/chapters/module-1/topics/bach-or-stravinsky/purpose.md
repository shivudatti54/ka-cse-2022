### **Learning Purpose: Bach or Stravinsky (BoS)**

**1. Why is this topic important?**
The Bach or Stravinsky (BoS) game is a fundamental model in game theory because it perfectly illustrates a "Battle of the Sexes" scenario—a classic coordination game with conflicting preferences. Unlike zero-sum games, it demonstrates how strategic interaction works when players have both common and competing interests. It is essential for understanding the concepts of multiple Nash Equilibria and the challenge of equilibrium selection in non-cooperative settings.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn to formally represent the BoS game in strategic (normal) form, identifying its two pure-strategy Nash Equilibria. They will analyze the strategic dilemma players face: the desire to coordinate versus the preference for coordinating on a specific outcome. This topic introduces the concept of mixed-strategy Nash Equilibrium and provides a benchmark for analyzing how communication, conventions, or correlation devices can facilitate coordination.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
BoS is a core case study that connects directly to broader concepts like coordination games, Pareto optimality, and equilibrium refinement. It serves as a critical contrast to the Prisoner's Dilemma (highlighting coordination vs. defection) and is a building block for more complex models, such as cheap talk games and correlated equilibria, where players can receive signals to guide their actions.

**4. Real-world applications**
This model has direct applications in analyzing standard-setting in technology (e.g., Blu-ray vs. HD-DVD), coordinating on platform choices (e.g., Mac vs. Windows), network effects, and any situation where parties benefit from coordinating but have different ideals for the coordinated outcome.
