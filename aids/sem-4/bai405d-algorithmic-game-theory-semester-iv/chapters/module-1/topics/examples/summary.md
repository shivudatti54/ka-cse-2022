# Examples in Strategic Games - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Strategic Game**: A mathematical model where players choose actions simultaneously, and payoff depends on all players' choices

- **Payoff Matrix**: A table showing outcomes for each combination of player strategies in normal form games

- **Best Response**: A strategy that yields the highest payoff given what other players have chosen

- **Nash Equilibrium**: A strategy profile where each player's strategy is a best response to what other players are playing

- **Mixed Strategy**: Randomization over pure strategies, used when no pure equilibrium exists

- **Indifference Principle**: In mixed equilibrium, opponent must be indifferent between the pure strategies being mixed

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Cournot Best Response**: q1* = (a - c - q2)/2 for demand P = a - Q and marginal cost c

- **Bertrand Equilibrium**: Price equals marginal cost in homogeneous product markets

- **Mixed Strategy Probability**: For player to mix between strategies, expected payoffs must be equal

## Key Points

- Battle of the Sexes has two pure Nash equilibria and one mixed equilibrium

- Matching Pennies has only mixed strategy equilibrium (no pure equilibrium)

- Stag Hunt illustrates conflict between payoff-dominant and risk-dominant equilibria

- Best response functions represent optimal actions given opponent's strategy choice

- Nash equilibrium requires mutual best responses, not individual optimization alone

- Multiple equilibria require selection criteria beyond game-theoretic analysis

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Confusing best responses with overall optimal strategies—they are conditional on others' choices

- Forgetting that Nash equilibrium applies to the entire strategy profile, not individual choices

- Incorrectly calculating mixed strategy probabilities by not applying indifference conditions properly

- Assuming equilibrium must be unique—many games have multiple equilibria

## Revision Tips

- Practice drawing and analyzing payoff matrices for at least five different game types

- Memorize the steps: identify best responses, find mutual best responses, verify equilibrium conditions

- Work through Cournot and Bertrand models to understand oligopoly applications

- Use theunderline method in exams to clearly show best responses in payoff matrices

- Review how equilibrium outcomes change with different payoff values in the same game structure
