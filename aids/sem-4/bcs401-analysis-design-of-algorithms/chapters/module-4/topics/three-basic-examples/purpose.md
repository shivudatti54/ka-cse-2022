### Learning Purpose: Module 4 - Three Basic Examples

**1. Why is this topic important?**
This topic is foundational because it provides concrete, tangible examples of applying algorithmic analysis and design principles. Moving from abstract theory to specific cases helps solidify understanding and demonstrates how to deconstruct a problem, formulate a solution, and rigorously analyze its efficiency.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn to apply core algorithmic techniques—such as iterative refinement, recursion, and combinatorial enumeration—to three classic problems (e.g., sorting, searching, or combinatorial problems like generating permutations). They will practice calculating the time and space complexity of each solution, comparing brute-force approaches with more efficient strategies.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
These examples serve as a bridge. They directly utilize earlier concepts like asymptotic notation and recursion (Module 2 & 3) and act as building blocks for more advanced data structures and complex algorithms (e.g., dynamic programming, greedy algorithms) covered in subsequent modules. Mastery here is essential for tackling larger-scale design problems.

**4. Real-world applications**
The principles learned are ubiquitous. Sorting algorithms organize vast datasets for search engines and databases. Efficient searching is critical for information retrieval. Combinatorial algorithms are used in logistics (route planning), bioinformatics (DNA sequencing), and scheduling (task assignment), making this a crucial skill for solving real-world computational problems.
