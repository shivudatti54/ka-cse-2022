# (8 hours)

## Introduction

### Historical Context

The concept of an 8-hour workday has its roots in the Industrial Revolution. In the late 18th and early 19th centuries, workers in the textile industry in England, known as the "Factory System," worked long hours in poor conditions. In 1847, the British government passed the Ten Hours Act, which limited the workday for women to 10 hours. However, it wasn't until 1914 that the 8-hour workday became the standard for most industries in the United States.

### Modern Developments

In recent years, there has been a growing movement to reevaluate the 8-hour workday. With the rise of remote work and the gig economy, many people are working longer hours and experiencing burnout. Some companies, such as Microsoft and Amazon, have experimented with shorter workweeks, with varying degrees of success.

### Definition

The 8-hour workday refers to the standard workday length of 8 hours, which is typically divided into two 4-hour shifts. This has become the norm in many industries, including manufacturing, finance, and healthcare.

## Compactness

### Definition

Compactness refers to the property of a set or space that is "small" or "bounded." In the context of metric spaces, compactness is an important concept that helps us understand the behavior of functions and sequences.

### Types of Compactness

There are several types of compactness, including:

- **Complete Compactness**: A metric space is compact if it is complete and totally bounded.
- **Total Boundedness**: A metric space is totally bounded if for every point in the space, there exists a finite number of points in the space such that the distance between the point and at least one of these points is less than a given positive number.
- **Boundedness**: A metric space is bounded if there exists a positive number such that the distance between any two points in the space is less than this number.

### Properties of Compactness

Some important properties of compactness include:

- **Compactness Implies Boundedness**: If a metric space is compact, then it is bounded.
- **Compactness Implies Total Boundedness**: If a metric space is compact, then it is totally bounded.
- **Compactness Implies Complete**: If a metric space is compact, then it is complete.

### Example 1: Compactness in a Closed Interval
