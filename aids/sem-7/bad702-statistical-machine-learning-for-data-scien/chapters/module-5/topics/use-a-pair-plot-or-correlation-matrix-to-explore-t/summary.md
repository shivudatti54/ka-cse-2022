# **Use a Pair Plot or Correlation Matrix to Explore Relationships between Variables**

### Key Points

- **Pair Plots**:
  - Visual representation of relationships between multiple pairs of variables
  - Helps identify correlations, outliers, and patterns in data
  - Can be used to check for linearity, non-linearity, and non-normality of relationships
- **Correlation Matrix**:
  - Square table showing correlation coefficients between all pairs of variables
  - Measures strength and direction of linear relationships between variables
  - Assesses assumptions for linear regression and other models

### Important Formulas and Definitions

- **Correlation Coefficient (r)**: Measures strength and direction of linear relationship between two variables: $-1 \leq r \leq 1$
- **Pearson's Correlation Coefficient**: Measures linear relationship between two continuous variables: $r = \frac{\sum{(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}}{\sqrt{\sum{(x_i - \bar{x})^2}\sum{(y_i - \bar{y})^2}}}$
- **Spearman's Correlation Coefficient**: Measures monotonic relationship between two variables (e.g., ranks)

### Theorems and Concepts

- **Covariance Matrix**: Measures spread and covariance between multiple variables
- **Independence Assumption**: Assumption that variables are uncorrelated, which is often violated in real-world data
- **Normality Assumption**: Assumption that relationships between variables are linear and normally distributed

### Tips for Revision

- Practice creating pair plots and correlation matrices to identify relationships between variables
- Understand the limitations of correlation coefficient (e.g., assumes linearity and normality)
- Review the assumptions for linear regression and other models to ensure independence and normality
