## **Symbolic Reasoning under Uncertainty and Statistical Reasoning**

## **7.1 Introduction**

In this chapter, we will explore two fundamental aspects of artificial intelligence: symbolic reasoning under uncertainty and statistical reasoning. These concepts are crucial in designing intelligent systems that can make decisions and draw conclusions from incomplete or uncertain information.

## **7.2 Symbolic Reasoning under Uncertainty**

Symbolic reasoning under uncertainty involves using logical rules and mathematical techniques to reason about uncertain information. This approach is based on the idea that the world is made up of symbols, such as words and numbers, that can be combined using logical operators to represent complex concepts.

**Key Concepts:**

- **Uncertainty**: the inability to predict the exact value of a probability distribution
- **Probabilistic Reasoning**: using probability theory to reason about uncertain information
- **Probabilistic Graphical Models**: graphical models that represent conditional dependencies between variables
- **Bayes' Theorem**: a mathematical formula for updating probability estimates based on new evidence

## **7.2.1 Probabilistic Reasoning**

Probabilistic reasoning involves using probability theory to reason about uncertain information. This approach is based on the idea that each possible outcome of an event has a certain probability of occurring.

**Example:**

Suppose we have a coin that is fair, meaning that the probability of heads is 0.5 and the probability of tails is 0.5. If we flip the coin 10 times, we can use probabilistic reasoning to estimate the probability of getting heads or tails.

## **7.2.2 Probabilistic Graphical Models**

Probabilistic graphical models are graphical models that represent conditional dependencies between variables. These models are useful for modeling complex relationships between variables and making predictions.

**Example:**

Suppose we have a dataset of customers who purchased either a car or a bike. We can use a probabilistic graphical model to represent the relationships between the variables. For example, we can model the relationship between the probability of purchasing a car and the probability of purchasing a bike.

## **7.3 Statistical Reasoning**

Statistical reasoning involves using statistical techniques to reason about data. This approach is based on the idea that data can be used to make informed decisions.

**Key Concepts:**

- **Statistical Hypothesis Testing**: testing a hypothesis about a population based on a sample of data
- **Regression Analysis**: modeling the relationship between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables
- **Clustering Analysis**: grouping similar data points together based on their characteristics

## **7.3.1 Statistical Hypothesis Testing**

Statistical hypothesis testing involves testing a hypothesis about a population based on a sample of data. This approach is useful for making inferences about a population based on a limited amount of data.

**Example:**

Suppose we want to test the hypothesis that the average height of adults in a population is 175 cm. We can collect a sample of data and use statistical hypothesis testing to determine whether the sample data supports or rejects the hypothesis.

## **7.3.2 Regression Analysis**

Regression analysis involves modeling the relationship between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables. This approach is useful for predicting the value of a dependent variable based on the values of one or more independent variables.

**Example:**

Suppose we want to predict the price of a house based on its size and number of bedrooms. We can use regression analysis to model the relationship between the dependent variable (price) and the independent variables (size and bedrooms).

## **7.3.3 Clustering Analysis**

Clustering analysis involves grouping similar data points together based on their characteristics. This approach is useful for identifying patterns and relationships in data.

**Example:**

Suppose we have a dataset of customers who purchased either a car or a bike. We can use clustering analysis to group customers based on their purchase history and demographic characteristics.

## **Conclusion**

In this chapter, we have explored two fundamental aspects of artificial intelligence: symbolic reasoning under uncertainty and statistical reasoning. These concepts are crucial in designing intelligent systems that can make decisions and draw conclusions from incomplete or uncertain information. By understanding these concepts, we can develop more effective and efficient artificial intelligence systems that can solve complex problems in a variety of domains.
