## **7.1 Historical Context of Artificial Intelligence**

The concept of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has been around for decades. The term "Artificial Intelligence" was coined in 1956 by John McCarthy, a computer scientist and cognitive scientist, at the Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence. This project brought together experts in computer science, cognitive psychology, and philosophy to explore the possibilities of creating machines that could simulate human intelligence.

In the early 1950s, the development of the first electronic computers led to a surge in interest in creating machines that could think and learn like humans. The Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence marked the beginning of a new era in AI research, which focused on developing algorithms and techniques that could enable machines to reason, learn, and problem-solve under uncertainty.

## **7.2 Symbolic Reasoning under Uncertainty**

Symbolic reasoning is a type of reasoning that involves using symbols, such as numbers, letters, and operators, to represent and manipulate knowledge. In the context of AI, symbolic reasoning is used to represent and reason about uncertainty. There are several approaches to symbolic reasoning under uncertainty, including:

- **Rule-based systems**: These systems use a set of rules to reason about a domain. Each rule is a conditional statement that specifies a set of inputs and a set of outputs.
- **Expert systems**: These systems are designed to mimic the decision-making abilities of a human expert in a particular domain. They use a set of rules and a knowledge base to reason about a domain.
- **First-order logic**: This is a formal system for reasoning about statements that can be expressed using first-order logic. It is widely used in AI and computer science.

## **7.3 Statistical Reasoning**

Statistical reasoning is a type of reasoning that involves using statistical methods to reason about data. In the context of AI, statistical reasoning is used to reason about uncertainty and make predictions about future outcomes. There are several approaches to statistical reasoning, including:

- **Bayesian inference**: This is a statistical method for updating probabilities based on new evidence. It is widely used in AI and computer science.
- **Decision theory**: This is a branch of mathematics that deals with decision-making under uncertainty. It provides a framework for making decisions by evaluating the expected outcomes of different actions.
- **Machine learning**: This is a type of algorithm that enables machines to learn from data and make predictions about future outcomes.

## **7.4 Applications of Symbolic Reasoning under Uncertainty and Statistical Reasoning**

Symbolic reasoning under uncertainty and statistical reasoning have numerous applications in AI, including:

- **Natural language processing**: These techniques are used to reason about language and make predictions about text and speech.
- **Computer vision**: These techniques are used to reason about images and make predictions about objects and scenes.
- **Robotics**: These techniques are used to reason about the world and make predictions about the behavior of robots.
- **Healthcare**: These techniques are used to reason about patient data and make predictions about disease diagnosis and treatment.

## **7.5 Case Study: Medical Diagnosis using Symbolic Reasoning under Uncertainty**

A classic example of symbolic reasoning under uncertainty is the medical diagnosis problem. Suppose we have a patient who presents with symptoms of a respiratory infection. We want to use a symbolic reasoning system to diagnose the patient's condition. We can use a rule-based system to reason about the patient's symptoms and determine the most likely diagnosis.

For example, we can use the following rule:

"If the patient has a fever and cough, and the patient has been in contact with someone who has a respiratory infection, then the patient is likely to have a respiratory infection."

We can represent this rule using a first-order logic statement:

"If (Fever ∧ Cough) ∧ (Contact ∧ Infection), then Respiratory Infection"

We can then use this rule to reason about the patient's symptoms and make a diagnosis.
