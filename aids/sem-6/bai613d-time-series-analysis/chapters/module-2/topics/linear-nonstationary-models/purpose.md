### Module 2: Linear Nonstationary Models

**1. Why is this topic important?**
Linear nonstationary models are foundational because most real-world time series data (e.g., stock prices, economic indicators) exhibit nonstationary behavior like trends or random walks. Analyzing such data with stationary assumptions leads to spurious results and poor forecasts. This topic provides the essential tools to model, transform, and extract meaningful insights from this ubiquitous class of data.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn to identify nonstationary processes through unit root testing (e.g., Dickey-Fuller test). They will master the core techniques for modeling them, including differencing to achieve stationarity and building Integrated (ARIMA) models. The module covers model identification, parameter estimation, and diagnostics to ensure robust model fitting.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This module is a direct extension of the stationary ARMA models from Module 1. It introduces the integral "I" component, forming the comprehensive ARIMA framework. Mastery of nonstationary models is a prerequisite for advanced topics like volatility modeling (ARCH/GARCH) and multivariate cointegration analysis, which explore long-run equilibrium relationships between nonstationary variables.

**4. Real-world applications**
These models are industry standards for forecasting in finance (predicting asset prices), economics (forecasting GDP, unemployment rates), supply chain management (demand forecasting), and any field where data shows clear trends or stochastic drift.
