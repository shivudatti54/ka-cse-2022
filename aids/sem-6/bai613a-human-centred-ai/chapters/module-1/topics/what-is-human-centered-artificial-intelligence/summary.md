# Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- HUMAN-CENTERED ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE (HCAI): An AI development approach that prioritizes human needs, values, and well-being throughout the entire AI lifecycle—from design to deployment and monitoring.

- HUMAN-IN-THE-LOOP (HITL): An AI architecture where humans participate in the decision-making process at key stages, ensuring human oversight and the ability to override AI recommendations.

- EXPLAINABLE AI (XAI): Techniques and methods that make AI system decisions interpretable to humans, including local explanations, global explanations, and counterfactual explanations.

- FAIRNESS, ACCOUNTABILITY, TRANSPARENCY (FAT): The three pillars of ethical AI that HCAI aims to achieve.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

HCAI does not rely primarily on formulas but on principles and frameworks:

- Human Control: AI systems should maintain human agency in consequential decisions
- Transparency: Users should understand when AI is used and how decisions are made
- Fairness: AI should avoid discrimination and ensure equitable treatment across groups
- Accountability: Clear responsibility must be assigned for AI system outcomes

## Key Points

- HCAI represents a paradigm shift from purely performance-focused AI to human-needs-focused design
- The core principles include human control, explainability, fairness, privacy protection, and accessibility
- Human-in-the-loop systems ensure that AI augments rather than replaces human judgment
- Explainable AI addresses the "black box" problem in complex machine learning models
- Fairness in AI is complex because different mathematical definitions can conflict
- Trust calibration is essential—HCAI aims for appropriate trust levels, avoiding both undertrust and overtrust
- HCAI applies to the entire AI lifecycle, not just the technical development phase

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Confusing HCAI with making AI "friendly" or "human-like"—HCAI is about human needs, not simulating humans.

2. Assuming more human involvement is always better—the appropriate level depends on context and stakes.

3. Believing fairness can be achieved purely through technical solutions—fairness involves value judgments beyond mathematics.

4. Treating explainability and accuracy as always compatible—more interpretable models sometimes sacrifice performance.

## Revision Tips

1. Focus on understanding the core principles rather than memorizing definitions—examiners value explanation over recitation.

2. Practice applying HCAI concepts to real-world scenarios, as application-based questions are common.

3. Remember that HCAI is an interdisciplinary field—think about ethical, social, and technical dimensions.

4. Review the trade-offs in HCAI (e.g., explainability vs. accuracy, fairness metrics conflicts) as these make good exam discussion points.
