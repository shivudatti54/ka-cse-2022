### Revision Notes: In the Same Category? - Automation, AI, and Robots and Unemployment

#### Introduction

- Definition: The question of whether automation, AI, and robots will lead to widespread unemployment
- Key concept: Human-centred AI vs. job displacement

#### Chapter 1: The Automation Paradox

- Definition: The idea that automation and AI will displace jobs, but not necessarily lead to widespread unemployment
- Key points:
  - Job displacement is a complex issue, influenced by factors like industry, skill level, and education
  - Automation can create new job opportunities in fields like AI development and maintenance
  - The concept of the "lump of labour" assumes that there is a fixed number of jobs available

#### Chapter 3: Theories of Unemployment

- Definition: Theories that attempt to explain the causes and consequences of unemployment
- Key points:
  - **The Classical Theory**: Unemployment is due to frictional unemployment (searching for a job) and structural unemployment (labor market mismatch)
  - **The Keynesian Theory**: Unemployment is due to aggregate demand shortages
  - **The Monetarist Theory**: Unemployment is due to money supply and monetary policy

#### Chapter 4: The Impact of Automation, AI, and Robots

- Definition: The effects of automation, AI, and robots on employment
- Key points:
  - **Automation bias**: The tendency to underestimate the impact of automation on employment
  - **Job displacement**: The replacement of human workers by machines and AI
  - **New job creation**: The creation of new job opportunities in fields like AI development and maintenance
  - **Education and training**: The need for workers to acquire new skills to remain employable in an automated economy

#### Important Formulas and Theorems:

- **Lump of labour theorem**: A^_ = L, where A^_ is the optimal amount of labor and L is the fixed amount of labor
- **Marginal product of labor**: MP_L = (W - R)^T, where W is wage, R is rent, and T is the marginal product of labor
