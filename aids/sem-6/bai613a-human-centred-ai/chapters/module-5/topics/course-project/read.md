# Module 5: Course Project – Applying Human-Centred AI Principles

## 1. Introduction

Welcome to the capstone module of our Human-Centred AI course. Until now, we have explored the theoretical frameworks, ethical guidelines, and design methodologies that underpin the creation of AI systems that augment human capabilities rather than replace them. This module translates that knowledge into practice. The **Course Project** is your opportunity to synthesize these concepts by designing, and potentially prototyping, a solution to a real-world problem using a Human-Centred AI (HCAI) approach. This is not just about building a model with high accuracy; it's about building a system that is **beneficial, usable, trustworthy, and understandable** for its human users.

## 2. Core Concepts: The Project Lifecycle

A successful HCAI project follows an iterative lifecycle that deeply integrates human stakeholders at every stage. Your project should be structured around the following phases:

### Phase 1: Problem Identification & Stakeholder Analysis
*   **Objective:** Move beyond a technical challenge to a human-centric problem statement.
*   **Action:** Identify a specific problem domain (e.g., healthcare diagnostics, smart agriculture, educational tools, assistive technology).
*   **Core HCAI Task:** Conduct a thorough **stakeholder analysis**. Who are the primary users (e.g., doctors, farmers, students)? Who are the secondary stakeholders (e.g., patients, policymakers)? What are their goals, needs, and potential fears regarding an AI system? Create user personas to keep these human perspectives central to your design.

### Phase 2: Human-Centred Design & Data Scoping
*   **Objective:** Define the system's requirements from a human-in-the-loop perspective.
*   **Action:** Decide on the AI's role. Will it be an assistant, a guide, or an autonomous actor? How will control be shared between the human and the AI (e.g., shared autonomy, vigilant collaboration)?
*   **Core HCAI Task:** Focus on **data ethics and fairness**. Where will your data come from? Could it contain biases that harm certain user groups? How will you ensure the dataset is representative of your diverse stakeholders? Plan for data collection and annotation that respects privacy and consent.

### Phase 3: Model Development & Prototyping
*   **Objective:** Build a model that balances performance with human-centric values.
*   **Action:** Select an appropriate algorithm (e.g., a decision tree for its interpretability or a neural network for complex patterns). Train and validate your model.
*   **Core HCAI Task:** Prioritize **Explainability & Transparency (XAI)**. Can your model explain its reasoning? For a medical app, this might mean providing confidence scores and highlighting the data features that led to a diagnosis. Choose or design your model with interpretability in mind from the start.

### Phase 4: Evaluation & Iteration
*   **Objective:** Test the system's efficacy and its impact on the human user.
*   **Action:** Develop a functional prototype (a simple UI mockup or a working script).
*   **Core HCAI Task:** Conduct **human-in-the-loop testing**. Do not just measure accuracy (F1-score, etc.). Measure **usability**:
    *   **Trust:** Does the user trust the AI's suggestions?
    *   **Understanding:** Can the user comprehend why the AI made a certain decision?
    *   **Agency:** Does the user feel in control?
    Gather feedback through surveys, interviews, and observation, then iterate on your design.

## 3. Example: An HCAI Project for Agriculture

*   **Problem:** Farmers need to optimize water usage but lack simple tools.
*   **Stakeholders:** Farmers (primary), agricultural experts, water regulatory bodies.
*   **HCAI Solution:** An app that recommends irrigation schedules.
*   **HCAI in Action:**
    *   **Design:** The AI doesn't auto-irrigate; it provides a recommendation ("70% chance your field needs water. Click to irrigate?"). The human maintains final control.
    *   **Explainability:** The app explains: "This recommendation is based on soil moisture sensor data from the north quadrant and the lack of rain forecast for the next 48 hours."
    *   **Feedback:** The farmer can provide feedback ("I irrigated, but it was too much"), which is used to retrain and improve the model.

## 4. Key Points & Summary

| Key Principle | Project Application |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Human Agency** | Design for **augmentation**, not replacement. Keep the user in control of critical decisions. |
| **Transparency** | Build **explainability (XAI)** into your model. Users must understand the "why" behind outputs. |
| **Fairness** | Audit your **data and model** for bias to ensure equitable outcomes across user groups. |
| **Evaluation** | Measure **usability and trust** alongside technical metrics like accuracy and precision. |
| **Iteration** | Treat the project as a cycle. **Continuous feedback** from users is essential for refinement. |

**Summary:** The goal of this course project is to demonstrate that you can engineer an AI system that is not only functionally effective but also socially responsible. By meticulously following an HCAI lifecycle—centering on stakeholders, designing for collaboration, prioritizing explainability, and rigorously testing with humans—you will create a project that showcases the true potential of AI as a tool for human empowerment.
