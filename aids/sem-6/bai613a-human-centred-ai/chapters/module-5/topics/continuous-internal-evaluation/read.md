# Module 5: Continuous Internal Evaluation in Human-Centred AI

## Introduction

In traditional education, learning is often assessed through a single high-stakes end-of-semester examination. This approach can be stressful, may not capture a student's full understanding, and provides feedback too late for meaningful correction. **Continuous Internal Evaluation (CIE)** is a pedagogical alternative designed to address these shortcomings. In the context of a complex and evolving field like **Human-Centred AI (HCAI)**, CIE is not just an evaluation method but a crucial tool for fostering the iterative, reflective, and user-focused mindset that the discipline demands. This module explores the role, components, and benefits of CIE in an HCAI curriculum.

## Core Concepts of CIE

CIE is a process of periodically assessing a student's learning progress throughout a semester. It moves away from the "one-shot" exam model to a more holistic, formative, and continuous process. The core objectives are:
*   **Formative Assessment:** To provide ongoing feedback that helps students identify their strengths and weaknesses *during* the learning process, allowing them to improve.
*   **Reducing Exam Stress:** To distribute the assessment load over multiple, smaller components, reducing the pressure associated with a single final exam.
*   **Holistic Development:** To evaluate a broader range of skills—such as critical thinking, collaboration, practical application, and communication—that are essential in engineering but difficult to test in a written exam.

## Components of CIE in an HCAI Course

For a subject like Human-Centred AI, which blends technical theory with humanistic principles and practical design, a well-structured CIE scheme is vital. It typically consists of several components:

**1. Theory-based Assessments:**
*   **Quizzes & Mid-Term Tests (MTEs):** These are typically written exams focused on specific modules. For example, a quiz on "AI Fairness Metrics" or an MTE covering "Participatory Design Methods." They ensure students grasp the fundamental concepts and theoretical underpinnings.

**2. Practical & Application-Based Assessments:**
*   **Assignments:** These are take-home tasks that require deeper research and analysis. An example could be: *"Critically analyze a real-world AI application (e.g., a resume screening tool) for potential bias and propose a mitigation strategy using the concepts learned in class."*
*   **Case Studies:** Students analyze documented HCAI successes or failures (e.g., the ethical implications of a chatbot, the user experience of a recommendation system). This develops their ability to apply theoretical knowledge to real-world scenarios.
*   **Mini-Projects:** Small-scale, hands-on projects are the heart of HCAI CIE. A typical mini-project might be: *"Design a low-fidelity prototype for an AI-powered assistive technology for the elderly. Conduct a simple usability test with at least one user and write a report on your findings."* This directly mirrors the iterative design-evaluate-build cycle central to HCAI.

**3. Collaborative & Communication Assessments:**
*   **Seminars / Presentations:** Students or groups research an HCAI topic (e.g., "Explainable AI in Healthcare") and present their findings. This assesses their ability to understand, synthesize, and communicate complex ideas—a key skill for any AI engineer working in multidisciplinary teams.
*   **Group Discussions:** Facilitated discussions on ethical dilemmas in AI (e.g., "The Trade-off between Privacy and Personalization") help evaluate critical thinking and the ability to articulate and defend a viewpoint.

## The HCAI Feedback Loop: CIE as a Mirror to the Design Process

The most significant alignment between CIE and HCAI is the principle of **iteration based on feedback**. In HCAI, we build systems, test them with users, gather feedback, and refine the design. CIE replicates this loop in the learning environment:
1.  **Learn a Concept:** E.g., the "Value Sensitive Design" framework.
2.  **Apply it (Task):** Complete an assignment to apply the framework to a scenario.
3.  **Receive Feedback (Evaluation):** Get graded feedback from the instructor on the application.
4.  **Iterate and Improve:** Use that feedback to perform better on the next task or the final project.

This process ensures that learning is active and adaptive, much like developing a human-centred AI system itself.

## Key Points and Summary

*   **Purpose:** CIE is a formative, continuous assessment method designed to evaluate and enhance learning throughout the semester, not just at the end.
*   **Components:** It includes a mix of theoretical tests (quizzes, MTEs), practical applications (assignments, mini-projects), and collaborative exercises (seminars, discussions).
*   **Relevance to HCAI:** The iterative nature of CIE directly mirrors the core HCAI principle of building, testing with users, and refining. It fosters the practical, reflective, and ethical mindset required to design AI for humans.
*   **Benefits:**
    *   Provides timely feedback for improvement.
    *   Reduces reliance on a single final exam.
    *   Assesses a wider range of skills crucial for an HCAI professional.
    *   Encourages consistent engagement with the course material.
*   **For the Student:** Engage actively with every CIE component. View feedback not as criticism but as essential data to iterate and improve your own understanding—treat your learning like an HCAI project.
