### Learning Purpose: Module 4 - Currency (ETH and ETC)

**1. Why is this topic important?**
Understanding Ethereum's native currencies, ETH and ETC, is fundamental because they are the lifeblood of their respective networks. They are not just digital cash but are required to pay for transaction fees and computational services (gas), secure the network, and govern its future. The split that created Ethereum Classic (ETC) offers a critical case study in blockchain immutability and how communities respond to protocol-level crises.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn the distinct functional purposes of ETH as the currency of the primary Ethereum network and ETC as the currency of the classic chain. This includes their roles in powering smart contracts, staking (for ETH), and mining. They will analyze the historical "DAO hack" event that caused the hard fork, comparing the philosophical and technical differences between the two chains.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This topic directly builds upon prior knowledge of blockchain fundamentals (Module 1), consensus mechanisms like Proof-of-Work and Proof-of-Stake (Module 2), and smart contracts (Module 3). It provides a concrete application for these concepts and sets the stage for exploring Decentralized Applications (DApps) and Decentralized Finance (DeFi) in subsequent modules, which are built primarily on the Ethereum network.

**4. Real-world applications**
ETH is the second-largest cryptocurrency by market cap and is essential for interacting with the vast majority of DeFi protocols, NFT marketplaces, and Web3 applications. Analyzing ETC provides insights into the valuation of "code-is-law" immutability and the resilience of a blockchain network. This knowledge is crucial for developers, investors, and anyone engaging with the Ethereum ecosystem.
