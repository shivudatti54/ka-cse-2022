### Requirement Modeling Strategies: Flow-Oriented Modeling

#### Key Points

- **Definition:** Flow-oriented modeling is a technique used to model business processes and requirements.
- **Key Characteristics:**
  - Focuses on the flow of activities and tasks within a system.
  - Emphasizes the relationships between different components and processes.
  - Typically used for business process re-engineering and requirements elicitation.
- **Flow-Oriented Modeling Techniques:**
  - Business process modeling (e.g., BPMN, UML Activity diagrams)
  - Value stream mapping
  - swimlane diagrams
- **Benefits:**
  - Improves understanding of business processes and requirements.
  - Enhances collaboration among stakeholders.
  - Provides a visual representation of the system's flow.
- **Important Formulas and Definitions:**
  - **Value:** The value of a process or activity is the difference between its output and input.
  - **Throughput:** The rate at which a process or system can produce output.
  - **Cycle Time:** The time it takes for a process or activity to complete.
  - **Lead Time:** The time it takes for a process or activity to complete and for the output to become available.

#### Theorems and Concepts

- **The 80/20 Rule:** 80% of the output comes from 20% of the inputs.
- **The Law of Diminishing Returns:** The rate of return on effort decreases as the size of the project increases.

#### Important Theorems

- **The Theory of Constraints (TOC):** A methodology for managing complex systems by identifying and optimizing the constraints that limit performance.
- **The Six Sigma Methodology:** A data-driven approach to quality management that aims to reduce defects and variations in processes.

#### Revision Tips

- Focus on understanding the key characteristics and techniques of flow-oriented modeling.
- Practice creating flow diagrams and value stream maps to improve your skills.
- Review the important formulas, definitions, and theorems to reinforce your knowledge.
