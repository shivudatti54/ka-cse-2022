# Learning Purpose: Defining Features of Data Warehousing

**1. Why is this topic important?**
Understanding the defining features is crucial because they form the foundational architecture of any data warehousing system. These features distinguish a data warehouse from traditional operational databases and dictate how data is integrated, stored, and accessed. Grasping these core principles is the first step in designing an effective system that delivers reliable, consistent, and business-critical information for strategic decision-making.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn the key characteristics that define a data warehouse, primarily summarized by Bill Inmon’s concepts: **Subject-Oriented, Integrated, Time-Variant, and Non-Volatile**. They will explore what each term means in a practical context—for example, how data is organized around business subjects like "sales" rather than applications, and how historical data is maintained for analysis.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This topic provides the essential vocabulary and conceptual framework for the entire module. It directly connects to subsequent concepts like **ETL processes** (which enable integration), **data marts** (subject-oriented subsets), and **OLAP** (which leverages time-variant data for multidimensional analysis). It is the cornerstone upon which more complex architectures like Kimball’s dimensional modeling are built.

**4. Real-world applications**
These defining features are applied whenever a business builds a central reporting system. For instance, a retail chain integrates data from point-of-sale, online, and inventory systems (Integrated) to analyze sales trends (Subject-Oriented & Time-Variant) over years without altering historical records (Non-Volatile), enabling executives to make informed decisions about stock and promotions.
