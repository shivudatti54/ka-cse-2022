# Chap-2 (2.2) Image Formation

## 2.2.1 Introduction to Image Formation

Computer vision is a field of study that deals with the interaction between computers and visual information. Image formation is a fundamental concept in computer vision, as it involves the process of creating a digital representation of the visual world.

In this chapter, we will explore the history of image formation, the different types of image sensors, and the various techniques used to capture and process images.

## 2.2.2 Historical Context of Image Formation

The concept of image formation dates back to the early days of photography. In 1826, Joseph Nicéphore Niépce invented the first permanent photograph, which was captured using a camera obscura. The camera obscura is a device that projects an inverted image of the outside world onto a surface inside the device.

Over the years, the technology for image formation evolved, and new types of image sensors were developed. In the early 20th century, the first electronic image sensors were invented, which used vacuum tubes to capture images. These early electronic image sensors were used in applications such as security cameras and video cameras.

## 2.2.3 Types of Image Sensors

There are several types of image sensors that are commonly used in computer vision applications. These include:

### 2.2.3.1 Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor (CMOS) Image Sensors

CMOS image sensors are the most widely used type of image sensor. They use a complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) technology to capture images. CMOS image sensors are known for their high resolution, low power consumption, and low cost.

### 2.2.3.2 Charge-Coupled Device (CCD) Image Sensors

CCD image sensors are another type of image sensor that uses a charge-coupled device (CCD) technology to capture images. CCD image sensors are known for their high resolution and low noise, but they are also more expensive than CMOS image sensors.

### 2.2.3.3 Complementary Image Sensors

Complementary image sensors are a type of image sensor that uses a combination of CMOS and CCD technology to capture images. They offer the benefits of both CMOS and CCD image sensors, including high resolution and low power consumption.

## 2.2.4 Image Formation Techniques

There are several techniques used to capture and process images. These include:

### 2.2.4.1 Illumination

Illumination is the process of exposing an image sensor to light. The type of illumination used can affect the quality of the image.

### 2.2.4.2 Exposure

Exposure is the process of adjusting the amount of light that reaches the image sensor. The exposure time, aperture, and gain can be adjusted to control the amount of light that reaches the image sensor.

### 2.2.4.3 Focus

Focus is the process of adjusting the distance between the image sensor and the object being imaged. The focus distance can be adjusted using a lens or a camera's autofocus mechanism.

### 2.2.4.4 Image Processing

Image processing is the process of enhancing and manipulating images using algorithms and techniques. Image processing techniques can include noise reduction, color correction, and object detection.

## 2.2.6 Applications of Image Formation

Image formation has a wide range of applications in computer vision, including:

### 2.2.6.1 Security Applications

Image formation is used in security applications such as surveillance cameras and passport scanners.

### 2.2.6.2 Medical Applications

Image formation is used in medical applications such as mammography and ultrasound imaging.

### 2.2.6.3 Industrial Applications

Image formation is used in industrial applications such as inspection and quality control.

### 2.2.6.4 Robotics Applications

Image formation is used in robotics applications such as object recognition and tracking.

## 2.2.7 Case Studies

Here are a few case studies that demonstrate the importance of image formation in computer vision:

### Case Study 1: Surveillance System

A surveillance system uses a camera with a CMOS image sensor to capture images of a parking lot. The images are then processed using image processing algorithms to detect and track vehicles.
