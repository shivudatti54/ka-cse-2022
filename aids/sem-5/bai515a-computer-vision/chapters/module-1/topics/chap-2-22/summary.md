# Chap-2 (2.2) - Image Formation: Photometric Image

=====================================================

## Key Concepts

- **Photometric Image**: A representation of the amount of light that reaches a camera's image sensor.
- **Radiometric Image**: A representation of the physical quantity of light (e.g., intensity, irradiance) that reaches the image sensor.
- **Intensity**: The amount of light per unit area that falls on a surface.

## Image Formation Theories

- **Geometric Model**: Describes the relationship between the object's structure and the resulting image.
- **Photometric Model**: Describes the relationship between the object's reflectance and the resulting image intensity.

## Important Formulas

- **Lambert's Cosine Law**: I(x, y) = R(x, y) \* cos(θ(x, y)), where I(x, y) is the image intensity, R(x, y) is the object reflectance, and θ(x, y) is the viewing angle.
- **Radiometric Image Formation**: I(x, y) = (1/4 \* π) \* (R(x, y) \* cos(θ(x, y)) \* Δx \* Δy), where I(x, y) is the image intensity, R(x, y) is the object reflectance, θ(x, y) is the viewing angle, and Δx and Δy are the pixel sizes.

## Definitions

- **Reflectance**: The amount of light that is reflected by a surface.
- **Viewing Angle**: The angle between the light source and the surface.

## Theorems

- **Kirchhoff's Law**: The radiometric image intensity at a point is proportional to the sum of the reflectances of the objects in that point.
- **Gray's Law**: The radiometric image intensity at a point is proportional to the product of the reflectance and the cosine of the viewing angle.

## Quick Revision Notes

- Photometric images are used to represent the amount of light that reaches a camera's image sensor.
- Geometric and photometric models are used to describe the relationship between the object's structure and the resulting image.
- Lambert's Cosine Law and Gray's Law are important formulas for calculating image intensity.
- Reflectance and viewing angle are important concepts in image formation.
