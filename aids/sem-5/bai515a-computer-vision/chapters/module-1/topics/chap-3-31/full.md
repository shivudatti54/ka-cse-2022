## 3.1 Introduction

---

In this chapter, we will delve into the process of image formation, which is a fundamental concept in computer vision. Image formation refers to the process of capturing and representing visual information in the form of digital images. This chapter will provide a comprehensive overview of the principles of image formation, including the role of light, lenses, and cameras.

## 3.1.1 Historical Context

---

The concept of image formation has been around for centuries, with ancient civilizations using various techniques to capture images. The invention of the camera in the 19th century revolutionized the process of image formation, allowing for the capture of images with greater accuracy and detail.

The early cameras used a process called daguerreotypy, which involved coating a copper plate with silver iodide and exposing it to light. The resulting images were highly detailed but fragile and required special care to preserve.

In the 20th century, the development of film cameras and digital imaging technology further improved the process of image formation. Today, we have a wide range of imaging technologies, including digital single-lens reflex (DSLR) cameras, smartphone cameras, and specialized imaging devices like 3D scanners and optical coherence tomography (OCT) scanners.

## 3.1.2 Photometric Image

---

A photometric image is a representation of the amount of light that is reflected or transmitted by an object or scene. It is a two-dimensional representation of the light intensity values in a scene, typically measured in units of intensity, such as pixels or gray levels.

The process of forming a photometric image involves the capture of light by a sensor, such as a camera's image sensor. The sensor converts the light into electrical signals, which are then processed and stored as a digital image.

### 3.1.2.1 Image Sensor

---

An image sensor is a critical component of a camera that captures light and converts it into electrical signals. There are several types of image sensors, including:

- **CCD (Charge-Coupled Device) sensors**: These sensors use a light-sensitive material to capture light, which is then transferred to an electronic circuit for processing.
- **CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor) sensors**: These sensors use a light-sensitive material to capture light, which is then transmitted to an electronic circuit for processing.

### 3.1.2.2 Lens

---

A lens is an optical component that focuses light onto an image sensor. The lens collects light from the scene, which is then focused onto the sensor using a process called refraction.

The type of lens used depends on the application and the desired image characteristics. For example, a wide-angle lens is used to capture a broad field of view, while a telephoto lens is used to capture a narrow field of view with more detail.

## 3.1.3 Image Formation Process

---

The image formation process involves the following steps:

1.  **Light Collection**: Light from the scene is collected by the lens and focused onto the image sensor.
2.  **Light Transmission**: The light is transmitted through the image sensor, which converts the light into electrical signals.
3.  **Signal Processing**: The electrical signals are processed by the camera's image processing system, which applies various algorithms to enhance the image quality.
4.  **Image Storage**: The final image is stored as a digital file, which can be viewed and edited using various software applications.

### 3.1.3.1 Lens Distortion

---

Lens distortion refers to the distortion of the image due to the lens's optical properties. There are several types of lens distortion, including:

- **Pincushion distortion**: This type of distortion occurs when the lens is convex, causing straight lines to appear curved.
- **Barrel distortion**: This type of distortion occurs when the lens is concave, causing straight lines to appear curved.

Lens distortion can be corrected using various techniques, including:

- **Image correction software**: This software applies algorithms to correct for lens distortion and produce a distorted-free image.
- **Image processing techniques**: Techniques such as deconvolution and deghosting can be used to correct for lens distortion.
