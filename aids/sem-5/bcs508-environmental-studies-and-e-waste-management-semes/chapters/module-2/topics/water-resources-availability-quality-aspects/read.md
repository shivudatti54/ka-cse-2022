# Module 2: Water Resources – Availability & Quality Aspects

## Introduction

Water is the most vital natural resource for all life forms and a fundamental requirement for industrial and agricultural development. For engineers, understanding the dynamics of water availability and the principles governing its quality is crucial, as our projects and innovations directly impact and are impacted by this resource. This module explores the global and national scenario of water availability, the stresses upon it, and the scientific parameters used to define and measure its quality.

## Core Concepts

### 1. Availability of Water Resources

Despite Earth being a "blue planet," only a tiny fraction of its water is readily available for human use.

*   **Global Distribution:** Approximately 97% of the world's water is saline (in oceans and seas). Of the remaining 3% freshwater, about 68.7% is locked in glaciers and ice caps, 29.9% is groundwater, and only about 0.3% is found in lakes, rivers, and swamps—the primary source for most human needs.
*   **Sources of Freshwater:**
    *   **Surface Water:** Water found in rivers, lakes, reservoirs, and ponds. It is the most visible and directly accessible source but also highly vulnerable to pollution and climate variations.
    *   **Groundwater:** Water stored in aquifers beneath the earth's surface. It is a critical source for irrigation and drinking water, especially in arid regions. Its over-exploitation leads to depletion of water tables.
*   **Water Stress & Scarcity:** Water stress occurs when the demand for water exceeds the available amount during a certain period or when poor quality restricts its use. Scarcity is a physical lack of water. Factors include:
    *   **Population Growth:** Increased demand for domestic, agricultural, and industrial use.
    *   **Urbanization & Industrialization:** Higher per capita consumption and pollution.
    *   **Climate Change:** Alters precipitation patterns, causing droughts and floods.
    *   **Pollution:** Contaminates existing sources, rendering them unusable.

**Example:** In India, the perennial rivers of the North (e.g., Ganges, Brahmaputra) are fed by Himalayan glaciers, while peninsular rivers (e.g., Godavari, Krishna) are primarily rain-fed, making them more susceptible to seasonal fluctuations and drought.

### 2. Quality Aspects of Water

Water quality describes the chemical, physical, and biological characteristics of water, usually in respect to its suitability for a designated use (e.g., drinking, irrigation, industrial cooling).

Key parameters used to assess water quality include:

#### A. Physical Parameters
*   **Turbidity:** Measures the cloudiness caused by suspended solids. High turbidity reduces light penetration, affecting aquatic life and indicating potential pollution.
*   **Temperature:** Affects the solubility of oxygen ( colder water holds more oxygen) and rates of chemical/biological reactions.
*   **Colour and Odour:** Indicate the presence of organic matter, chemicals, or biological contamination.

#### B. Chemical Parameters
*   **pH:** A measure of how acidic or basic water is on a scale of 0-14 (7 is neutral). Most natural waters have a pH between 6.5 and 8.5. Extreme pH can be harmful to aquatic life and corrode pipes.
*   **Dissolved Oxygen (DO):** The amount of oxygen dissolved in water. Essential for the survival of aquatic organisms. Low DO (< 5 mg/L) indicates pollution, often from organic waste.
*   **Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD):** The amount of oxygen required by aerobic microorganisms to decompose organic matter in a water sample. It is a key indicator of organic pollution. **Higher BOD means more pollution.**
*   **Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD):** Measures the oxygen equivalent of the organic matter that can be oxidized by a strong chemical oxidant. It provides a faster but less specific measure than BOD.
*   **Total Dissolved Solids (TDS):** The total amount of mobile ions (e.g., salts, minerals, metals) dissolved in water. High TDS can make water unpalatable and unsuitable for irrigation or industrial use.
*   **Nutrients (Nitrates & Phosphates):** Essential for plant growth, but excess amounts from agricultural runoff or sewage can cause **eutrophication**—a process where water bodies become overly enriched with nutrients, leading to excessive algal growth and oxygen depletion.

#### C. Biological Parameters
*   **Pathogens:** Disease-causing microorganisms like bacteria (e.g., *E. coli*), viruses, and protozoa. Their presence indicates fecal contamination and a high risk of waterborne diseases (e.g., cholera, typhoid).

## Key Points / Summary

| Aspect | Key Takeaway |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Availability** | Only ~0.3% of Earth's water is readily accessible freshwater. Sources are surface water (rivers, lakes) and groundwater (aquifers). |
| **Water Stress** | Caused by over-exploitation, population growth, pollution, and climate change, leading to physical scarcity or quality-related unusability. |
| **Quality Parameters** | Assessed through Physical (Turbidity, Temperature), Chemical (pH, DO, BOD, COD, TDS, Nutrients), and Biological (Pathogens) indicators. |
| **Critical Indicator** | **BOD (Biochemical Oxygen Demand)** is a paramount measure of organic pollution in water. A high BOD value signifies high levels of degradable organic waste and low water quality. |
| **Engineer's Role** | Understanding these concepts is essential for designing sustainable water supply systems, wastewater treatment plants, and pollution control strategies, ensuring responsible resource management. |
