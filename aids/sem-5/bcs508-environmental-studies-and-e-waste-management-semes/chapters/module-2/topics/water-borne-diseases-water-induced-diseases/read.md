# **Module 2: Water Borne Diseases & Water Induced Diseases**

### **Introduction**

For an engineer, water is a resource to be managed, treated, and distributed. However, its critical link to public health is a paramount consideration in any civil or environmental engineering project. Contaminated water is a primary vehicle for the transmission of numerous diseases, causing significant morbidity and mortality worldwide. Understanding these diseases—their causes, transmission, and prevention—is not just a biological concern but a foundational element of sanitary and environmental engineering. This knowledge directly informs the design of water treatment plants, sewerage systems, and public health policies. These diseases are broadly categorized as **water-borne** and **water-induced (or water-washed)**.

---

## **Core Concepts and Explanation**

### **1. Water Borne Diseases**

These diseases are caused by pathogenic microorganisms (bacteria, viruses, protozoa, helminths) that are transmitted primarily through the **ingestion of contaminated water**. The water acts as a passive carrier for the infectious agent.

*   **Causative Agents:** The contamination usually occurs due to the mixing of human feces (faecal-oral route) or other wastes with drinking water sources. This highlights the engineering criticality of proper sewage disposal and water treatment.
*   **Transmission:** Direct ingestion of water containing the pathogens.
*   **Examples:**
    *   **Cholera:** Caused by the bacterium *Vibrio cholerae*. Leads to severe, life-threatening dehydration due to profuse watery diarrhea. It is a classic example of a disease that outbreaks where sanitation infrastructure is poor.
    *   **Typhoid Fever:** Caused by *Salmonella typhi*. Spread through water contaminated with the feces of an infected person. Symptoms include sustained high fever, weakness, and abdominal pain.
    *   **Hepatitis A:** A viral infection that inflames the liver. Transmitted through ingestion of faecally contaminated water or food.
    *   **Dysentery:** Often caused by *Shigella* bacteria or the protozoan *Entamoeba histolytica* (Amoebiasis), resulting in bloody diarrhea.
    *   **Polio:** A viral disease that can lead to paralysis, also spread through the faecal-oral route.
*   **Engineering Control:** Prevention is achieved through reliable **water treatment** (coagulation, sedimentation, filtration, disinfection) and a protected, closed **water distribution system** that prevents recontamination.

### **2. Water Induced Diseases (Water-Washed Diseases)**

These diseases are not necessarily transmitted by ingesting contaminated water but are facilitated by a **lack of adequate water for hygiene and personal cleanliness**. The primary issue is water scarcity, not water quality. These are also known as water-washed diseases.

*   **Causative Agents:** These diseases are often caused by parasites, bacteria, or fungi that infect the skin or eyes.
*   **Transmission:** Spread through person-to-person contact or contact with contaminated surfaces, exacerbated by the inability to wash regularly.
*   **Examples:**
    *   **Trachoma:** A bacterial infection of the eye and a leading cause of preventable blindness worldwide. It is spread through contact with eye discharge from an infected person, often on fingers or towels, and is strongly linked to water scarcity and poor facial hygiene.
    *   **Scabies:** A skin infestation by mites that causes intense itching. It spreads rapidly in overcrowded conditions with limited access to water for bathing and washing clothes.
    *   **Leprosy:** While not highly contagious, poor hygiene and overcrowding can facilitate its transmission.
*   **Engineering Control:** Prevention is achieved by ensuring **sufficient water quantity** and **accessibility**. Engineering projects like building borewells, rainwater harvesting structures, and efficient piped water supply systems to communities are crucial in combating these diseases.

### **3. Water-Based Diseases**

This is a sub-category where the pathogen spends a part of its life cycle in an aquatic organism (a secondary host). Transmission occurs when the parasitic larvae in water penetrate the skin or are ingested.

*   **Transmission:** Contact with infested water (e.g., wading, swimming) or ingestion.
*   **Example:**
    *   **Schistosomiasis (Bilharzia):** Caused by parasitic blood flukes (trematodes). The larvae released from freshwater snails penetrate human skin during contact with infested water. It is a major public health problem in tropical regions and is directly linked to water resource development projects like dams and irrigation canals, which create ideal habitats for the snail host.

### **4. Water-Related Vector Diseases**

Here, water is the breeding ground for insect vectors that transmit diseases. The water itself is not consumed.

*   **Transmission:** Diseases are spread by insects that breed in or near water.
*   **Examples:**
    *   **Malaria & Dengue:** Spread by mosquitoes that breed in stagnant water (e.g., in blocked drains, uncovered tanks, discarded tires, and puddles).
    *   **Filariasis:** Also spread by mosquitoes breeding in polluted water.
*   **Engineering Control:** This involves **water management** strategies like proper drainage to prevent water stagnation, designing water storage tanks with lids, and managing construction sites to avoid creating temporary breeding grounds.

## **Key Points & Summary**

| Category | Primary Cause | Transmission Route | Key Examples | Engineering Solution |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Water-Borne** | Pathogens in water | Ingestion of contaminated water | Cholera, Typhoid, Hepatitis A | **Water Quality** Management: Treatment & Sanitation |
| **Water-Induced/Wasched** | Lack of water for hygiene | Person-to-person contact due to poor hygiene | Trachoma, Scabies | **Water Quantity & Access**: Supply & Distribution |
| **Water-Based** | Aquatic host (e.g., snails) | Skin penetration by larvae in water | Schistosomiasis | Managing water resources to control secondary hosts |
| **Water-Related Vector** | Insect vectors breeding in water | Bite from insects (mosquitoes) | Malaria, Dengue | **Water Management**: Drainage & preventing stagnation |

*   The distinction between these categories is crucial for implementing the correct public health intervention.
*   For an engineer, the goal is to **break the chain of transmission**. This involves:
    1.  Providing a **safe, adequate, and continuous** water supply (**potable water**).
    2.  Designing and maintaining an efficient **sewage collection and treatment system** (**sanitation**).
    3.  Incorporating **environmental safeguards** in infrastructure projects to avoid creating disease hotspots (e.g., snail habitats for Schistosomiasis).
*   A holistic engineering approach that integrates water supply, sanitation, and drainage is the most effective way to prevent the vast majority of water-related diseases.
