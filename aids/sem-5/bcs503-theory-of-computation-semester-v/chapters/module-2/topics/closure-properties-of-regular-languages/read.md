# Closure Properties of Regular Languages
## Introduction
Regular languages are a fundamental concept in the theory of computation, and understanding their closure properties is essential for working with them. In this chapter, we will explore the closure properties of regular languages, including union, intersection, complement, and concatenation.

## Union of Regular Languages
The union of two regular languages is also a regular language. This means that if we have two regular languages, L1 and L2, then the language L1 ∪ L2 is also regular.

### Example
Let L1 = {a, b} and L2 = {c, d}. Then L1 ∪ L2 = {a, b, c, d}, which is also a regular language.

## Intersection of Regular Languages
The intersection of two regular languages is also a regular language. This means that if we have two regular languages, L1 and L2, then the language L1 ∩ L2 is also regular.

### Example
Let L1 = {a, b, c} and L2 = {b, c, d}. Then L1 ∩ L2 = {b, c}, which is also a regular language.

## Complement of Regular Languages
The complement of a regular language is also a regular language. This means that if we have a regular language L, then the language L' (the complement of L) is also regular.

### Example
Let L = {a, b}. Then L' = {c, d, e, ...}, which is also a regular language.

## Concatenation of Regular Languages
The concatenation of two regular languages is also a regular language. This means that if we have two regular languages, L1 and L2, then the language L1L2 is also regular.

### Example
Let L1 = {a, b} and L2 = {c, d}. Then L1L2 = {ac, ad, bc, bd}, which is also a regular language.

## Kleene Closure of Regular Languages
The Kleene closure of a regular language is also a regular language. This means that if we have a regular language L, then the language L* (the Kleene closure of L) is also regular.

### Example
Let L = {a, b}. Then L* = {ε, a, b, aa, ab, ba, bb, ...}, which is also a regular language.
