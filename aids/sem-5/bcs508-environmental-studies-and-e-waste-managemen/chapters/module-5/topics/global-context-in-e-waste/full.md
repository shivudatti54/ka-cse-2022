# Global Context in E-Waste

### Introduction

E-waste, or electronic waste, refers to the disposal of electronic devices such as computers, smartphones, televisions, and other electrical equipment. The rapid obsolescence of electronic devices, driven by technological advancements and consumer demand, has led to a significant increase in e-waste generation. This article provides an in-depth exploration of the global context in e-waste, including historical context, modern developments, and applications.

### Historical Context

The concept of e-waste dates back to the 1990s, when the first electronic devices began to reach the end of their lifespan. However, it wasn't until the early 2000s that e-waste started to gain attention as a significant environmental concern. The rapid growth of the global IT industry, particularly in Asia, led to a surge in e-waste generation.

In 2001, the Basel Convention on the Control of Transboundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes and Their Disposal was ratified by over 100 countries, including the United States, the European Union, and China. The convention aimed to prevent the transboundary movement of hazardous wastes, including e-waste.

### Modern Developments

In recent years, the global e-waste landscape has undergone significant changes. The rise of emerging markets, particularly in Asia, has led to an increase in e-waste generation. According to the United Nations University (UNU), the global e-waste generation is expected to reach 74 million metric tons by 2030.

**Key statistics:**

- 50% of e-waste is generated in Asia, with China, India, and Indonesia being the top three generators.
- 15% of e-waste is generated in Europe, with the United States, Germany, and the United Kingdom being the top three generators.
- 20% of e-waste is generated in North America, with the United States and Canada being the top two generators.

### Applications and Case Studies

E-waste management is a complex issue that requires a multifaceted approach. Several applications and case studies demonstrate the importance of e-waste management:

1.  **Recycling:** Recycling is an essential component of e-waste management. The recycling industry can recover valuable materials such as copper, gold, and silver from e-waste.
2.  **Reusing:** Reusing e-waste is another approach that has gained attention. For example, old computers can be repurposed as servers, storage devices, or even art pieces.
3.  **Energy Recovery:** Energy recovery is a technique that involves converting e-waste into energy. This can be achieved through incineration or gasification.

**Case Study:** E-waste Management in the European Union

The European Union has implemented several policies to address e-waste management:

- The EU Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) Directive sets targets for e-waste recycling and recovery.
- The EU Restriction of Hazardous Substances (RoHS) Directive restricts the use of hazardous substances in electronic devices.
- The EU Ecodesign Directive sets energy efficiency standards for electronic devices.
