# Fluoride Problem in Drinking Water

**Definition and Importance of Fluoride**

- Fluoride (F) is a naturally occurring compound that can be found in water at various concentrations.
- Fluoride is essential for human health in small amounts, particularly for dental health.

**Sources of Fluoride in Drinking Water**

- Natural sources: fluorapatite in rocks, soil, and water
- Industrial sources: aluminum smelting, phosphate fertilizers, and pharmaceuticals
- Contaminants: sewage, effluent, and agricultural runoff

**Effects of Excessive Fluoride on Human Health**

- Dental fluorosis: white or brown spots on teeth
- Skeletal fluorosis: joint pain, bone thickening, and reduced mobility
- Neurotoxicity: reduced cognitive function and memory loss
- Thyroid problems: goiter and hypothyroidism

**Fluoride Levels in Drinking Water**

- WHO recommended maximum limit: 1.5 mg/L
- US EPA maximum limit: 4.0 mg/L

**Prevention and Mitigation**

- Fluoride removal technologies: distillation, reverse osmosis, and activated alumina
- Alternative sources: well water, spring water, and rainwater harvesting

**Key Formulas and Theorems**

- Fluoride ion concentration: C = (F- / V) x 18,000 (mg/L)
- Fluoride ion hardness: FWH = C x 18,000 (mg/L)

**Revision Key Points**

- Fluoride is essential for dental health, but excessive levels can cause health problems.
- Sources of fluoride in drinking water include natural and industrial sources.
- Excessive fluoride can cause dental fluorosis, skeletal fluorosis, neurotoxicity, and thyroid problems.
- Fluoride levels in drinking water must be monitored and controlled to prevent health problems.
- Fluoride removal technologies and alternative sources can help mitigate fluoride contamination.
