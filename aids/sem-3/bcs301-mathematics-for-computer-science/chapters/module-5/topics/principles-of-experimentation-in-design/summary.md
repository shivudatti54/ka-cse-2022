# Design of Experiments & ANOVA

### Principles of Experimentation in Design

- **Definition**: An experiment is a controlled procedure to test a hypothesis about a phenomenon.
- **Key Components**:
  - **Independent Variable**: The variable manipulated or changed by the experimenter.
  - **Dependent Variable**: The variable measured or observed in response to the independent variable.
  - **Control Group**: A group that does not receive the treatment or independent variable.
  - **Treatment Group**: A group that receives the treatment or independent variable.
- **Types of Experiments**:
  - **Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT)**: A type of experiment that involves randomly assigning participants to treatment or control groups.
  - **Factorial Design**: An experiment that involves manipulating multiple independent variables.
- **Statistical Principles**:
  - **Null Hypothesis**: A statement that there is no significant difference or relationship between variables.
  - **Alternative Hypothesis**: A statement that there is a significant difference or relationship between variables.
  - **Significance Level (α)**: The probability of rejecting the null hypothesis when it is true (usually set to 0.05).
- **Formulas and Theorems**:
  - **ANOVA Formula**: F = MSbetween / MSwithin
  - **Tukey's HSD**: A multiple comparison test used to compare means of different groups.
  - **Confidence Interval**: A range of values within which a population parameter is likely to lie.

### Important Definitions

- **Variation**: The degree of difference or similarity between observations.
- **Error**: The difference between the observed value and the true value.
- **Standard Error (SE)**: The standard deviation of the sampling distribution.

### Key Theorems

- **Central Limit Theorem (CLT)**: States that the distribution of sample means approaches a normal distribution as the sample size increases.
- **Law of Large Numbers (LLN)**: States that the average of a large number of independent and identically distributed random variables will converge to the population mean.
