## Introduction

Mathematical expectation, also known as expected value, is a fundamental concept in probability theory and statistics. It is used to quantify the average outcome of a random experiment or process. In this chapter, we will delve into the concept of mathematical expectation, its historical context, definition, properties, calculation methods, and applications.

## Historical Context

The concept of mathematical expectation dates back to the 17th century, when Pierre de Fermat first introduced the idea of a "probability" of winning a game. However, it was not until the 19th century that the concept of mathematical expectation was fully developed by mathematicians such as Abraham de Moivre and Augustin-Louis Cauchy. The modern concept of mathematical expectation was formalized by mathematicians such as André-Marie Ampère and James Joseph Sylvester.

## Definition and Notation

Mathematical expectation is defined as the sum of each possible outcome multiplied by its probability. It can be represented mathematically as:

E(X) = ∑x \* P(x)

where E(X) is the mathematical expectation of the random variable X, x represents the possible outcomes, and P(x) represents the probability of each outcome.

## Properties of Expectation

There are several important properties of mathematical expectation:

- Linearity: E(aX + b) = aE(X) + b, where a and b are constants.
- Additivity: E(X + Y) = E(X) + E(Y), where X and Y are independent random variables.
- Independence: E(X \* Y) = E(X) \* E(Y), where X and Y are independent random variables.
- Normalization: E(1) = 1, where 1 is a constant.

## Calculating Expectation

There are several methods to calculate mathematical expectation, including:

- Direct calculation: This involves calculating the sum of each possible outcome multiplied by its probability.
- Integral calculation: This involves using integration to calculate the expected value.
- Moment generating function: This involves using the moment generating function to calculate the expected value.

Here are a few case studies that demonstrate the application of mathematical expectation:

- **Coin Toss**: Suppose we want to calculate the expected number of heads in a coin toss. The probability of getting heads is 0.5, and the probability of getting tails is 0.5. The expected number of heads is therefore 0.5 \* 1 + 0.5 \* 0 = 0.5.
- **Roll of a Die**: Suppose we want to calculate the expected value of rolling a fair six-sided die. The possible outcomes are 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6, each with a probability of 1/6. The expected value is therefore (1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 + 6) / 6 = 3.5.
