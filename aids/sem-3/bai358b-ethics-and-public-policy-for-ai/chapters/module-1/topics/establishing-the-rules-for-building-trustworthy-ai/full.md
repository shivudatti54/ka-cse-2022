# Establishing the Rules for Building Trustworthy AI

## Chapter 3: Ethics and Public Policy for AI

### Introduction

As AI technology continues to advance at an unprecedented pace, the need for establishing clear rules and guidelines for building trustworthy AI has become increasingly important. The consequences of AI failures or misuses can be severe, ranging from economic losses to damage to individuals and society. In this chapter, we will delve into the historical context of AI ethics, modern developments, and the importance of establishing rules for building trustworthy AI.

### Historical Context

The concept of AI ethics has its roots in the 1950s and 1960s, when the first AI programs were developed. At that time, there was little concern about the potential risks and consequences of AI. However, as AI technology advanced, concerns about its potential impact on society began to grow.

In the 1970s and 1980s, the field of AI ethics began to take shape, with the publication of works such as John Searle's "Minds, Brains, and Programs" (1980) and Eliezer Yudkowsky's "Artificial Intelligence: The Truth in Artificial Intelligence" (1998). These works highlighted the potential risks and consequences of AI and laid the foundation for modern AI ethics.

### Modern Developments

In recent years, the field of AI ethics has experienced significant growth and development. The 2017 publication of the Asilomar AI Principles, developed by a group of experts in the field, marked a significant milestone in the development of AI ethics. The principles outlined a set of guidelines for the development and use of AI, including the importance of transparency, accountability, and fairness.

The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the AI Now Institute's AI Now Report have also highlighted the need for greater regulation and oversight of AI. These developments demonstrate a growing recognition of the need for clear rules and guidelines for building trustworthy AI.

### Establishing Rules for Building Trustworthy AI

Establishing rules for building trustworthy AI requires a multidisciplinary approach, involving experts from fields such as computer science, philosophy, law, and social sciences. The following are some key principles and guidelines for establishing rules for building trustworthy AI:

#### 1. Transparency

Transparency is critical for building trustworthy AI. This means that the development and deployment of AI systems should be transparent, including the data used to train the system, the algorithms used, and the potential biases and limitations of the system.

- Example: Google's TensorFlow allows developers to visualize and understand the decision-making process of their AI models.
- Case Study: The New York Times used AI to detect fake news, but the algorithm was later found to be biased towards conservative sources.

#### 2. Accountability

Accountability is essential for building trustworthy AI. This means that developers and deployers of AI systems should be held accountable for the consequences of their actions.

- Example: The development of autonomous vehicles requires accountability for the safety and well-being of passengers and other road users.
- Case Study: The Uber Self-Driving Car Project was criticized for its lack of transparency and accountability in its development and deployment.

#### 3. Fairness

Fairness is critical for building trustworthy AI. This means that AI systems should be designed to avoid discrimination and bias, and to promote fairness and equality.

- Example: The development of AI-powered hiring tools requires fairness to avoid discrimination against certain groups.
- Case Study: The use of facial recognition technology in law enforcement has been criticized for its potential to perpetuate racial bias.

#### 4. Safety

Safety is essential for building trustworthy AI. This means that AI systems should be designed to avoid harm to individuals and society, and to promote safety and security.

- Example: The development of AI-powered medical diagnosis requires safety to avoid misdiagnosis or misinterpretation of medical data.
- Case Study: The use of AI in healthcare has been criticized for its potential to compromise patient safety and well-being.

#### 5. Explainability

Explainability is critical for building trustworthy AI. This means that AI systems should be designed to provide transparent and understandable explanations for their decisions and actions.

- Example: The development of AI-powered financial forecasting requires explainability to avoid misinterpretation of financial data.
- Case Study: The use of AI in finance has been criticized for its lack of transparency and explainability.

### Case Studies

The following case studies demonstrate the importance of establishing rules for building trustworthy AI:

- **Google's AlphaGo**: In 2016, Google's AlphaGo AI system defeated a world champion Go player, demonstrating the potential of AI to surpass human capabilities. However, the development and deployment of AlphaGo required significant transparency and accountability measures, including the use of open-source code and the deployment of a transparent and explainable model.
- **Amazon's Echo**: In 2018, Amazon's Echo AI-powered speaker was criticized for its potential to perpetuate bias and discrimination. The development and deployment of Echo required significant fairness and transparency measures, including the use of diverse data sets and the deployment of a transparent and explainable model.

### Applications

The following applications demonstrate the importance of establishing rules for building trustworthy AI:

- **Healthcare**: AI-powered medical diagnosis requires safety and transparency to avoid misdiagnosis or misinterpretation of medical data.
- **Finance**: AI-powered financial forecasting requires explainability to avoid misinterpretation of financial data.
- **Transportation**: AI-powered autonomous vehicles require safety and transparency to avoid harm to passengers and other road users.
