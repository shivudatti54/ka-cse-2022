# From What to How: An Initial Review of Publicly Available AI Ethics Tools

## **Introduction**

The rapid development and deployment of Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems have raised significant concerns about their potential impact on society. As AI becomes increasingly integral to our daily lives, it is essential to ensure that these systems are designed and used in ways that prioritize human values, dignity, and well-being. One critical aspect of achieving this goal is the development of AI ethics tools that can help organizations and developers make informed decisions about the development and deployment of AI systems.

In this review, we will explore the concept of AI ethics tools and examine the publicly available tools that can help organizations transition from "what" to "how" - from identifying potential risks and biases to designing and implementing AI systems that are fair, transparent, and accountable.

## **Historical Context**

The concept of AI ethics emerged in the early 2010s, as the first AI systems began to be deployed in various industries. Initially, the focus was on ensuring that AI systems were safe and reliable, but as the field continued to evolve, concerns about ethics and responsibility began to grow.

In 2017, the Partnership on AI (PAI) was established to bring together industry leaders, researchers, and policymakers to address the ethical challenges associated with AI. Since then, there has been a growing recognition of the need for AI ethics tools that can help organizations navigate the complexities of AI development and deployment.

## **Publicly Available AI Ethics Tools**

There are numerous publicly available AI ethics tools that can help organizations transition from "what" to "how." These tools can be broadly categorized into three tiers:

### Tier 1: Awareness and Education

These tools provide an overview of AI ethics and its relevance to organizations. They often include resources, such as whitepapers, case studies, and webinars, that help organizations understand the importance of AI ethics and the potential risks associated with AI systems.

- **Google's AI Ethics Blog**: A comprehensive resource that features articles, case studies, and research on AI ethics.
- **Microsoft's AI Ethics Framework**: A framework that provides a structured approach to AI ethics, including guidelines for developers and policymakers.

### Tier 2: Risk Assessment and Analysis

These tools provide a more detailed assessment of the risks associated with AI systems. They often include tools for analyzing data, identifying biases, and evaluating the potential impact of AI systems on various stakeholders.

- **AI Now Institute's AI Risk Assessment Tool**: A tool that helps organizations assess the risks associated with AI systems, including bias, transparency, and accountability.
- **IBM's AI Ethics Scanner**: A tool that provides a comprehensive assessment of an AI system's ethics, including bias, fairness, and transparency.

### Tier 3: Design and Implementation

These tools provide guidance on designing and implementing AI systems that are fair, transparent, and accountable. They often include tools for developing AI systems that are explainable, auditable, and testable.

- **Google's AI Design Principles**: A set of principles that guide the design of AI systems, including transparency, accountability, and explainability.
- **Microsoft's AI Fairness 360**: A tool that provides a framework for developing AI systems that are fair, transparent, and accountable.

## **Case Studies and Applications**

Several organizations have successfully implemented AI ethics tools to transition from "what" to "how." Here are a few examples:

- **Google's AI Ethics Project**: A project that aims to promote AI ethics and responsible AI development. Google has developed several AI ethics tools, including the AI Ethics Blog and the AI Ethics Framework.
- **Microsoft's AI Ethics Initiative**: A initiative that aims to promote AI ethics and responsible AI development. Microsoft has developed several AI ethics tools, including the AI Ethics Scanner and the AI Fairness 360.
- **Amazon's AI Ethics Framework**: A framework that provides a structured approach to AI ethics, including guidelines for developers and policymakers. Amazon has implemented this framework in several of its AI systems, including Alexa.

## **Conclusion**

In conclusion, publicly available AI ethics tools can help organizations transition from "what" to "how" - from identifying potential risks and biases to designing and implementing AI systems that are fair, transparent, and accountable. These tools can be broadly categorized into three tiers: awareness and education, risk assessment and analysis, and design and implementation. By understanding the strengths and limitations of these tools, organizations can make informed decisions about the development and deployment of AI systems that prioritize human values, dignity, and well-being.
