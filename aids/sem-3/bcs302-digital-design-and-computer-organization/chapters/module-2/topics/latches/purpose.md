### Learning Purpose: Latches

**1. Why is this topic important?**
Latches are the fundamental building blocks of all sequential logic circuits. They provide the basic memory capability required to store state information (a 0 or a 1), which is essential for building registers, memory units, and finite state machines. Understanding latches is a critical first step toward comprehending how computers store and process information over time.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn the operational principles of basic latches, primarily the Set-Reset (SR) latch. They will analyze its circuit design using NOR or NAND gates, its truth table, and its behavior in different input conditions (Set, Reset, Hold, and the forbidden state). The concept of feedback and how it creates a bistable device will be a key takeaway.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This knowledge directly builds upon the combinational logic (gates) from Module 1. Latches form the foundation for more complex and practical flip-flops (Module 3), which are clocked and eliminate unstable states. These flip-flops are then used to construct central components like registers, counters, and ultimately, the CPU's control logic and memory.

**4. Real-world applications**
Latches are used in simple storage and control applications, such as debouncing switches in keyboards or controllers, and in basic interrupt request circuits. While often replaced by flip-flops in complex synchronous systems, the underlying latching principle is ubiquitous in data storage, temporary buffering, and as a core component within more advanced memory cells.
