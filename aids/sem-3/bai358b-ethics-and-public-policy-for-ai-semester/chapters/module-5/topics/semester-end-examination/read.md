# Semester-End Examination Guide: Module 5 - Ethics and Public Policy for AI

## Introduction
Module 5 serves as the crucial capstone of the course, moving from theoretical ethical frameworks to their practical application through public policy. The semester-end examination will test your ability to synthesize the entire course material, analyzing how abstract AI ethics principles are translated into concrete laws, regulations, and governance structures. Success requires understanding the interplay between technological capability, ethical reasoning, and policy-making.

## Core Concepts for Examination

### 1. From Ethics to Policy
The core challenge is bridging the gap between high-level ethical principles (e.g., fairness, accountability, transparency) and enforceable policy. You must be able to explain:
*   **Why self-regulation is often insufficient:** Tech companies may prioritize innovation and profit, leading to ethics washing—paying lip service to ethics without substantive change.
*   **The role of governmental bodies:** Governments and international organizations (like the EU, UN, or national AI authorities) create binding rules to ensure compliance and protect public interest.
*   **The Policy Lifecycle:** Issues are identified (e.g., algorithmic bias in hiring), policies are formulated (e.g., draft AI Act), implemented, and then evaluated for effectiveness.

**Example:** The principle of "Transparency" becomes the policy of a **"Right to Explanation"** in the EU's GDPR, where individuals can demand to know the logic behind an automated decision that affects them.

### 2. Key Regulatory Frameworks and Acts
You will likely be expected to compare and contrast major existing and proposed regulatory frameworks.
*   **EU AI Act (Most Important):** This is the world's first comprehensive AI law. Understand its **risk-based approach**:
    *   **Unacceptable Risk:** Banned applications (e.g., social scoring by governments, real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces).
    *   **High Risk:** Subject to strict obligations (e.g., AI used in critical infrastructure, medical devices, employment). This includes requirements for data quality, documentation, human oversight, and robustness.
    *   **Limited Risk:** Subject to transparency obligations (e.g., chatbots must inform users they are interacting with an AI).
    *   **Minimal Risk:** Mostly unregulated (e.g., AI-powered spam filters).

*   **India's Approach:** Contrast the EU's hard-law approach with India's current **softer, enabling approach**. Understand the mandate of the **NITI Aayog** and its documents like the "#AIForAll" strategy. Be aware of the recent shift towards more concrete discussions on a **Digital India Act** that would encompass AI regulation.

### 3. Standardization and Certification
Policies often rely on technical standards to be implementable.
*   **Role of Standards:** Organizations like **IEEE** and **ISO** are developing technical standards for AI ethics (e.g., for algorithmic bias assessment, data quality).
*   **Certification Marks:** Think of this as an "ISO certification for AI." It would allow companies to prove their AI systems meet certain ethical and safety benchmarks, building trust with consumers and regulators.

### 4. Auditing and Assurance
How do we ensure compliance? This is a key exam topic.
*   **Algorithmic Auditing:** The process of assessing an AI system for compliance with laws and ethical norms. It can be **internal** (first-party) or **external** (third-party).
*   **Audit Triggers:** An audit might be required before deployment (pre-market) or after an incident occurs (post-market).
*   **Challenges:** Auditing is complex due to proprietary "black-box" models and a lack of universally accepted audit procedures.

**Example:** A bank using an AI for loan approvals must undergo a **bias audit** to ensure its model does not discriminate against applicants based on zip code (a proxy for race).

### 5. Global Governance Challenges
AI is a global technology, but laws are national/regional. This creates tension.
*   **The Brussels Effect:** The idea that the EU's strict regulations become a global standard because it's easier for multinational companies to comply with the highest standard everywhere.
*   **Fragmentation vs. Harmonization:** A risk that different countries will create conflicting rules (e.g., EU vs. US vs. China approach), stifling innovation and creating legal uncertainty.
*   **Role of Multilateral Bodies:** Understand the discussions happening at forums like the **G20** and **UNESCO** to create a global consensus on AI governance.

## Key Points & Summary

*   **Synthesis is Key:** The exam will test your ability to connect ethical theories (from previous modules) to their policy implications.
*   **EU AI Act is Central:** Thoroughly understand its risk-based categorization and its potential global impact.
*   **Contrast Regulatory Approaches:** Be prepared to compare the proactive, hard-law regulation of the EU with the more cautious, enabling approach currently seen in India.
*   **Focus on Mechanisms:** Move beyond principles. Understand *how* ethics are enforced—through **audits**, **certification**, **standardization**, and **legal obligations**.
*   **Think Globally:** Consider the challenges of governing a borderless technology with national laws and the push for international cooperation.

**Prepare for questions** that ask you to: "Analyze a proposed AI application using the EU AI Act's risk framework," or "Discuss the challenges in implementing a transparency policy for complex neural networks," or "Compare the regulatory approaches of two different regions." Good luck
