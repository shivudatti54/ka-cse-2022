# Module 5: Case Study - The Facial Recognition Debate: The Case of Aaron Aboagye

**Subject:** Ethics and Public Policy for AI
**Target Audience:**  Engineering Students

## 1. Introduction

As future engineers and developers of AI systems, it is crucial to understand that the technology you build operates in the real world, with real-world consequences. Technical proficiency must be paired with ethical consideration. The case of **Aaron Aboagye** serves as a powerful, real-life example of the ethical and policy challenges posed by one specific AI application: facial recognition technology (FRT). This case study moves beyond abstract principles and illustrates the direct human impact of algorithmic decisions, highlighting why robust public policy is essential.

## 2. Core Concepts and Explanation

### Who is Aaron Aboagye?

In January 2023, Aaron Aboagye, a student at Georgia Institute of Technology, was wrongfully arrested by the Atlanta Police Department for a crime he did not commit. The arrest was based primarily on a **facial recognition match** generated by an algorithm. This is a prime example of a **"false positive"** match, where the system incorrectly identified an innocent person as a suspect.

### The Technical and Ethical Breakdown

1.  **How the Error Likely Occurred:**
    *   FRT systems are not infallible. They work by mapping facial features from an image (e.g., a grainy still from security footage) and comparing them against a database of known faces (e.g., a mugshot database or DMV records).
    *   These algorithms can be biased. Numerous studies, including a landmark one from the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), have shown that many FRTs have higher error rates for people of color, women, and younger people—a phenomenon known as **algorithmic bias**.
    *   Aaron Aboagye is a Black man. His case immediately raises questions about whether racial bias in the algorithm contributed to the false match.

2.  **The Flawed Human-in-the-Loop Process:**
    *   A critical ethical failure in this case was the over-reliance on the AI's output by the investigators. FRT should never be the sole piece of evidence for an arrest; it should be treated as an **investigative lead**.
    *   Best practices dictate that a potential match must be thoroughly verified by human investigators through traditional police work—checking alibis, reviewing other evidence, and ensuring the match is not just algorithmic but also logical.
    *   In Aboagye's case, the algorithm's match was seemingly treated as definitive proof, leading directly to the traumatic experience of a wrongful arrest.

3.  **The Role of Public Policy:**
    *   This incident occurred in a regulatory vacuum. At the time, there were no strong federal or state-level laws in Georgia governing the use of FRT by law enforcement.
    *   The lack of policy meant there were no mandated standards for:
        *   **Accuracy Requirements:** Minimum accuracy thresholds, especially for demographic groups known to be vulnerable to higher error rates.
        *   **Training Requirements:** Ensuring officers using the technology understand its limitations and potential for error.
        *   **Operational Protocols:** Strict rules requiring human verification and prohibiting sole reliance on a FRT match for an arrest warrant.
        *   **Oversight and Auditing:** Regular third-party audits of the systems to check for bias and accuracy.

## 3. Examples and Wider Implications

*   **Clearview AI:** This controversial company scraped billions of images from social media and the web without consent to build its facial recognition database, which it then sold to thousands of law enforcement agencies. Its use highlights the privacy concerns and lack of consent inherent in some FRT practices.
*   **Detroit, Michigan:** Another Black man, Robert Williams, was wrongfully arrested based on a flawed facial recognition match in 2020. The pattern demonstrates that Aboagye's case is not an isolated incident but part of a systemic problem.
*   **Policy Responses:** In response to cases like these, cities like San Francisco and Boston have banned government use of facial recognition technology. The European Union's AI Act proposes to ban real-time FRT for law enforcement in public spaces, with very limited exceptions.

## 4. Key Points and Summary

| Key Point | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **AI is Not Infallible** | FRT, like all AI, can and does make errors. These errors are not distributed equally and often disproportionately affect minority communities. |
| **Human Oversight is Critical** | AI should augment human decision-making, not replace it. A "match" is a lead to be investigated, not proof of guilt. Engineers must design systems that support, not supplant, human judgment. |
| **Bias is a Real Technical Issue** | Algorithmic bias is not just a social concept; it's a measurable technical flaw resulting from unrepresentative training data and flawed model design. Mitigating it is an engineering challenge. |
| **Policy Lags Behind Technology** | The rapid deployment of FRT has often outpaced the development of laws and regulations to govern its ethical use. This creates a risky environment for citizens. |
| **Engineers Have an Ethical Duty** | The choices you make in data collection, algorithm selection, and system design have profound ethical implications. Building responsibly is a core professional requirement. |

**Conclusion:** The case of Aaron Aboagye is a cautionary tale for AI engineers. It underscores the urgent need to develop accurate, fair, and transparent AI systems and to advocate for and comply with strong public policies that protect individuals from the harmful consequences of technological failure. Your technical work does not exist in a vacuum; it is deeply intertwined with ethics, law, and human rights.
