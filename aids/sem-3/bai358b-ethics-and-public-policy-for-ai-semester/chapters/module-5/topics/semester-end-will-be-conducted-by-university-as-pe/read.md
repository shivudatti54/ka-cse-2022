## 1. Introduction

This module concludes our structured learning for the course "Ethics and Public Policy for AI." As we reach the end of this critical subject, the focus now shifts to consolidating your knowledge and preparing for the formal assessment of your understanding. The Semester-End Examination (SEE) is the primary method through which the university evaluates your grasp of the ethical frameworks, policy challenges, and responsible innovation principles discussed throughout the course. This content will guide you on what to expect and how to approach this examination effectively.

## 2. Core Concepts: Understanding the Examination Process

The Semester-End Examination is a standardized test conducted by  according to a pre-defined schedule. It is designed to assess your comprehensive understanding of the entire syllabus, with a significant weightage placed on your ability to apply concepts rather than just recall information.

Key aspects of the SEE include:

*   **Scheduling:** The exam is held on a date and time specified in the official university timetable. It is your responsibility to check this timetable regularly on the  website or through your college's examination cell. **There will be no individual notifications.**
*   **Format:** Typically, the SEE is a written examination of 3 hours duration, carrying 100 marks. The question paper pattern often includes:
    *   **Part A:** Multiple-choice, short-answer, or definition-based questions (covering the entire syllabus).
    *   **Part B:** Analytical questions requiring detailed answers (usually with internal choice, e.g., "answer any three out of five").
*   **Syllabus Coverage:** While the exam covers all modules, questions from Module 5 (and other later modules) are highly probable as they encapsulate and build upon earlier concepts. You must study the entire syllabus thoroughly.

## 3. Application to Ethics and Public Policy for AI

For this specific subject, merely memorizing definitions is insufficient. The exam will test your ability to:

*   **Analyze Ethical Dilemmas:** You may be presented with a case study (e.g., a biased algorithm used in hiring, a fatal autonomous vehicle accident, misuse of generative AI) and asked to analyze it using ethical frameworks like utilitarianism, deontology, or virtue ethics.
    *   *Example: "A social media company's AI algorithm amplifies divisive content to increase user engagement, leading to real-world violence. Analyze this using two different ethical frameworks."*
*   **Evaluate Policies:** Questions may require you to critique existing AI policies (e.g., the EU AI Act, India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act) or propose policy measures for a given scenario.
    *   *Example: "Suggest public policy measures to mitigate the risks of deepfake technology while protecting freedom of expression."*
*   **Synthesize Concepts:** You might need to connect technical concepts from your core engineering branches (e.g., Computer Science, Electronics) with their ethical and policy implications, demonstrating a holistic understanding.
    *   *Example: "Discuss the ethical considerations an electrical engineer must account for when designing smart grid systems that utilize AI for load forecasting and consumer data analysis."*
