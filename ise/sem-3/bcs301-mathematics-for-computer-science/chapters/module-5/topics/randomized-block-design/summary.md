# **Randomized Block Design**

### Definition

- A type of experimental design used to control for extraneous variation in a study
- A subset of observations (blocks) are randomly assigned to each treatment or condition

### Key Points

- **Blocking**: Dividing experimental units into groups (blocks) to control for extraneous variation
- **Randomization**: Assigning treatments or conditions to blocks randomly to minimize bias
- **Confounding variable**: A variable that affects both the treatment and the error term
- **Orthogonality**: The interaction between treatments and blocks should be independent of the error term

### Important Formulas

- **Block size**: The number of experimental units in each block
- **Number of blocks**: The total number of blocks used in the experiment
- **Repeatability**: The number of times each block is repeated (usually k = 2)
- **Total sum of squares**: SST = Σ[(Y_ij - μ)²], where Y_ij is the response variable for the i-th treatment and j-th block
- **Model equation**: Y_ij = μ + α_i + β_j + ε_ij, where α_i is the treatment effect, β_j is the block effect, and ε_ij is the error term

### Theorems

- **Central limit theorem**: The sampling distribution of the treatment mean is approximately normal
- **Fisher's theorem**: The variance of the treatment mean is approximately equal to the variance of the error term

### Assumptions

- Randomization: Treatments are assigned randomly to blocks
- Independence: Observations within blocks are independent
- Homogeneity: Variance of the response variable is constant across all treatments and blocks

### Quick Revision Key

- Understand the concept of blocking and randomization
- Know the importance of confounding variables and orthogonality
- Familiarize yourself with the model equation and formulas
- Review the assumptions of the randomized block design
