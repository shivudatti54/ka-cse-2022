# **3 Design Verilog HDL to implement simple circuits using structural**

## **Introduction**

Verilog Hardware Description Language (HDL) is a standardized language used to design and describe digital electronic systems. It is a popular choice for designing and verifying digital circuits due to its ease of use and flexibility. In this module, we will delve into the world of Verilog HDL and explore its capabilities in implementing simple circuits using the structural design style.

## **Historical Context**

Verilog HDL was first introduced in 1984 by Mentor Graphics Corporation. It was designed to provide a more intuitive and user-friendly alternative to the existing VHDL (VHSIC Hardware Description Language) language. Since its introduction, Verilog has become one of the most widely used HDLs in the industry, with applications in a wide range of fields, including digital logic design, computer networks, and embedded systems.

## **Structural Design Style**

The structural design style is a method of designing digital circuits that focuses on the physical structure of the circuit rather than its logical behavior. In this style, the designer describes the circuit's components and their connections using a combination of modules, interfaces, and signals.

## **Verilog Structural Syntax**

The Verilog structural syntax is composed of the following elements:

- **Modules**: These are the basic building blocks of a Verilog design. A module represents a reusable piece of functionality that can be instantiated multiple times in a design.
- **Inputs**: These are the signals that are connected to the module's ports.
- **Outputs**: These are the signals that are generated by the module.
- **Wires**: These are the signals that connect modules, inputs, and outputs.
- **Assignments**: These are the statements that define the relationships between modules, inputs, outputs, and wires.

## **Example: Simple AND Gate**

Here is an example of a simple AND gate implemented using the Verilog structural syntax:

```verilog
module and_gate(a, b, o);
    input a, b;
    output o;

    assign o = a & b;
endmodule

// Instantiation
and_gate and gate1(a, b, o);
```

In this example, we define a module called `and_gate` that takes two inputs, `a` and `b`, and produces an output, `o`. The `assign` statement defines the relationship between the output and the inputs.

## **Applications**

Verilog HDL is widely used in a variety of applications, including:

- **Digital logic design**: Verilog is used to design and verify digital logic circuits, such as gates, flip-flops, and microprocessors.
- **Computer networks**: Verilog is used to design and verify network protocols, such as Ethernet and Wi-Fi.
- **Embedded systems**: Verilog is used to design and verify embedded systems, such as microcontrollers and robots.
- **FPGA design**: Verilog is used to design and verify Field-Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGAs).

## **Modern Developments**

In recent years, there have been several developments in the field of Verilog HDL, including:

- **SystemVerilog**: This is a superset of Verilog that adds features such as concurrency and assertions.
- **TikZ**: This is a tool that allows users to create diagrams and simulations using a high-level language.
- **VCS**: This is a tool that allows users to verify and simulate digital circuits.
