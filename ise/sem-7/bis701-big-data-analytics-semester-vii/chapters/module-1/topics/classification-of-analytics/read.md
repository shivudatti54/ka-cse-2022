# Classification of Analytics

## 1. Introduction

In the world of Big Data, possessing vast amounts of information is only the first step. The real value lies in extracting meaningful insights to support decision-making. This process is known as **analytics**. However, not all analytics are the same; they serve different purposes and answer different types of questions. For an engineer or data scientist, understanding how to classify analytics is fundamental to choosing the right tool and approach for a given business or research problem. Analytics is broadly classified into four main types: Descriptive, Diagnostic, Predictive, and Prescriptive.

## 2. Core Concepts

### a. Descriptive Analytics (What Happened?)

**Descriptive Analytics** is the most common and fundamental type. It focuses on summarizing historical data to understand what has happened in the past. It uses techniques like data aggregation and mining to provide a view of past events, often presented through dashboards, reports, and visualizations.

*   **Purpose:** To provide a clear, factual summary of past performance.
*   **Techniques/Tools:** Business Intelligence (BI) tools (e.g., Tableau, Power BI), SQL queries, basic statistical analysis (mean, median, mode, standard deviation).
*   **Example:** A university's student management system generates a report showing the pass percentage for each branch in the last semester. A company's weekly sales report is another classic example.

### b. Diagnostic Analytics (Why Did It Happen?)

**Diagnostic Analytics** goes a step further to dig into the data to understand the *causes* of past events and outcomes. It is essentially the process of discovery and determination of the root causes behind the trends and patterns identified by descriptive analytics.

*   **Purpose:** To identify the reasons behind past successes or failures.
*   **Techniques/Tools:** Drill-down, data discovery, data mining, and correlation analysis.
*   **Example:** If descriptive analytics showed a 20% drop in sales last quarter (what happened), diagnostic analytics would investigate *why*. It might reveal that the drop was correlated with a competitor's launch of a new product or a flaw in a marketing campaign.

### c. Predictive Analytics (What Will Happen?)

**Predictive Analytics** uses historical data to make forecasts about future outcomes. It moves beyond explaining the past to estimating the likelihood of a future event. It employs statistical models and machine learning algorithms to identify patterns and predict trends.

*   **Purpose:** To forecast future probabilities and trends.
*   **Techniques/Tools:** Machine Learning algorithms (e.g., Linear Regression, Decision Trees, Neural Networks), forecasting models.
*   **Example:** An e-commerce platform predicting which products a customer is most likely to buy next based on their browsing history. Netflix recommending movies you might like or a weather forecasting model predicting next week's rainfall are also examples.

### d. Prescriptive Analytics (What Should We Do?)

**Prescriptive Analytics** is the most advanced class of analytics. It not only predicts what will happen but also suggests actions to benefit from the predictions and shows the implications of each decision option. It uses optimization and simulation algorithms to advise on possible outcomes.

*   **Purpose:** To recommend the best course of action to achieve a desired outcome.
*   **Techniques/Tools:** Complex optimization algorithms, simulation, recommendation engines.
*   **Example:** A ride-sharing app like Uber uses prescriptive analytics to not only predict demand (predictive) but also to dynamically adjust prices (surge pricing) and recommend optimal routes for drivers to maximize efficiency and earnings. It answers: "Given the predicted demand, what is the best price and route to balance supply and demand?"

## 3. Key Points & Summary

| Type of Analytics | Key Question | Purpose | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Descriptive** | What happened? | Summarize past data | Last month's sales report |
| **Diagnostic** | Why did it happen? | Find causes of past outcomes | Analysis of a sales drop |
| **Predictive** | What will happen? | Forecast future outcomes | Customer churn prediction |
| **Prescriptive** | What should we do? | Recommend optimal actions | Dynamic pricing & route optimization |

**Summary:** The classification of analytics forms a hierarchy of value and complexity. **Descriptive** and **Diagnostic** analytics look at the past to learn from it. **Predictive** and **Prescriptive** analytics use that learning to inform future decisions. As you move from descriptive to prescriptive, the complexity, required computational power, and potential business value increase significantly. A robust analytics strategy often involves leveraging all four types to move from simply reporting on the past to actively shaping the future.
