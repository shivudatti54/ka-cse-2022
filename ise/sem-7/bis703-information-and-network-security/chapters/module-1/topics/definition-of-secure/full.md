# **Definition of Secure**

In the context of information and network security, "secure" is a term used to describe a system, network, or data that is protected from unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction. In this module, we will delve into the definition of secure, exploring its historical context, key aspects, and modern developments.

## **Historical Context**

The concept of security dates back to ancient times, with the use of physical barriers and locks to protect people, assets, and information. However, with the advent of technology, the notion of security evolved to include digital technologies, such as encryption, firewalls, and intrusion detection systems.

In the 1960s, the concept of information security was formalized through the establishment of the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) in the United States. NIST developed the first set of guidelines for information security, which emphasized the importance of confidentiality, integrity, and availability (CIA Triad).

## **Key Aspects of Secure**

A secure system or network must meet several key criteria, including:

### 1. Confidentiality

Confidentiality refers to the protection of sensitive information from unauthorized access or disclosure. This includes data at rest (stored data) and data in transit (transmitted data).

### 2. Integrity

Integrity refers to the accuracy and completeness of data. A secure system must ensure that data is not modified or deleted without authorization.

### 3. Availability

Availability refers to the ability to access and use data and systems when needed. A secure system must ensure that data and systems are accessible and usable.

### 4. Authentication

Authentication refers to the process of verifying the identity of users, devices, or systems. A secure system must ensure that only authorized parties can access or interact with the system.

### 5. Authorization

Authorization refers to the process of controlling access to resources based on user identity, role, or permissions. A secure system must ensure that only authorized parties can access or modify resources.

### 6. Non-Repudiation

Non-repudiation refers to the ability to prove that a message or action was sent or taken by a specific party. A secure system must ensure that the sender of a message or the performer of an action cannot deny its authenticity.

### 7. Recoverability

Recoverability refers to the ability to recover data and systems in the event of a disaster or failure. A secure system must ensure that data and systems can be recovered quickly and efficiently.

## **Types of Secure Systems**

There are several types of secure systems, including:

### 1. Physical Security

Physical security refers to the protection of physical assets, such as buildings, equipment, and personnel. This includes measures such as access control, surveillance, and alarm systems.

### 2. Network Security

Network security refers to the protection of computer networks from unauthorized access, use, or disruption. This includes measures such as firewalls, intrusion detection systems, and encryption.

### 3. Application Security

Application security refers to the protection of software applications from unauthorized access, use, or modification. This includes measures such as secure coding practices, input validation, and error handling.

### 4. Data Security

Data security refers to the protection of sensitive data from unauthorized access, use, or disclosure. This includes measures such as encryption, access controls, and data backup and recovery.

## **Examples and Case Studies**

### 1. Financial Institutions

Financial institutions, such as banks and credit unions, require secure systems to protect customer data and prevent financial losses. For example, a bank's online banking system must be secure to prevent unauthorized access to customer accounts.

### 2. Government Agencies

Government agencies require secure systems to protect sensitive information and prevent national security breaches. For example, the US Department of Defense requires secure systems to protect classified information.

### 3. Healthcare Organizations

Healthcare organizations require secure systems to protect patient data and prevent medical identity theft. For example, a hospital's electronic health record (EHR) system must be secure to prevent unauthorized access to patient information.

## **Modern Developments**

In recent years, there have been several modern developments in the field of secure systems, including:

### 1. Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML)

AI and ML are being used to improve the security of systems by detecting and preventing cyber attacks.
