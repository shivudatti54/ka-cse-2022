# Modern Crypto History

=====================================

## Overview

---

Modern cryptography has its roots in World War II, with the development of the first practical encryption techniques.

## Key Events

---

- 1939: **Abba Eban's Cipher**: A simple substitution cipher used during World War II.
- 1940s: **Codebreaking at Bletchley Park**: British mathematicians and computer scientists, including Alan Turing, developed techniques to break enemy codes.
- 1950s: **Turing Test**: A theoretical model for determining whether a machine is capable of thinking like a human.
- 1960s: **RSA Algorithm**: Developed by Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Leonard Adleman.
- 1970s: **Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange**: A method for securely exchanging cryptographic keys over an insecure channel.
- 1980s: **Secure Sockets Layer (SSL)**: Developed by Netscape to secure online transactions.
- 1990s: **Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)**: A widely used symmetric-key block cipher.

## Important Formulas and Definitions

---

- **RSA Algorithm**: RSA: n = p^e \* q^d, where p and q are prime numbers, and e and d are coprime.
- **Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange**: K = g^s \* g^t (mod p), where g is a primitive root, s and t are secret keys.
- **AES**: AES: E(x) = x \* key (mod m), where x is the plaintext, key is the secret key, and m is the block size.

## Theories and Concepts

---

- **Public-Key Cryptography**: A method for secure communication using a pair of keys: a public key for encryption and a private key for decryption.
- **Hash Functions**: A one-way function that maps input to output, used for data integrity and authenticity verification.
- **Digital Signatures**: A method for verifying the authenticity of a message, using a hash function and a digital certificate.

## Important Theorems

---

- **Shannon's Source-Coding Theorem**: A fundamental theorem in information theory, stating that the minimum expected length of a message is proportional to the entropy of the message.
- **Bell's Theorem**: A theorem in quantum mechanics, stating that any attempt to measure a quantum system without disturbing it will necessarily introduce error.
