# Learning Purpose: Connectivity in Graph Theory

**1. Why is this topic important?**
Connectivity is a fundamental concept in graph theory that measures the robustness and resilience of a network. Understanding it is crucial because it allows us to quantify the minimum number of elements (vertices or edges) whose removal disconnects a graph, which is vital for analyzing network vulnerability and designing robust systems.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn to define and differentiate between vertex connectivity and edge connectivity. They will explore key concepts like cuts, cut-vertices (articulation points), and bridges. Furthermore, they will study Menger’s Theorems, which provide a deep link between connectivity and the number of disjoint paths between vertices.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This topic directly builds upon earlier concepts of paths, cycles, and walks. It provides a critical foundation for subsequent modules on network flows, where disjoint paths are essential, and for studying special graphs like trees (which are minimally connected) and Eulerian/Hamiltonian graphs, which have specific connectivity properties.

**4. Real-world applications**
Analyzing connectivity is essential for securing computer networks against targeted attacks, designing reliable transportation and utility grids, optimizing communication networks, and understanding the stability of social networks and biological ecosystems. It helps answer questions like, "How many routers must fail to break the internet?" or "Which bus stops are critical to the entire city's network?"
