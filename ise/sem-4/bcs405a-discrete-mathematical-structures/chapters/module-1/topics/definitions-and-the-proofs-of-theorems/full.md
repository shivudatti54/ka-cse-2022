## **Axioms and Definitions**

An axiom is a statement that is accepted as true without proof. Axioms serve as the foundation for mathematical theories, providing a set of rules and assumptions that govern the behavior of mathematical structures. In discrete mathematics, axioms often concern the properties of sets, relations, and functions.

A definition, on the other hand, is a statement that provides a clear and concise description of a mathematical concept. Definitions are used to establish the meaning of terms and provide a framework for further investigation.

## **Types of Definitions**

### 1. Explicit Definition

An explicit definition provides a clear and concise description of a mathematical concept. It specifies the properties and behavior of the concept, often using logical statements and predicates.

Example: Define the set of natural numbers (ℕ) as {x ∈ ℤ | x > 0}, where ℤ represents the set of integers.

### 2. Implicit Definition

An implicit definition relies on the properties and behavior of a mathematical concept to define it. Implicit definitions often involve axioms or postulates that establish the concept's meaning.

Example: Define the concept of a group (G, ⋅) as a set G together with a binary operation ⋅ that satisfies the group axioms: closure, associativity, identity, and invertibility.

### 3. Recursive Definition

A recursive definition establishes a mathematical concept in terms of more fundamental concepts. Recursive definitions often involve a base case and an inductive step.

Example: Define the factorial function (n!) as n! = 1 if n = 0, and n! = n × (n-1)! if n > 0.

## **Theorems and Proof Techniques**

A theorem is a statement that has been rigorously proven to be true. Theorems are often established using various proof techniques, including:

### 1. Direct Proof

A direct proof involves showing that a statement is true by establishing a series of logical steps.

Example: Prove that the sum of two even numbers is even. Assume x and y are even numbers, then x = 2a and y = 2b for some integers a and b. Show that x + y = 2(a + b), which is even.

### 2. Indirect Proof (Proof by Contradiction)

An indirect proof involves assuming the opposite of what is to be proven and showing that this assumption leads to a logical contradiction.

Example: Prove that there are no odd perfect squares. Assume, if possible, that there exists an odd perfect square. Show that this assumption leads to a contradiction, such as the existence of a square root of an odd number.

### 3. Proof by Contrapositive

A proof by contrapositive involves showing that the negation of a statement is false. This is equivalent to proving that the original statement is true.

Example: Prove that if a number is even, then it is divisible by 2. Show that the negation of this statement (i.e., a number is even but not divisible by 2) is false.

### 4. Proof by Mathematical Induction

A proof by mathematical induction involves establishing a statement for a specific case and then showing that the statement holds for all cases using a mathematical induction step.

Example: Prove that the formula 1 + 2 + 3 + ... + n = n(n+1)/2 holds for all positive integers n. Establish the base case (n = 1), and then show that the induction step (n+1) is true using the inductive hypothesis.

## **Case Studies**

### 1. The Four Color Theorem

The Four Color Theorem states that any planar map can be colored using four colors such that no two adjacent regions have the same color. The proof of this theorem involves recursive definitions, graph theory, and the application of the Four Color Theorem to smaller sub-maps.

### 2. The P versus NP problem

The P versus NP problem is a fundamental question in computer science that concerns the relationship between computational complexity and verifiability. The problem involves definitions of computational complexity and theorems that establish the limits of efficient computation.
