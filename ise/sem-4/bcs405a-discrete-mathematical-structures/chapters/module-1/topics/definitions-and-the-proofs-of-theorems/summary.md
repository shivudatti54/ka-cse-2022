# Definitions and the Proofs of Theorems

## Fundamentals of Logic

### Key Points

- **Definition**: A statement that is true by definition is called a _trivial proposition_.
- **Definition of a Theorem**: A theorem is a statement that has been rigorously proved to be true.
- **Theorems vs. Axioms**: Theorems are derived from axioms, while axioms are not derived from any other statement.

### Important Formulas and Definitions

- **Lattice Theory**:
  - Definition: A lattice is a partially ordered set in which every two elements have a least upper bound (join) and a greatest lower bound (meet).
  - Formula: `a ∨ b` represents the join of `a` and `b`, while `a ∧ b` represents the meet of `a` and `b`.
- **Propositional Logic**:
  - Definition: A proposition is a statement that can be either true or false.
  - Formula: `p → q` represents the implication of `p` implying `q`.

### Theorems and Proofs

- **Direct Proof**: A direct proof is a proof that establishes the truth of a theorem by direct application of previously established theorems.
- **Indirect Proof**: An indirect proof is a proof that establishes the truth of a theorem by assuming the negation of the theorem and then showing that this assumption leads to a contradiction.

### Important Theorems

- **Laws of Logic**:
  - The Law of Non-Contradiction: `p → ¬p` is false.
  - The Law of Excluded Middle: `p → p ∨ ¬p` is true.
- **Axiomatic Systems**:
  - The Axiom of Choice: Every set of non-empty sets has a choice function.

### Quick Revision Tips

- Focus on understanding the definitions and axioms of the subject.
- Practice proving theorems using direct and indirect proofs.
- Review the laws of logic and their applications.
