# The Well Ordering Principle – Mathematical Induction

### Introduction

The Well Ordering Principle (WOP) is a fundamental property of the integers that states every non-empty subset of the natural numbers has a least element. This principle is crucial in mathematical induction, a powerful technique used to prove statements about all natural numbers. In this study material, we will explore the WOP, its significance, and its role in mathematical induction.

### Definition of Well Ordering Principle

The Well Ordering Principle states that every non-empty subset of the natural numbers has a least element. Mathematically, this can be expressed as:

- Let $S$ be a non-empty subset of $\mathbb{N}$.
- Then, there exists a least natural number $n$ such that $n \in S$.

### Significance of Well Ordering Principle

The WOP is significant because it allows us to define the concept of the least element in a subset of natural numbers. This principle is used extensively in mathematical induction to prove statements about all natural numbers.

### Mathematical Induction

Mathematical induction is a technique used to prove statements about all natural numbers. It consists of two main steps:

1.  **Base Case**: Prove that the statement is true for the smallest natural number, i.e., $n=1$.
2.  **Inductive Step**: Prove that if the statement is true for some arbitrary natural number $k$, then it is also true for $k+1$.

### Key Concepts

- **Base Case**: The smallest natural number that satisfies the statement.
- **Inductive Hypothesis**: The assumption that the statement is true for some arbitrary natural number $k$.
- **Inductive Step**: The proof that if the statement is true for $k$, then it is also true for $k+1$.
- **Strong Induction**: An extension of mathematical induction that uses the inductive hypothesis to prove the statement for all natural numbers.

### Example

Let's consider the statement: "Every natural number greater than 1 is odd."

- **Base Case**: The smallest natural number greater than 1 is 2, which is even. However, we can choose 3 as the base case since the question is about numbers greater than 1.
- **Inductive Hypothesis**: Assume that every natural number less than or equal to $k$ is odd.
- **Inductive Step**: Show that if the inductive hypothesis is true, then $k+1$ is odd.

If $k$ is even, then $k+1$ is odd by the WOP. If $k$ is odd, then $k+1$ is even. Therefore, if the inductive hypothesis is true, then $k+1$ is odd.

By the WOP, every natural number greater than 1 is odd.

### Conclusion

The Well Ordering Principle is a fundamental property of the integers that allows us to define the concept of the least element in a subset of natural numbers. Mathematical induction is a powerful technique used to prove statements about all natural numbers, and the WOP is essential to its validity. The key concepts of base case, inductive hypothesis, inductive step, and strong induction are crucial to mathematical induction.
