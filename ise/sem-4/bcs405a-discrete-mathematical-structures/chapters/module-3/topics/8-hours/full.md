## Historical Context

---

The 8-hour workday has its roots in the Industrial Revolution. In the late 18th and early 19th centuries, factory owners and industrialists sought to maximize productivity by controlling the work environment and scheduling. The 8-hour day emerged as a compromise between the needs of laborers and the demands of industry.

In 1847, the Working Men's Club Movement in England advocated for an 8-hour workday. The movement gained momentum, and in 1879, the American Federation of Labor (AFL) standardized the 8-hour workday for its members. The 8-hour day became the standard for many industries, eventually spreading globally.

## Mathematical Context

---

From a mathematical perspective, the 8-hour concept can be explored through various lenses:

### Time and Frequency

An 8-hour day consists of 96 minutes (8 x 60/60). This duration can be represented as a rational number:

96/60 = 8/5

In terms of frequency, an 8-hour day can be viewed as a repeating pattern. For example, if you work 8 hours a day, 7 days a week, your weekly work schedule can be expressed as:

7 × 8 = 56 hours

This pattern can be generalized to other time intervals, such as minutes, hours, or even days.

### Ratios and Proportions

The 8-hour day can be expressed as a ratio of work hours to total hours in a day:

8/24 = 1/3

This ratio can be applied to various contexts, such as scheduling, budgeting, or time management.

### Algebraic Expressions

Let's represent the 8-hour day algebraically:

Let x be the number of hours worked per day.
Let y be the number of days worked per week.

The total hours worked per week can be expressed as:

x × 7 = y

Substituting x = 8, we get:

8 × 7 = y
56 = y

This equation highlights the relationship between the number of hours worked per day and the number of days worked per week.
