# **Cartesian Products and Relations**

## **Introduction**

In discrete mathematical structures, a Cartesian product and a relation are fundamental concepts used to describe and analyze relationships between sets. In this study material, we will explore the definitions, properties, and applications of Cartesian products and relations.

## **Cartesian Product**

A **Cartesian product** of two sets A and B, denoted by A × B, is the set of all ordered pairs (a, b) where a ∈ A and b ∈ B.

**Definition:** A × B = {(a, b) | a ∈ A, b ∈ B}

**Example:** Let A = {1, 2, 3} and B = {a, b, c}. Then, A × B = {(1, a), (1, b), (1, c), (2, a), (2, b), (2, c), (3, a), (3, b), (3, c)}

**Properties:**

- A × ∅ = ∅
- (A × B) × C = A × (B × C)
- The Cartesian product is commutative: A × B = B × A

## **Relation**

A **relation** from set A to set B, denoted by R, is a subset of the Cartesian product A × B.

**Definition:** R ⊆ A × B

**Example:** Let A = {1, 2, 3} and B = {a, b, c}. Then, R = {(1, a), (2, b), (3, c)}

**Types of Relations:**

- **Equivalence relation**: reflexive, symmetric, and transitive
- **Partial order**: reflexive, transitive, and antisymmetric
- **Function**: a relation that is both injective and surjective

**Key Concepts:**

- **Domain** (A): the set of all first elements in the relation
- **Range** (B): the set of all second elements in the relation
- **Image**: the set of all second elements in the relation
- **Pre-image**: the set of all first elements in the relation

## **Applications**

- **Graph theory**: relations are used to represent edges and vertices in graphs
- **Database management**: relations are used to organize and store data
- **Computer science**: relations are used in algorithms and data structures

By mastering the concepts of Cartesian products and relations, you will have a solid foundation in discrete mathematical structures and be well-prepared to tackle more advanced topics in mathematics and computer science.
