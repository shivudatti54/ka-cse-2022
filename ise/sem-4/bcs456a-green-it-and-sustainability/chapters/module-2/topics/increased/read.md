# Module 2: Green IT and Sustainability - The Challenge of "Increased"

## Introduction

For engineering students, the technology lifecycle is often viewed through the lens of creation: faster processors, higher-resolution displays, and more efficient algorithms. However, the flip side of this innovation is a set of escalating environmental challenges. This module focuses on the core driver of these challenges: **Increased** demand and consumption. Understanding the "what" and "why" behind these increases is the first step toward designing sustainable technological solutions.

## Core Concepts: The Three Pillars of "Increased"

The environmental footprint of Information and Communication Technology (ICT) is growing rapidly, primarily fueled by three key areas of increase:

### 1. Increased Energy Consumption

This is the most direct environmental impact. The entire ICT ecosystem—from massive data centers to the device in your pocket—requires electricity.

- **Data Centers:** Often called the "factories of the digital age," data centers power the cloud, streaming services, social media, and enterprise applications. Their energy demand comes from two main areas:
  - **Computing Power (IT Load):** The energy used by servers, storage systems, and networking equipment to process and store data.
  - **Cooling Infrastructure:** A significant portion (often 30-40%) of a data center's energy is used for cooling systems to prevent servers from overheating. The PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) is a key metric here—the closer to 1.0, the more efficient the data center.
- **Networks:** The physical infrastructure that transmits data (e.g., fiber optic cables, routers, cellular towers) also consumes substantial energy.
- **End-User Devices:** The proliferation of smartphones, laptops, IoT devices, and peripherals adds billions of energy-consuming devices globally.

**Example:** Streaming a single hour of video consumes enough energy to power a modern LED TV for roughly three years. Now, scale that to billions of users streaming daily.

### 2. Increased E-Waste (Electronic Waste)

E-waste is the fastest-growing waste stream on the planet. It refers to discarded electronic and electrical equipment.

- **Causes:**
  - **Short Product Lifecycles:** Rapid technological obsolescence, driven by marketing and software updates that slow down older devices, encourages frequent replacement.
  - **"Throwaway" Culture:** The reducing cost of devices makes repair often less economical than replacement.
  - **Lack of Proper Recycling:** Only a small fraction of e-waste is formally collected and recycled.
- **Hazards:** E-waste contains precious metals (like gold and copper) but also toxic substances (like lead, mercury, and cadmium). When improperly disposed of in landfills, these toxins can leach into soil and groundwater. Informal recycling practices in developing countries also pose severe health risks.

**Example:** An old CRT monitor can contain up to 4 kg of lead. A modern smartphone contains over 60 different elements, many of which are rare and difficult to extract.

### 3. Increased Resource Extraction and Depletion

The manufacturing of ICT hardware requires the extraction of vast quantities of raw materials.

- **Rare Earth Elements (REEs):** These are crucial for manufacturing hard drives, smartphones, displays, and speakers. Elements like Neodymium, Dysprosium, and Yttrium are geographically concentrated, making their supply a geopolitical issue.
- **Conflict Minerals:** Tin, tantalum, tungsten, and gold (often referred to as 3TG) are mined in conflict zones and sold to perpetuate fighting. Their sourcing raises significant ethical concerns.
- **General Resource Use:** The production of a single computer chip requires an estimated 30+ kg of water, fossil fuels, and chemicals.

The "cradle-to-grave" environmental impact of a device is often highest at this manufacturing stage, _before it's even turned on_.

## The Vicious Cycle

These three increases form a vicious cycle:

1.  **Increased demand** for new devices drives **increased resource extraction**.
2.  **Manufacturing** consumes energy and water.
3.  **Using** the devices consumes more energy.
4.  **Disposing** of them creates e-waste, polluting the environment and wasting the embedded energy and materials.

## Key Points & Summary

| Concept                    | Description                                                                                      | Key Concern                                                                                    |
| :------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Increased Energy Use**   | High demand from data centers, networks, and billions of user devices.                           | Contributes to carbon emissions and climate change.                                            |
| **Increased E-Waste**      | Rapidly growing stream of discarded electronics due to short lifecycles and low recycling rates. | Toxins pollute the environment; valuable materials are wasted.                                 |
| **Increased Resource Use** | High consumption of rare, toxic, and conflict-prone materials for hardware manufacturing.        | Depletes finite resources, causes environmental damage from mining, and raises ethical issues. |

**Conclusion:** The trend of "Increased" is unsustainable. The role of engineers is to break this cycle through Green IT strategies, which we will explore in subsequent modules. This includes designing for energy efficiency, promoting a circular economy (repair, reuse, recycle), and developing software and systems that minimize resource intensity.
