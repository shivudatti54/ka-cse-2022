# Learning Purpose: Increased Number of Separate Functions

**1. Why is this topic important?**
This topic is crucial because the proliferation of separate, dedicated hardware functions (e.g., specialized servers for networking, storage, and security) has dramatically increased the energy consumption and physical footprint of data centers. Understanding this trend is fundamental to identifying key inefficiencies and is the first step toward implementing sustainable IT solutions that consolidate and optimize these functions for a lower environmental impact.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn to identify the various discrete functions within a typical IT infrastructure and analyze their individual resource usage (power, cooling, space). They will explore the historical reasons for this functional separation and be introduced to core modern solutions like virtualization and software-defined infrastructure (SDI), which enable the consolidation of these functions onto fewer, more efficient physical assets.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This concept directly builds on foundational knowledge of data center components (Module 1). It is the core problem that solutions like **server virtualization** (consolidating multiple functions onto one server) and **cloud computing** (shared, pooled resources) are designed to solve. It also links directly to measuring **Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE)** and achieving sustainability goals by reducing e-waste and energy use.

**4. Real-world applications**
Students will be able to audit an organization's existing infrastructure to pinpoint inefficiencies caused by underutilized, dedicated hardware. This knowledge enables them to propose and justify modernization projects, such as migrating to virtualized environments or cloud platforms, resulting in significant cost savings and a reduced carbon footprint for their organization.
