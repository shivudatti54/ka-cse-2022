### **Learning Purpose: Obsolescence & Backward Compatibility**

**1. Why is this topic important?**
This topic is critical because planned and technological obsolescence directly opposes IT sustainability goals. The constant cycle of replacing incompatible hardware and software generates massive e-waste, consumes resources, and increases the carbon footprint of the technology sector. Understanding this conflict is the first step toward creating more sustainable systems.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn to define planned and technological obsolescence and distinguish them from natural innovation cycles. They will analyze how a lack of backward compatibility forces upgrades and evaluate design strategies that extend product lifespans, such as modular architecture, open standards, and upgradable components.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This topic connects directly to life-cycle assessment (LCA), circular economy principles (e.g., reuse, repair, recycling), and green software design. It provides a concrete example of the tension between business models driven by new sales and the environmental imperative of sustainability.

**4. Real-world applications**
This knowledge is applied in IT procurement (choosing longer-lasting, upgradable devices), software development (writing efficient, compatible code), and policy advocacy (supporting right-to-repair legislation). It empowers professionals to make decisions that reduce e-waste and promote a more sustainable technology ecosystem.
