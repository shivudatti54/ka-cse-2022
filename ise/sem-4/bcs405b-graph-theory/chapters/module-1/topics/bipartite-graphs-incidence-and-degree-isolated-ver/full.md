# Bipartite Graphs: Incidence and Degree - Isolated Vertex

## Introduction

Graph theory is a branch of mathematics that deals with the study of graphs, which are non-linear data structures consisting of nodes or vertices connected by edges. Bipartite graphs, a special type of graph, have gained significant attention in various fields, including computer science, social network analysis, and operations research. In this article, we will delve into the world of bipartite graphs, exploring their incidence and degree, isolated vertices, and discussing historical context, modern developments, and applications.

## Definition of Bipartite Graphs

A bipartite graph is a graph whose vertices can be divided into two disjoint sets U and V such that every edge connects a vertex in U to one in V. In other words, the vertices of a bipartite graph are partitioned into two sets, and edges only connect vertices between these two sets.

Example: A social network where people are divided into two groups, friends and acquaintances, is an example of a bipartite graph.

## Incidence and Degree of Bipartite Graphs

## Incidence of a Bipartite Graph

The incidence of a bipartite graph refers to the number of edges incident on each vertex. If a vertex has m edges incident on it, we say it has m incidence.

Example: In the social network example mentioned earlier, if a person has 10 friends, they have 10 incidence.

## Degree of a Bipartite Graph

The degree of a bipartite graph is the sum of the incidence of all vertices. If a graph has n vertices with incidence m, i, and degree d, we say it has d degree.

Example: In the social network example, if there are 1000 people, and each person has an average of 10 friends, then the graph has a degree of 10000.

## Isolated Vertex in Bipartite Graphs

An isolated vertex in a bipartite graph is a vertex with zero incidence. This means that the vertex is not connected to any other vertex in the graph.

Example: In the social network example, if a person is not friends with anyone, they are an isolated vertex.

## Thomson's Conjecture

Thomson's conjecture states that every 3-regular bipartite graph is 2-regular, meaning that every vertex has degree 2, except for some isolated vertices.

Example: If a bipartite graph is 3-regular, it means that every vertex has 3 incident edges.
