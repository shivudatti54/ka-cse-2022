# **Teaching-Learning Process: Chalk and Talk Method / PowerPoint Presentation**

## **Introduction**

The teaching-learning process is a crucial aspect of education, and the method used to deliver information can significantly impact student engagement, understanding, and retention. In this section, we will explore the chalk and talk method and PowerPoint presentation as effective teaching tools for teaching graph theory, specifically planar graphs.

## **Chalk and Talk Method**

The chalk and talk method is a traditional teaching approach where the instructor writes on a blackboard or whiteboard, using chalk or a marker, to illustrate complex concepts. This method has been used for centuries, and its effectiveness lies in its ability to:

- Engage students through visual aids
- Help students understand relationships between concepts
- Facilitate discussion and Q&A sessions

## **PowerPoint Presentation**

PowerPoint presentation is a modern teaching tool that has revolutionized the way instructors deliver information. Its key benefits include:

- Visual appeal: engaging images, charts, and graphs
- Organization: clear structure and concise content
- Interactivity: animations, hyperlinks, and multimedia

## **Teaching Planar Graphs using Chalk and Talk Method**

Teaching planar graphs using the chalk and talk method involves the following steps:

1.  **Introduction**: Write the definition of planar graphs on the board, followed by a brief explanation of their importance in graph theory.
2.  **Graph Representation**: Draw a simple planar graph on the board, labeling its vertices, edges, and faces.
3.  **Kuratowski's Theorem**: Explain Kuratowski's theorem, stating that a planar graph is non-planar if and only if it contains a subgraph homeomorphic to K5 or K3,3.
4.  **Counterexamples**: Provide examples of planar graphs that are non-planar, such as the Petersen graph.
5.  **Discussion**: Open the floor for discussion, asking students to consider the implications of Kuratowski's theorem and how it can be used to determine the planarity of a graph.

## **Teaching Planar Graphs using PowerPoint Presentation**

Teaching planar graphs using PowerPoint presentation involves the following steps:

1.  **Slide 1: Introduction**: Create a title slide with the definition of planar graphs and a brief overview of their importance in graph theory.
2.  **Slide 2-3: Graph Representation**: Create a diagram of a simple planar graph, labeling its vertices, edges, and faces.
3.  **Slide 4-5: Kuratowski's Theorem**: Create a slide explaining Kuratowski's theorem, using diagrams and mathematical equations to illustrate the concept.
4.  **Slide 6-7: Counterexamples**: Create a slide with examples of planar graphs that are non-planar, such as the Petersen graph.
5.  **Slide 8-10: Discussion**: Create a slide with discussion questions, encouraging students to consider the implications of Kuratowski's theorem and how it can be used to determine the planarity of a graph.

## **Historical Context**

The concept of planar graphs has been studied for centuries, with contributions from mathematicians such as Leonhard Euler and Emil Artin. Euler's famous formula, which relates the number of vertices, edges, and faces in a planar graph, is a key result in the field.
