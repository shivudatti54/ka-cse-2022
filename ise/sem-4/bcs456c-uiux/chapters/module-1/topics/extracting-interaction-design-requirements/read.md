# Extracting Interaction Design Requirements

**Subject:** UI/UX Design
**Module:** Module 1
**Topic:** Extracting Interaction Design Requirements

## Introduction

Before a single pixel is designed or a line of code is written, a successful UI/UX project begins with a deep understanding of _what_ needs to be built and _for whom_. Extracting Interaction Design Requirements is the foundational process of gathering, analyzing, and documenting all the necessary information to define a product's purpose, functionality, and behavior. It's the blueprint that ensures the final design is not just aesthetically pleasing but also useful, usable, and desirable for the end-user. For engineers, this phase is crucial as it provides the clear specifications needed for development, reducing costly rework and ensuring the final product aligns with user expectations.

## Core Concepts

The process of extracting requirements is systematic and user-centric. It involves several key activities:

### 1. Stakeholder Interviews

This involves conversations with everyone who has a vested interest in the project's success (e.g., clients, project managers, marketing teams, engineers). The goal is to understand the **business goals**, **technical constraints**, **budget**, and **timeline**.

- **Example:** Interviewing a hospital administrator for a new patient app might reveal a business goal to reduce front-desk call volume by 30%.

### 2. User Research

This is the heart of user-centered design. The aim is to understand the target audience's behaviors, needs, motivations, and pain points. Common methods include:

- **User Interviews:** One-on-one conversations to gain qualitative depth.
- **Surveys & Questionnaires:** Reaching a larger audience for quantitative data.
- **Observation:** Watching users interact with existing systems in their natural context.

- **Example:** Observing nurses reveals they need to input patient data quickly without removing their gloves, leading to a requirement for large, tappable UI elements.

### 3. Creating User Personas

Based on research data, user personas are fictional archetypes that represent key user groups. They include details like demographics, goals, frustrations, and technical proficiency. Personas keep the design team focused on designing for real people, not abstract users.

- **Example:** "Tech-Shy Thomas, 72," a persona whose primary goal is to video call his grandchildren easily, requiring an extremely simple interface with minimal steps.

### 4. Task Analysis

This involves breaking down the goals users want to achieve into a sequence of steps or tasks. This helps identify the necessary functions and the optimal flow for completing them.

- **Example:** The task "book a doctor's appointment" can be broken down into: search for doctor, check availability, select time slot, enter patient details, confirm booking. Each step generates specific functional requirements.

### 5. Defining Functional and Non-Functional Requirements

The findings from the above activities are synthesized into clear requirements.

- **Functional Requirements:** These describe _what_ the system should _do_. They are specific actions or features.
  - _Example:_ "The user shall be able to filter search results by date, rating, and price."
- **Non-Functional Requirements (Quality Attributes):** These describe _how_ the system should _perform_. They are often related to usability, performance, and reliability.
  - _Example:_ "The app shall load search results in less than 2 seconds." or "The checkout process shall be completable in 3 steps or less."

### 6. User Stories and Scenarios

These are concise, simple descriptions of a feature told from the perspective of the user. They follow a common format:

- **Format:** "As a [type of user], I want to [perform some task] so that I can [achieve some goal]."
- **Example:** "As a frequent traveler, I want to quickly re-book my previous order so that I can save time." This story implies requirements for a "re-order" function and a history log.

## Key Points / Summary

- **Foundation of Design:** Extracting requirements is the critical first step that informs all subsequent design and development decisions.
- **User-Centered Process:** The primary focus is on understanding user needs, behaviors, and goals through direct research (interviews, observation).
- **Bridging the Gap:** It translates vague ideas and business objectives into clear, actionable **functional and non-functional requirements**.
- **Essential Tools:** The process utilizes tools like **stakeholder interviews**, **user research**, **personas**, **task analysis**, and **user stories**.
- **Engineering Value:** For  engineers, well-documented requirements provide a clear specification to code against, reducing ambiguity, preventing feature creep, and ensuring the final product is built on a foundation of validated user needs, not assumptions.

By meticulously extracting and defining interaction design requirements, you ensure that the resulting product is not only technically sound but also genuinely valuable and intuitive for the people who use it.
