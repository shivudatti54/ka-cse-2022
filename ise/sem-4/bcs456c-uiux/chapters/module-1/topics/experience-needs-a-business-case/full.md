# **Experience Needs a Business Case**

## **Introduction**

Experience design is not just about creating a visually appealing interface; it's about crafting an emotional connection with the user. However, to justify the investment in experience design, businesses need a solid business case. In this section, we will delve into the importance of a business case for experience design, its historical context, and modern developments.

## **What is a Business Case?**

A business case is a document that outlines the costs, benefits, and expected return on investment (ROI) of a project or initiative. It provides a framework for stakeholders to evaluate the project's feasibility, make informed decisions, and allocate resources. In the context of experience design, a business case helps to:

- Justify the investment in experience design
- Align experience design with business objectives
- Measure the effectiveness of experience design
- Identify potential risks and mitigation strategies

## **Historical Context**

The concept of a business case dates back to the 1950s, when the US Department of Defense introduced the concept of a "business case" to justify military spending. In the 1980s, the concept gained traction in the private sector, particularly in the IT industry. However, experience design as a field did not receive the same attention until the early 2000s, when the rise of user-centered design and Agile methodologies created a growing demand for experience designers.

## **Modern Developments**

In recent years, the importance of a business case for experience design has become increasingly recognized. The rise of digital transformation, customer experience (CX) management, and product-led growth has created a growing need for experience designers to provide a solid business case for their work.

Some key developments that have shaped the modern business case for experience design include:

- **Lean UX**: A methodology that emphasizes rapid prototyping, customer feedback, and continuous improvement.
- **Design Thinking**: A human-centered approach to problem-solving that prioritizes empathy, creativity, and experimentation.
- **Customer Experience (CX)**: A business strategy that focuses on delivering exceptional experiences to drive loyalty and growth.

## **Components of a Business Case for Experience Design**

A comprehensive business case for experience design should include the following components:

1. **Problem Statement**: Clearly define the problem or opportunity that experience design can address.
2. **Goals and Objectives**: Outline the specific goals and objectives of the experience design project.
3. **Target Audience**: Identify the target audience and their needs, preferences, and pain points.
4. **Experience Strategy**: Describe the experience strategy, including the user journey, interactions, and feedback mechanisms.
5. **Design Requirements**: Outline the design requirements, including the design principles, patterns, and standards.
6. **Costs and Resources**: Estimate the costs and resources required to deliver the experience design project.
7. **Benefits and ROI**: Quantify the benefits and ROI of the experience design project.
8. **Risk Management**: Identify potential risks and develop mitigation strategies.
9. **Implementation Plan**: Outline the implementation plan, including timelines, milestones, and dependencies.

## **Case Study: Amazon's Experience Design**

Amazon's experience design is a prime example of a successful business case in action. In the early 2000s, Amazon's management realized that their website was not meeting customer expectations. They developed a business case to redesign the website, with the goal of increasing sales and customer satisfaction.

The business case included:

- A problem statement: "Our website is not meeting customer expectations, resulting in lost sales and revenue."
- Goals and objectives: "Increase sales by 10% and improve customer satisfaction by 20%."
- Target audience: "Prime members and new customers."
- Experience strategy: "Create a seamless and intuitive user experience, with personalized recommendations and fast checkout."
- Design requirements: "Use a clean and simple design, with clear typography and navigation."
- Costs and resources: "Estimate $500,000 for design and development."
- Benefits and ROI: "Estimated ROI of 500% over 2 years."
- Risk management: "Mitigate risk by using Agile methodologies and continuous testing."
- Implementation plan: "Develop a phased implementation plan, with milestones and dependencies."

## **Best Practices for Creating a Business Case for Experience Design**

To create an effective business case for experience design, follow these best practices:

1. **Conduct thorough research**: Understand the customer needs, preferences, and pain points.
2. **Define clear goals and objectives**: Align experience design with business objectives.
3. **Develop a detailed experience strategy**: Outline the user journey, interactions, and feedback mechanisms.
4. **Estimate costs and resources**: Provide a realistic estimate of the costs and resources required.
5. **Quantify benefits and ROI**: Use data and metrics to demonstrate the benefits and ROI.
6. **Identify potential risks**: Develop mitigation strategies to address potential risks.
7. **Create a phased implementation plan**: Break down the project into manageable phases and milestones.
