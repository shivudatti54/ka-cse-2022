# Module 2: Water Borne Diseases & Water Induced Diseases

## 1. Introduction

For an engineer, water is often viewed as a resource for industrial processes, power generation, or urban supply. However, its role in public health is paramount. The quality of water directly impacts community well-being. Contaminated water is a primary vehicle for the transmission of numerous diseases, which are broadly categorized as **water-borne** and **water-related (water-induced)** diseases. Understanding the difference between these, their causes, and their prevention is a critical aspect of environmental studies, as engineers play a key role in designing systems that ensure safe water and sanitation.

## 2. Core Concepts

### Water Borne Diseases

These are diseases caused by the ingestion of water contaminated with human or animal feces or urine containing pathogenic microorganisms (bacteria, viruses, protozoa). The transmission is primarily **fecal-oral**.

- **Cause:** Pathogens enter a water source due to inadequate sewage treatment, broken pipes, or open defecation near water bodies.
- **Transmission:** A person drinks the contaminated water, introducing the pathogen into their digestive system.
- **Key Factor:** The **quality** of the water is the direct cause of the disease.

#### Examples of Water Borne Diseases:

- **Cholera:** Caused by the bacterium _Vibrio cholerae_. Leads to severe watery diarrhea and dehydration, which can be fatal if untreated. Often occurs in epidemics following natural disasters or in areas with poor sanitation.
- **Typhoid Fever:** Caused by the bacterium _Salmonella Typhi_. Symptoms include high fever, weakness, stomach pain, and headache. Spread through food or water contaminated with the feces of an infected person.
- **Hepatitis A:** A viral infection that inflames the liver. Spread through ingestion of contaminated food and water or close contact with an infectious person.
- **Dysentery:** Often caused by the protozoan _Entamoeba histolytica_ (Amoebic dysentery) or the bacterium _Shigella_ (Bacillary dysentery). Characterized by severe diarrhea with blood and mucus.
- **Polio:** A highly infectious viral disease that can lead to irreversible paralysis. While now largely eradicated, it spreads through the fecal-oral route.

### Water Induced / Water Based Diseases

These are diseases where water serves as the **habitat** for the intermediate host (e.g., a snail or insect). The pathogen has a complex life cycle that requires a specific aquatic organism. The disease is not caused by drinking the water but by **contact** with contaminated water.

- **Cause:** The presence of specific aquatic organisms (hosts) in water bodies that are part of the parasite's life cycle.
- **Transmission:** Transmission occurs through skin contact with infested water (e.g., wading, bathing) or through the bite of an insect vector that breeds in water.
- **Key Factor:** The **presence** of the specific aquatic host is the critical factor, not necessarily the fecal contamination of the water itself.

#### Examples of Water Induced Diseases:

- **Schistosomiasis (Bilharzia):** Caused by parasitic worms (blood flukes). The larvae released by freshwater snails penetrate human skin during contact with infested water. The adult worms live in blood vessels, causing abdominal pain, diarrhea, and organ damage.
- **Dracunculiasis (Guinea Worm Disease):** Caused by a parasitic nematode (_Dracunculus medinensis_). People become infected by drinking water containing tiny crustaceans (copepods) that harbor the Guinea worm larvae. This is a prime example of a disease eradicated through simple engineering interventions like point-of-use water filters.
- **Vector-Borne Diseases (where water is the breeding ground):**
  - **Malaria:** Caused by a parasite (_Plasmodium_). Transmitted through the bite of an infected _Anopheles_ mosquito, which breeds in stagnant freshwater.
  - **Dengue:** Caused by a virus. Transmitted by the _Aedes aegypti_ mosquito, which breeds in artificial water containers (e.g., discarded tires, flower pots, stored water tanks).
  - **Filariasis:** Caused by parasitic worms. Transmitted by mosquitoes that breed in a variety of freshwater habitats.

## 3. The Engineer's Role in Prevention and Control

The distinction between these disease types informs different engineering solutions:

1.  **For Water-Borne Diseases:** The focus is on **water quality engineering**.
    - Designing and maintaining water treatment plants (coagulation, sedimentation, filtration, disinfection).
    - Ensuring leak-proof, separate water and sewer pipelines to prevent cross-contamination.
    - Designing and implementing community-level sewage collection and treatment systems (e.g., Activated Sludge Process, UASB reactors).
    - Promoting point-of-use solutions like chlorination or bio-sand filters in remote areas.

2.  **For Water-Induced Diseases:** The focus is on **water resource management and environmental modification**.
    - Designing proper drainage systems to eliminate stagnant water (preventing mosquito breeding).
    - Lining canals to reduce habitats for snails (for Schistosomiasis control).
    - Designing covered water storage tanks to prevent mosquito access.
    - Implementing community education programs on safe water storage practices.

## 4. Key Points & Summary

| Aspect                 | Water-Borne Diseases                                              | Water-Induced / Water-Based Diseases                                         |
| :--------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Primary Cause**      | Ingestion of **microbiologically contaminated** water.            | **Contact** with water hosting intermediate vectors (snails, mosquitoes).    |
| **Type of Pathogen**   | Bacteria, Viruses, Protozoa.                                      | Parasitic Worms (Helminths), Protozoa (in some cases).                       |
| **Transmission Route** | Fecal-Oral route.                                                 | Penetration of skin, bite of an insect vector.                               |
| **Key Intervention**   | **Improving Water Quality:** Treatment, disinfection, sanitation. | **Managing Water Resources:** Drainage, vector control, habitat destruction. |
| **Examples**           | Cholera, Typhoid, Hepatitis A, Dysentery.                         | Schistosomiasis, Malaria, Dengue, Guinea Worm.                               |

**Conclusion:** A clear understanding of the etiology and transmission pathways of these diseases is crucial for civil and environmental engineers. It allows for the design of targeted, effective, and sustainable infrastructure projects—be it a water treatment plant to prevent cholera or a well-designed drainage system to combat dengue—ultimately protecting public health and ensuring the well-being of communities.
