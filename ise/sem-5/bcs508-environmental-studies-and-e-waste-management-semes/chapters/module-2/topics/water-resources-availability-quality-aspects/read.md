## 1. Introduction

Water is the most crucial natural resource, fundamental for sustaining all forms of life and a critical input for virtually every sector of the economy, including agriculture, industry, and power generation. For engineers, understanding water's availability, distribution, and quality is paramount, as our projects and innovations directly and indirectly impact this vital resource. This module focuses on the global and national scenario of water availability, the growing stress on these resources, and the fundamental principles of water quality that engineers must consider.

## 2. Core Concepts

### A. Availability of Water Resources

Despite Earth being the "blue planet," only a tiny fraction of its water is readily available for human use.

- **Global Distribution:**
  - **97.5%** is saline water (oceans and seas).
  - **2.5%** is freshwater.
    - ~**70%** of freshwater is locked in glaciers and ice caps.
    - ~**30%** is groundwater (often deep and inaccessible).
    - **<1%** is surface water (lakes, rivers, reservoirs, soil moisture) and atmospheric water vapor—the primary source for most human needs.

- **Water Stress and Scarcity:** This occurs when the demand for water exceeds the available amount during a certain period or when poor quality restricts its use. India, with **~4%** of the world's freshwater resources supporting **~18%** of the global population, is already a water-stressed nation. Factors exacerbating this include:
  - **Population Growth:** Increased demand for domestic, agricultural, and industrial use.
  - **Urbanization:** Concrete surfaces reduce groundwater recharge and create flood disposal problems.
  - **Industrialization:** High water demand and pollution.
  - **Climate Change:** Alters precipitation patterns, causing droughts and floods.

### B. Quality Aspects of Water

Water quality defines its suitability for a specific use (e.g., drinking, irrigation, industrial cooling). It is determined by its physical, chemical, and biological characteristics.

- **Physical Parameters:**
  - **Turbidity:** Measures the clarity of water. High turbidity (muddy water) reduces light penetration, affecting aquatic life.
  - **Temperature:** Affects dissolved oxygen levels; higher temperature means less oxygen, harming fish and other organisms.
  - **Color and Odor:** Indicate the presence of undesirable organic matter or pollutants.

- **Chemical Parameters:**
  - **pH:** A measure of acidity or alkalinity. Most natural waters have a pH between 6.5 and 8.5. Highly acidic or alkaline water can be corrosive and harmful.
  - **Dissolved Oxygen (DO):** Essential for aquatic life. Low DO (< 5 mg/L) indicates pollution, often from organic waste that decomposes and consumes oxygen.
  - **Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD):** The amount of oxygen consumed by bacteria while decomposing organic matter in water. A **high BOD** is a key indicator of **organic pollution** (e.g., from sewage or food processing plants).
  - **Total Dissolved Solids (TDS):** Measures inorganic salts (e.g., chlorides, sulfates) and organic matter. High TDS makes water unfit for drinking and irrigation.
  - **Toxic Substances:** Presence of heavy metals (Lead, Mercury, Arsenic), nitrates from fertilizers, and fluorides beyond permissible limits poses severe health risks.

- **Biological Parameters:**
  - Refers to the presence of pathogenic organisms like bacteria (e.g., _E. coli_), viruses, and protozoa, which cause water-borne diseases such as cholera, typhoid, and dysentery.

**Example:** The pollution of the Bellandur Lake in Bengaluru is a classic case of quality degradation. Untreated sewage (high BOD) and industrial effluents (with toxic chemicals) discharged into the lake have led to foam formation, algal blooms, and a complete disruption of the aquatic ecosystem.

## 3. Key Points & Summary

- **Scarce Resource:** Only **<1%** of Earth's water is readily accessible freshwater.
- **Water Stress:** India faces significant challenges due to uneven distribution, over-exploitation of groundwater, and pollution.
- **Quality = Usability:** Water quality is defined by its physical, chemical, and biological properties.
- **Key Indicators:** **BOD** is a critical measure of organic pollution. **DO** is essential for aquatic health. **pH** and **TDS** determine suitability for various uses.
- **Engineer's Role:** As future engineers, you have a responsibility to design systems that conserve water, prevent pollution (e.g., efficient ETPs), and promote sustainable water resource management.
