# Project Evaluation: Evaluation of Individual Projects

=====================================================

## Introduction

---

Project evaluation is a critical component of project management that involves assessing the performance and outcome of individual projects. It enables project managers to identify areas of improvement, make informed decisions, and ensure that projects are aligned with organizational goals and objectives. In this chapter, we will delve into the world of project evaluation, exploring various techniques and methods used to evaluate individual projects.

## Historical Context

---

The concept of project evaluation has been around for decades. In the 1960s, the United States Department of Defense developed the Constructive Approach to Program and Project Evaluation (CAPPE), which emphasized the importance of evaluating projects based on their expected outcomes. In the 1970s, the Carnegie Commission on Higher Education introduced the concept of "cost-benefit analysis," which became a widely accepted method for evaluating projects.

## Modern Developments

---

In recent years, there has been a shift towards more comprehensive and participatory approaches to project evaluation. The Project Management Institute (PMI) introduced the Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBOK), which emphasizes the importance of stakeholder involvement in the evaluation process. Additionally, the use of agile methodologies has led to a greater emphasis on iterative and incremental evaluation techniques.

## Cost-Benefit Evaluation Techniques

---

Cost-benefit evaluation is a widely used technique for evaluating projects. It involves comparing the costs and benefits of a project to determine its overall value. There are several types of cost-benefit evaluation techniques, including:

### 1. Simple Cost-Benefit Analysis

Simple cost-benefit analysis involves comparing the costs and benefits of a project in a straightforward manner.

|     | Costs    | Benefits |
| --- | -------- | -------- |
| A   | $100,000 | $150,000 |
| B   | $50,000  | $75,000  |

Simple cost-benefit analysis would conclude that the project has a net benefit of $25,000.

### 2. Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA)

MCDA involves evaluating projects based on multiple criteria, such as cost, time, and quality.

|     | Criteria | Weight | Score |
| --- | -------- | ------ | ----- |
| A   | Cost     | 0.3    | 8     |
| A   | Time     | 0.2    | 6     |
| A   | Quality  | 0.5    | 9     |
| B   | Cost     | 0.3    | 7     |
| B   | Time     | 0.2    | 5     |
| B   | Quality  | 0.5    | 8     |

MCDA would conclude that project A has a higher overall score and is therefore preferred.

### 3. Cost-Effectiveness Analysis (CEA)

CEA involves evaluating projects based on their cost-effectiveness, i.e., the ratio of cost to benefit.

|     | Costs    | Benefits |
| --- | -------- | -------- |
| A   | $100,000 | $150,000 |
| B   | $50,000  | $75,000  |

CEA would conclude that project A is more cost-effective, as it provides more benefit per dollar spent.

## Risk Evaluation

---

Risk evaluation is an essential component of project evaluation. It involves identifying and assessing potential risks that could impact the project's success.

### 1. Risk Categories

There are several categories of risks, including:

- **Project Risks**: Risks that are inherent to the project itself, such as delays or cost overruns.
- **External Risks**: Risks that are external to the project, such as changes in market conditions or competitor activity.
- **Organizational Risks**: Risks that are associated with the organization, such as changes in leadership or organizational culture.

### 2. Risk Assessment Techniques

There are several risk assessment techniques, including:

- **Hazard and Occurrence Analysis (HOA)**: A qualitative technique that involves identifying potential hazards and their likelihood of occurrence.
- **Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA)**: A quantitative technique that involves identifying potential failure modes and their effects on the project.
- **Sensitivity Analysis**: A quantitative technique that involves analyzing the impact of changes in variables on the project's outcome.

## Evaluating Individual Projects

---

Evaluating individual projects involves assessing their performance based on various criteria, such as cost, time, quality, and risks.

### 1. Project Evaluation Framework

A project evaluation framework consists of several components, including:

- **Scope**: The scope of the project, including its objectives and deliverables.
- **Time**: The time schedule for the project, including milestones and deadlines.
- **Cost**: The costs associated with the project, including budget and resource allocation.
- **Quality**: The quality of the project's deliverables, including standards and metrics.
- **Risks**: The potential risks associated with the project, including their likelihood and impact.

### 2. Project Evaluation Tools

There are several project evaluation tools, including:

- **Gantt Charts**: A graphical representation of the project schedule, including tasks and dependencies.
- **Pert Charts**: A graphical representation of the project schedule, including tasks and dependencies, using a probability distribution.
- **Brainstorming Sessions**: A facilitated discussion to generate ideas and identify potential risks.
