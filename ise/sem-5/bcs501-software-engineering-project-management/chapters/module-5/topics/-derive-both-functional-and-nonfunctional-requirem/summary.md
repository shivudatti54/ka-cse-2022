# **Deriving Functional and Non-Functional Requirements**

### Introduction

Functional requirements describe the desired behavior of a system, while non-functional requirements describe the constraints and limitations of a system.

### Functional Requirements

---

- **Definition:** What the system should do (e.g., user interfaces, business processes)
- **Types:**
  - **Functional requirements**: describe the desired behavior of a system
  - **Performance requirements**: describe the desired speed, accuracy, and reliability of a system
  - **Security requirements**: describe the measures to protect the system from unauthorized access or damage
- **Example:** A banking system should allow users to deposit and withdraw money.

### Non-Functional Requirements

---

- **Definition:** Constraints and limitations of a system (e.g., time, cost, scalability)
- **Types:**
  - **Availability requirements**: describe the system's availability and uptime
  - **Scalability requirements**: describe how the system should handle increased load or user growth
  - **Maintainability requirements**: describe how easily the system can be modified or updated
- **Example:** A social media platform should be available 24/7 and scalable to handle millions of users.

### Important Formulas and Definitions

---

- **MOD (Modularity of Design)**: a measure of how well a system is modular and reusable.
- **Y (Yin-Yang)**: a measure of how well a system balances competing requirements (e.g., performance and security).
- **The 5 Whys**: a method for identifying and drilling down into the root cause of a problem or requirement.

### Important Theorems

---

- **The Law of Demeter**: a principle that states that objects should know as little about other objects as possible.
- **The Don't Repeat Yourself (DRY) principle**: a principle that states that data should be stored in one place and accessed from multiple places.
