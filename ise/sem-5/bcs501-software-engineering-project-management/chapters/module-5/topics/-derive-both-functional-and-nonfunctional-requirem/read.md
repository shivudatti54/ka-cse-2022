# **Deriving Functional and Nonfunctional Requirements**

## **What are Requirements?**

Requirements are the specifications of what a software system should do and how it should behave. They serve as a blueprint for the development process, ensuring that the final product meets the needs of its users, stakeholders, and sponsors.

## **Functional Requirements**

Functional requirements describe what a system should do, without specifying how it should be implemented. They focus on the "what" and not the "how".

### Examples of Functional Requirements:

- The system should allow users to login and logout.
- The system should display a list of available products.
- The system should enable users to search for products by name, price, or category.

### Deriving Functional Requirements:

To derive functional requirements, follow these steps:

1. **Identify the problem domain**: Understand the problem you are trying to solve.
2. **Define the user needs**: Identify the users of the system and their needs.
3. **Brainstorm possible solutions**: Generate a list of possible solutions to meet the user needs.
4. **Prioritize the solutions**: Prioritize the solutions based on importance and feasibility.
5. **Refine the solutions**: Refine the solutions to ensure they meet the user needs and are feasible to implement.

## **Nonfunctional Requirements**

Nonfunctional requirements describe the limitations and constraints of a system, such as performance, security, and usability.

### Examples of Nonfunctional Requirements:

- The system should respond within 2 seconds.
- The system should be secure and protect user data.
- The system should be easy to use and navigate.

### Deriving Nonfunctional Requirements:

To derive nonfunctional requirements, follow these steps:

1. **Identify the constraints**: Identify the constraints of the problem domain.
2. **Define the performance requirements**: Define the performance requirements of the system.
3. **Define the security requirements**: Define the security requirements of the system.
4. **Define the usability requirements**: Define the usability requirements of the system.
5. **Prioritize the requirements**: Prioritize the nonfunctional requirements based on importance and feasibility.

## **Benefits of Deriving Requirements**

Deriving functional and nonfunctional requirements has several benefits:

- **Improved system quality**: Requirements ensure that the system meets the user needs and is of high quality.
- **Reduced development time**: Requirements help to identify and address potential issues early in the development process, reducing development time.
- **Increased customer satisfaction**: Requirements ensure that the system meets the customer needs, leading to increased customer satisfaction.

## **Case Study: Online Shopping System**

The following case study illustrates how to derive functional and nonfunctional requirements for an online shopping system.

**Problem Domain**: E-commerce

**User Needs**: The online shopping system should allow users to browse products, search for products, add products to cart, checkout, and track orders.

**Functional Requirements**:

- The system should allow users to browse products by category.
- The system should enable users to search for products by name, price, or category.
- The system should enable users to add products to cart.
- The system should enable users to checkout.
- The system should enable users to track orders.

**Nonfunctional Requirements**:

- The system should respond within 2 seconds.
- The system should be secure and protect user data.
- The system should be easy to use and navigate.

By deriving functional and nonfunctional requirements, we can ensure that the online shopping system meets the user needs and is of high quality.

## **Conclusion**

Deriving functional and nonfunctional requirements is an essential step in software engineering and project management. By understanding the problems, user needs, and constraints of a system, we can create a requirements document that ensures the final product meets the needs of its users, stakeholders, and sponsors.
