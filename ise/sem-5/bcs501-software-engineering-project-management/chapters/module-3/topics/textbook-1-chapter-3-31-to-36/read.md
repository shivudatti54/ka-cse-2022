## **3.1: Introduction to Software Requirements**

- Software requirements are the specifications that describe what a software system should do.
- They are used to guide the development process and ensure that the final product meets the needs of its users.
- Requirements can be described in various ways, including natural language, use cases, and formal specifications.

**Key Concepts:**

- Functional requirements: describe what the software should do
- Non-functional requirements: describe the characteristics of the software (e.g. performance, security)
- User requirements: describe the needs and expectations of the users

**Example:**

Suppose we are developing a banking system and the requirement is "The system should be able to track the balance of an account." This is a functional requirement. A non-functional requirement might be "The system should respond to user input within 2 seconds." A user requirement might be "The system should be easy to use for users who are not tech-savvy."

## **3.2: Types of Requirements**

- **Functional requirements**: describe what the software should do
- **Non-functional requirements**: describe the characteristics of the software (e.g. performance, security)
- **User requirements**: describe the needs and expectations of the users
- **Performance requirements**: describe the expected performance of the software
- **Security requirements**: describe the measures taken to ensure the security of the software

**Key Concepts:**

- Functional requirements are typically described in terms of what the software should do
- Non-functional requirements are typically described in terms of how the software should behave
- User requirements are typically described in terms of what the users expect from the software

**Example:**

Suppose we are developing a web application and the functional requirement is "The system should be able to process orders." The non-functional requirement might be "The system should respond to user input within 2 seconds." The user requirement might be "The system should be easy to use for users who are not tech-savvy."

## **3.3: Requirements Elicitation**

- Requirements elicitation is the process of gathering and documenting the requirements of a software system.
- There are several techniques for eliciting requirements, including:
  - Interviews
  - Surveys
  - Focus groups
  - Use cases
- The goal of requirements elicitation is to gather a comprehensive set of requirements that meet the needs of the users.

**Key Concepts:**

- Requirements elicitation is the process of gathering and documenting the requirements of a software system
- There are several techniques for eliciting requirements, including interviews, surveys, focus groups, and use cases
- The goal of requirements elicitation is to gather a comprehensive set of requirements that meet the needs of the users

**Example:**

Suppose we are developing a new mobile application and we want to gather requirements from potential users. We could conduct a focus group with a small group of users to gather feedback and ideas.

## **3.4: Requirements Analysis**

- Requirements analysis is the process of examining the requirements gathered during requirements elicitation.
- The goal of requirements analysis is to ensure that the requirements are complete, consistent, and unambiguous.
- There are several techniques for analyzing requirements, including:
  - Use cases
  - State machines
  - Use case diagrams
  - Activity diagrams

**Key Concepts:**

- Requirements analysis is the process of examining the requirements gathered during requirements elicitation
- The goal of requirements analysis is to ensure that the requirements are complete, consistent, and unambiguous
- There are several techniques for analyzing requirements, including use cases, state machines, use case diagrams, and activity diagrams

**Example:**

Suppose we are developing a new online banking system and we want to analyze the requirements gathered during requirements elicitation. We could use use cases to examine the requirements and ensure that they are complete, consistent, and unambiguous.

- Requirements documentation is the process of writing down and maintaining the requirements of a software system.
- Requirements documentation should be clear, concise, and easy to understand.

## **3.6: Requirements Management**

- Requirements management is the process of managing the requirements of a software system throughout its lifecycle.
- Requirements management involves:
  - Requirements elicitation
  - Requirements analysis
  - Requirements documentation
  - Requirements validation
  - Requirements maintenance
- The goal of requirements management is to ensure that the requirements are complete, consistent, and unambiguous throughout the development process.

**Key Concepts:**

- Requirements management is the process of managing the requirements of a software system throughout its lifecycle
- Requirements management involves requirements elicitation, requirements analysis, requirements documentation, requirements validation, and requirements maintenance
- The goal of requirements management is to ensure that the requirements are complete, consistent, and unambiguous throughout the development process

**Example:**

Suppose we are developing a new software system and we want to manage the requirements throughout its lifecycle. We could create a requirements management plan that outlines the steps involved in requirements elicitation, analysis, documentation, validation, and maintenance.
