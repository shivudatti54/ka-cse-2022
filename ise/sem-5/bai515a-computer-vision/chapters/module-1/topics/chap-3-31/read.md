# Chap-3 (3.1) Image Formation

====================================

## 3.1 Photometric Image Formation

---

In computer vision, photometric image formation refers to the process of creating a digital image from the physical world. This process involves capturing light and converting it into an electronic signal that can be processed by a computer.

### Light and Its Properties

---

Light is a form of electromagnetic radiation that is visible to the human eye. It has both wave-like and particle-like properties, and its behavior can be described by various physical laws, including reflection, refraction, and diffraction.

- **Intensity**: The intensity of light is a measure of its brightness or strength.
- **Wavelength**: The wavelength of light is the distance between two consecutive peaks or troughs of the light wave.
- **Frequency**: The frequency of light is the number of oscillations or cycles per second.

### Image Formation

---

The process of image formation can be described as follows:

1.  **Light Source**: A light source, such as the sun or a light bulb, emits light into the environment.
2.  **Object**: An object, such as a person or a car, reflects or emits light towards the light source.
3.  **Light Reflection**: The light is reflected off the object and travels through the air towards the light source.
4.  **Image Formation**: The reflected light reaches the light source and is converted into an electronic signal, which is then captured by a camera or other imaging device.

### Image Capturing Devices

---

There are several types of image capturing devices, including:

- **Cameras**: Cameras are the most common type of image capturing device. They use a lens to focus light onto a sensor or film, which captures the image.
- **Sensors**: Sensors are electronic devices that convert light into an electrical signal. They are often used in industrial and medical imaging applications.
- **Scanners**: Scanners are devices that capture images of physical objects, such as paper or fabric.

### Image Sensors

---

Image sensors are the heart of most image capturing devices. They convert light into an electrical signal, which is then processed by a computer.

- **CCDs (Charge-Coupled Devices)**: CCDs are a type of image sensor that use a charge-coupled device to capture light.
- **CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor)**: CMOS sensors use a complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) technology to capture light.
- **Backside Illumination**: Backside illumination is a technique used in CMOS sensors to improve light sensitivity.

### Key Concepts

---

- **Resolution**: The resolution of an image is a measure of its sharpness or clarity.
- **Field of View**: The field of view of an image is the area that the image sensor can capture.
- **Dynamic Range**: The dynamic range of an image is the range of light intensities that the image sensor can capture.

### Example Use Cases

---

- **Security Surveillance**: Photometric image formation is used in security surveillance systems to capture images of people, vehicles, or other objects.
- **Medical Imaging**: Photometric image formation is used in medical imaging applications, such as X-ray or CT scans.
- **Industrial Inspection**: Photometric image formation is used in industrial inspection applications, such as quality control or defect detection.

### Key Terms

---

- **Photometry**: The study of the measurement of light.
- **Imaging**: The process of capturing an image.
- **Sensor**: A device that converts light into an electrical signal.

### Key Equations

---

- **Intensity**: I = E / A (Intensity is equal to energy per unit area)
- **Wavelength**: λ = c / f (Wavelength is equal to speed of light divided by frequency)
- **Frequency**: f = 1 / T (Frequency is equal to inverse of period)
