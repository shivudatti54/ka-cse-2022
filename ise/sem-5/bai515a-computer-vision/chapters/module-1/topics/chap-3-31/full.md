# Chap-3 (3.1): Image Formation

## Introduction

In computer vision, image formation is the process of generating a digital image from a physical scene or object. This process involves capturing light, which is then converted into an electrical signal that can be processed and displayed on a digital device. In this chapter, we will explore the fundamentals of image formation, including the historical context, photometric images, and the role of sensors and detectors.

## Historical Context

The concept of image formation dates back to the early days of photography. In 1826, Joseph Nicephore Niepce invented the first permanent photograph using a process called heliography. This process involved coating a plate with a light-sensitive substance called bitumen of Judea, which was then exposed to light through a camera obscura. The resulting image was a negative image that could be used as a positive image by inverting it.

In the early 20th century, the invention of the camera and the development of 35mm film revolutionized the field of photography. The first commercial cameras were introduced in the late 1800s, and by the mid-20th century, color film was introduced, allowing for the capture of color images.

## Photometric Images

A photometric image is a digital representation of an image that contains information about the amount of light that is reflected or transmitted by the scene. This information can be used to create a digital image that is identical to the physical scene. There are several types of photometric images, including:

- **Intensity images**: These images contain information about the amount of light that is reflected or transmitted by the scene. They are commonly used in applications such as medical imaging and astronomical imaging.
- **Reflection images**: These images contain information about the amount of light that is reflected by the scene. They are commonly used in applications such as surveillance and security.
- **Transmittance images**: These images contain information about the amount of light that is transmitted through the scene. They are commonly used in applications such as atmospheric imaging and remote sensing.

## Image Formation Process

The image formation process involves several steps, including:

1. **Light collection**: Light is collected from the scene using a sensor or detector.
2. **Signal conversion**: The collected light is converted into an electrical signal.
3. **Amplification**: The electrical signal is amplified to increase its strength.
4. **Filtering**: The amplified signal is filtered to remove noise and other unwanted signals.
5. **Sampling**: The filtered signal is sampled to create a digital image.

## Sensors and Detectors

Sensors and detectors play a crucial role in the image formation process. They are responsible for collecting light and converting it into an electrical signal. There are several types of sensors and detectors, including:

- **CCD (Charge-Coupled Device) sensors**: These sensors are commonly used in digital cameras and are capable of capturing high-quality images.
- **CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor) sensors**: These sensors are commonly used in mobile devices and are capable of capturing high-quality images.
- **Photodiodes**: These sensors are commonly used in applications such as optical sensors and are capable of detecting light in a wide range of wavelengths.
- **Phototransistors**: These sensors are commonly used in applications such as optical sensors and are capable of detecting light in a wide range of wavelengths.

## Applications

Image formation has a wide range of applications, including:

- **Medical Imaging**: Image formation is used in medical imaging applications such as MRI and CT scans to create detailed images of the body.
- **Astronomical Imaging**: Image formation is used in astronomical imaging applications such as telescopes to capture images of celestial objects.
- **Surveillance**: Image formation is used in surveillance applications such as security cameras to capture images of people and objects.
- **Remote Sensing**: Image formation is used in remote sensing applications such as satellite imaging to capture images of the Earth's surface.
