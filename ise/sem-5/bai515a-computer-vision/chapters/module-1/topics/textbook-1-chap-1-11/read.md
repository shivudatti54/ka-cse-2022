## **Introduction to Computer Vision**

### What is Computer Vision?

Computer vision is a field of study that focuses on enabling computers to interpret and understand visual data from the world around us. It involves a range of techniques and algorithms that allow computers to process and analyze visual information, such as images and videos, to make sense of the visual world.

### Brief History of Computer Vision

The field of computer vision has its roots in the 1950s and 1960s, when the first computer vision systems were developed. These early systems were primarily focused on image processing and analysis, and were used in applications such as image enhancement and thresholding.

In the 1980s and 1990s, computer vision began to gain more widespread acceptance, with the development of algorithms and techniques that enabled computers to perform tasks such as object recognition and tracking.

Today, computer vision is a rapidly growing field, with applications in a wide range of areas, including:

- **Robotics**: Computer vision is used in robotics to enable robots to navigate and interact with their environment.
- **Image recognition**: Computer vision is used in image recognition applications, such as facial recognition and object detection.
- **Surveillance**: Computer vision is used in surveillance applications, such as monitoring traffic flow and detecting anomalies.
- **Healthcare**: Computer vision is used in healthcare applications, such as medical image analysis and diagnosis.

### Image Formation

---

An image is a representation of the world around us, and is formed through a process called image formation.

#### Photometric Image

A photometric image is a representation of the world around us, where each pixel in the image corresponds to a specific intensity of light. Photometric images can be represented in various formats, such as:

- **Grayscale**: A grayscale image is a photometric image where each pixel is represented by a single intensity value.
- **Color**: A color image is a photometric image where each pixel is represented by a combination of red, green, and blue intensity values.

### Key Concepts

---

- **Image formation**: The process of creating an image through the interaction of light with an object.
- **Photometric image**: A representation of the world around us, where each pixel corresponds to a specific intensity of light.
- **Image representation**: The way in which an image is represented, such as in a grayscale or color format.

### Example

---

Consider a photograph of a person in a room. The image is formed through the interaction of light with the person and the room, and is captured by a camera. The resulting image is a photometric image, where each pixel corresponds to a specific intensity of light.

- **Light**: The light that enters the camera lens and illuminates the scene.
- **Object**: The person in the room, which interacts with the light to form the image.
- **Image**: The representation of the world around us, which is formed through the interaction of light with the object.

### Key Terms

---

- **Pixel**: A single point in an image that corresponds to a specific intensity of light.
- **Intensity**: The amount of light that is incident on a pixel.
- **Image resolution**: The number of pixels in an image that defines its resolution.
