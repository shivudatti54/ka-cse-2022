# Global Context in E-Waste

### Overview

E-waste, short for electronic waste, refers to discarded electronic devices that contain toxic substances such as lead, mercury, and cadmium. The rapid obsolescence of electronic devices, coupled with the growing global demand for technology, has led to a significant increase in e-waste generation. This section provides an in-depth exploration of the global context in e-waste, including historical context, modern developments, and case studies.

### Historical Context

The concept of e-waste dates back to the 1980s, when the first electronic devices began to be manufactured. However, it wasn't until the 1990s that e-waste started to gain attention as a significant environmental concern. The rapid growth of the technology industry in the 1990s and early 2000s led to a surge in e-waste generation.

In the early 2000s, the Basel Convention, an international treaty aimed at reducing transboundary movement of hazardous waste, was signed. However, the convention did not specifically address e-waste, leading to a lack of regulation and oversight.

In 2006, the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) launched the E-Waste: A Global Perspective report, which highlighted the growing problem of e-waste and its environmental and health impacts. The report also called for increased regulation and international cooperation to address the issue.

### Modern Developments

In recent years, there has been a growing recognition of the importance of e-waste management. The Basel Convention was amended in 2014 to include e-waste in its scope, and many countries have established their own e-waste regulations.

In 2019, the European Union launched the Circular Economy Action Plan, which includes measures to reduce e-waste and increase recycling rates. Similarly, the United States has passed the Secure and Responsible Recycling of Electronics Act, which aims to increase recycling rates and reduce e-waste.

### Global E-Waste Generation

According to the UNEP, the global e-waste generation has been growing at a rate of 10-15% per year. In 2018, an estimated 50 million metric tons of e-waste were generated, with the majority coming from Asia.

The top five countries in terms of e-waste generation are:

1. China: 14.1 million metric tons
2. United States: 7.3 million metric tons
3. India: 3.5 million metric tons
4. Japan: 2.3 million metric tons
5. South Korea: 2.2 million metric tons

### E-Waste Management Practices

E-waste management practices vary widely across countries and regions. Some countries have established effective e-waste management systems, while others lack adequate infrastructure and regulation.

In some countries, e-waste is collected and disposed of through informal waste management systems, which can lead to environmental and health problems. In other countries, e-waste is exported to developing countries, where it is often dumped or burned, releasing toxic substances into the environment.

### Case Studies

- **China's e-waste management crisis**: In the early 2000s, China became a major destination for e-waste, with many countries exporting their waste to China for disposal. However, the rapid increase in e-waste generation and lack of regulation led to a crisis, with many Chinese cities experiencing serious environmental and health problems.
- **India's e-waste recycling efforts**: In recent years, India has made significant efforts to develop its e-waste recycling industry. The government has established a number of recycling facilities and has implemented regulations to reduce e-waste generation.
- **Sweden's e-waste management model**: Sweden has developed a comprehensive e-waste management model, which includes recycling, reuse, and proper disposal. The country's e-waste management system is considered one of the most effective in the world.

### Applications

E-waste management has a range of applications, including:

- **Product design**: E-waste management can inform product design, with manufacturers designing products that are more recyclable and less hazardous.
- **Recycling technologies**: E-waste management can drive innovation in recycling technologies, with companies developing new technologies to recycle e-waste.
- **Regulation and policy**: E-waste management can inform regulation and policy, with governments establishing e-waste management regulations and guidelines.
