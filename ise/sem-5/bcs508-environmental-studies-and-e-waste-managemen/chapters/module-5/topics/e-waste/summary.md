# **E-Waste Summary and Revision Notes**

**Definition:** E-waste, also known as electronic waste, is waste generated from electronic devices such as computers, smartphones, televisions, and other electrical equipment.

**Key Points:**

- **Composition:**
  - 70% plastics
  - 15% metals (copper, gold, silver)
  - 10% glass
  - 5% other materials
- **Generation:**
  - 50 million metric tons per year (estimated)
  - 20% of global waste (estimated)
- **Impact:**
  - Pollution of air, water, and soil
  - Toxic chemicals and heavy metals released into the environment
  - Contribution to greenhouse gas emissions

**Environmental Impact:**

- **Toxicity:** E-waste contains toxic chemicals such as lead, mercury, and cadmium
- **Pollution:** E-waste can contaminate soil, water, and air
- **Health Risks:** Exposure to toxic chemicals can cause cancer, neurological damage, and other health problems

**E-Waste Management:**

- **Recycling:** Recycling of metals, plastics, and other materials
- **Disposal:** Landfill disposal of non-recyclable materials
- **Recovery:** Recovery of valuable materials such as copper, gold, and silver

**Important Formulas and Definitions:**

- **E-Waste Hierarchy:**
  1.  Design for recyclability
  2.  Product take-back
  3.  Product reuse
  4.  Recycling
  5.  Recovery
- **Polluter Pays Principle:** The party responsible for the pollution pays for the cleanup

**Theorems:**

- **The Waste Reduction and Recycling Act of 1992:** Established a framework for e-waste management in the United States
- **The Electronics Take-Back Act of 2011:** Required manufacturers to take back e-waste from consumers

**Quick Revision Tips:**

- Understand the composition and generation of e-waste
- Familiarize yourself with the environmental impact and health risks associated with e-waste
- Learn about e-waste management strategies and the importance of the e-waste hierarchy
