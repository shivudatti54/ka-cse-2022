## 1. Introduction

While the name "Mike Cotterell" might be a common point of confusion, the core of this module aligns with the teachings of **Mike Cohn**, a highly influential figure in the Agile software development community. Mike Cohn is a founder of the Scrum Alliance and a author of seminal books like _Succeeding with Agile_, _Agile Estimating and Planning_, and _User Stories Applied_. This module delves into the Agile philosophy and the Scrum framework, heavily drawing from Cohn's practical techniques for planning, estimating, and managing software projects in a dynamic environment. Understanding these concepts is crucial for modern software engineers to deliver value incrementally and respond to changing customer needs.

## 2. Core Concepts Explained

### The Agile Manifesto

Before Scrum, there is Agile. The Agile Manifesto, formulated in 2001, is a declaration of four core values and twelve principles that prioritize:

- **Individuals and interactions** over processes and tools.
- **Working software** over comprehensive documentation.
- **Customer collaboration** over contract negotiation.
- **Responding to change** over following a plan.

This mindset shift from traditional "Waterfall" models is the foundation upon which Scrum is built.

### The Scrum Framework

Scrum is a lightweight, iterative, and incremental Agile framework for managing complex product development. Its key roles, artifacts, and events are designed to foster teamwork, accountability, and iterative progress.

#### A. Key Roles

- **Product Owner:** Represents the stakeholders and the voice of the customer. Responsible for maximizing the product's value by managing the Product Backlog.
- **Scrum Master:** A servant-leader for the Scrum Team. Ensures the team follows Scrum theory, rules, and practices and removes impediments to progress.
- **Development Team:** A cross-functional, self-organizing group of professionals who do the work of delivering a potentially releasable "Done" product increment at the end of each Sprint.

#### B. Key Artifacts

- **Product Backlog:** An ordered list of everything that is known to be needed in the product. It is dynamic and constantly evolves. Its items are often written as **User Stories** (a concept Mike Cohn helped popularize)—simple, customer-centric descriptions of a feature written in the format: "As a [type of user], I want [some goal] so that [some reason]."
- **Sprint Backlog:** The set of Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint, plus a plan for delivering them.
- **Increment:** The sum of all the Product Backlog items completed during a Sprint, integrated with the work of all previous Sprints.

#### C. Key Events (Ceremonies)

- **Sprint Planning:** A time-boxed meeting where the team plans the work for the upcoming Sprint.
- **Daily Scrum:** A 15-minute stand-up meeting for the Development Team to synchronize activities and create a plan for the next 24 hours.
- **Sprint Review:** Held at the end of the Sprint to inspect the Increment and adapt the Product Backlog.
- **Sprint Retrospective:** A meeting for the team to reflect on its own process and create a plan for improvements for the next Sprint.

### Mike Cohn's Contributions: Estimation and Planning

Mike Cohn provided practical techniques to make Agile planning more effective:

- **Planning Poker:** A consensus-based technique for relatively estimating the effort of User Stories. The team uses a deck of cards with numbers like 1, 2, 3, 5, 8 (often Fibonacci sequence) to assign **story points**, which represent the complexity, effort, and uncertainty of a task, not hours.
- **Velocity:** A measure of the amount of work a team can tackle during a single Sprint, calculated by summing the story points of all completed backlog items. It is used for forecasting future work.

**Example:** If a team's average velocity is 30 story points, and the next Sprint's backlog has 5 stories estimated at 5, 8, 3, 8, and 5 points (sum=29), the Sprint is well-planned. If the sum were 40, it would be over-committed.

## 3. Key Points & Summary

- **Agile & Scrum:** Agile is a mindset; Scrum is a specific framework that implements this mindset through defined roles, events, and artifacts.
- **Iterative & Incremental:** Work is done in short, time-boxed cycles (Sprints), producing a potentially shippable product increment at the end of each cycle.
- **Empirical Process Control:** Scrum is founded on transparency, inspection, and adaptation. The three pillars allow teams to manage uncertainty and complexity.
- **User-Centric:** Features are defined as User Stories to keep the focus on delivering value to the end-user.
- **Relative Estimation:** Techniques like Planning Poker use story points for faster, more accurate team estimates than traditional hourly forecasting.
- **Continuous Improvement:** The Sprint Retrospective is a critical ceremony for the team to inspect and adapt its process, fostering a culture of constant learning.

Mastering these concepts is essential for any engineer entering the industry, as Agile and Scrum dominate modern software project management.
