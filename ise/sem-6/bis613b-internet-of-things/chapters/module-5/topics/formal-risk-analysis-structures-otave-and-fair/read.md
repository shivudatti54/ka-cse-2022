# **Formal Risk Analysis Structures: OTAVE and FAIR**

## **Introduction**

The Internet of Things (IoT) has introduced new security challenges, particularly in the Operational Technology (OT) domain. Formal risk analysis structures are essential tools for assessing and mitigating security risks in OT systems. This study material covers two widely used formal risk analysis structures: OT-Analytical Tool for Evaluating Vulnerabilities (OTAVE) and Fault-Infected Risk Estimation (FAIR).

## **OT-Analytical Tool for Evaluating Vulnerabilities (OTAVE)**

### Overview

OTAVE is a formal risk analysis tool developed by the Department of Defense (DoD) to assess vulnerabilities in OT systems. It provides a structured approach to evaluating the likelihood and potential impact of security breaches.

### Key Components

- **Vulnerability Identification**: OTAVE identifies potential security vulnerabilities in the OT system.
- **Risk Assessment**: OTAVE evaluates the likelihood and potential impact of each identified vulnerability.
- **Risk Mitigation**: OTAVE provides recommendations for mitigating identified risks.

### Example

Suppose we have an OT system that uses a remote access protocol for monitoring and control. The system is vulnerable to unauthorized access, which could lead to data breaches or device compromise. Using OTAVE, we would:

1.  **Identify Vulnerability**: Determine the likelihood of unauthorized access and the potential impact on the system.
2.  **Risk Assessment**: Evaluate the likelihood and potential impact of unauthorized access.
3.  **Risk Mitigation**: Implement access controls, such as multi-factor authentication, to mitigate the risk.

## **Fault-Infected Risk Estimation (FAIR)**

### Overview

FAIR is a comprehensive risk management framework that provides a structured approach to evaluating and managing risks in OT systems.

### Key Components

- **Risk Assessment**: FAIR evaluates the likelihood and potential impact of security breaches.
- **Risk Prioritization**: FAIR prioritizes risks based on their likelihood and potential impact.
- **Risk Mitigation**: FAIR provides recommendations for mitigating identified risks.

### Example

Suppose we have an OT system that uses a distributed control system (DCS) for process control. The system is vulnerable to cyber-physical attacks, which could lead to unintended device behavior or system downtime. Using FAIR, we would:

1.  **Risk Assessment**: Evaluate the likelihood and potential impact of cyber-physical attacks.
2.  **Risk Prioritization**: Prioritize risks based on their likelihood and potential impact.
3.  **Risk Mitigation**: Implement security measures, such as intrusion detection systems and network segmentation, to mitigate the risk.

## **Comparison of OTAVE and FAIR**

|                    | OTAVE                                                          | FAIR                                                   |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Purpose**        | Evaluate vulnerabilities and risks in OT systems               | Comprehensive risk management framework for OT systems |
| **Key Components** | Vulnerability identification, risk assessment, risk mitigation | Risk assessment, risk prioritization, risk mitigation  |
| **Scalability**    | Limited to small-scale assessments                             | Suitable for large-scale OT systems                    |

## **Conclusion**

Formal risk analysis structures like OTAVE and FAIR are essential tools for assessing and mitigating security risks in OT systems. Understanding the differences between these tools can help you choose the most suitable approach for your OT security needs.
