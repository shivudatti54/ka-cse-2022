**Introduction**

The Internet of Things (IoT) has revolutionized the way we live and work, with billions of devices connected to the internet and communicating with each other. However, the IoT infrastructure requires a wide range of communication technologies to support the diverse needs of various applications. In this chapter, we will delve into the world of low-power wide-area networks (LPWANs) and explore the key technologies that enable IoT connectivity, including 802.15.4g, IEEE 802.15.4e, IEEE 1901.2a, IEEE 802.11ah, LoRaWAN, and NB-IoT.

**Historical Context**

The history of IoT communication technologies dates back to the 1990s, when the IEEE 802.15 working group began developing the IEEE 802.15.1 standard for wireless personal area networks (WPANs). In the early 2000s, the IEEE 802.15.4 standard for WPANs was completed, which paved the way for the development of LPWANs.

In the late 2000s, the need for LPWANs became increasingly evident, particularly in applications such as smart energy management, industrial automation, and smart cities. In response, the IEEE 802.15.4g standard was developed to provide a more efficient and cost-effective LPWAN solution.

**802.15.4g and IEEE 802.15.4e**

In 2013, the IEEE 802.15.4g standard was completed, which built upon the IEEE 802.15.4 standard by introducing new features such as:

- **Grid-based channel hopping**: This feature allows devices to hop between channels using a grid-based approach, reducing interference and improving overall network performance.
- **Beamforming**: This feature enables devices to focus the transmitted signal on specific devices, increasing energy efficiency and reducing interference.

In 2015, the IEEE 802.15.4e standard was completed, which introduced new features such as:

- **Spatial frequency reuse**: This feature allows devices to reuse spatial frequencies, reducing interference and improving overall network performance.
- **Transmit power control**: This feature enables devices to adjust their transmit power based on the distance from the access point, reducing interference and improving overall network performance.

**IEEE 1901.2a**

In 2015, the IEEE 1901.2a standard was completed, which provides a high-speed, low-latency wireless communication standard for industrial automation applications. The key features of this standard include:

- **High-speed data rates**: Up to 1 Gbps data rates, making it suitable for high-speed industrial automation applications.
- **Low-latency**: Latency as low as 1 ms, making it suitable for real-time industrial automation applications.
- **Reliability**: High reliability, making it suitable for industrial automation applications where device failure can have significant consequences.

**IEEE 802.11ah**

In 2016, the IEEE 802.11ah standard was completed, which provides a low-power, low-bandwidth wireless communication standard for IoT applications. The key features of this standard include:

- **Low power consumption**: Up to 10 mW of transmit power, making it suitable for battery-powered IoT devices.
- **Low bandwidth**: Up to 250 kbps data rates, making it suitable for low-bandwidth IoT applications.
- **Reliability**: High reliability, making it suitable for IoT applications where device failure can have significant consequences.

**LoRaWAN**

LoRaWAN is a low-power, wide-area network (LPWAN) technology that provides a low-cost, low-power solution for IoT applications. The key features of LoRaWAN include:

- **Low power consumption**: Up to 10 mW of transmit power, making it suitable for battery-powered IoT devices.
- **Wide coverage**: Up to 15 km of range, making it suitable for outdoor IoT applications.
- **Low cost**: Low-cost hardware and software, making it suitable for mass-market IoT applications.

**NB-IoT**

NB-IoT is a narrowband internet of things (NB-IoT) technology that provides a low-power, low-bandwidth wireless communication standard for IoT applications. The key features of NB-IoT include:

- **Low power consumption**: Up to 10 mW of transmit power, making it suitable for battery-powered IoT devices.
- **Wide coverage**: Up to 10 km of range, making it suitable for outdoor IoT applications.
- **Low cost**: Low-cost hardware and software, making it suitable for mass-market IoT applications.
