# Module 4: Blockchain Currency - ETH and ETC

## Introduction

In the world of blockchain, currency is more than just a medium of exchange; it is the fundamental fuel that powers decentralized networks. While Bitcoin pioneered the concept of digital cash, Ethereum introduced a revolutionary idea: a blockchain that could execute programmable logic, known as **smart contracts**. This module focuses on the native cryptocurrencies of the Ethereum ecosystem: **Ether (ETH)** and **Ethereum Classic (ETC)**. Understanding the distinction between them is crucial, as it stems from a pivotal philosophical divide in the blockchain community.

## Core Concepts

### 1. Ether (ETH): The Fuel of Ethereum

Ether (ETH) is the native cryptocurrency of the **Ethereum Mainnet**, the dominant and most widely adopted chain originating from the original Ethereum project.

*   **Purpose and Utility:**
    *   **Gas Fees:** Primarily, ETH is used to pay for transaction fees and computational services on the network. Every operation, from a simple transfer to executing a complex smart contract, requires "gas," which is paid for in ETH. This mechanism prevents spam and allocates resources efficiently.
    *   **Store of Value:** Often referred to as "digital oil," ETH is a valuable crypto-asset held for investment and used as collateral within the decentralized finance (DeFi) ecosystem.
    *   **Staking:** With Ethereum's transition to Proof-of-Stake (PoS) consensus in "The Merge," ETH is used to secure the network. Users can stake their ETH to become validators, who propose and attest to new blocks, earning rewards in return.

*   **Governance:** The Ethereum Mainnet is governed by a loose consensus of developers, miners (now validators), and users. Decisions, including protocol upgrades, are managed by the Ethereum Foundation and the wider community.

### 2. Ethereum Classic (ETC): The Original Chain Preserved

Ethereum Classic is the original Ethereum blockchain that continued unchanged after a major philosophical schism. Its existence is a direct result of **The DAO Hack** in 2016.

*   **The DAO Hack and The Fork:**
    *   **The Incident:** A decentralized autonomous organization (The DAO) built on Ethereum was exploited, draining ~3.6 million ETH (a significant portion of the total supply at the time).
    *   **The Response:** The community proposed a **hard fork** to reverse the hack and return the stolen funds. This required changing the blockchain's history, a move that violated the core principle of "**code is law**" – the idea that a blockchain's immutable history is sacred.
    *   **The Split:** The chain split in two. The new chain, which reversed the transactions, became the Ethereum Mainnet (ETH) we know today. The original chain, which did not alter its history and upheld immutability above all else, continued as Ethereum Classic (ETC).

*   **Key Principles:**
    *   **Immutability is Paramount:** ETC's core philosophy is that blockchain transactions should be irreversible and tamper-proof, no matter what. This is its defining feature.
    *   **Technical Differences:** Ethereum Classic has remained committed to the **Proof-of-Work (PoW)** consensus mechanism, unlike ETH's move to PoS. It shares much of its technical foundation with the pre-merge Ethereum.

### Comparison: ETH vs. ETC

| Feature | **Ether (ETH)** | **Ethereum Classic (ETC)** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Origin** | Result of a hard fork to reverse The DAO hack. | The original, unaltered Ethereum blockchain. |
| **Core Philosophy** | Pragmatism; willing to intervene for the greater good of the ecosystem. | "Code is Law"; absolute immutability is non-negotiable. |
| **Consensus** | **Proof-of-Stake (PoS)** | **Proof-of-Work (PoW)** |
| **Market Position** | The second-largest cryptocurrency by market cap; the center of DeFi & Web3. | A smaller, niche cryptocurrency with a smaller ecosystem. |
| **Development** | Highly active, with a large developer community and frequent upgrades. | Smaller, more conservative development community. |

## Example: The DAO Hack in Practice

Imagine a vending machine (a smart contract) that malfunctions and gives out all its snacks for free due to a bug. The ETH philosophy would argue that the owners should be able to "fork" the machine's history to get their snacks (or money) back because the outcome was unintended. The ETC philosophy would argue that the machine's rules, however flawed, were executed correctly, and the outcome, however unfortunate, must stand forever to preserve trust in the machine's incorruptible nature.

## Key Points and Summary

*   **Ether (ETH)** is the currency of the dominant Ethereum network, used for paying gas fees, staking, and as a digital asset. It represents a pragmatic approach to blockchain governance.
*   **Ethereum Classic (ETC)** is the original chain that continued after the 2016 hard fork. It exists to uphold the principle of **absolute immutability** ("code is law").
*   The split between ETH and ETC was not a technical failure but a **philosophical divergence** on how to handle a major crisis, highlighting a core tension in blockchain between immutability and adaptability.
*   ETH has since evolved significantly (e.g., moving to PoS), while ETC has remained more conservative, primarily using PoW.
*   Understanding this split is essential for grasping the broader ethical and philosophical debates within the blockchain space.
