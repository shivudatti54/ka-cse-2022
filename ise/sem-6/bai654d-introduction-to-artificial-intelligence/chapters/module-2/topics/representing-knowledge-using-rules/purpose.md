### **Learning Purpose: Representing Knowledge Using Rules**

**1. Why is this topic important?**
Knowledge representation is the cornerstone of classical AI, enabling systems to store, reason with, and apply information. Rule-based systems are one of the most intuitive and historically significant methods for this, forming the foundation for early expert systems. Understanding this approach is crucial as it provides a clear model for how symbolic AI interprets and processes knowledge to make logical deductions, a paradigm that still underlies many modern systems.

**2. What will students learn?**
Students will learn the structure and components of rule-based systems, including the syntax of "IF-THEN" production rules, working memory, and the inference engine. They will understand the process of forward and backward chaining to derive new facts and achieve goals. This includes hands-on analysis of how a system matches rules against known data to trigger actions and draw conclusions.

**3. How does it connect to other concepts?**
This topic builds directly on foundational AI concepts like search algorithms (which the inference engine uses) and logic (propositional and first-order). It provides a practical implementation of symbolic reasoning. Furthermore, it serves as a critical contrast to subsequent topics like statistical learning and neural networks, highlighting the difference between explicit, rule-driven AI and data-driven, probabilistic models.

**4. Real-world applications**
Rule-based systems are extensively used in areas requiring clear, logical, and auditable decision-making. Key applications include:
*   **Expert Systems:** MYCIN for medical diagnosis, DENDRAL for chemical analysis.
*   **Business Automation:** Loan application approval, fraud detection in banking.
*   **Smart Devices:** Configuring complex network equipment.
*   **Customer Support:** Powering chatbots and troubleshooting assistants.
