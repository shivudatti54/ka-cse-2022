# Research Problem Formulation

## Introduction
Research problem formulation is the cornerstone of scientific inquiry in computer science. It involves defining a clear, focused question that addresses a knowledge gap or practical challenge in the field. For MSc CS students at DU, this process determines the trajectory of their research projects and impacts the novelty and feasibility of their contributions.

In emerging domains like quantum machine learning and ethical AI, precise problem formulation prevents scope creep and aligns research with real-world needs. A well-structured problem statement enables researchers to: 
1. Establish theoretical significance
2. Guide methodology selection
3. Facilitate reproducible experiments
4. Enable meaningful comparisons with prior work

The National Education Policy 2024 emphasizes interdisciplinary problem-solving, making this skill critical for DU students tackling complex challenges like cybersecurity in IoT ecosystems or AI-driven climate modeling.

## Key Concepts
1. **Problem Identification**: 
   - Techniques: Technology forecasting, trend analysis, Delphi method
   - Tools: Bibliometric analysis (using VOSviewer or CiteSpace)
   - Example: Identifying edge computing challenges through ACM Digital Library citation networks

2. **Research Gap Analysis**:
   - Types: Theoretical (missing models), methodological (flawed techniques), empirical (unverified claims)
   - Framework: PICOS (Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcomes, Setting)

3. **Hypothesis Formulation**:
   - Structure: Null (H₀) vs Alternative (H₁) hypotheses
   - Validation: Statistical power analysis for CS experiments

4. **Scope Delimitation**:
   - Boundary conditions: Temporal, geographical, technological constraints
   - Trade-off analysis: Depth vs breadth using complexity metrics

5. **Significance Justification**:
   - Impact metrics: Algorithmic efficiency gains, energy savings, security improvements
   - Stakeholder analysis: Industry needs vs academic contributions

## Examples

**Example 1: Federated Learning Privacy Concerns**
1. **Observation**: Centralized ML models leak sensitive data
2. **Gap Identification**: Existing differential privacy methods reduce model accuracy by 15-20%
3. **Problem Statement**: "Develop a federated learning framework that maintains >90% model accuracy while achieving ε ≤ 2 differential privacy"
4. **Validation**: Compare with Google's TensorFlow Federated baseline using MNIST dataset

**Example 2: Blockchain Scalability**
1. **Context**: Ethereum processes 15 TPS vs Visa's 24,000 TPS
2. **Problem**: "Design a sharding protocol that increases throughput to 10,000 TPS without compromising Byzantine fault tolerance"
3. **Parameters**: Node count (N=1000), malicious nodes (f=0.3N), latency (<2s)
4. **Evaluation**: Deploy on Hyperledger Fabric testnet with Caliper benchmarks

## Exam Tips
1. Use SMART criteria (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) when framing problems
2. Always ground problems in recent literature - cite 3-5 papers from IEEE/ACM journals
3. For applied projects, include quantifiable metrics (e.g., "Reduce inference time by 40%")
4. Differentiate between research *problem* (what's wrong) and *question* (how to fix it)
5. Anticipate methodology limitations in your problem statement
6. Use visual aids: Flowcharts for problem derivation, tables for gap analysis
7. Practice defending problem relevance using DSRM (Design Science Research Methodology)
