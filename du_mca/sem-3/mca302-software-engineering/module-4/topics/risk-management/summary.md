# Risk Management in Software Engineering - Summary

## Key Definitions and Concepts

- **Risk**: A potential future event or condition that, if it occurs, has a negative impact on project objectives. Distinguished from issues by its uncertainty—risks may or may not materialize.
- **Risk Management**: The systematic process of identifying, assessing, prioritizing, and responding to risks throughout the project lifecycle.
- **Risk Register**: A document recording all identified risks, their characteristics, owners, and response plans.
- **Risk Appetite**: The degree of risk an organization is willing to accept in pursuit of its objectives.

## Important Formulas and Theorems

- **Risk Rating = Probability × Impact**: The fundamental formula for prioritizing risks, where higher ratings indicate greater priority.
- **Expected Monetary Value (EMV) = Probability × Impact**: Calculates the average cost if a risk materializes, useful for quantitative analysis and decision-making.

## Key Points

- Risk management follows a lifecycle: Identify → Assess → Plan Response → Monitor and Control.
- Four primary response strategies exist: Avoid (eliminate), Mitigate (reduce), Transfer (shift to third party), and Accept (acknowledge without action).
- Risk identification techniques include brainstorming, Delphi method, SWOT analysis, and checklist review.
- Risk assessment can be qualitative (using categories like High/Medium/Low) or quantitative (using statistical methods).
- The risk prioritization matrix plots probability against impact to categorize risks into priority zones.
- Risk monitoring is continuous—not a one-time activity—throughout the project lifecycle.
- Secondary risks can emerge from implementing risk responses themselves.
- Common software project risks include scope creep, technical debt, personnel changes, and technology obsolescence.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing risks with issues**: Risks are potential future problems; issues are current problems requiring immediate resolution.
2. **Overlooking risk identification**: Failing to identify risks means failing to manage them—thorough identification is foundational.
3. **Ignoring low-probability risks**: Even unlikely risks can have severe impacts and warrant consideration.
4. **Accepting all risks without planning**: Acceptance should be an active decision with contingency plans, not passive neglect.

## Revision Tips

1. Practice applying response strategies to various scenario descriptions—examiners frequently present scenarios requiring strategy selection.
2. Memorize the four response strategies and be able to explain when each is appropriate.
3. Review the risk management process flow and be able to sequence activities correctly.
4. Understand the risk matrix concept thoroughly—it appears frequently in multiple-choice and short-answer questions.
5. Connect risk management to other software engineering topics—estimation, project planning, and quality management all interact with risk considerations.
